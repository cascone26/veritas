# Dei Verbum: Dogmatic Constitution on Divine Revelation

## Preface

**1.** Hearing the word of God with reverence and proclaiming it with faith, the sacred synod takes direction from St. John's words about announcing eternal life. Following the Council of Trent and First Vatican Council, this council establishes authentic doctrine on divine revelation and its transmission, so the world may believe, hope, and love.

## Chapter I: Revelation Itself

**2.** God chose to reveal himself and make known his hidden purpose through Christ, the Word made flesh. Through revelation, the invisible God speaks to humans as friends and invites them into fellowship. This plan of revelation occurs through deeds and words with inner unity: deeds manifest and confirm the teaching, while words proclaim the deeds and clarify the mystery. Christ is both mediator and fullness of all revelation.

**3.** God gives humans enduring witness through created realities. Planning to make known heavenly salvation, he manifested himself to our first parents, promised redemption after their fall, and maintained care for humanity. He called Abraham to make him a great nation, and through patriarchs, Moses, and prophets, taught his people to acknowledge him as the one true God and await the promised Savior.

**4.** After speaking through prophets in varied ways, "God has now spoken to us in his Son." The eternal Word was sent to dwell among humans and reveal God's innermost being. Seeing Jesus is seeing the Father. Jesus perfected revelation through his presence, words, deeds, signs, and wonders—especially through death, resurrection, and sending the Spirit of truth. The Christian dispensation as the new and definitive covenant will never pass away, and no further public revelation is expected before Christ's glorious return.

**5.** "The obedience of faith" requires giving oneself wholly to God freely, offering complete submission of intellect and will while assenting to revealed truth. God's grace and the Holy Spirit's interior help must precede and assist this act, moving the heart toward God, opening the mind's eyes, and giving joy in assenting to truth. The Holy Spirit brings faith to completion through his gifts for ever-deeper understanding of revelation.

**6.** Through divine revelation, God chose to communicate himself and his eternal decisions regarding human salvation—sharing divine treasures transcending human understanding. God, the beginning and end of all things, can be known with certainty from creation by human reason, but revelation permits religious truths accessible to reason to be known by all with ease, solid certitude, and without error.

## Chapter II: Handing On Divine Revelation

**7.** God ensured that his revelation for salvation would endure perpetually in full integrity and be handed to all generations. Christ commissioned the Apostles to preach the Gospel—source of all saving truth and moral teaching—to all people. The Apostles fulfilled this commission through oral preaching, example, and observance, handing on what they received from Christ. Apostolic men committed the message to writing under the Holy Spirit's inspiration. To keep the Gospel forever whole and alive, the Apostles left bishops as successors, handing them teaching authority. Sacred tradition and Sacred Scripture are like a mirror in which the pilgrim Church views God until seeing him face to face.

**8.** The apostolic preaching, expressed specially in inspired books, was to be preserved by unending succession of preachers until time's end. The Apostles warned the faithful to hold fast to traditions learned orally or by letter, defending the faith handed down once and for all. What was handed on includes everything contributing to holiness of life and increased faith. The Church in teaching, life, and worship perpetuates and hands on to all generations all that she is and believes.

This tradition develops in the Church with the Holy Spirit's help through contemplation and study by believers treasuring these things in their hearts, through penetrating spiritual understanding from experience, and through preaching of those receiving truth's sure gift through episcopal succession. Through centuries, the Church moves toward fullness of divine truth until God's words reach complete fulfillment in her. The holy fathers' words witness to this living tradition's presence, whose wealth pours into the believing and praying Church's practice and life. Through this tradition, the Church's full canon of sacred books is known, and the writings themselves are more profoundly understood and unceasingly made active. God, who spoke anciently, uninterruptedly converses with his beloved Son's bride, and the Holy Spirit, through whom the Gospel's living voice resounds in the Church and through her in the world, leads believers unto all truth.

**9.** Sacred tradition and Sacred Scripture have close connection and communication, flowing from the same divine wellspring and merging into unity toward the same end. Sacred Scripture is God's word consigned to writing under divine Spirit inspiration, while sacred tradition takes the word entrusted by Christ and the Holy Spirit to the Apostles and hands it on to their successors in full purity, so that led by the Spirit of truth's light, they may proclaim, preserve, explain, and make it more widely known. The Church does not draw her certainty about all revealed things from Scripture alone. Both sacred tradition and Sacred Scripture must be accepted and venerated with equal loyalty and reverence.

**10.** Sacred tradition and Sacred Scripture form one sacred deposit of God's word committed to the Church. Holding fast to this deposit, the entire holy people united with their shepherds remain steadfast in the Apostles' teaching, common life, bread-breaking, and prayers, so that holding, practicing, and professing the faith heritage becomes a single common effort of bishops and faithful.

The task of authentically interpreting God's word, written or handed on, has been entrusted exclusively to the Church's living teaching office, whose authority is exercised in Jesus Christ's name. This office is not above God's word but serves it, teaching only what has been handed on, listening devoutly, guarding scrupulously, and explaining faithfully according to divine commission with the Holy Spirit's help. It draws from this one faith deposit everything presented for belief as divinely revealed.

Sacred tradition, Sacred Scripture, and the Church's teaching authority, according to God's wise design, are so linked together that one cannot stand without the others. All together and each in its own way, under the one Holy Spirit's action, contribute effectively to souls' salvation.

## Chapter III: Sacred Scripture, Its Divine Inspiration and Interpretation

**11.** Divinely revealed realities contained in Sacred Scripture have been committed to writing under the Holy Spirit's inspiration. Holy Mother Church, relying on the Apostles' belief, holds that the Old and New Testament books in their entirety, with all their parts, are sacred and canonical because written under the Holy Spirit's inspiration, having God as their author and handed on as such to the Church. In composing sacred books, God chose men and while employed by him, they made use of their powers and abilities, so that with him acting in and through them, they as true authors consigned to writing everything and only those things he wanted.

Since everything asserted by inspired authors must be held as asserted by the Holy Spirit, Scripture's books must be acknowledged as teaching solidly, faithfully, and without error the truth God wanted in sacred writings for salvation's sake. All Scripture is divinely inspired and useful for teaching truth, refuting error, reformation of manners, and right living discipline, so the man belonging to God may be efficient and equipped for every good work.

**12.** Since God speaks in Sacred Scripture through humans in human fashion, Scripture's interpreter, to see clearly what God wanted to communicate, should carefully investigate what the sacred writers really intended and what God wanted manifested through their words.

Searching out the sacred writers' intention requires attention to literary forms, since truth is expressed differently in texts that are variously historical, prophetic, poetic, or other discourse forms. The interpreter must investigate what meaning the sacred writer intended to express and actually expressed in particular circumstances using contemporary literary forms according to his time and culture. Understanding what the sacred author wanted to assert requires attention to customary and characteristic styles of feeling, speaking, and narrating prevailing at the sacred writer's time, and patterns humans normally employed in their everyday dealings.

Since Holy Scripture must be read and interpreted in the sacred spirit in which it was written, serious attention must be given to Scripture's content and unity if the sacred texts' meaning is to be correctly worked out. The living tradition of the whole Church must be taken into account along with the harmony existing between faith elements. Exegetes must work according to these rules toward better understanding and explanation of Sacred Scripture's meaning, so the Church's judgment matures through preparatory study. All said about Scripture interpretation is finally subject to the Church's judgment, which carries out the divine commission and ministry of guarding and interpreting God's word.

**13.** In Sacred Scripture, while God's truth and holiness remain intact, eternal wisdom's marvelous "condescension" is clearly shown—that we may learn God's gentle kindness, expressed through words, and how far he has gone adapting his language with thoughtful concern for our weak human nature. God's words, expressed in human language, have been made like human discourse, just as the eternal Father's word, taking human weakness's flesh, was made like humans in every way.

## Chapter IV: The Old Testament

**14.** In carefully planning and preparing salvation for all humanity, God of infinite love, by special dispensation, chose a people for himself to whom he would entrust his promises. First he entered into covenant with Abraham, then through Moses with Israel. To this people which he acquired for himself, he manifested himself through words and deeds as the one true and living God, so Israel knew by experience God's ways with humans. When God spoke through the prophets' mouths, Israel daily gained deeper, clearer understanding of his ways and made them more widely known among nations. The plan of salvation foretold by sacred authors, recounted and explained by them, is found as God's true word in Old Testament books: these books, written under divine inspiration, remain permanently valuable for instruction, so by Scripture's steadfastness and encouragement we might have hope.

**15.** The old covenant's principal purpose was preparing for Christ's coming, the redeemer of all and the messianic kingdom, announcing this coming through prophecy and indicating its meaning through various types. The Old Testament books, according to mankind's state before Christ established salvation, reveal to all humans knowledge of God and humans and the ways God, just and merciful, deals with humans. Though containing some incomplete and temporary things, these books show us true divine pedagogy, express a lively sense of God, contain sublime teachings about God, sound wisdom about human life, and wonderful prayers treasure, with salvation's mystery hidden within them. Christians should receive them with reverence.

**16.** God, inspirer and author of both Testaments, wisely arranged that the New Testament be hidden in the Old and the Old be made manifest in the New. Though Christ established the new covenant in his blood, the Old Testament books with all their parts, caught up into the Gospel's proclamation, acquire and show forth their full meaning in the New Testament and in turn shed light on it and explain it.

## Chapter V: The New Testament

**17.** God's word, which is the power for salvation of all believers, is set forth and shows its power most excellently in New Testament writings. When the fullness of time arrived, the Word was made flesh and dwelt among us in graces and truth's fullness. Christ established God's kingdom on earth, manifested his Father and himself through deeds and words, and completed his work through death, resurrection, glorious Ascension, and the Holy Spirit's sending. Lifted from the earth, he draws all humans to himself, he who alone has eternal life's words. This mystery had not been manifested to other generations as it was now revealed to his holy Apostles and prophets in the Holy Spirit, so they might preach the Gospel, stir up faith in Jesus Christ and Lord, and gather the Church together. The New Testament writings stand as perpetual and divine witness to these realities.

**18.** Among all Scriptures, even New Testament ones, the Gospels have special preeminence and rightly so, for they are the principal witness for the incarnate Word's life and teaching, our savior. The Church has always and everywhere held and continues holding that the four Gospels are of apostolic origin. What the Apostles preached in fulfilling Christ's commission, they themselves and apostolic men, under divine Spirit inspiration, handed on to us in writing: the faith's foundation, namely, the fourfold Gospel according to Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John.

**19.** Holy Mother Church has firmly and with absolute constancy held and continues holding that the four Gospels, whose historical character the Church unhesitatingly asserts, faithfully hand on what Jesus Christ, while living among humans, really did and taught for their eternal salvation until the day he was taken into heaven. Indeed, after the Lord's Ascension, the Apostles handed on to their hearers what he said and did. They did this with that clearer understanding which they enjoyed after being instructed by Christ's glorious life events and taught by the Spirit of truth's light. The sacred authors wrote the four Gospels, selecting some things from many handed on orally or in writing, reducing some to synthesis, explaining some things in view of their churches' situations, and preserving the proclamation's form but always telling us honest truth about Jesus. Their intention in writing was that either from their own memory and recollections, or from those who were eyewitnesses and the Word's ministers from the beginning, we might know the truth concerning matters about which we have been instructed.

**20.** Besides the four Gospels, the New Testament canon also contains St. Paul's epistles and other apostolic writings, composed under Holy Spirit inspiration, which according to God's wise plan confirm matters concerning Christ the Lord, state his true teaching more and more fully, preach the divine work's saving power, tell the Church's story and marvelous growth, and foretell its glorious fulfillment. Lord Jesus was with his apostles as he promised and sent them the advocate Spirit who would lead them into truth's fullness.

## Chapter VI: Sacred Scripture in the Life of the Church

**21.** The Church has always venerated divine Scripture just as she venerates the Lord's body, since especially in sacred liturgy she unceasingly receives and offers to the faithful the life's bread from the table of both God's word and Christ's body. She has always maintained them, and continues doing so, together with sacred tradition, as the faith's supreme rule, since inspired by God and committed once and for all to writing, they impart God's word itself without change and make the Holy Spirit's voice resound in the prophets' and Apostles' words. Like Christian religion itself, all Church preaching must be nourished and regulated by Sacred Scripture. In sacred books, the Father in heaven meets his children with great love and speaks with them; God's word force and power is so great that it stands as the Church's support and energy, faith's strength for her sons, the soul's food, the pure and everlasting spiritual life source. Consequently these words perfectly apply to Sacred Scripture: "For God's word is living and active" and "it has power to build you up and give you your heritage among all those sanctified."

**22.** Easy access to Sacred Scripture should be provided for all Christian faithful. That is why the Church from the very beginning accepted as her own the very ancient Greek translation of the Old Testament called the Septuagint; she has always given a place of honor to other Eastern translations and Latin ones, especially the Latin translation known as the Vulgate. But since God's word should be accessible at all times, the Church by her authority and with maternal concern sees to it that suitable and correct translations are made into different languages, especially from the sacred books' original texts. Should the opportunity arise and Church authorities approve, if these translations are produced in cooperation with separated brethren as well, all Christians will be able to use them.

**23.** The incarnate Word's bride, the Church taught by the Holy Spirit, is concerned to move ahead toward deeper Sacred Scripture understanding so she may increasingly feed her sons with divine words. Therefore, she also encourages studying the holy Fathers of both East and West and sacred liturgies. Catholic exegetes then and other sacred theology students, working diligently together and using appropriate means, should devote their energies, under the sacred teaching office's watchful care, to exploring and expounding divine writings. This should be done so as many divine word ministers as possible will effectively provide Scripture nourishment for God's people, enlighten their minds, strengthen their wills, and set humans' hearts on fire with God's love. The sacred synod encourages the Church's sons and Biblical scholars to continue energetically, following the Church's mind, with the work they have well begun, with constant renewal of vigor.

**24.** Sacred theology rests on the written God's word, together with sacred tradition, as its primary and perpetual foundation. By scrutinizing in faith's light all truth stored up in Christ's mystery, theology is most powerfully strengthened and constantly rejuvenated by that word. For Sacred Scripture contains God's word and since inspired, really is God's word; so the sacred page's study is, as it were, sacred theology's soul. By the same Scripture word the word's ministry also—that is, pastoral preaching, catechetics, and all Christian instruction, in which the liturgical homily must hold foremost place—is nourished healthily and flourishes holily.

**25.** Therefore, all clergy must hold fast to Sacred Scripture through diligent sacred reading and careful study, especially Christ's priests and others, such as deacons and catechists legitimately active in the word's ministry. This is so none of them will become an empty preacher of God's word outwardly who is not a listener inwardly, since they must share abundant divine word wealth with the faithful committed to them, especially in sacred liturgy. The sacred synod also earnestly and especially urges all Christian faithful, especially Religious, to learn by frequent divine Scripture reading the excellent knowledge of Jesus Christ. For "ignorance of Scripture is ignorance of Christ." Therefore, they should gladly put themselves in touch with the sacred text itself, whether through the liturgy rich in divine word, or through devotional reading, or through suitable instructions and other aids which, in our time, with shepherd approval and active support, are commendably spread everywhere. Let them remember that prayer should accompany Sacred Scripture reading, so God and humans may talk together; for "we speak to him when we pray; we hear him when we read the divine saying."

It devolves on sacred bishops "who have the apostolic teaching" to give the faithful entrusted to them suitable instruction in the divine books' right use, especially the New Testament and above all the Gospels. This can be done through sacred text translations, which are to be provided with necessary and really adequate explanations so the Church's children may safely and profitably become conversant with Sacred Scripture and be penetrated with their spirit.

Furthermore, editions of Sacred Scripture, provided with suitable footnotes, should be prepared also for non-Christians' use and adapted to their situation. Soul pastors and Christians generally should see to these' wise distribution in one way or another.

**26.** In this way, therefore, through sacred books' reading and study "God's word may spread rapidly and be glorified" and the revelation treasure, entrusted to the Church, may more and more fill humans' hearts. Just as the Church's life is strengthened through more frequent Eucharistic mystery celebration, similarly we may hope for a new stimulus for spiritual life from growing reverence for God's word, which "lasts forever."
