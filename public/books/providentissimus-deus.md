# Providentissimus Deus

## Encyclical of Pope Leo XIII on the Study of Holy Scripture

**November 18, 1893**

---

## Introduction

**1.** God has bestowed upon humanity a "splendid gift and safeguard" by revealing divine mysteries through supernatural means. While some truths are accessible to reason, supernatural revelation is "necessary because God has ordained man to a supernatural end." This revelation exists in both unwritten Tradition and "written Books, which are therefore called sacred and canonical." The Church has perpetually held that these Scriptures are authored by God Himself, composed under the "inspiration of the Holy Ghost."

**2.** The Pope expresses his desire to promote the study of Holy Scripture, urging both defense and proper understanding of these texts. He commends those already laboring in this cause while exhorting others to contribute their talents. The goal is increasing "the number of the approved and persevering labourers" dedicated to Scripture study, particularly among the clergy.

---

## Holy Scripture Most Profitable To Doctrine and Morality

**3.** Scripture's supreme value lies in its "innumerable benefits" as testimony confirms: "All Scripture, inspired of God, is profitable to teach, to reprove, to correct, to instruct in justice." Christ and the Apostles consistently appealed to Scripture, citing it for proof and doctrine. St. Jerome teaches that "to be ignorant of the Scripture is not to know Christ," while Scripture provides guidance on God, salvation, the Church, morality, and discipline.

**4.** Holy Scripture possesses distinctive "eloquence that is wonderfully varied and rich" derived from divine inspiration. Preachers relying solely on human wisdom produce "feeble and cold" discourse lacking the "fire of the utterance of God." The sacred writers throughout history acknowledge Scripture as their chief source of credibility and power.

**5.** The Church's Fathers describe Scripture as an "inexhaustible treasury of heavenly doctrine" and "overflowing fountain of salvation." St. Jerome counsels that "holy reading be always in thy hand." Such study requires not only intellectual effort but also "piety and an innocent life," as the Holy Spirit's "light and grace" come through "humble prayer and guarded by holiness of life."

---

## What the Bible Owes to the Catholic Church

**6.** The Church has demonstrated "watchful care" through laws ensuring Scripture's availability and study. Cathedral Churches and monasteries maintain exposition by capable interpreters, while the faithful receive "saving words of the Gospel" regularly. This institutional commitment has preserved Scripture's cultivation across centuries.

**7.** From Christianity's beginning, renowned holy figures devoted themselves to Scripture study. The great Fathers—Origen, Augustine, Jerome, Chrysostom, and countless others—produced commentaries that earned those centuries the title "golden age of biblical exegesis." Through the Middle Ages and beyond, the Church maintained this scholarly tradition, producing editions and defenses of Scripture's integrity.

**8.** From Clement V forward, the Church established chairs for Oriental languages and promoted biblical studies. The invention of printing dramatically expanded Scripture's availability. Successive popes from Pius IV to Clement VIII produced celebrated editions of the Vulgate and Septuagint. The Church "has never failed in taking due measures to bring the Scriptures within reach of her children."

---

## How to Study Holy Scripture

**9.** Effective biblical study requires proper counsel suited to contemporary challenges.

**10.** Present adversaries include Rationalists who "deny that there is any such thing as revelation or inspiration, or Holy Scripture at all." They characterize biblical narratives as "stupid fables and lying stories" and dismiss miracles as natural effects. These critics disguise their "rashness and pride" under honorable titles while spreading "deadly poison" through publications and educational institutions captured from the Church. This assault particularly targets "the more ignorant masses of the people," especially the young.

**11.** Seminary instruction in Scripture demands careful professorial selection. Teachers must demonstrate genuine familiarity with biblical texts, combined with appropriate learning and proven character.

**12.** Institutions should cultivate succeeding generations of Scripture professors by selecting promising theological students for exclusive devotion to biblical study, providing complete research facilities.

**13.** Introductory courses must systematically train students to both defend Scripture and penetrate its meaning, addressing integrity, authority, sense-determination, and objection-refutation. Subsequent instruction in interpretation emphasizes practical application for "religion and piety." While examining complete books proves ideal, selected passages should receive thorough treatment enabling lifelong Scripture engagement. The Vulgate serves as primary text per Council of Trent decree, though "other versions which Christian antiquity has approved" merit consultation, particularly ancient manuscripts. Careful textual discussion must precede meaning investigation, following St. Augustine's principle that commentators present authorial content rather than personal preferences.

---

## Holy Scripture and Theology; Interpretation; the Fathers

**14.** Beyond ordinary interpretive challenges, Scripture contains divine mysteries "beyond the power and scope of the reason of man." The literal sense frequently admits other meanings adapted to "illustrate dogma or to confirm morality." Scripture thus wrapped in "religious obscurity" requiring guidance; St. Irenaeus established that truth dwells where "the charismata of God" exist—with those possessing Apostolic succession. The Council of Vatican affirmed that "the true sense of Holy Scripture" is "that which has been held and is held by our Holy Mother the Church." This decree protects rather than restrains biblical science, leaving "a wide field" for scholarly advancement. Scholars may prepare for Church judgment on undefined passages, while work on defined passages strengthens Church proclamation through clearer exposition or vigorous defense. Catholic commentators must interpret Church-authenticated passages identically with demonstrated hermeneutical propriety. Undefined passages require adherence to "the analogy of faith" and Church doctrine as "supreme law," since God cannot contradict Himself. The Scripture professor requires thorough theological knowledge and extensive Patristic familiarity. St. Augustine justly protests that mastering Scripture demands studying "those who have interpreted them," following ancient precedent of acquiring understanding through "the writings and authority of the ancients." The Fathers transmit interpretation "received in direct line from the Apostles." Their unanimous biblical interpretation concerning faith or morals possesses "supreme authority," as such unanimity evidences Apostolic transmission. Patristic opinions also carry "very great weight" through their theological expertise, sanctity, and divine illumination.

**15.** Scholars may legitimately advance beyond Patristic teaching when "just cause exists," carefully observing St. Augustine's rule: avoid departing from "the literal and obvious sense, except only where reason makes it untenable or necessity requires." This rule demands particular strictness "when the thirst for novelty and unrestrained freedom of thought make the danger of error most real." Allegorical interpretations, especially when justified literarily and supported by multiple authorities, merit consideration, as the Church received this method from the Apostles and employs it liturgically. Other Catholic interpreters' authority, though lesser than the Fathers, remains valuable as Scripture study continually advances. However, consulting non-Catholic works to the "detriment of sound doctrine" risks faith itself, since Scripture's genuine sense exists "nowhere incorrupt outside of the Church"—non-Catholic writers merely "gnaw the bark" without reaching the substance.

**16.** All theology should be "pervaded and animated by the use of the divine Word of God," following Patristic example of establishing faith articles through Sacred Writings. St. Thomas of Aquin exemplifies this approach, recognizing that "Theology does not receive her first principles from any other science, but immediately from God by revelation." Scripture provides proofs unavailable through reasoning alone; against rationalist opponents denying revelation entirely, biblical authority provides the only recourse. Beginners require thorough philosophical and theological grounding, particularly under Thomistic guidance, to pursue Scripture studies safely and advance satisfactorily in both biblical and positive theology.

---

## The Authority of Holy Scripture; Modern Criticism; Physical Science

**17.** Beyond exposition and illustration, Scripture requires "the strongest possible" authority maintenance through "the living and proper magisterium of the Church." The Church's credibility rests partly on Scripture's trustworthiness as human testimony, establishing Christ's divinity, His mission, hierarchical Church institution, and Petrine primacy. Clergy must prepare for this contest using both "armour of God" and "modern methods of attack." Priests must be "acquainted with the engines and the arts of all" opponents—understanding that ignorance of any "trick and turn of war" permits the "devil" entry to destroy the flock. Defense requires Oriental language study and criticism mastery. Professors and students should cultivate the languages wherein Sacred Books were originally composed; academic institutions should establish chairs in Semitic and related subjects. Biblical scholars especially must master "the art of true criticism." An inept "higher criticism" pretending to judge books' "origin, integrity and authority" from "internal indications alone" has caused "great detriment of religion." Historical witness proves primary in historical questions; internal evidence rarely holds great value except as confirmation. Elevating internal evidence falsely encourages enemies attacking Scripture more boldly, making critics' "higher criticism" merely reflect "the bias and the prejudice of the critics." This supposed science produces "disagreement and dissension," characteristic "notes of error," eliminating prophecy, miracle, and all supernaturals from Scripture through false philosophy and rationalism.

**18.** Physical science misuse presents particular dangers, as enemies scrutinize Scripture seeking writer errors to vilify its contents. Such attacks particularly endanger the ignorant masses and the young, who, losing reverence for Scripture at any point, readily abandon it altogether. Improperly taught science corrupts youth morality and true philosophy. Scripture professors require natural science knowledge for detecting and refuting such attacks. Genuine discord between theologian and physicist never occurs when each remains within proper boundaries, both avoiding "rash assertions." St. Augustine provides the rule: Whatever demonstrably true in physical nature must reconcile with Scripture; whatever contradicts Scripture we must prove false or unhesitatingly reject. Scripture's authors, more accurately the Holy Ghost "Who spoke by them, did not intend to teach men...the essential nature of the things of the visible universe, things in no way profitable unto salvation." They described things figuratively or in common contemporary usage, even now employed by eminent scientists. "Ordinary speech primarily and properly describes what comes under the senses," similarly to how sacred writers "went by what sensibly appeared."

**19.** Scripture's defense need not defend all Patristic or modern interpreter opinions, as Fathers sometimes expressed contemporary scientific ideas now abandoned as incorrect. Interpreters must carefully distinguish what the Fathers established as faith-related from their divergent opinions on non-obligatory matters. St. Thomas rightly notes: "When philosophers are agreed upon a point, and it is not contrary to our faith, it is safer...neither [to] lay down such a point as a dogma of faith...nor [to] reject it as against faith." Catholic interpreters must demonstrate that established scientific facts don't contradict rightly-explained Scripture, remembering that much formerly certain knowledge subsequently faced rejection. When physics writers carry erroneous teaching into philosophy, philosophers should address such errors.

---

## Inspiration Incompatible with Error

**20.** These principles apply to cognate sciences, particularly History. Many laboriously investigate antiquity monuments, national manners and institutions, seeking Scripture mistakes to weaken its authority. Some display extreme hostility and unfairness—accepting profane books unhesitatingly while dismissing Scripture with minimal discussion upon suspicion of error. Copyist mistakes occur; such questions require careful consideration before admitting error, restricting admission to passages with clear proof. Passage ambiguity may be clarified through good hermeneutical methods. However, it is "absolutely wrong and forbidden, either to narrow inspiration to certain parts only of Holy Scripture, or to admit that the sacred writer has erred." Those claiming divine inspiration concerns only faith and morals—not truth-value depending on authorial intent—propose an "intolerable system." All Church-received sacred canonical books were "written wholly and entirely, with all their parts, at the dictation of the Holy Ghost; and so far is it from being possible that any error can co-exist with inspiration, that inspiration not only is essentially incompatible with error, but excludes and rejects it as absolutely and necessarily as it is impossible that God Himself, the supreme Truth, can utter that which is not true." This "ancient and unchanging faith" received solemn definition in Florence and Trent Councils, confirmed and more expressly formulated by Vatican Council. Vatican Council declares: "The Books of the Old and New Testament, whole and entire, with all their parts...are to be received as sacred and canonical...because, having been written under the inspiration of the Holy Ghost, they have God for their author."

The Holy Ghost employed human instruments without implying human fallibility; rather, through "supernatural power, He so moved and impelled them to write...that the things which He ordered, and those only, they, first, rightly understood, then willed faithfully to write down, and finally expressed in apt words and with infallible truth." The Fathers universally held this. St. Augustine states: "since they wrote the things which He showed and uttered to them, it cannot be pretended that He is not the writer; for His members executed what their Head dictated." St. Gregory the Great pronounces: "Most superfluous it is to inquire who wrote these things—we loyally believe the Holy Ghost to be the Author of the book."

**21.** Those maintaining error-possibility in genuine sacred passages "either pervert the Catholic notion of inspiration, or make God the author of such error." All Fathers and Doctors unanimously affirmed that divine writings, as left by hagiographers, are "free from all error," laboring earnestly to reconcile apparently variant passages—the very passages "higher criticism" employs. They unanimously established that writings "in their entirety and in all their parts were equally from the afflatus of Almighty God, and that God, speaking by the sacred writers, could not set down anything but what was true." St. Augustine summarizes their teaching: "it is only to those Books of Scripture which are now called canonical that I have learned to pay such honour and reverence as to believe most firmly that none of their writers has fallen into any error. And if in these Books I meet anything which seems contrary to truth, I shall not hesitate to conclude either that the text is faulty, or that the translator has not expressed the meaning of the passage, or that I myself do not understand."

**22.** Scripture's complete defense requires cooperation beyond commentators and theologians—all Catholics distinguished in learning should contribute. The Church has never lacked accomplished children's support; may their services increase! Nothing seems more needful than truth finding "more powerful and more numerous" defenders than enemies. Learned men's bold proclamation impresses masses with truth-respect, while silencing objectors' "bitter tongues" claiming faith opposes science. Those whom "Almighty God has bestowed...great natural talent" should select suitable study branches, excelling therein to repulse Word-of-God assaults with distinction. The Pope praises Catholics forming societies and contributing funds enabling studious learned men with complete study assistance—"excellent fashion of investing money, well-suited to the times!" As public Catholic study patronage diminishes, private liberality should increase, those divinely enriched willingly safeguarding revealed doctrine treasure.

---

## Summary

**23.** For these endeavors' real advantage, scholars must steadfastly follow laid-down principles. They must loyally maintain that God, "Creator and Ruler of all things, is also the Author of the Scriptures—and that therefore nothing can be proved either by physical science or archaeology which can really contradict the Scriptures." Where apparent contradiction meets, removal efforts should follow. Judicious theologians and commentators should clarify passage meaning; hostile arguments require careful weighing. Even if difficulty persists and discrepancy seems to remain, "the contest must not be abandoned; truth cannot contradict truth, and we may be sure that some mistake has been made either in the interpretation of the sacred words, or in the polemical discussion itself; and if no such mistake can be detected, we must then suspend judgment for the time being." Numerous objections perseveringly directed against Scripture have proven futile and disappeared; interpretations have been rectified through careful investigation. "As time goes on, mistaken views die and disappear; but truth remaineth and groweth stronger for ever and ever." As no one should presume complete Scripture understanding (even St. Augustine confessed more ignorance than knowledge), encountering seemingly insoluble passages requires heeding Augustine's "cautious rule: It is better even to be oppressed by unknown but useful signs, than to interpret them uselessly and thus to throw off the yoke only to be caught in the trap of error."

**24.** Such admonitions and instructions are offered to Bishops for observation and practice with "all due reverence and exactness," proving gratitude for God's Word communication and achieving greatly desired results, especially regarding Church student training—the Pope's "great solicitude" and Church's hope. Bishops should exert themselves ensuring these studies flourish in Seminaries and educational institutions under their jurisdiction, under Church direction, following "salutary teaching and example of the Holy Fathers and the laudable traditions of antiquity," extending and widening as "the interests and glory of truth may require—the interest of that Catholic Truth which comes from above, the never-failing source of man's salvation." The Pope paternally admonishes Church students and ministers always approaching Sacred Writings with "reverence and piety"; profitable understanding requires abandoning "arrogance of earthly science" and exciting "the holy desire for that wisdom which is from above." Thus admitted intelligence, illuminated and strengthened, will "acquire a marvellous facility in detecting and avoiding the fallacies of human science, and in gathering and using for eternal salvation all that is valuable and precious; whilst at the same time the heart will grow warm, and will strive with ardent longing to advance in virtue and in divine love. Blessed are they who examine His testimonies; they shall seek Him with their whole heart."

**25.** Filled with hope in divine assistance and trusting pastoral solicitude, the Pope imparts Apostolic Benediction "to you all, and to the Clergy and the whole flock entrusted to you...in Our Lord."

---

*Given at St. Peter's, Rome, November 18, 1893, the eighteenth year of Leo XIII's Pontificate.*

**LEO XIII**
