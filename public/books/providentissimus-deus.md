# Providentissimus Deus

## Encyclical of Pope Leo XIII on the Study of Holy Scripture

**November 18, 1893**

---

## Introduction

**1.** God has bestowed upon humanity a splendid gift through supernatural revelation, making known "the hidden mysteries of His Divinity, His wisdom and His mercy." This revelation exists in both unwritten Tradition and written Books, which are "called sacred and canonical because, being written under the inspiration of the Holy Ghost, they have God for their author." The Church has perpetually held that God composed the Canonical Scriptures through the Prophets, His own mouth, and the Apostles, delivering them as "His own oracles and words—a Letter, written by our heavenly Father."

**2.** The Pope expresses his desire to advance the study of Holy Scripture and ensure it remains "safely and abundantly accessible to the flock of Jesus Christ" while protecting it from those who "impiously and openly assail the Scriptures, or of those who are led astray into fallacious and imprudent novelties." He urges clergy and learned Catholics to devote themselves to defending and explaining the sacred writings.

---

## Holy Scripture Most Profitable to Doctrine and Morality

**3.** Scripture is worthy of commendation because it is "profitable to teach, to reprove, to correct, to instruct in justice." Christ Himself and the Apostles regularly appealed to Scripture to prove doctrine and defend against objectors. Clergy should understand that Scripture provides "more ample matter or more abundant exhortation" on God, His glory, the Savior, the Church, and moral discipline than any other source.

**4.** The peculiar power of Holy Scripture derives from "the inspiration of the Holy Ghost," which gives authority to sacred orators and "communicates force and power to his eloquence." Preachers who rely only on human reasoning produce discourse that is "feeble and cold" compared to the power of God's Word.

**5.** The Holy Fathers recognized Scripture as "an inexhaustible treasury of heavenly doctrine" and urged constant study. St. Jerome advised that "holy reading be always in thy hand," while St. Gregory the Great insisted those engaged in preaching "must never cease the study of the written word of God." St. Augustine warned that understanding Scripture requires not only "docility and attention, but also piety and an innocent life."

---

## What the Bible Owes to the Catholic Church

**6.** The Church has demonstrated "watchful care" by establishing laws ensuring Scripture remains accessible. She prescribes that clergy read and reflect upon substantial portions daily, orders exposition and interpretation in Cathedral Churches and monasteries, and ensures the faithful receive Gospel instruction regularly.

**7.** From Christianity's beginning, those renowned for holiness and learning have devoted themselves to Scripture study. The Fathers at Alexandria and Antioch established catechetical schools focused on Scripture interpretation. Notable Eastern Fathers include Origen, St. Cyril, Eusebius, St. Basil the Great, the two St. Gregories, and St. John Chrysostom. Western luminaries include Tertullian, St. Cyprian, St. Hilary, St. Ambrose, St. Leo the Great, St. Gregory the Great, St. Augustine, and St. Jerome, "whom the Church...by reason of his pre-eminent knowledge of Scripture...the name of the 'great Doctor.'"

Through the medieval period, clergy preserved ancient biblical knowledge through glosses and commentaries. St. Bernard excelled in allegorical exposition. The scholastics advanced interpretation through careful textual analysis, distinctions of meaning, and investigation of authorial intent. St. Thomas of Aquin remains preeminent among them.

**8.** Following the invention of printing and revival of Greek learning, biblical editions proliferated. Catholic scholars between the Council of Vienne and Council of Trent produced excellent work. The Vulgate editions prepared under Pius IV through Clement VIII, authorized by Sixtus V and Clement, remain in common use. The Church has consistently demonstrated that "she has never required, nor does she now require, any stimulation from without" regarding Scripture study.

---

## How to Study Holy Scripture

**9.** The Pope now provides counsel for successful biblical science study.

**10.** First, scholars must understand their opponents. Earlier heretics relied on private judgment against Church tradition. Now, Rationalists reject revelation, inspiration, and Scripture itself, viewing biblical narratives as "stupid fables and lying stories" and miracles as "mere tricks and myths." They attempt to disguise their views under the banner of "free science." These attacks, spread through "books, pamphlets, and newspapers," particularly target "the more ignorant masses of the people" and corrupt youth in schools "by ridicule and scurrilous jesting."

**11.** Seminaries and academic institutions must place Scripture study "on such a footing as its own importance and the circumstances of the time demand." Professors must be carefully chosen based on demonstrated "love of, and their long familiarity with, the Bible, and by suitable learning and study."

**12.** Institutions should select promising young theologians for exclusive Scripture study, providing complete facilities for their development. Such prepared professors can undertake their work "with confidence."

**13.** Professors should begin by teaching Introduction—how to prove Scripture's integrity and authority, investigate its true sense, and refute objections. This foundation must accompany theological study throughout. Professors should then address Interpretation, the fruitful division concerned with using God's Word "for the advantage of religion and piety."

While not every biblical book can be separately studied, selected passages should receive suitable treatment, teaching students to "love and use the remainder of the sacred Book during the whole of their lives." The Vulgate serves as the primary text, as decreed by the Council of Trent, though other Christian antiquity's versions, particularly ancient manuscripts, warrant consultation for clarification. However, "the greatest prudence is required," remembering that the commentator's office is "to set forth not what he himself would prefer, but what his author says."

---

## Holy Scripture and Theology; Interpretation; the Fathers

**14.** Scripture presents unique interpretive challenges beyond those of other ancient writings. The biblical language expresses "divine mysteries and all that is related to them" under the Holy Ghost's inspiration, sometimes containing "a fullness and a hidden depth of meaning which the letter hardly expresses." Scripture admits multiple senses adapted to illustrate doctrine and confirm morality.

The sacred writings possess "a certain religious obscurity," requiring a guide to enter their interior. This divine arrangement encourages investigation with greater ardor, allowing difficult truths to "sink more deeply into the mind and heart," and helps men understand that "God has delivered the Holy Scriptures to the Church, and that in reading and making use of His Word, they must follow the Church as their guide."

St. Irenaeus established that truth resided where God's gifts were found and that "Holy Scripture was safely interpreted by those who had the Apostolic succession." The Vatican Council renews Trent's decree: "in things of faith and morals, belonging to the building up of Christian doctrine, that is to be considered the true sense of Holy Scripture which has been held and is held by our Holy Mother the Church, whose place it is to judge of the true sense and interpretation of the Scriptures."

This wise decree does not prevent biblical science but "protects it from error, and largely assists its real progress." Private scholars may contribute valuable work in passages not yet receiving definitive Church interpretation, and may clarify or defend already-defined passages. The Catholic commentator's first duty is interpreting passages receiving authentic Church interpretation "in that identical sense, and to prove...that sound hermeneutical laws admit of no other interpretation."

In other passages, "the analogy of faith should be followed, and Catholic doctrine...should be held as the supreme law," since the same God authored both Scripture and the Church's doctrine. "Hence it follows that all interpretation is foolish and false which either makes the sacred writers disagree one with another, or is opposed to the doctrine of the Church."

Scripture professors must master theology's entirety and study thoroughly "the commentaries of the Holy Fathers and Doctors, and other interpreters of mark." St. Augustine justly complains: "If there is no branch of teaching...which does not require a master, what can be a greater sign of rashness and pride than to refuse to study the Books of the divine mysteries by the help of those who have interpreted them?"

The Holy Fathers acquired understanding "not by their own lights and ideas, but from the writings and authority of the ancients, who...received the rule of interpretation in direct line from the Apostles." The Fathers possess "supreme authority, whenever they all interpret in one and the same manner any text of the Bible, as pertaining to the doctrine of faith or morals; for their unanimity clearly evinces that such interpretation has come down from the Apostles."

**15.** Scholars need not consider investigation beyond the Fathers forbidden "when just cause exists," provided they "carefully observe the rule so wisely laid down by St. Augustine—not to depart from the literal and obvious sense, except only where reason makes it untenable or necessity requires." This adherence becomes especially necessary "when the thirst for novelty and unrestrained freedom of thought make the danger of error most real."

Passages the Fathers understood allegorically or figuratively should not be neglected "more especially when such interpretation is justified by the literal, and when it rests on the authority of many." This method "has been received by the Church from the Apostles, and has been approved by her own practice, as the holy Liturgy attests," though the Fathers "did not thereby pretend directly to demonstrate dogmas of faith, but used it as a means of promoting virtue and piety."

Other Catholic interpreters' authority, while less great, serves valuable purposes in refuting assailants and explaining difficulties. However, it is "most unbecoming to pass by...the excellent work which Catholics have left in abundance, and to have recourse to the works of non-Catholics." While non-Catholic studies "used with prudence, may sometimes be of use," scholars must remember that "the sense of Holy Scripture can nowhere be found incorrupt outside of the Church, and cannot be expected to be found in writers who...only gnaw the bark of the Sacred Scripture, and never attain its pith."

**16.** All Theology teaching should be "pervaded and animated by the use of the divine Word of God." The Fathers and greatest theologians have derived Articles of Faith from Sacred Writings, finding therein heretical error refutation and truth's reasonableness, meaning, and mutual relations. Scripture's eminent position among revelation sources means Theology cannot be "placed on its true footing, or treated as its dignity demands" without "assiduous study and use."

While students should chiefly acquire scientific dogma knowledge through reasoning, "the judicious and instructed theologian will by no means pass by that method of doctrinal demonstration which draws its proof from the authority of the Bible," since "Theology does not receive her first principles from any other science, but immediately from God by revelation."

St. Thomas of Aquin exemplifies this approach, demonstrating how Christian Theology defends its principles: "If the adversary do but grant any portion of the divine revelation, we have an argument against him; thus, against a heretic we can employ Scripture authority...But if our opponent reject divine revelation entirely, there is then no way left to prove the Article of Faith by reasoning."

Beginners require thorough preparation in philosophy and theology under St. Thomas of Aquin's guidance, following the Pope's earlier direction. Through this preparation, "both in Biblical studies and in that part of Theology which is called positive, they will pursue the right path and make satisfactory progress."

---

## The Authority of Holy Scripture; Modern Criticism; Physical Science

**17.** Proving, expounding, and illustrating Catholic doctrine through legitimate Scripture interpretation is much; maintaining Scripture's full authority is equally important and difficult. This cannot be done completely without the Church's "living and proper magisterium."

Vindicating Scripture's trustworthiness as human documents is essential, from which can be clearly proved "the Divinity and the mission of Christ our Lord, the institution of a hierarchical Church and the primacy of Peter and his successors." The clergy must prepare themselves "to enter upon a contest of this nature, and to repulse hostile assaults, chiefly trusting in that armour of God."

St. John Chrysostom beautifully describes priestly duties: "We must use every endeavour that the Word of God may dwell in us abundantly" and be "prepared" for many-sided contests, knowing "every trick and turn of war, lest the devil...get in his fierce bands and carry off the sheep."

The first defensive means is studying Oriental languages and criticism arts, held in high estimation. Clergy should acquire familiarity "as time and place may demand" to discharge their office "with becoming credit" and remain "ready to satisfy every one that asketh them a reason for the hope that is in them."

Professors of Sacred Scripture and theologians should master the languages "in which the sacred Books were originally written," and Church students, especially those pursuing academic degrees, should cultivate them. Academic institutions should establish "chairs of the other ancient languages, especially the Semitic, and of subjects connected therewith."

An "inept method...dignified by the name of the 'higher criticism'" has arisen, "which pretends to judge of the origin, integrity and authority of each Book from internal indications alone." However, "in historical questions...the witness of history is of primary importance, and...historical investigation should be made with the utmost care; and...in this matter internal evidence is seldom of great value, except as confirmation."

This erroneous approach emboldens Scripture attackers and proves that "vaunted 'higher criticism' will resolve itself into the reflection of the bias and the prejudice of the critics." It produces "disagreement and dissension, those sure notes of error," and, since critics are "tainted with false philosophy and rationalism, it must lead to the elimination from the sacred writings of all prophecy and miracle."

**18.** Second, scholars must contend against those abusing physical science to detect writer errors and vilify Scripture contents. Such attacks, "bearing as they do on matters of sensible experience, are peculiarly dangerous to the masses, and also to the young," who easily lose reverence and cease believing entirely.

Science, "admirably adapted to show forth the glory of the Great Creator" when properly taught, becomes "most fatal in destroying the principles of true philosophy and in the corruption of morality" when perverted. Scripture professors require "a knowledge of natural science...in detecting such attacks...and in refuting them."

"There can never...be any real discrepancy between the theologian and the physicist, as long as each confines himself within his own lines, and both are careful...not to make rash assertions." St. Augustine provides the rule: "Whatever they can really demonstrate to be true of physical nature, we must show to be capable of reconciliation with our Scriptures; and whatever they assert...which is contrary to these Scriptures...we must either prove it...to be entirely false, or at all events we must...believe it to be so."

The sacred writers, or more accurately, "the Holy Ghost Who spoke by them, did not intend to teach men these things (that is to say, the essential nature of the things of the visible universe), things in no way profitable unto salvation." They described matters "in more or less figurative language, or in terms which were commonly used at the time, and which in many instances are in daily use...even by the most eminent men of science."

**19.** Defending Holy Scripture does not require upholding all opinions that Fathers or interpreters expressed when explaining passages containing physical matters. They "sometimes expressed the ideas of their own times, and thus made statements which in these days have been abandoned as incorrect."

In their interpretations, one must carefully distinguish "what they lay down as belonging to faith, or as intimately connected with faith—what they are unanimous in." St. Thomas wisely notes: "When philosophers are agreed upon a point, and it is not contrary to our faith, it is safer...neither to lay down such a point as a dogma of faith...nor to reject it as against faith, lest we thus give to the wise of this world an occasion of despising our faith."

Catholic interpreters should demonstrate that certain natural science facts do not contradict rightly-explained Scripture. However, they must remember that "much which has been held and proved as certain has afterwards been called in question and rejected."

---

## Inspiration Incompatible with Error

**20.** The principles apply to cognate sciences, especially History. Investigators often carry out "investigations on the monuments of antiquity...whose chief purpose...is too often to find mistakes in the sacred writings and so to shake and weaken their authority." These writers frequently display extreme hostility, accepting profane books without hesitation while rejecting Scripture on slight suspicion of error.

It is true that copyists made mistakes; this should be "carefully considered on its merits, and the fact not too easily admitted, but only in those passages where the proof is clear." Ambiguous passages may be clarified through good hermeneutical methods. However, "it is absolutely wrong and forbidden, either to narrow inspiration to certain parts only of Holy Scripture, or to admit that the sacred writer has erred."

The system claiming divine inspiration addresses faith and morals only "cannot be tolerated." All biblical books that the Church receives as sacred and canonical are written "wholly and entirely, with all their parts, at the dictation of the Holy Ghost; and so far is it from being possible that any error can co-exist with inspiration, that inspiration not only is essentially incompatible with error, but excludes and rejects it as absolutely and necessarily as it is impossible that God Himself, the supreme Truth, can utter that which is not true."

This ancient Church faith was "solemnly defined in the Councils of Florence and of Trent, and finally confirmed and more expressly formulated by the Council of the Vatican." The Vatican Council declares: "The Books of the Old and New Testament, whole and entire, with all their parts...are to be received as sacred and canonical...because, having been written under the inspiration of the Holy Ghost, they have God for their author."

Since "the Holy Ghost employed men as His instruments," we cannot say "that it was these inspired instruments who...have fallen into error, and not the primary author." God so moved and impelled the writers "that the things which He ordered, and those only, they, first, rightly understood, then willed faithfully to write down, and finally expressed in apt words and with infallible truth. Otherwise, it could not be said that He was the Author of the entire Scripture."

The Fathers always held this persuasion. St. Augustine says: "since they wrote the things which He showed and uttered to them, it cannot be pretended that He is not the writer; for His members executed what their Head dictated." St. Gregory the Great pronounces: "Most superfluous it is to inquire who wrote these things—we loyally believe the Holy Ghost to be the Author of the book. He wrote it Who dictated it for writing."

**21.** Those maintaining that error is possible "either pervert the Catholic notion of inspiration, or make God the author of such error." The Fathers and Doctors unanimously agreed that divine writings, "as left by the hagiographers, are free from all error," laboring earnestly to reconcile seemingly contradictory passages. They unanimously held that writings, "in their entirety and in all their parts were equally from the afflatus of Almighty God, and that God, speaking by the sacred writers, could not set down anything but what was true."

St. Augustine's words to St. Jerome summarize their teaching: "it is only to those Books of Scripture which are now called canonical that I have learned to pay such honour and reverence as to believe most firmly that none of their writers has fallen into any error."

**22.** Defending Holy Scripture fully requires more than commentators and theologians alone can provide. The Church has the right to expect "the co-operation of all those Catholics who have acquired reputation in any branch of learning whatever." Seeing learned and distinguished men "show towards faith the most marked honour and respect" will silence objectors claiming "faith is the enemy of science."

Those possessing great natural talent alongside faith should "select each of them the branch of study most suitable to his circumstances, and endeavour to excel therein, and thus be prepared to repulse with credit and distinction the assaults on the Word of God." The Pope praises Catholics forming "societies and the contribution of considerable sums of money, for the purpose of supplying studious and learned men with every kind of help and assistance." This represents "an excellent fashion of investing money," especially as "the less hope of public patronage there is for Catholic study, the more ready and the more abundant should be the liberality of private persons."

---

## Summary

**23.** For these endeavours to benefit Scripture's cause, scholars must steadfastly keep the Letter's principles. They should loyally hold that "God, the Creator and Ruler of all things, is also the Author of the Scriptures—and that therefore nothing can be proved either by physical science or archaeology which can really contradict the Scriptures."

When apparent contradictions arise, "every effort should be made to remove it." Theologians and commentators should be consulted about the passage's true or most probable meaning, and hostile arguments carefully weighed. "Even if the difficulty is after all not cleared up and the discrepancy seems to remain, the contest must not be abandoned; truth cannot contradict truth."

Many objections "perseveringly directed against the Scripture for many a long year, which have been proved to be futile and are now never heard of," and interpretations have been "rectified by more careful investigations." As time passes, "mistaken views die and disappear; but truth remaineth and groweth stronger for ever and ever."

No one should presume to understand all Scripture entirely; should anything seem incapable of solution, scholars should remember St. Augustine's cautious rule: "It is better even to be oppressed by unknown but useful signs, than to interpret them uselessly and thus to throw off the yoke only to be caught in the trap of error."

**24.** These admonitions constitute the Pope's counsel "on the study of Holy Scripture." Bishops must ensure the Letter's observance "with all due reverence and exactness," proving gratitude for God's wisdom communication. Exert efforts so these studies "may be held in just regard and may flourish, in Seminaries and in the educational Institutions which are under your jurisdiction," under Church direction, following Holy Fathers' teaching and antiquity's traditions.

All students and Church ministers should "always approach the Sacred Writings with reverence and piety; for it is impossible to attain to the profitable understanding thereof unless the arrogance of earthly science be laid aside, and there be excited in the heart the holy desire for that wisdom which is from above." Through these studies, intelligence will acquire "a marvellous facility in detecting and avoiding the fallacies of human science, and in gathering and using for eternal salvation all that is valuable and precious"; the heart will grow warm, advancing "in virtue and in divine love."

**25.** The Pope concludes, "filled with hope in the divine assistance," and imparts "the Apostolic Benediction" to all bishops, clergy, and their flocks.

---

*Given at St. Peter's, at Rome, the 18th day of November, 1893, the eighteenth year of Our Pontificate.*

**LEO XIII**
