# Question 36: The Causes of Sorrow or Pain

## Article 1: Whether Sorrow is Caused by the Loss of Good or by the Presence of Evil?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Augustine states that sorrow results from the loss of temporal goods, suggesting all sorrow stems from losing something good rather than from present evil.

**Objection 2:** Since sorrow is contrary to pleasure, and pleasure concerns good objects, sorrow should primarily concern the loss of good.

**Objection 3:** Because love (which Augustine identifies as the cause of sorrow) has good as its object, sorrow arises from losing good rather than from present evil.

### On the Contrary

Damascene teaches that "the present evil is the cause of sorrow," distinguishing it from fear which concerns dreaded future evil.

### I Answer That

While evil and the privation of good are identical in reality, they differ in how the mind apprehends them. Sorrow results from appetitive movement following apprehension. A privation, when apprehended, appears as a contrary being. Therefore, sorrow's focus differs depending on whether it regards present evil or lost good.

Drawing an analogy to natural movements: just as a heavy body withdraws from higher places and approaches lower places, the primary cause is gravity's inclination downward rather than withdrawal upward. Similarly, sorrow (a kind of withdrawal) primarily regards present evil as its object, while love (its underlying cause) regards good.

Consequently, present evil more properly causes sorrow than lost good does.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** The loss itself is apprehended as an evil, so Augustine's statement about temporal goods' loss means we perceive that loss as an evil present to our mind.

**Reply 2:** Pleasure and sorrow share the same object under contrary aspects: presence versus absence. One contrary includes the privation of the other, so sorrow indirectly concerns the same thing pleasure does, but inversely.

**Reply 3:** When multiple appetitive movements arise from one cause, they don't all regard what the cause primarily regards—only the first movement does. Each subsequent movement concerns what suits its own nature.

## Article 2: Whether Desire is a Cause of Sorrow?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Sorrow concerns evil while desire concerns good; movements toward contrary objects cannot cause one another, so desire doesn't cause sorrow.

**Objection 2:** Pain concerns present things while desire's object is future, so desire cannot cause pain.

**Objection 3:** Desire is inherently pleasant, so something pleasant cannot cause pain or sorrow.

### On the Contrary

Augustine states: "When ignorance of necessary things and desire of hurtful things found their way in: error and pain stole an entrance."

### I Answer That

Appetitive movement resembles natural movement with two types of causes: the end and the principle of movement. Present evil causes sorrow as the movement's object. However, the inward appetite's inclination serves as the principle, regarding good first and evil's rejection consequently.

Love represents the first appetitive inclination toward good possession; hatred represents inclination toward evil avoidance. Since desire (concupiscence) is love's first effect, Augustine sometimes uses "desire" to mean love universally, calling desire the universal sorrow cause.

Properly understood, desire becomes sorrow's cause when it's hindered—when we grieve over a desired good's delay or complete removal. However, desire isn't sorrow's universal cause, since we sorrow more for losing present good we've enjoyed than for losing future goods we merely desire.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** The appetite's inclination toward good causes inclination to flee from evil, so appetitive movements regarding good cause those regarding evil.

**Reply 2:** Though the desired good is really future, it's present through hope. Alternatively, the hindrance itself is present, causing sorrow.

**Reply 3:** Desire produces pleasure while hope exists. When obstacles remove hope, desire causes sorrow instead.

## Article 3: Whether the Craving for Unity is a Cause of Sorrow?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Aristotle notes that attributing pleasure to repletion and sorrow to division "seems to have originated in pains and pleasures connected with food," suggesting this isn't universal. Therefore craving for unity isn't universally sorrow's cause.

**Objection 2:** If sorrow came from craving unity, no separation could be pleasant, yet separating superfluous things is clearly pleasant.

**Objection 3:** We desire both good's conjunction and evil's removal equally. Since conjunction means unity while separation opposes it, removal shouldn't be credited to craving unity over craving separation as sorrow's cause.

### On the Contrary

Augustine teaches: "From the pain that dumb animals feel, it is quite evident how their souls desire unity, in ruling and quickening their bodies. For what else is pain but a feeling of impatience of division or corruption?"

### I Answer That

Since good-desire causes sorrow, craving for unity likewise causes sorrow. Each thing's good consists in certain unity—the unified elements composing its perfection. The Platonists held "one" as a principle like "good." Everything naturally desires unity as it desires goodness; therefore love or craving for unity causes sorrow as does love for good.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Not all union produces perfect goodness, only that upon which a thing's perfect being depends. So not all unity-desire causes pain. Repletion isn't always pleasant—eating to excess brings no further pleasure because that union contradicts rather than supports perfect being.

**Reply 2:** Separation is pleasant when it removes things contrary to perfection or when it involves union (like sense uniting with its object).

**Reply 3:** We desire separation from harmful things because they destroy due unity. Such separation-desire isn't sorrow's primary cause; unity-craving is.

## Article 4: Whether an Irresistible Power is a Cause of Sorrow?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** A greater power's effects are future, not present, but sorrow concerns present evil, so greater power doesn't cause sorrow.

**Objection 2:** Hurt causes sorrow, yet lesser powers inflict hurt, so greater power shouldn't be reckoned as sorrow's cause.

**Objection 3:** Appetitive movements' causes are interior soul inclinations, not external things like greater power.

### On the Contrary

Augustine states: "Sorrow in the soul is caused by the will resisting a stronger power: while pain in the body is caused by sense resisting a stronger body."

### I Answer That

Since present evil causes sorrow as object, whatever makes evil present should count as sorrow's cause. Evil's presence contradicts appetite's inclination; whatever contradicts something's inclination only happens through something stronger. Thus greater power causes sorrow.

However, if the stronger power transforms the contrary inclination into its own inclination, repugnance ceases. A heavier agent depriving a body of downward tendency makes upward movement natural, not violent.

Sorrow results only when contrary appetite-inclination persists. If the will or sensitive appetite yields by consenting rather than resisting, pleasure results instead of sorrow. Augustine notes sorrow comes from the will "resisting" stronger power; without resistance—through consent—pain becomes pleasure.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Greater power causes sorrow by acting actually, not potentially, by effecting the corruptive evil's actual presence.

**Reply 2:** A power need not be simply greater to be greater in some respect, enabling some harm. But if nowise stronger, it cannot harm at all, so cannot cause sorrow.

**Reply 3:** External agents cause appetitive movements by effecting the object's presence; thus greater power counts as sorrow's cause.
