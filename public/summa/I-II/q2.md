# Question 2: Things in Which Man's Happiness Consists

## Article 1: Whether man's happiness consists in wealth?

### Objection 1
Since happiness is man's last end, it must consist in that which has the greatest hold on man's affections. Wealth has this quality, as Scripture states: "All things obey money." Therefore man's happiness consists in wealth.

### Objection 2
According to Boethius, happiness is "a state of life made perfect by the aggregate of all good things." Money serves as a means to possess all things, as the Philosopher notes—it was invented to guarantee acquisition of whatever man desires. Therefore happiness consists in wealth.

### Objection 3
The desire for the sovereign good never fails and is infinite. This characterizes riches more than anything else: "a covetous man shall not be satisfied with riches." Therefore happiness consists in wealth.

### On the contrary
Man's good consists in retaining happiness rather than spreading it. Yet wealth "shines in giving rather than in hoarding: for the miser is hateful, whereas the generous man is applauded." Therefore man's happiness does not consist in wealth.

### I answer that
It is impossible for man's happiness to consist in wealth. The Philosopher distinguishes two types of wealth: natural and artificial. Natural wealth serves man's natural needs—food, drink, clothing, dwellings. Artificial wealth, like money, is invented for exchange convenience and measurement of salable things.

Man's happiness cannot consist in natural wealth because such wealth is sought for something else—supporting human nature. It therefore cannot be man's last end but rather is ordained to man as to its end. All such things rank below man and exist for him, as Scripture affirms: "Thou hast subjected all things under his feet."

Artificial wealth is sought only for natural wealth's sake; man pursues it because it procures life's necessaries. Therefore it even less qualifies as man's last end. Since happiness is man's ultimate end, it cannot consist in wealth.

### Reply to Objection 1
Material things obey money only concerning fools who know no goods except material ones obtainable for money. We should estimate human goods from the wise, not the foolish—as one whose taste is properly ordered judges palatability.

### Reply to Objection 2
All salable things can be obtained for money, but spiritual things cannot be sold. Scripture confirms this: "What doth it avail a fool to have riches, seeing he cannot buy wisdom."

### Reply to Objection 3
Natural riches do not involve infinite desire—they suffice for nature within measure. However, artificial wealth's desire is infinite, serving disordered concupiscence uncurbed by reason. Yet this differs from the sovereign good's infinite desire: possessing it more perfectly increases both love and despise for other things. By contrast, possessing temporal goods brings contempt and new desires, as Our Lord stated: "Whosoever drinketh of this water shall thirst again."

## Article 2: Whether man's happiness consists in honors?

### Objection 1
Happiness or bliss is "the reward of virtue," according to the Philosopher. Honor especially seems virtue's reward, as the Philosopher affirms. Therefore happiness consists especially in honor.

### Objection 2
What belongs to God and persons of great excellence seems especially to constitute happiness, which is the perfect good. This is honor, as the Philosopher states. The Apostle also declares: "To...the only God be honor and glory." Therefore happiness consists in honor.

### Objection 3
Man desires happiness above all. Nothing seems more desirable to man than honor: he suffers loss in all other things rather than lose honor. Therefore happiness consists in honor.

### On the contrary
Happiness is in the happy person. But honor is not in the honored person but in him who honors—the one offering deference to the honored person, as the Philosopher states. Therefore happiness does not consist in honor.

### I answer that
It is impossible for happiness to consist in honor. Honor is given to a man because of some excellence in him; consequently it is a sign and attestation of excellence within that person. Man's excellence is proportionate especially to his happiness, which is man's perfect good, and to its component parts—goods conferring some share of happiness. Therefore honor can result from happiness, but happiness cannot principally consist therein.

### Reply to Objection 1
The Philosopher states that honor is not virtue's reward for which the virtuous work. Rather, virtuous persons receive honor from men as reward "from those who have nothing greater to offer." Virtue's true reward is happiness itself, for which the virtuous work. If they worked for honor, it would constitute ambition, not virtue.

### Reply to Objection 2
Honor is due to God and persons of great excellence as a sign attesting already-existing excellence—not as making them excellent.

### Reply to Objection 3
Man's desire for honor arises from his natural desire for happiness, from which honor results. Consequently, man seeks honor especially from the wise, whose judgment he believes indicates his excellence or happiness.

## Article 3: Whether man's happiness consists in fame or glory?

### Objection 1
Happiness seems to consist in what is paid to saints for their worldly trials. This is glory: the Apostle states, "The sufferings of this time are not worthy to be compared with the glory to come." Therefore happiness consists in glory.

### Objection 2
Good is diffusive of itself, as Dionysius states. Man's good spreads abroad in others' knowledge through glory more than anything else: according to Ambrose, glory consists "in being well known and praised." Therefore man's happiness consists in glory.

### Objection 3
Happiness is the most enduring good. This seems to be fame or glory, because thereby men attain eternity after a fashion. Boethius says: "You seem to beget unto yourselves eternity, when you think of your fame in future time." Therefore happiness consists in fame or glory.

### On the contrary
Happiness is man's true good. But fame or glory is often false: Boethius notes, "many owe their renown to lying reports spread among the people. Can anything be more shameful? For those receiving false fame must blush at their own praise." Therefore happiness does not consist in fame or glory.

### I answer that
Man's happiness cannot consist in human fame or glory. Glory consists "in being well known and praised," as Ambrose states. The known thing relates to human knowledge differently than to God's knowledge: human knowledge is caused by known things, while God's knowledge causes known things. Therefore, human knowledge cannot cause human happiness—the perfection of human good. Rather, human knowledge of another's happiness proceeds from and is caused by that happiness itself, whether inchoate or perfect. Thus man's happiness cannot consist in fame or glory.

Man's good depends on God's knowledge as its cause. Therefore man's beatitude depends on the glory he possesses with God as its cause, according to Scripture: "I will deliver him, and I will glorify him; I will fill him with length of days."

Furthermore, human knowledge frequently errs, especially regarding contingent particulars like human acts. Therefore human glory is often deceptive. Since God cannot be deceived, His glory is always true; hence Scripture states: "He...is approved...whom God commendeth."

### Reply to Objection 1
The Apostle speaks not of glory with men but of glory from God with His Angels. Scripture confirms this: "The Son of Man shall confess him in the glory of His Father, before His angels."

### Reply to Objection 2
A man's good, through fame or glory, reaches others' knowledge. If this knowledge is true, it must derive from good existing in the man himself; thus it presupposes happiness, perfect or inchoate. But if the knowledge is false, it does not harmonize with reality; thus good does not exist in the seemingly famous person. Hence fame cannot make man happy.

### Reply to Objection 3
Fame lacks stability and is easily ruined by false report. If it endures, this occurs accidentally. But happiness endures of itself and forever.

## Article 4: Whether man's happiness consists in power?

### Objection 1
All things desire to become like God, as to their last end and first beginning. Men possessing power seem most like God through power similarity; Scripture calls them "gods." Therefore happiness consists in power.

### Objection 2
Happiness is the perfect good. Man's highest perfection is ruling others, which belongs to those in power. Therefore happiness consists in power.

### Objection 3
Happiness is supremely desirable and contrary to what is supremely shunned. More than anything, men shun servitude, contrary to power. Therefore happiness consists in power.

### On the contrary
Happiness is the perfect good. But power is most imperfect. Boethius states: "the power of man cannot relieve the gnawings of care, nor can it avoid the thorny path of anxiety" and further: "Think you a man is powerful who is surrounded by attendants, whom he inspires with fear indeed, but whom he fears still more?"

### I answer that
It is impossible for happiness to consist in power for two reasons. First, power is a principle by nature, while happiness is a last end. Secondly, power relates to both good and evil; whereas happiness is man's proper and perfect good. Therefore some happiness might consist in power's good use, which proceeds through virtue, rather than in power itself.

Four general reasons demonstrate that happiness consists in none of these external goods. First, since happiness is man's supreme good, it is incompatible with any evil. Yet all foregoing goods exist in both good and evil men. Secondly, happiness by nature "satisfies of itself"; having attained happiness, man cannot lack any necessary good. Yet acquiring any foregoing good, man may still lack many necessary goods—wisdom, bodily health, and such like. Thirdly, since happiness is the perfect good, no evil accrues therefrom. This cannot be said of the foregoing: Scripture notes that "riches" are sometimes "kept to the hurt of the owner"; the same applies to the other three. Fourthly, man is ordained to happiness through principles within him, since he is ordained thereto naturally. Yet these four goods result from external causes and mostly from fortune; therefore they are called goods of fortune. Thus happiness nowise consists in the foregoing.

### Reply to Objection 1
God's power is His goodness; hence He cannot use His power except well. But this is not so with men. Consequently, man's happiness does not require merely becoming like God in power unless he becomes like Him in goodness also.

### Reply to Objection 2
Using power well to rule many is very good; using it badly is very bad. Thus power relates to both good and evil.

### Reply to Objection 3
Servitude hinders power's good use; therefore men naturally shun it—not because man's supreme good consists in power.

## Article 5: Whether man's happiness consists in any bodily good?

### Objection 1
Scripture states: "There is no riches above the riches of the health of the body." Happiness consists in what is best. Therefore it consists in bodily health.

### Objection 2
Dionysius says that "to be" is better than "to live," and "to live" is better than all following things. Man's being and living require bodily health. Since happiness is man's supreme good, bodily health seems to belong especially to happiness.

### Objection 3
Higher causes penetrate their effects more deeply than secondary causes. The end's causality consists in attracting appetite. Thus that most moving appetite answers to the last end's notion. Being itself is universally desired. Therefore man's happiness consists especially in things pertaining to his being, such as bodily health.

### On the contrary
Man surpasses all other animals regarding happiness. Yet in bodily goods he is surpassed by many: the elephant in longevity, the lion in strength, the stag in swiftness. Therefore man's happiness does not consist in bodily goods.

### I answer that
It is impossible for man's happiness to consist in bodily goods for two reasons. First, if a thing is ordained to another as its end, its last end cannot consist in preserving its being. A ship's captain does not intend the ship's preservation as a last end, since a ship is ordained to something else—navigation. Similarly, man is given over to his will and reason, as Scripture affirms: "God made man from the beginning and left him in the hand of his own counsel." Since man is clearly ordained to something as his end—man is not the supreme good—man's reason and will's last end cannot be preserving man's being.

Secondly, if man's will and reason's end were preserving man's being, one still could not say man's end consists in some bodily good. Man's being comprises soul and body; the body's being depends on the soul, yet the human soul's being does not depend on the body, as previously shown. The body itself serves the soul as matter serves form, and as instruments serve the man wielding them, enabling his work. Therefore all bodily goods are ordained to soul's goods as their end. Consequently, happiness—man's last end—cannot consist in bodily goods.

### Reply to Objection 1
As the body is ordained to the soul as its end, external goods are ordained to the body itself. Therefore the body's good is rightly preferred to external goods (signified by "riches"), just as the soul's good is preferred to all bodily goods.

### Reply to Objection 2
Being considered absolutely, including all perfection of being, surpasses life and all following things; in this sense Dionysius speaks, for being itself includes these. But if we consider being as participated in particular things lacking the whole perfection of being—having imperfect being, as all creatures do—then being together with additional perfection is more excellent. Indeed, Dionysius states that living things surpass merely existing things, and intelligent things surpass merely living things.

### Reply to Objection 3
Since the end corresponds to the beginning, this argument proves the last end is being's first beginning, in Whom all perfection of being exists: whose likeness, according to their proportion, some desire as to being only, some as to living being, some as to being that is living, intelligent and happy. This belongs to few.

## Article 6: Whether man's happiness consists in pleasure?

### Objection 1
Since happiness is the last end, it is not desired for something else, but other things for it. This characterizes pleasure more than anything else: "it is absurd to ask anyone what is his motive in wishing to be pleased." Therefore happiness consists principally in pleasure and delight.

### Objection 2
"The first cause goes more deeply into the effect than the second cause." The end's causality consists in attracting appetite. Therefore that which moves appetite most answers to the last end's notion. This is pleasure: delight so absorbs man's will and reason that it causes him to despise other goods. Therefore man's last end—happiness—consists principally in pleasure.

### Objection 3
Since desire is for good, what all desire is best. All desire delight: both wise and foolish, and even irrational creatures. Therefore delight is the best of all. Therefore happiness, the supreme good, consists in pleasure.

### On the contrary
Boethius states: "Any one that chooses to look back on his past excesses, will perceive that pleasures had a sad ending: and if they can render a man happy, there is no reason why we should not say that the very beasts are happy too."

### I answer that
Because bodily delights are more generally known, "the name of pleasure has been appropriated to them," although other delights excel them; yet happiness does not consist therein. In everything, what pertains to its essence differs from its proper accident: thus in man it is one thing that he is a mortal rational animal, and another that he is a risible animal. Every delight is a proper accident resulting from happiness, or from some happiness part; since a man is delighted because he possesses some fitting good—in reality, hope, or at least memory. A fitting good, if indeed it be the perfect good, is precisely man's happiness; if imperfect, it is happiness's share, either proximate, remote, or at least apparent. Therefore delight resulting from the perfect good is not happiness's very essence, but something resulting therefrom as its proper accident.

But bodily pleasure cannot result from the perfect good even in this way. For it results from a good apprehended by sense—a soul power using the body. Good pertaining to the body and apprehended by sense cannot be man's perfect good. The rational soul exceeds corporeal matter's capacity; consequently that soul part independent of a corporeal organ possesses a certain infinity regarding the body and those soul parts bound to the body: as immaterial things are somewhat infinite compared to material things, since a form, being contracted and bounded by matter, an independent form is in a way infinite. Therefore sense, a body power, knows the singular, determined through matter; while intellect, independent of matter, knows the universal, abstracted from matter, containing an infinite number of singulars. Consequently, good fitting the body and causing bodily delight through sense apprehension is not man's perfect good, but quite a trifle compared with the soul's good. Scripture confirms this: "all gold in comparison of her, is as a little sand." Therefore bodily pleasure is neither happiness itself, nor a proper accident thereof.

### Reply to Objection 1
It comes to the same whether we desire good, or desire delight—which is nothing else than appetite's rest in good: as a weighty body is borne downwards and rests there through the same natural force. Consequently as good is desired for itself, delight is desired for itself—if "for" denotes the final cause. But if "for" denotes the formal or motive cause, delight is desirable for something else: for the good which is that delight's object and consequently its principle, giving it form. Delight is desired because it is rest in the desired thing.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sensible delight's vehement desire arises because sense operations—principles of our knowledge—are more perceptible. Thus sensible pleasures are desired by the majority.

### Reply to Objection 3
All desire delight as they desire good; yet they desire delight by reason of the good, not conversely, as stated above. Consequently it does not follow that delight is the supreme and essential good, but that every delight results from some good, and some delight results from what is the essential and supreme good.

## Article 7: Whether some good of the soul constitutes man's happiness?

### Objection 1
Happiness is man's good. This is threefold: external goods, bodily goods, and soul goods. Happiness does not consist in external or bodily goods, as shown above. Therefore it consists in soul goods.

### Objection 2
We love what we desire good for, more than the good desired for it: we love a friend for whom we desire money more than money. But whatever good man desires, he desires it for himself. Therefore he loves himself more than all other goods. Happiness is what is loved above all, evident from its being loved and desired for itself. Therefore happiness consists in some good of man himself: not bodily goods; therefore, in soul goods.

### Objection 3
Perfection belongs to what is perfected. Happiness is man's perfection. Therefore happiness is something belonging to man. But it is not something belonging to the body, as shown above. Therefore it is something belonging to the soul; thus it consists in soul goods.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "that which constitutes the life of happiness is to be loved for its own sake." But man is not to be loved for his own sake; rather, whatever is in man is to be loved for God's sake. Therefore happiness consists in no soul good.

### I answer that
As stated above, the end is twofold: namely, the thing itself which we desire to attain, and the use—namely, the attainment or possession of that thing. If we speak of man's last end, it is impossible for man's last end to be the soul itself or something belonging to it. The soul, considered in itself, is something existing in potentiality: it becomes actually knowing from being potentially knowing; actually virtuous from being potentially virtuous. Since potentiality exists for act's sake as its fulfillment, what is in itself potentially cannot be the last end. Therefore the soul itself cannot be its own last end.

Similarly, nothing belonging to it—whether power, habit, or act—can be the last end. The last end good is the perfect good fulfilling desire. Man's will is appetite for universal good. Any good inherent to the soul is a participated good, and consequently a portioned good. Therefore none can be man's last end.

But if we speak of man's last end as to attainment or possession thereof, or as to any use of the thing itself desired as end, thus something of man, in respect of his soul, belongs to his last end: since man attains happiness through his soul. Therefore the thing itself desired as end constitutes happiness and makes man happy; but that thing's attainment is called happiness. Consequently, happiness is something belonging to the soul; but what constitutes happiness is something outside the soul.

### Reply to Objection 1
Inasmuch as this division includes all goods man can desire, the soul good is not only power, habit, or act, but also the object of these—something outside. And thus nothing prevents saying what constitutes happiness is a soul good.

### Reply to Objection 2
Happiness is loved above all as the desired good; whereas a friend is loved as that for which good is desired; thus man loves himself. Consequently the love is not identical. Whether man loves anything more than himself with friendship love will be examined when treating Charity.

### Reply to Objection 3
Happiness itself, since it is soul perfection, is an inherent soul good; but what constitutes happiness—making man happy—is something outside his soul, as stated above.

## Article 8: Whether any created good constitutes man's happiness?

### Objection 1
Dionysius states that Divine wisdom "unites the ends of first things to the beginnings of second things," from which we gather that a lower nature's summit touches the higher nature's base. But man's highest good is happiness. Since the angel surpasses man in nature's order, it seems man's happiness consists in man somehow reaching the angel.

### Objection 2
Each thing's last end is that which, in relation to it, is perfect: the part exists for the whole as its end. The universe of creatures (macrocosm) is compared to man (microcosm) as perfect to imperfect. Therefore man's happiness consists in the whole universe of creatures.

### Objection 3
Man is made happy by what lulls his natural desire. Man's natural desire does not reach a good surpassing his capacity. Since man's capacity does not include good surpassing all creation's limits, man can be made happy by some created good. Consequently some created good constitutes man's happiness.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "As the soul is the body's life, so God is man's happiness life: of Whom it is written: 'Happy is that people whose God is the Lord.'"

### I answer that
It is impossible for any created good to constitute man's happiness. Happiness is the perfect good lulling appetite altogether; else it would not be the last end if something yet remained desirable. The will's object—man's appetite—is universal good; just as the intellect's object is universal truth. Hence naught can lull man's will except universal good. This is not found in any creature, but in God alone; every creature has goodness by participation. Therefore God alone can satisfy man's will, according to Scripture: "Who satisfieth thy desire with good things." Therefore God alone constitutes man's happiness.

### Reply to Objection 1
Man's summit does indeed touch the angelic nature's base by a kind of likeness; but man does not rest there as in his last end, but reaches out to the universal good's fount itself, which is the common happiness object of all the blessed, as the infinite and perfect good.

### Reply to Objection 2
If a whole is not the last end but ordained to a further end, then a part thereof's last end is not the whole itself, but something else. The creature universe, to which man is compared as part to whole, is not the last end but ordained to God as its last end. Therefore man's last end is not the universe's good, but God himself.

### Reply to Objection 3
Created good is not less than that good of which man is capable as something intrinsic and inherent to him: but it is less than that good of which he is capable as an object, which is infinite. The participated good existing in an angel and the whole universe is finite and restricted good.
