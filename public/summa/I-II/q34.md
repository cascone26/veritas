# Question 34: The Goodness and Malice of Pleasures

## Article 1: Whether Every Pleasure is Evil?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Pleasure appears inherently evil because it "destroys prudence and hinders the use of reason," which constitutes humanity's proper good. Sexual pleasures particularly prevent rational understanding. Additionally, Jerome notes that divine grace withdraws during conjugal relations, even for prophets fulfilling marital duties.

**Objection 2:** Virtuous persons avoid certain pleasures while the vicious seek them. Since the virtuous person serves as the measure of human action, and children lacking virtue pursue pleasure while the self-controlled person does not, pleasures must be intrinsically evil and avoidable.

**Objection 3:** "Virtue and art are concerned about the difficult and the good." Since no art aims at pleasure itself, pleasure cannot constitute something good.

### On the Contrary

Scripture states: "Delight in the Lord." Divine authority directs toward no evil, so not every pleasure is evil.

### I Answer That

Some ancient philosophers wrongly held that all pleasure is evil, focusing exclusively on bodily pleasures while failing to distinguish intelligible from sensible experience. However, this teaching proves self-defeating: if teachers claiming all pleasures are evil are observed taking pleasure, their example contradicts their words more persuasively than doctrine influences.

Pleasure constitutes "a repose of the appetitive power in some loved good, and resulting from some operation." Thus two reasons establish that some pleasures are good while others are evil:

**First reason:** In the moral order, goodness and evil depend on "agreement or disagreement with reason." As natural repose occurs when things rest in accordance with their nature, moral pleasure arises when appetite rests in what accords with reason, while immoral pleasure occurs when appetite rests in what contradicts reason and God's law.

**Second reason:** Pleasures accompanying good actions are more akin to those actions than preceding desires. Since desires for good actions are themselves good, and desires for evil actions are evil, the pleasures resulting from good actions are good, while those from evil actions are evil.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Pleasures arising from rational acts do not hinder reason or destroy prudence; rather, extraneous bodily pleasures do so. These hinder reason either through appetite resting in something repugnant to reason (making pleasure morally bad) or by fettering reason itself. In conjugal intercourse, though pleasure accords with reason, accompanying bodily change fetters rational use—yet this carries no moral malice, as reason itself permits interrupting its own use. This fettering proceeds from the sin of our first parent, conditions differing in the state of innocence.

**Reply 2:** The temperate person shuns only immoderate pleasures contrary to reason. Children and animals seeking pleasures merely demonstrates they possess natural appetite from God suited to their nature—not that all pleasures are evil.

**Reply 3:** Art concerns external making rather than internal actions and passions, which belong more properly to prudence and virtue. Nevertheless, certain arts produce pleasure itself, such as cookery and rhetoric.

## Article 2: Whether Every Pleasure is Good?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Three kinds of good exist: the virtuous, the useful, and the pleasant. Everything virtuous is good, as is everything useful. Therefore every pleasure is also good.

**Objection 2:** Things not sought for other reasons are good in themselves. Since pleasure is not sought for something else (it seems absurd to ask why someone seeks pleasure), pleasure is good in itself. What holds universally of something considered in itself applies universally to that thing, so every pleasure must be good.

**Objection 3:** Whatever all desire appears good in itself, since "good is what all things seek." Everyone, including children and animals, pursues some pleasure. Therefore pleasure is intrinsically good and all pleasures are good.

### On the Contrary

Scripture writes: "Who are glad when they have done evil, and rejoice in most wicked things."

### I Answer That

Stoics claimed all pleasures are evil while Epicureans maintained all pleasures are good—both erred by confusing what is good absolutely with what is good relative to particular individuals.

What is good absolutely is intrinsically good. What is not intrinsically good may be good for someone in two ways: either because it suits a disposition, albeit unnatural (like toxic food sometimes beneficial to lepers); or because something unsuitable is esteemed suitable. Since pleasure is "repose of the appetite in some good," if appetite rests in what is absolutely good, pleasure will be absolutely good. If appetite rests only in what is good for that particular person, pleasure is relative to him—neither absolutely good nor absolutely evil, but apparently good.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** The virtuous and useful depend on accordance with reason, so nothing is virtuous or useful without being good. The pleasant depends on appetite-agreement, which sometimes conflicts with reason. Consequently, not every pleasure-object is good in the moral order depending on rational ordering.

**Reply 2:** Pleasure is not sought beyond itself because it constitutes rest in the end. Ends may be good or evil; nothing becomes an end except insofar as it appears good to some person—similarly regarding pleasure.

**Reply 3:** All desire pleasure as they desire good: pleasure being appetite-repose in good. Just as not every desired good is truly good in itself, not every pleasure is truly and intrinsically good.

## Article 3: Whether Any Pleasure is the Greatest Good?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Nothing generated constitutes the greatest good, since generation cannot be the final end. Pleasure results from generation—from something becoming established in its nature. Therefore no pleasure is the greatest good.

**Objection 2:** The greatest good cannot improve through addition. Yet pleasure improves with virtue-addition: pleasure with virtue exceeds pleasure without it. Therefore pleasure is not the greatest good.

**Objection 3:** The greatest good is universally good, being intrinsically good—prior to and greater than what is accidentally good. Pleasure is not universally good. Therefore pleasure is not the greatest good.

### On the Contrary

Happiness constitutes the greatest good as humanity's life-end. Happiness is inseparable from pleasure: "Thou shalt fill me with joy with Thy countenance; at Thy right hand are delights."

### I Answer That

Plato rejected both Stoic claims (all pleasures evil) and Epicurean claims (all pleasures good), holding some pleasures good and some evil, yet none sovereign. However, he erred doubly:

**First error:** Observing that bodily pleasure involves "becoming" and movement (like satiation), he concluded all pleasure involves "becoming." Since becoming constitutes imperfect acts, he deemed pleasure non-ultimate. Yet intellectual pleasures clearly counter this: one delights not only in knowledge-becoming (learning, wondering) but also in contemplative acts using acquired knowledge.

**Second error:** He understood "greatest good" as the Supreme Good absolutely—existing apart from all else, in which sense God alone qualifies. We speak of humanity's greatest good, which is the final end. The end occurs twofold: the thing itself and its use (as the miser's end is money or possessing money). Humanity's final end may be God (the Supreme Good absolutely) or enjoying God—implying pleasure in the final end. In this sense, certain human pleasure may be called the greatest among human goods.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Not all pleasure arises from "becoming"; some results from perfect operations. Nothing prevents some pleasure being the greatest good, though not every pleasure qualifies.

**Reply 2:** This applies to the greatest good absolutely, by participating in which all things are good—no addition improves it. Regarding other goods, any good improves through another good's addition. Moreover, pleasure accompanies virtue-operations rather than existing extraneous to them.

**Reply 3:** That pleasure constitutes the greatest good derives not from mere pleasure-quality but from perfect repose in perfect good. Thus not every pleasure is supremely good or even good. Similarly, certain sciences are supremely good, though not every science is.

## Article 4: Whether Pleasure is the Measure or Rule by Which to Judge Moral Good and Evil?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** "What is first in a genus measures all others." Pleasure is not first morally; love and desire precede it. Therefore pleasure is not the rule of moral goodness and malice.

**Objection 2:** Rules or measures require uniformity; the most uniform movement measures all movements. Yet pleasures vary multiformly—some good, some evil. Therefore pleasure is not the moral measure and rule.

**Objection 3:** Judging effects from causes proves more certain than judging causes from effects. Operation-goodness or malice causes pleasure-goodness or malice: "Those pleasures are good which result from good operations, and those are evil which arise from evil operations." Therefore pleasures are not the rule measuring moral goodness and malice.

### On the Contrary

Augustine, commenting on Psalm 7, notes: "The end of care and thought is the pleasure which each one aims at achieving." The Philosopher states that "pleasure is the architect," the principal end, regarding which "we say absolutely that this is evil, and that, good."

### I Answer That

Moral goodness and malice depend chiefly on will. We primarily discern whether will is good or evil from its end—that in which will rests. The appetitive repose in good constitutes pleasure. Therefore, humanity is reckoned good or bad chiefly according to human will's pleasure: the good person takes pleasure in virtue-works; the evil person takes pleasure in evil works.

However, sensitive appetite-pleasures do not measure moral goodness and malice. Food universally pleases both good and evil persons' sensitive appetites. The good person's will takes such pleasure according to reason, while the evil person's will disregards reason.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Love and desire precede pleasure genetically but pleasure precedes them regarding the end, which serves as principle in actions. We form judgments from the principle serving as rule and measure.

**Reply 2:** All pleasures are uniform regarding being appetite-repose in something good, making pleasure capable of serving as rule or measure. That man is good whose will rests in true good; that man is evil whose will rests in evil.

**Reply 3:** Since pleasure perfects operation as its end, operations cannot be perfectly good without accompanying good-pleasure, as thing-goodness depends on its end. Thus, pleasure-goodness somehow causes operation-goodness.
