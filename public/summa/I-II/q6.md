# Question 6: The Voluntary and the Involuntary

## Article 1: Whether there is anything voluntary in human acts?

### Objection 1
That which is voluntary "has its principle within itself." But the principle of human acts is outside man, since man's appetite is moved by external appetible objects. Therefore there is nothing voluntary in human acts.

### Objection 2
The Philosopher proves that in animals no new movement arises without prior external motion. Since all human acts are new, their principle comes from without; therefore nothing voluntary exists in them.

### Objection 3
He that acts voluntarily can act of himself. But man cannot, as Scripture states: "Without Me you can do nothing." Therefore there is nothing voluntary in human acts.

### On the Contrary
Damascene says that "the voluntary is an act consisting in a rational operation." Such are human acts. Therefore something voluntary exists in human acts.

### I Answer That
Something voluntary must exist in human acts. An act's principle may originate within or outside the agent. Those things moved by intrinsic principles that possess knowledge of the end move themselves and act for an end. Since man possesses knowledge of his work's end and moves himself, voluntariness is especially found in human acts.

### Reply to Objection 1
It is not essential to voluntary acts that their intrinsic principle be a first principle. An intrinsic principle may be caused or moved by extrinsic principles. The cognitive and appetitive power constitutes the first principle within appetitive movement, though external principles may affect it.

### Reply to Objection 2
New movements in animals are preceded by external motion in two respects: first, when senses encounter something sensible that moves appetite; second, when external motion produces physical bodily change affecting sensitive appetite. Neither contradicts voluntariness, as these external movements belong to different categories of motion.

### Reply to Objection 3
God moves man to act by proposing appetibles, effecting bodily changes, and moving the will itself. Every movement proceeds from God as First Mover. Just as natural movement from God is compatible with nature being His instrument, voluntary acts proceeding from God remain compatible with the will's essential nature as proceeding from an intrinsic principle.

## Article 2: Whether there is anything voluntary in irrational animals?

### Objection 1
"Voluntary" derives from "voluntas" (will). Will resides in reason, so irrational animals lack will. Therefore nothing voluntary exists in them.

### Objection 2
Man is master of his actions insofar as they are voluntary. Irrational animals are not masters of their actions. Therefore nothing voluntary exists in them.

### Objection 3
Voluntary acts lead to praise and blame. Irrational animals deserve neither. Therefore their acts are not voluntary.

### On the Contrary
The Philosopher states that "both children and irrational animals participate in the voluntary." Damascene and Gregory of Nyssa affirm this.

### I Answer That
Knowledge of the end is twofold: perfect and imperfect. Perfect knowledge involves understanding the end as an end and the relationship of means to it—belonging only to rational nature. Imperfect knowledge consists in mere apprehension of the end without understanding it as such—exercised by irrational animals through senses and natural estimative power. Perfect knowledge leads to perfect voluntariness; imperfect knowledge leads to imperfect voluntariness. Voluntariness in its perfection belongs only to rational nature; imperfect voluntariness belongs even to irrational animals.

### Reply to Objection 1
"Will" names rational appetite and cannot exist in unreasoning things. Yet "voluntary" derives from "will" and extends to things participating in will by likeness. Irrational animals are said to act voluntarily insofar as they are moved to an end through some knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 2
Man's mastery of actions results from his ability to deliberate. Since deliberating reason is indifferently disposed toward opposites, the will can incline either way. Voluntariness in irrational animals does not involve such deliberation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Praise and blame result from perfect voluntariness, which irrational animals lack.

## Article 3: Whether there can be voluntariness without any act?

### Objection 1
What is voluntary proceeds from the will. Nothing proceeds from the will except through some act. Therefore voluntariness cannot exist without act.

### Objection 2
When willing ceases, one is said not to wish. Not wishing implies involuntariness. Therefore nothing voluntary exists when the will's act ceases.

### Objection 3
Knowledge is essential to voluntariness. Knowledge involves an act. Therefore voluntariness cannot exist without some act.

### On the Contrary
"Voluntary" applies to that of which we are masters. We are masters regarding both acting and not acting, willing and not willing. Therefore not acting and not willing are voluntary just as acting and willing are.

### I Answer That
Voluntary proceeds from the will in two ways: directly, when the will acts; indirectly, when the will does not act but could and should. The cause of what follows from non-action belongs to the agent only when the agent can and ought to act. The will, by willing and acting, can and sometimes should hinder non-willing and non-acting; thus non-willing and non-acting are imputed to the will. Consequently voluntariness can exist without external act, sometimes without interior act, or sometimes with interior act only (as when one wills not to act).

### Reply to Objection 1
"Voluntary" applies not only to what proceeds from the will's action directly, but also to what proceeds from the will's inaction.

### Reply to Objection 2
"Not to wish" has two senses. First, as one word meaning "to wish not," implying involuntariness. Second, as a sentence affirming no act of will, not implying involuntariness.

### Reply to Objection 3
Voluntariness requires an act of knowledge as it requires an act of will: to make consideration, willing, and acting within one's power. Just as non-willing and non-acting when one should act are voluntary, so is failing to consider.

## Article 4: Whether violence can be done to the will?

### Objection 1
Everything more powerful can compel what is weaker. God is more powerful than human will. Therefore the will can be compelled, at least by Him.

### Objection 2
Every passive subject is compelled by its active principle when changed by it. The will is passive, a "mover moved." Therefore it is sometimes compelled.

### Objection 3
Violent movement opposes nature. The will's movement to sin opposes nature. Therefore the will's movement can be compelled.

### On the Contrary
Augustine states that what is done by the will is not done of necessity. Whatever is done under compulsion is necessary. Therefore the will cannot be compelled to act.

### I Answer That
The will's act is twofold: immediate acts (like "to wish"), elicited by the will; commanded acts (like "to walk," "to speak"), executed through other powers. Violence can prevent exterior members from executing the will's command, so the will can suffer violence regarding commanded acts. But violence cannot be done to the will's proper act. The will's act is inclination from an interior principle of knowledge, while compulsion comes from an exterior principle. Thus compulsion contradicts the will's nature as acting does the natural inclination's nature. A stone's upward movement from violence contradicts being dragged by force against one's own will.

### Reply to Objection 1
God can move man's will according to Proverbs: "The heart of the king is in the Lord's hand." But if this occurred through compulsion, it would no longer be an act of will, nor would the will itself move, but something else against the will.

### Reply to Objection 2
Violent movement occurs only when a passive subject is moved against its interior inclination. Otherwise all alteration and generation would be unnatural and violent. When the will is moved according to its own inclination by an appetible object, this movement is voluntary, not violent.

### Reply to Objection 3
What the will tends toward through sin, though evil in reality and contrary to rational nature, is apprehended as good and suitable to nature through pleasurable sensation or vicious habit.

## Article 5: Whether violence causes involuntariness?

### Objection 1
Involuntariness concerns the will. But violence cannot affect the will. Therefore violence cannot cause involuntariness.

### Objection 2
That done involuntarily is done with grief. Sometimes compulsion occurs without grief. Therefore violence does not cause involuntariness.

### Objection 3
What proceeds from the will cannot be involuntary. Some violent actions proceed from the will: when a heavy person goes upward or contorts limbs unnaturally. Therefore violence does not cause involuntariness.

### On the Contrary
The Philosopher and Damascene state that "things done under compulsion are involuntary."

### I Answer That
Violence directly opposes both the voluntary and the natural, since both originate from intrinsic principles while violence originates from external ones. What opposes nature is "unnatural"; what opposes the will is "involuntary." Therefore violence causes involuntariness.

### Reply to Objection 1
Not only acts immediately proceeding from the will are called voluntary, but also commanded acts. Violence cannot affect the will's immediate act; thus violence cannot make that involuntary. But regarding commanded acts, the will can suffer violence, so violence causes involuntariness in this respect.

### Reply to Objection 2
A thing is natural in two ways: as proceeding from nature as active principle; or according to passive principle, as when nature is inclined to receive external action. The heavens' movement is natural through natural aptitude despite the external mover. Similarly, an act is voluntary two ways: regarding action; or regarding passive reception. When an extrinsic agent acts and the recipient wills to suffer that action, violence does not exist simply, for the patient does something by consenting to suffer. Therefore this cannot be called involuntary.

### Reply to Objection 3
The Philosopher notes that animal movement, though contrary to bodily natural inclination, is somewhat natural to the animal moved according to appetite. Thus it is violent in a certain respect but not simply. Similarly, one contorting limbs unnaturally acts violently in a certain respect, not simply as to man himself.

## Article 6: Whether fear causes involuntariness simply?

### Objection 1
Violence regards present will-opposition; fear regards future evil repugnant to the will. Since violence causes involuntariness simply, fear does too.

### Objection 2
What is such of itself remains such when things are added. That done through fear is involuntary in itself. Therefore even with added fear, it remains involuntary.

### Objection 3
What is such conditionally is such in a certain respect; what is such absolutely is such simply. That done through fear is absolutely involuntary and voluntary only conditionally (to avoid feared evil). Therefore that done through fear is involuntary simply.

### On the Contrary
Gregory of Nyssa and the Philosopher state that such things done through fear are "voluntary rather than involuntary."

### I Answer That
Things done through fear are "of mixed character," partly voluntary and partly involuntary. Considered in itself, what is done through fear is not voluntary; it becomes voluntary in this particular case to avoid feared evil. Properly considered, such things are voluntary rather than involuntary. A thing is simply according as it is in act; but according as it is merely apprehended, it is not simply but in a certain respect. What is done through fear is in act insofar as it is done here and now. Throwing cargo overboard during a storm, done through fear of danger, is voluntary simply—it hinders greater evil feared. Thus it is essentially voluntary because its principle is within. Considered outside this particular case and as repugnant to the will, this is merely mental consideration. Therefore what is done through fear is involuntary in that respect only, outside actual circumstances.

### Reply to Objection 1
Things done through fear and compulsion differ: in compulsion, the will does not consent and is moved counter to what is done; in fear, the will is moved toward what is done, albeit not for its own sake but to avoid feared evil. The conditions of a voluntary act are satisfied if done for the sake of something else voluntary. In compulsion, the will does nothing inwardly; in fear, the will does something. To exclude things done through fear, violent action is defined not only as having "principal origin from without" but with the addition "in which he that suffers violence concurs not at all"—because the will of one in fear does concur somewhat in what fear produces.

### Reply to Objection 2
Things absolutely such remain such when things are added; but things relatively such vary according to comparison. What is big compared to one thing is small compared to another. Voluntariness exists not only absolutely, for its own sake, but relatively, for something else's sake. Therefore what was not voluntary compared with one thing becomes voluntary when compared with another.

### Reply to Objection 3
That done through fear is voluntary without condition as actually done; it is involuntary under condition if such fear were not threatening. Therefore this argument proves the opposite.

## Article 7: Whether concupiscence causes involuntariness?

### Objection 1
Fear is a passion causing involuntariness to some extent. Concupiscence is also a passion. Therefore concupiscence causes involuntariness too.

### Objection 2
The timid man through fear acts counter to his proposal; the incontinent man through concupiscence does similarly. Since fear causes involuntariness to some extent, concupiscence does also.

### Objection 3
Knowledge is necessary for voluntariness. Concupiscence impairs knowledge; the Philosopher states that pleasure "destroys the judgment of prudence." Therefore concupiscence causes involuntariness.

### On the Contrary
Damascene states: "The involuntary act deserves mercy or indulgence, and is done with regret." Neither applies to actions done from concupiscence. Therefore concupiscence does not cause involuntariness.

### I Answer That
Concupiscence does not cause involuntariness; rather it makes something voluntary. What is voluntary results from the will being moved toward it. Concupiscence inclines the will to desire its object. Therefore concupiscence's effect is making something voluntary rather than involuntary.

### Reply to Objection 1
Fear regards evil; concupiscence regards good. Evil of itself opposes the will, while good harmonizes with it. Therefore fear tends more than concupiscence to cause involuntariness.

### Reply to Objection 2
He who acts from fear retains the will's repugnance to what he does, considered in itself. But he who acts from concupiscence (like an incontinent man) does not retain his former will repudiating concupiscence's object; his will changes so he now desires what he previously repudiated. Thus what is done from fear is involuntary to some extent; what is done from concupiscence is not involuntary. The man yielding to concupiscence acts counter to his former purpose but not counter to his present desire; the timid man acts counter to what he presently desires.

### Reply to Objection 3
If concupiscence destroyed knowledge entirely, as with those rendered mad by it, concupiscence would remove voluntariness. Yet properly speaking this would not make the act involuntary, since in things bereft of reason there is neither voluntary nor involuntary. But in actions done from concupiscence, knowledge is not completely destroyed; the power of knowing is not entirely taken away, only actual consideration in some particular act. Nevertheless this itself is voluntary, according as voluntary means what is in the will's power—for example, "not to act" or "not to will," and similarly "not to consider." The will can resist passion, as stated elsewhere.

## Article 8: Whether ignorance causes involuntariness?

### Objection 1
"The involuntary act deserves pardon," as Damascene says. But sometimes what is done through ignorance does not deserve pardon, per Scripture: "If any man know not, he shall not be known." Therefore ignorance does not cause involuntariness.

### Objection 2
Every sin implies ignorance: "They err, that work evil." If ignorance causes involuntariness, every sin would be involuntary. This opposes Augustine's teaching that "every sin is voluntary."

### Objection 3
"Involuntariness is not without sadness," per Damascene. But sometimes things are done from ignorance without sadness: a man wishing to kill a foe may think he is killing a stag and does so without regret. Therefore ignorance does not cause involuntariness.

### On the Contrary
Damascene and the Philosopher state that "what is done through ignorance is involuntary."

### I Answer That
Ignorance causes involuntariness insofar as it deprives one of knowledge necessary to voluntariness. Not every ignorance does this. Ignorance relates to the will's act threefold: concomitantly, consequently, or antecedently. Concomitantly: ignorance of what is done such that even if known, it would be done. Ignorance does not induce one to wish this; it simply happens simultaneously with not knowing. Thus a man wishing to kill his foe but thinking to kill a stag demonstrates this. Such ignorance does not cause involuntariness but "non-voluntariness," since the unknown cannot be actually willed. Consequently: ignorance itself is voluntary in two ways. First, the will acts upon ignorance: wishing not to know to excuse sin or avoid hindrance (as Job 21:14 illustrates). This is "affected ignorance." Second, ignorance concerns what one can and ought to know. "Not to act" and "not to will" are voluntary as stated elsewhere; similarly "not to consider." Such ignorance arises from passion or habit. Though voluntary, it causes involuntariness in a certain respect, preceding the will's movement toward the act. Antecedently: ignorance is involuntary yet causes man to will what he would not otherwise will. A man unaware of circumstances he was not bound to know may shoot an arrow, slaying a passerby unintentionally. Such ignorance causes involuntariness simply.

### Reply to Objection 1
The first objection addresses ignorance of what one is bound to know.

### Reply to Objection 2
The second addresses ignorance of choice, which is voluntary to some extent as stated above.

### Reply to Objection 3
The third addresses ignorance concomitant with the will's act.
