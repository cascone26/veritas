# Question 32: The Cause of Pleasure

## Article 1: Whether Operation is the Proper Cause of Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Operation should not be considered the primary cause of pleasure, since the Philosopher states that "pleasure consists in a perception of the senses." Knowledge is required for pleasure, and the objects of operations are knowable before the operations themselves occur. Therefore, operation is not the proper cause of pleasure.

**Objection 2:** Pleasure consists especially in an end that has been gained, which is chiefly desired. Yet the end is not always an operation but sometimes the effect of an operation. Therefore operation is not the direct cause of pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Leisure and rest, which constitute a cessation from work, are objects of pleasure. Therefore operation is not the proper cause of pleasure.

### On the Contrary

The Philosopher teaches that "pleasure is a connatural and uninterrupted operation."

### I Answer That

Two things are necessary for pleasure: the attainment of a suitable good and knowledge of this attainment. Each of these consists in a kind of operation, since actual knowledge is an operation and the attainment of suitable good occurs through operation. Moreover, proper operation itself constitutes a suitable good. Therefore, every pleasure must result from some operation.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Objects of operations become pleasurable only when united to us—either through knowledge alone (as when we enjoy thinking of or viewing certain things) or through knowledge combined with possession. We take pleasure in knowing we possess goods like riches or honor because they are apprehended as belonging to us. As the Philosopher observes, "we take great pleasure in looking upon a thing as our own, by reason of the natural love we have for ourselves." Possessing such things involves using them or being able to use them, which requires operation. Therefore, every pleasure traces to some operation as its cause.

**Reply 2:** When the end is not an operation but the effect of an operation, that effect becomes pleasant insofar as it is possessed or effected, which implies use or operation.

**Reply 3:** Operations are pleasant insofar as they are proportionate and connatural to the agent. Since human power is finite, operation must accord with a certain measure. When it exceeds that measure, it becomes painful and irksome rather than pleasant. Leisure, play, and repose are pleasant because they remove sadness resulting from excessive labor.

## Article 2: Whether Movement is a Cause of Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** The good that is obtained and actually possessed causes pleasure. The Philosopher states that "pleasure is not compared with generation, but with the operation of a thing already in existence." Something being moved toward something has it not yet; it is, so to speak, being generated regarding it. Therefore movement is not a cause of pleasure.

**Objection 2:** Movement is the chief cause of toil and fatigue in our works. Operations that are toilsome and fatiguing are disagreeable rather than pleasant. Therefore movement is not a cause of pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Movement implies innovation, which opposes custom. But "things which we are accustomed to, are pleasant." Therefore movement is not a cause of pleasure.

### On the Contrary

Augustine asks: "What means this, O Lord my God, whereas Thou art everlasting joy to Thyself, and some things around Thee evermore rejoice in Thee? What means this, that this portion of things ebbs and flows alternately displeased and reconciled?" This indicates that humans rejoice in certain alterations, so movement seems to cause pleasure.

### I Answer That

Three things are requisite for pleasure: the one pleased, the pleasurable object conjoined with that person, and knowledge of this conjunction. In respect of these three, movement is pleasant. Regarding those who feel pleasure, change is pleasant because our nature is changeable; what suits us at one time may not suit us at another (warming by fire suits us in winter but not summer).

Concerning the pleasing good united to us, change is pleasant because continued action of an agent increases its effect. The natural mode of being consists in a certain measure; when the continued presence of a pleasant object exceeds this measure, the removal of that object becomes pleasant.

Regarding knowledge itself, change becomes pleasant because humans desire to know something whole and perfect. When a thing cannot be apprehended all at once, change is pleasant so one part passes and another succeeds, allowing the whole to be perceived. Augustine notes that syllables should not stay but "fly away, that others may come, and thou hear the whole. And so whenever any one thing is made up of many, all of which do not exist together, all would please collectively more than they do severally."

If something has an unchangeable nature, its natural mode cannot be exceeded by continued pleasant objects, and it can behold its entire delight at once—to such a being change affords no delight. The more pleasures approach this state, the more capable they are of being continual.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Although the subject of movement has not yet perfectly acquired that toward which it moves, it is beginning to have something of it; in this respect movement has something of pleasure. Yet it falls short of perfect pleasure, since more perfect pleasures regard unchangeable things. Moreover, movement causes pleasure insofar as something previously unsuitable becomes suitable or ceases to be.

**Reply 2:** Movement causes toil and fatigue when it exceeds our natural aptitude. It causes pleasure not in this way but by removing obstacles to our natural aptitude.

**Reply 3:** What is customary becomes pleasant insofar as it becomes natural; custom is like a second nature. The movement that gives pleasure is not that departing from custom but that preventing corruption of the natural mode of being resulting from continued operation. From the same cause of connaturalness, both custom and movement become pleasant.

## Article 3: Whether Hope and Memory Cause Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Pleasure is caused by present good, as Damascene states. Hope and memory regard absent things: memory concerns the past, hope concerns the future. Therefore memory and hope do not cause pleasure.

**Objection 2:** The same thing is not the cause of contraries. Hope causes affliction, according to Proverbs: "Hope that is deferred afflicteth the soul." Therefore hope does not cause pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Just as hope agrees with pleasure in regarding good, so do desire and love. Therefore hope should not be assigned as a cause of pleasure more than desire or love.

### On the Contrary

Scripture states: "Rejoicing in hope" and "I remembered God, and was delighted."

### I Answer That

Pleasure is caused by the presence of suitable good, insofar as it is felt or perceived in any way. A thing is present to us in two ways: first, in knowledge (the thing known is in the knower by its likeness); second, in reality (one thing is in real conjunction with another, actually or potentially). Since real conjunction is greater than conjunction by knowledge, and actual is greater than potential conjunction, the greatest pleasure arises from sensation, which requires the sensible object's presence.

The second place belongs to the pleasure of hope, wherein pleasurable conjunction exists not only regarding apprehension but also regarding the faculty or power of obtaining the pleasurable object. The third place belongs to the pleasure of memory, which has only the conjunction of apprehension.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Hope and memory concern things which, absolutely speaking, are absent; yet those things are, after a fashion, present—either according to apprehension only or according to apprehension and the possibility of attainment.

**Reply 2:** Nothing prevents the same thing, in different ways, being the cause of contraries. Hope, inasmuch as it implies a present appraising of a future good, causes pleasure; whereas, inasmuch as it implies absence of that good, it causes affliction.

**Reply 3:** Love and desire also cause pleasure. Everything loved becomes pleasing to the lover, since love is a kind of union of lover and beloved. Every object of desire is pleasing to one who desires it, since desire chiefly craves pleasure. However, hope, as implying a certainty of the real presence of the pleasing good not implied by love or desire, is reckoned as causing pleasure in preference to them, and also in preference to memory, which concerns what has already passed away.

## Article 4: Whether Sadness Causes Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Nothing causes its own contrary. Sadness is contrary to pleasure. Therefore sadness does not cause pleasure.

**Objection 2:** Contraries have contrary effects. But pleasures, when called to mind, cause pleasure. Therefore sad things, when remembered, cause sorrow and not pleasure.

**Objection 3:** As sadness is to pleasure, so is hatred to love. But hatred does not cause love; rather the reverse occurs. Therefore sadness does not cause pleasure.

### On the Contrary

Scripture states: "My tears have been my bread day and night," where bread denotes the refreshment of pleasure. Therefore tears, which arise from sadness, can give pleasure.

### I Answer That

Sadness may be considered in two ways: as actually existing and as existing in memory. In both ways sadness can cause pleasure.

Sadness as actually existing causes pleasure inasmuch as it brings to mind that which is loved, the absence of which causes sadness; yet the mere thought of it gives pleasure. The recollection of sadness becomes a cause of pleasure on account of the deliverance that ensued: because absence of evil is regarded as something good. Insofar as someone thinks that he has been delivered from that which caused him sorrow and pain, so much reason he has to rejoice.

Augustine states that "oftentimes in joy we call to mind sad things...and in the season of health we recall past pains without feeling pain...and in proportion are the more filled with joy and gladness," and again that "the more peril there was in the battle, so much the more joy will there be in the triumph."

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Sometimes accidentally a thing is the cause of its contrary; thus "that which is cold sometimes causes heat." In like manner sadness is the accidental cause of pleasure, insofar as it gives rise to the apprehension of something pleasant.

**Reply 2:** Sad things called to mind cause pleasure not insofar as they are sad and contrary to pleasant things, but insofar as one is delivered from them. Similarly, the recollection of pleasant things may cause sadness by reason of these being lost.

**Reply 3:** Hatred also can be the accidental cause of love—insofar as some love one another inasmuch as they agree in hating one and the same thing.

## Article 5: Whether the Actions of Others are a Cause of Pleasure to Us?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** The cause of pleasure is our own good when conjoined to us. The actions of others are not conjoined to us. Therefore they are not a cause of pleasure to us.

**Objection 2:** Action is the agent's own good. If the actions of others cause pleasure to us, then for the same reason all goods belonging to others will be pleasing to us, which is evidently untrue.

**Objection 3:** Action is pleasant through proceeding from an innate habit; Ethic. ii states that "we must reckon the pleasure which follows after action, as being the sign of a habit existing in us." But the actions of others do not proceed from habits existing in us. Therefore the actions of others are not pleasing to us.

### On the Contrary

Scripture states: "I was exceeding glad that I found thy children walking in truth."

### I Answer That

Two things are requisite for pleasure: the attainment of one's proper good and knowledge of having obtained it. The action of another may cause pleasure to us in three ways.

First, from the fact that we obtain some good through the action of another. The actions of those who do good to us are pleasing to us, since it is pleasant to be benefited by another.

Second, from the fact that another's action makes us know or appreciate our own good. Men take pleasure in being praised or honored by others because they thereby become aware of good existing in themselves. This appreciation receives greater weight from the testimony of good and wise men, hence men take greater pleasure in being praised and honored by them. Because a flatterer appears to praise, flattery is pleasing to some. As love is for something good while admiration is for something great, so it is pleasant to be loved and admired by others, inasmuch as one thereby becomes aware of his own goodness or greatness.

Third, from the fact that another's actions, if they be good, are reckoned as one's own good, by reason of the power of love, which makes one regard his friend as one with himself. On account of hatred, which makes one reckon another's good as being in opposition to oneself, the evil action of an enemy becomes an object of pleasure. Scripture states that charity "rejoiceth not in iniquity, but rejoiceth with the truth."

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Another's action may be conjoined to me either by its effect, as in the first way, or by knowledge, as in the second way, or by affection, as in the third way.

**Reply 2:** This argument avails for the third mode but not for the first two.

**Reply 3:** Although the actions of another do not proceed from habits in me, yet they either produce in me something that gives pleasure, or they make me appreciate or know a habit of mind, or they proceed from the habit of one who is united to me by love.

## Article 6: Whether Doing Good to Another is a Cause of Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Pleasure is caused by one's obtaining one's proper good. Doing good pertains not to obtaining but to spending one's proper good. Therefore it seems to be the cause of sadness rather than pleasure.

**Objection 2:** The Philosopher states that "illiberality is more connatural to man than prodigality." Doing good to others marks prodigality; desisting from doing good marks illiberality. Since everyone takes pleasure in a connatural operation, doing good to others is not a cause of pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Contrary effects proceed from contrary causes. Man takes a natural pleasure in certain kinds of ill-doing, such as overcoming, contradicting or scolding others, or, if angry, in punishing them. Therefore doing good to others is a cause of sadness rather than pleasure.

### On the Contrary

The Philosopher states that "it is most pleasant to give presents or assistance to friends and strangers."

### I Answer That

Doing good to another may give pleasure in three ways.

First, in consideration of the effect, which is the good conferred on another. Through being united to others by love, we regard their good as being our own, so we take pleasure in the good we do to others, especially to our friends, as in our own good.

Second, in consideration of the end: as when a man, from doing good to another, hopes to get some good for himself, either from God or from man, since hope is a cause of pleasure.

Third, in consideration of the principle—threefold with respect to principle. One is the faculty of doing good: doing good to another becomes pleasant insofar as it arouses in man an imagination of abundant good existing in him, whereof he is able to give others a share. Therefore men take pleasure in their children and in their own works, as being things on which they bestow a share of their own good.

Another principle is man's habitual inclination to do good, by reason of which doing good becomes connatural to him: for which reason the liberal man takes pleasure in giving to others.

The third principle is the motive: for instance when a man is moved by one whom he loves to do good to someone, for whatever we do or suffer for a friend is pleasant, because love is the principal cause of pleasure.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Spending gives pleasure as showing forth one's good. But insofar as it empties us of our own good it may be a cause of sadness—for instance when it is excessive.

**Reply 2:** Prodigality is an excessive spending, which is unnatural; wherefore prodigality is said to be contrary to nature.

**Reply 3:** To overcome, to contradict, and to punish give pleasure not as tending to another's ill but as pertaining to one's own good, which man loves more than he hates another's ill. It is naturally pleasant to overcome inasmuch as it makes one appreciate his own superiority. All games wherein there is striving for mastery afford the greatest pleasure, as do all contests insofar as they admit hope of victory.

To contradict and to scold can give pleasure in two ways. First, as making man imagine himself to be wise and excellent, since it belongs to wise men and elders to reprove and scold. Second, insofar as by scolding and reproving, one does good to another, which gives one pleasure.

It is pleasant to an angry man to punish insofar as he thinks himself to be removing an apparent slight, which seems due to a previous hurt: for when hurt by another, one seems to be slighted thereby; therefore one wishes to be quit of this slight by paying back the hurt. Thus it is clear that doing good to another may be of itself pleasant; whereas doing evil to another is not pleasant except insofar as it seems to affect one's own good.

## Article 7: Whether Likeness is a Cause of Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Ruling and presiding seem to imply unlikeness. But "it is natural to take pleasure in ruling and presiding." Therefore unlikeness, rather than likeness, is a cause of pleasure.

**Objection 2:** Nothing is more unlike pleasure than sorrow. But those burdened by sorrow are most inclined to seek pleasures. Therefore unlikeness, rather than likeness, is a cause of pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Those satiated with certain delights derive not pleasure but disgust from them, such as when one is satiated with food. Therefore likeness is not a cause of pleasure.

### On the Contrary

Likeness is a cause of love, as stated above, and love is the cause of pleasure. Therefore likeness is a cause of pleasure.

### I Answer That

Likeness is a kind of unity; hence that which is like us, as being one with us, causes pleasure, just as it causes love. If that which is like us does not hurt our own good but increases it, it is pleasurable simply—for instance one man in respect of another, one youth in relation to another. But if it be hurtful to our own good, it accidentally causes disgust or sadness, not as being like and one with us but as hurtful to that which is yet more one with us.

Something like can be hurtful to our own good in two ways. First, by destroying the measure of our own good, by a kind of excess; because good, especially bodily good such as health, is conditioned by a certain measure. Wherefore superfluous good or any bodily pleasure causes disgust. Second, by being directly contrary to one's own good: thus a potter dislikes other potters, not because they are potters but because they deprive him of his own excellence or profits, which he seeks as his own good.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Since ruler and subject are in communion with one another, there is a certain likeness between them; but this likeness is conditioned by a certain superiority, since ruling and presiding pertain to the excellence of a man's own good. They belong to men who are wise and better than others; the result being that they give man an idea of his own excellence. Another reason is that by ruling and presiding, a man does good to others, which is pleasant.

**Reply 2:** That which gives pleasure to the sorrowful man, though unlike sorrow, bears some likeness to the man that is sorrowful, because sorrows are contrary to his own good. Wherefore the sorrowful man seeks pleasure as making for his own good, insofar as it is a remedy for its contrary. Thus bodily pleasures, which are contrary to certain sorrows, are more sought than intellectual pleasures, which have no contrary sorrow. This is why all animals naturally desire pleasure and why young people are most inclined to seek pleasures, on account of the many changes to which they are subject while growing. The melancholic has a strong desire for pleasures in order to drive away sorrow, because his "body is corroded by a base humor."

**Reply 3:** Bodily goods are conditioned by a certain fixed measure. Wherefore surfeit of such things destroys the proper good and consequently gives rise to disgust and sorrow, through being contrary to the proper good of man.

## Article 8: Whether Wonder is a Cause of Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Wonder is the act of one who is ignorant of the nature of something, as Damascene states. Knowledge, rather than ignorance, is a cause of pleasure. Therefore wonder is not a cause of pleasure.

**Objection 2:** Wonder is the beginning of wisdom, being as it were the road to the search of truth. But "it is more pleasant to think of what we know, than to seek what we know not," as the Philosopher states. In the latter case we encounter difficulties and hindrances; in the former not. Pleasure arises from an unhindered operation. Therefore wonder hinders rather than causes pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Everyone takes pleasure in what he is accustomed to: wherefore the actions of habits acquired by custom are pleasant. But "we wonder at what is unwonted," as Augustine states. Therefore wonder is contrary to the cause of pleasure.

### On the Contrary

The Philosopher states that wonder is the cause of pleasure.

### I Answer That

It is pleasant to get what one desires, and therefore the greater the desire for the thing loved, the greater the pleasure when it is attained. Indeed the very increase of desire brings with it an increase of pleasure, according as it gives rise to hope of obtaining that which is loved, since desire resulting from hope is a cause of pleasure.

Now wonder is a kind of desire for knowledge. A desire comes to man when he sees an effect of which the cause either is unknown to him, or surpasses his knowledge or faculty of understanding. Consequently wonder is a cause of pleasure, insofar as it includes a hope of getting the knowledge which one desires to have. For this reason whatever is wonderful is pleasing, for instance things that are scarce.

Representations of things, even of those which are not pleasant in themselves, give rise to pleasure; for the soul rejoices in comparing one thing with another, because comparison of one thing with another is the proper and connatural act of reason. This is why "it is more delightful to be delivered from great danger, because it is something wonderful."

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Wonder gives pleasure not because it implies ignorance but insofar as it includes the desire of learning the cause and insofar as the wonderer learns something new—that the cause is other than he had thought it to be.

**Reply 2:** Pleasure includes two things: rest in the good and perception of this rest. As to the former, since it is more perfect to contemplate the known truth than to seek for the unknown, the contemplation of what we know is in itself more pleasing than the research of what we do not know. Nevertheless, as to the second, it happens that research is sometimes more pleasing accidentally, insofar as it proceeds from a greater desire: for greater desire is awakened when we are conscious of our ignorance. This is why man takes the greatest pleasure in finding or learning things for the first time.

**Reply 3:** It is pleasant to do what we are wont to do, inasmuch as this is connatural to us. Yet things that are of rare occurrence can be pleasant, either regarding knowledge, from the fact that we desire to know something about them insofar as they are wonderful, or regarding action, from the fact that "the mind is more inclined by desire to act intensely in things that are new." Since more perfect operation causes more perfect pleasure, this explains why rare things give pleasure.
