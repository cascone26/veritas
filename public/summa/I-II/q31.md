# Question 31: Pleasure Considered in Itself

## Article 1: Whether Pleasure is a Passion?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Damascene distinguishes operation from passion, stating that "operation is a movement in accord with nature, while passion is a movement contrary to nature." Since pleasure constitutes an operation according to Aristotle, it appears not to be a passion.

**Objection 2:** To be passive means to be moved. However, pleasure consists in having already been moved—it arises from good already obtained. Therefore pleasure is not a passion.

**Objection 3:** Pleasure perfects operation, as Aristotle notes. But perfection does not involve passivity or alteration. Therefore pleasure is not a passion.

### On the Contrary

Augustine reckons delight, joy, and gladness among the soul's passions.

### I Answer That

The movements of the sensitive appetite are properly called passions. Every emotion arising from sensitive apprehension constitutes a movement of the sensitive appetite. Pleasure must be classified thus, as Aristotle describes it: "delight is a certain movement of the soul and a sensible establishing thereof all at once, in keeping with the nature of the thing."

When animals attain their natural perfections, unlike insensate things, they perceive this establishment. This perception generates a movement in the sensitive appetite—delight itself. The term "movement" identifies pleasure's genus; "establishing in keeping with nature" indicates its cause (presence of suitable good); "all at once" emphasizes its completeness rather than process; and "sensible" excludes perfections in insensate things.

Therefore, since pleasure represents an appetitive movement arising from sensory apprehension, it constitutes a passion of the soul.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Unobstructed connatural operation represents a second perfection. When something attains such operation, pleasure follows as a state of completion. When we term pleasure an "operation," we designate not its essence but its cause.

**Reply 2:** Two movements occur in animals: one according to intention (appetitive), the other according to execution (external). Though the movement of execution ceases once the good is obtained, the appetitive movement continues. The appetite previously desired what it lacked; now it delights in what it possesses. Despite being a repose of appetite regarding the good's satisfaction, the impression on appetite by its object remains, making delight a kind of movement.

**Reply 3:** Though "passion" more appropriately names movements with corruptive, evil tendencies (bodily ailments, sadness, fear), some passions tend toward good, as stated elsewhere. In this sense, delight qualifies as a passion.

## Article 2: Whether Pleasure is Subject to Time?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Aristotle states that "delight is a kind of movement." All movement exists in time. Therefore pleasure exists in time.

**Objection 2:** Some pleasures are called "morose" or lingering. Duration pertains to time. Therefore pleasure exists in time.

**Objection 3:** Soul's passions belong to one genus. Some soul's passions exist in time. Therefore pleasure exists in time.

### On the Contrary

Aristotle states: "no one takes pleasure according to time."

### I Answer That

Things exist in time in two ways: essentially, when succession pertains to them (movement, speech); or accidentally, when they are subject to something successive yet succession is not essential to them (being human).

Pleasure itself is not essentially in time—it regards good already gained, like a movement's terminus. However, if that good is subject to change, pleasure therein exists in time accidentally. If the good is entirely unchangeable, pleasure in it exists in neither way.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Movement is twofold. One is "act of something imperfect"—successive and in time. Another is "act of something perfect" (understanding, sensing, willing, delighting)—not successive or essentially in time.

**Reply 2:** Pleasure is called long-lasting or morose according to its accidental existence in time.

**Reply 3:** Other passions differ from pleasure in not having obtained good as their object. Thus they contain more imperfect movement, making pleasure distinctively not essentially temporal.

## Article 3: Whether Pleasure Differs from Joy?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Passions' species derive from objects. Pleasure and joy share the same object—obtained good. Therefore they are identical.

**Objection 2:** One movement yields one terminus. The desire movement ends in both joy and pleasure. Therefore they are identical.

**Objection 3:** If joy differs from pleasure, equal reason exists to distinguish gladness, exultation, and cheerfulness from pleasure, making multiple passions. This seems untrue. Therefore joy does not differ from pleasure.

### On the Contrary

We speak of joy only in rational creatures, not in irrational animals. We attribute pleasure to both. Therefore joy and pleasure differ.

### I Answer That

As Avicenna states, joy constitutes a type of delight. Just as some desires are natural while others follow reason, some pleasures are natural while others are rational. Or as Damascene and Gregory of Nyssa describe it, some pleasures are bodily, others of the soul—amounts to the same thing.

We experience pleasure in naturally desired things upon obtaining them, and in things desired through reason. However, we reserve "joy" for pleasure following reason. Thus joy is not attributed to irrational animals—only pleasure.

Whatever we naturally desire can become the object of reasoned desire and joy, but not necessarily vice versa. Thus pleasure extends to more objects than joy, since one may feel bodily delight without rejoicing according to reason.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Object diversity relates to apprehension diversity. Soul's delights, also called joys, distinguish themselves from bodily delights, not otherwise called joys—corresponding to the distinction noted regarding desires.

**Reply 2:** Similar distinction appears in desires: pleasure corresponds to concupiscence; joy corresponds to desire, pertaining more to soul's appetite. Hence repose types differ accordingly.

**Reply 3:** These other terms derive from delight's effects: "gladness" from heart "dilation"; "exultation" from inward joy bursting outwardly; "cheerfulness" from particular gladness signs. All belong to joy, employed only of rational beings.

## Article 4: Whether Pleasure is in the Intellectual Appetite?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Aristotle calls delight "a sensible movement." Sensible movement belongs not to intellectual powers. Therefore pleasure does not exist in the intellectual appetite.

**Objection 2:** Every passion exists in the sensitive appetite. Therefore pleasure exists only in the sensitive appetite.

**Objection 3:** Pleasure is common to us and irrational animals. Therefore it exists only in powers shared with them.

### On the Contrary

Scripture says: "Delight in the Lord." The sensitive appetite cannot reach God; only the intellectual appetite can. Therefore pleasure exists in the intellectual appetite.

### I Answer That

Certain pleasure arises from reason's apprehension. When reason apprehends something, both the sensitive appetite is moved regarding particular application and the intellectual appetite (will) is moved. Thus in the will occurs joy—the pleasure from reason's apprehension—though not bodily pleasure.

The difference is this: sensitive appetite's pleasure accompanies bodily transformation, while intellectual appetite's pleasure constitutes mere will's movement. Augustine says: "desire and joy are nothing else but a volition of consent to the things we wish."

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** The Philosopher uses "sensible" broadly for any perception. Aristotle states that delight attends every sense and every intellect act and contemplation. Alternatively, he defines sensitive appetite's delight specifically.

**Reply 2:** Pleasure properly has passion's character when accompanied by bodily transformation. It is not thus in the intellectual appetite, but as simple movement—existing also in God and angels. Aristotle states God "rejoices by one simple act." Dionysius says angels are not susceptible to our passible delight, but rejoice with God with "incorruption's gladness."

**Reply 3:** In us, pleasure exists not only shared with dumb animals but also with angels. Dionysius notes that "holy men often take part in angelic delights." We have pleasure in both the sensitive appetite (shared with animals) and intellectual appetite (shared with angels).

## Article 5: Whether Bodily Pleasures Exceed Spiritual Pleasures

### Objections

**Objection 1:** All seek some pleasure according to Aristotle. More seek sensible than intelligible spiritual pleasures. Therefore bodily pleasures are greater.

**Objection 2:** Cause-greatness is known through effects. Bodily pleasures alter the body's state and cause madness in some. Therefore bodily pleasures are greater.

**Objection 3:** Bodily pleasures require tempering by reason due to vehemence. Spiritual pleasures require no such checking. Therefore bodily pleasures are greater.

### On the Contrary

Scripture states: "How sweet are Thy words to my palate; more than honey to my mouth!" Aristotle says: "the greatest pleasure is derived from the operation of wisdom."

### I Answer That

Pleasure arises from union with suitable perceived or known object. Soul's operations, especially sensitive and intellectual operations, do not pass into external matter—they are the agent's acts and perfections (understanding, feeling, willing). Actions passing into external matter are rather the matter's perfections. Thus sensitive and intellectual soul's aforementioned operations constitute the agent's good, known by sense and intellect, producing pleasure from the operations themselves, not merely their objects.

Comparing intellectual with sensible pleasures regarding the very acts (sensitive and intellectual knowledge), intellectual pleasures vastly exceed sensible ones. Man delights much more in understanding something than in sensing it. Intellectual knowledge is more perfect, better known (intellect reflects on its own act more than sense does), and more beloved. No one would forfeit intellectual vision rather than bodily sight.

However, comparing intellectual spiritual with sensible bodily pleasures absolutely and in themselves, spiritual pleasures are greater, evident from three elements of pleasure: the good conjoined, the recipient, and the conjunction itself.

Spiritual good is greater and more beloved than bodily good—men forsake even greatest bodily pleasures rather than suffer honor loss (intellectual good). The intellectual faculty is far more noble and knowing than the sensitive faculty. The conjunction is more intimate (intellect penetrates essence while senses perceive accidents), more perfect (sensible pleasures involve movement—imperfect acts—thus realized gradually; intelligible things are unmoved, realized all at once), and more firm (bodily pleasure objects are corruptible and passing; spiritual goods are incorruptible).

However, relative to us, bodily pleasures prove more vehement for three reasons: First, sensible things are better known than intelligible things to us. Second, sensible pleasures, as sensitive appetite passions, involve bodily alteration; spiritual pleasures do not, except through superior appetite's reaction on the lower. Third, bodily pleasures remedy bodily defects and troubles producing grief, thus felt more acutely and welcomed more than spiritual pleasures lacking contrary griefs.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** More pursue bodily pleasures because sensible goods are better and more generally known. Men need pleasures as remedies for many sorrows. Since most cannot attain spiritual pleasures (proper to virtuous persons), they seek bodily ones.

**Reply 2:** Bodily transformation arises more from bodily pleasures as sensitive appetite passions.

**Reply 3:** Bodily pleasures are realized in the sensitive faculty governed by reason, thus requiring tempering. Spiritual pleasures exist in mind, which is itself the rule, thus inherently sober and moderate.

## Article 6: Whether the Pleasures of Touch Exceed Those of the Other Senses?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Greatest pleasure seems to be that without which all joy ceases. Sight provides this—one dwelling in darkness without heaven's light has no joy. Therefore sight affords the greatest pleasure.

**Objection 2:** Everyone finds treasure in what he loves. Aristotle states sight is most loved among senses. Therefore sight affords greatest pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Friendship for the pleasant begins principally through sight. Pleasure causes such friendship. Therefore sight affords greatest pleasure.

### On the Contrary

Aristotle states: the greatest pleasures are those afforded by touch.

### I Answer That

Everything gives pleasure according as it is loved. Senses are loved for two reasons: knowledge and usefulness. Senses afford pleasure both ways. Since man peculiarly apprehends knowledge itself as good, those pleasures arising from knowledge are proper to man, while usefulness-related pleasures are common to all animals.

Regarding sensible pleasure from knowledge, sight clearly affords greater pleasure than other senses. However, regarding sensible pleasure from usefulness, touch affords greatest pleasure. Usefulness is gauged by relation to animal nature's preservation. Touch's sensible objects bear closest relation to this—touch perceives vital things (hot, cold, etc.). Thus touch's pleasures relate most closely to the end. Other animals deriving pleasure only from usefulness take delight through other senses only as subordinated to touch's objects: dogs delight not in hares' smell but in eating them; lions feel no pleasure in oxen's lowing but in devouring them.

Since touch's pleasure is greatest regarding usefulness, and sight's greatest regarding knowledge, comparison requires consideration. Touch's pleasure is absolutely greater than sight's, remaining within sensible pleasure limits. Natural things are most powerful; natural concupiscences (food, sexual union) are ordained to touch's pleasures.

However, considering sight's pleasures as mind's handmaid, these are greater—intellectual pleasures exceed sensible ones.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Joy denotes soul's pleasure, belonging principally to sight. Natural pleasure belongs principally to touch.

**Reply 2:** Sight is most loved "for knowledge, helping distinguish many things."

**Reply 3:** Pleasure causes carnal love one way; sight, another. Touch's pleasure is the friendship-for-the-pleasant's final cause. Sight is a cause like movement's beginning—the beholder's impression from the lovable object entices love and delight-seeking.

## Article 7: Whether Any Pleasure is Non-Natural?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Pleasure is to soul's emotions what repose is to bodies. Natural body's appetite reposes only in its connatural place. Neither can the animal appetite's repose (pleasure) be elsewhere than in something connatural. Therefore no pleasure is non-natural.

**Objection 2:** What is against nature is violent. Whatever is violent causes grief. Therefore nothing unnatural can give pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Being established in one's nature, if perceived, produces pleasure, evident from Aristotle's stated definition. Every thing naturally is established in its nature; natural movement tends to natural ends. Therefore every pleasure is natural.

### On the Contrary

Aristotle states: some things are pleasant "not from nature but from disease."

### I Answer That

That is natural which accords with nature. In man, nature can be understood two ways. First, inasmuch as intellect and reason constitute man's principal part—his specific nature—those pleasures derived from things pertaining to man regarding reason can be called natural to man: for instance, pleasure in contemplating truth and performing virtue acts. Second, nature in man contrasts with reason, denoting what is common to man and other animals, especially the part disobeying reason. In this sense, things preserving the body (food, drink, sleep) or the species (sexual intercourse) afford natural pleasure.

Under each pleasure kind, some are not natural absolutely, yet connatural in some respects. When an individual's natural species principle is corrupted, something contrary to specific nature becomes accidentally natural to that individual: thus hot water naturally gives heat.

Consequently, something not natural to man—either regarding reason or body preservation—becomes connatural to an individual man through nature-corruption. This corruption may be bodily (ailment—fever makes sweet things seem bitter), temperament (evil temperament—some take pleasure in eating earth, coals), or soul (custom—some take pleasure in cannibalism, unnatural intercourse, things contrary to human nature).

This suffices for objection-answers.

## Article 8: Whether One Pleasure Can Be Contrary to Another?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Soul's passions derive species and contrariety from objects. Pleasure's object is good. Good is not contrary to good; good is contrary to evil, evil to good. Therefore one pleasure is not contrary to another.

**Objection 2:** To one thing, one contrary exists. Sadness is contrary to pleasure. Therefore pleasure is not contrary to pleasure.

**Objection 3:** If one pleasure is contrary to another, this is only from the pleasurable things' contrariety. But this difference is material; contrariety is form-difference. Therefore no contrariety exists between pleasures.

### On the Contrary

Things of the same genus impeding each other are contraries, as Aristotle states. Some pleasures impede one another, as Aristotle notes. Therefore some pleasures are contrary.

### I Answer That

Pleasure in soul's emotions resembles repose in natural bodies. One repose is contrary to another when they are in contrary termini—thus "high place repose is contrary to low place repose." Similarly, in soul's emotions, one pleasure can be contrary to another.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** The Philosopher's saying applies to good and evil as applied to virtues and vices—one vice may be contrary to another, while no virtue is contrary to another virtue. However, nothing prevents one good from being contrary to another (hot is good regarding fire, cold regarding water). One pleasure can be contrary to another similarly. That this is impossible regarding virtue's good is because virtue's good depends on fittingness to reason.

**Reply 2:** Soul's emotions' pleasure resembles natural bodies' natural repose—its object is suitable and connatural. Sadness resembles violent repose—its object is disagreeable to animal appetite, as violent repose-place is disagreeable to natural appetite. Natural repose is contrary both to the same body's violent repose and to another's natural repose. Similarly, pleasure is contrary both to another pleasure and to sadness.

**Reply 3:** Pleasure-giving things, being pleasure's objects, cause not only material but also formal difference if pleasurableness formality differs. Object-difference causes specific acts and passions difference, as stated elsewhere.
