# Question 5: The Attainment of Happiness

## Article 1: Whether Man Can Attain Happiness

### Objection 1
The rational nature exceeds the sensitive nature, just as the intellectual surpasses the rational. Since irrational animals cannot attain the end of rational nature, man cannot attain the end of intellectual nature, which is Happiness.

### Objection 2
True Happiness consists in seeing God, who is pure Truth. Man naturally understands through material things and phantasms. Therefore he cannot attain Happiness.

### Objection 3
Happiness consists in attaining the Sovereign Good. The angelic nature, positioned between God and human nature, must be surpassed first. Thus man cannot attain Happiness.

### On the Contrary
"Blessed is the man whom Thou shalt instruct, O Lord" (Psalm 93:12).

### I Answer That
Happiness is the attainment of the Perfect Good. Man is capable of the Perfect Good because his intellect apprehends universal and perfect good, and his will can desire it. Man is capable of seeing God, in which perfect Happiness consists.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellectual surpasses the rational in the mode of knowing the same intelligible truth. Reason reaches by inquiry what intellect grasps immediately. Therefore rational nature can attain Happiness, though differently than angels—man attains it over time while angels attained it instantly.

### Reply to Objection 2
In the present life, man knows intelligible truth through phantasms. However, after this life, he possesses another natural way of knowing.

### Reply to Objection 3
Man cannot surpass angels in natural degree. Yet by intellectual operation, he can understand that something above the angels makes men happy, and upon attaining it, he will be perfectly happy.

## Article 2: Whether One Man Can Be Happier Than Another

### Objection 1
Happiness is the reward of virtue. Equal reward is given for all virtuous works—all laborers received a penny. Therefore one man cannot be happier than another.

### Objection 2
Happiness is the supreme good. Nothing surpasses the supreme. Therefore one man's Happiness cannot surpass another's.

### Objection 3
Happiness brings rest to man's desire. If he lacks no good he can obtain and there is no greater good, then either he is not happy or no other Happiness can be greater.

### On the Contrary
"In My Father's house there are many mansions" signifying diverse dignities of merits in eternal life, which is Happiness itself (John 14:2; Augustine).

### I Answer That
Happiness involves two things: the Sovereign Good itself, and attainment or enjoyment of that Good. As to the Good itself, one Happiness cannot exceed another—there is but one Sovereign Good, God. However, regarding enjoyment of this Good, one man can be happier than another through being better disposed to enjoy Him.

### Reply to Objection 1
The one penny signifies that Happiness is one in its object. The many mansions signify manifold Happiness in diverse degrees of enjoyment.

### Reply to Objection 2
Happiness is supreme as the perfect possession or enjoyment of the Supreme Good.

### Reply to Objection 3
The Blessed lack no desirable good, possessing the Infinite Good itself. One is happier than another through diverse participation in the same good. "He who knows Thee, and others besides, is not the happier for knowing them."

## Article 3: Whether One Can Be Happy in This Life

### Objection 1
"Blessed are the undefiled in the way, who walk in the law of the Lord" (Psalm 118:1). This happens in this life. Therefore one can be happy in this life.

### Objection 2
Imperfect participation in the Sovereign Good does not destroy the nature of Happiness. Men can participate in the Sovereign Good in this life by knowing and loving God, albeit imperfectly. Therefore man can be happy in this life.

### Objection 3
What many say comes apparently from nature, which does not fail altogether. Many say Happiness can be had in this life. Therefore one can be happy in this life.

### On the Contrary
"Man born of a woman, living for a short time, is filled with many miseries" (Job 14:1). Happiness excludes misery. Therefore man cannot be happy in this life.

### I Answer That
A certain participation of Happiness can be had in this life; but perfect and true Happiness cannot. Since Happiness is a "perfect and sufficient good," it excludes every evil and fulfills every desire. In this present life, every evil cannot be excluded—man experiences ignorance, inordinate affection, and bodily penalties. Furthermore, desire for good cannot be satiated because goods of this life pass away, and man naturally desires abiding good while naturally shrinking from death. Additionally, perfect Happiness requires vision of the Divine Essence, which man cannot obtain in this life.

### Reply to Objection 1
Some are called happy in this life either through hope of obtaining future Happiness or through a certain participation of Happiness via enjoyment of the Sovereign Good.

### Reply to Objection 2
Imperfection due to not seeing God's Essence in Its Essence destroys true Happiness. However, imperfection on the participator's part—attaining God imperfectly compared to God's self-enjoyment—does not destroy true Happiness, since Happiness's nature derives from its object, not the subject.

### Reply to Objection 3
Men esteem some Happiness exists in this life due to likeness to true Happiness. Thus they do not fail altogether in their estimate.

## Article 4: Whether Happiness Once Had Can Be Lost

### Objection 1
Happiness is a perfection existing in the perfected thing according to its mode. Since man is changeable by nature, Happiness participates in this changeability. Consequently man can lose Happiness.

### Objection 2
Happiness consists in an intellect act; the intellect is subject to the will. The will can direct itself to opposites. Therefore it can cease from the operation making man happy, and man will cease being happy.

### Objection 3
The end corresponds to the beginning. Man's Happiness has a beginning—he was not always happy. Therefore it has an end.

### On the Contrary
"They shall go...into life everlasting" (Matthew 25:46), which is the Happiness of the saints. What is eternal ceases not. Therefore Happiness cannot be lost.

### I Answer That
Imperfect Happiness in this life can be lost through forgetfulness, sickness, or occupations withdrawing from contemplation. Active happiness can be lost when man's will changes from virtue to vice. However, perfect Happiness after this life cannot be lost.

Origen wrongly held that man could become unhappy after final Happiness. This is false for two reasons. First, Happiness as "perfect and sufficient good" must set man's desire at rest and exclude every evil. Man naturally desires to hold the good he has with surety; otherwise fear of loss or sorrow at inevitable loss troubles him. Therefore true Happiness requires assured opinion of never losing the possessed good. If this opinion is true, he never loses Happiness; if false, it constitutes evil, since falsehood is the intellect's evil.

Second, perfect Happiness consists in vision of the Divine Essence. One seeing the Divine Essence cannot wish not to see It. Every good one possesses and wishes to be without is either insufficient or has inconvenience attached. The Divine Essence vision fills the soul with all good and unites it to all goodness's source. It has no inconvenience; "Her conversation hath no bitterness, nor her company any tediousness." The happy man cannot forsake Happiness voluntarily. God, the just Judge, cannot withdraw it as punishment without fault, yet one seeing God cannot fall into fault since rectitude of will necessarily results from that vision. No other agent can sever the mind united to God. Therefore it seems unreasonable that man should pass from happiness to misery over time, as such vicissitudes apply only to things subject to time and movement.

### Reply to Objection 1
Happiness is consummate perfection excluding every defect. Whoever has Happiness has it altogether unchangeably—Divine power raises man to participation in eternity transcending all change.

### Reply to Objection 2
The will can direct itself to opposites regarding things ordained to the end; but it is ordained by natural necessity to the last end. Man cannot fail to wish being happy.

### Reply to Objection 3
Happiness has a beginning owing to the participator's condition; it has no end due to the good's condition whose participation makes man happy. Thus beginning and endlessness derive from different causes.

## Article 5: Whether Man Can Attain Happiness by His Natural Powers

### Objection 1
Nature does not fail in necessary things. Nothing is so necessary as that by which man attains his last end. Therefore this is not lacking to human nature, and man can attain Happiness by natural powers.

### Objection 2
Man is more noble than irrational creatures. Irrational creatures attain their end by natural powers. Therefore man can attain Happiness by natural powers.

### Objection 3
Happiness is a "perfect operation." The beginning of a thing belongs to the same principle as its perfecting. Imperfect operation is subject to man's natural power. Therefore he can attain perfect operation—Happiness—by natural powers.

### On the Contrary
Man is naturally the principle of his action by intellect and will. Final Happiness prepared for saints surpasses man's intellect and will. "Eye hath not seen, nor ear heard, neither hath it entered into the heart of man, what things God hath prepared for them that love Him" (1 Corinthians 2:9).

### I Answer That
Imperfect Happiness in this life can be acquired by man through natural powers in the same way as virtue. However, perfect Happiness—vision of the Divine Essence—surpasses not only man's nature but every creature's nature. Every creature's natural knowledge accords with its substance's mode. But knowledge according to created substance's mode falls short of Divine Essence's vision, which infinitely surpasses all created substance. Consequently, neither man nor any creature can attain final Happiness by natural powers.

### Reply to Objection 1
As nature provides man reason and hands to obtain weapons and clothing rather than providing them directly, so it provides free will whereby man can turn to God for making him happy. "What we do by means of our friends, is done, in a sense, by ourselves."

### Reply to Objection 2
A nature capable of perfect good needing external help is more noble than one incapable of perfect good but attaining imperfect good without help. The rational creature able to attain perfect Happiness-good with Divine assistance is more perfect than the irrational creature incapable of this but attaining some imperfect good naturally.

### Reply to Objection 3
When imperfect and perfect are of the same species, the same power can cause both. But if they are of different species, this necessarily does not follow. Operation takes its species from its object. Imperfect operation subject to man's natural power is not the same species as perfect operation constituting Happiness.

## Article 6: Whether Man Attains Happiness Through the Action of Some Higher Creature

### Objection 1
Two orders exist: parts of the universe to each other, and the whole universe to a good outside it. The former is ordained to the latter as its end. Higher creatures act upon lower ones; Happiness consists in man's order to God outside the universe. Therefore man is made happy through a higher creature, an angel, acting upon him.

### Objection 2
What is such in potentiality is reduced to act by what is such actually. Man is potentially happy. An actually happy angel can make man actually happy.

### Objection 3
Happiness consists in intellect operation. Angels enlighten man's intellect. Therefore an angel can make man happy.

### On the Contrary
"The Lord will give grace and glory" (Psalm 83:12).

### I Answer That
Every creature is subject to nature's laws, its power and action being limited. What surpasses created nature cannot be done by creature power. Whatever exceeds nature is done by God immediately—raising the dead, restoring sight to the blind. Happiness surpasses created nature. Therefore it cannot be bestowed through any creature's action but only by God alone, regarding perfect Happiness. Regarding imperfect Happiness, the same applies as to virtue in whose act it consists.

### Reply to Objection 1
In active powers ordained to each other, the highest reaches the last end while lower powers contribute by causing disposition thereto. In the universe's order, angels help man attain his last end regarding preliminary dispositions; man attains the last end itself through God, the First Agent.

### Reply to Objection 2
A form existing perfectly and naturally in something can principle action upon another. But if a form exists imperfectly and unnaturally, it cannot be communicated. The "intention" of color in the pupil cannot whiten things; not everything enlightened or heated can heat or light others, else heating and enlightening would proceed to infinity. God's light of glory exists perfectly and naturally in God; in creatures, imperfectly and by participation. Consequently no creature can communicate its Happiness to another.

### Reply to Objection 3
A happy angel enlightens man's intellect regarding notions of Divine works, but not regarding Divine Essence vision. To see this, all are immediately enlightened by God.

## Article 7: Whether Any Good Works Are Necessary That Man May Obtain Happiness From God

### Objection 1
No works of man are necessary for obtaining Happiness from God. God, an infinite-power agent, requires neither matter nor matter disposition before acting but can immediately produce the whole effect. Man's works, not required as Happiness's efficient cause, could be required only as dispositions. God, not requiring dispositions, bestows Happiness without previous works.

### Objection 2
As God is Happiness's immediate cause, He is nature's immediate cause. When God first established nature, He produced creatures without previous disposition or creature action, making each perfect immediately in its species. Therefore He bestows Happiness without previous works.

### Objection 3
"Happiness is of the man to whom God reputeth justice without works" (Romans 4:6).

### On the Contrary
"If you know these things, you shall be blessed if you do them" (John 13:17). Therefore Happiness is obtained through works.

### I Answer That
Will's rectitude—the will's right order to the last end—is necessary for Happiness, as the right matter disposition is necessary to receive form. This does not prove man's work must precede his Happiness; God could make a rightly-tending will simultaneously attaining the end. However, Divine wisdom's order demands otherwise. "Of those things having natural capacity for perfect good, one has it without movement, some by one movement, some by several." Possessing perfect good without movement belongs to what naturally has it—God alone. Happiness surpasses every created nature; no pure creature can becomingly gain Happiness without operation's movement tending thereto. The angel obtained it by one meritorious work; man obtains it through many meritorious works. Thus, according to the Philosopher, "Happiness is the reward of works of virtue."

### Reply to Objection 1
Works are necessary to man gaining Happiness not from Divine power's insufficiency bestowing Happiness, but that the order in things be observed.

### Reply to Objection 2
God produced first creatures perfect immediately without previous disposition or operation because He instituted the first individuals of various species so nature might propagate to progeny. Similarly, Happiness was bestowed through Christ, God and Man, who "had brought many children into glory"; therefore, from His conception's beginning, His soul was happy without previous meritorious operation. This is peculiar to Him; Christ's merit avails baptized children for Happiness though they have no merits, becoming Christ's members through Baptism.

### Reply to Objection 3
The Apostle speaks of Happiness of Hope, bestowed by sanctifying grace, not given for previous works. Grace is not Happiness's term of movement; rather it is the principle of movement tending toward Happiness.

## Article 8: Whether Every Man Desires Happiness

### Objection 1
No man can desire what he knows not. The apprehended good is the appetite's object. Many know not what Happiness is—some thought it consists in bodily pleasures, some in soul virtue, some in other things. Therefore not all desire Happiness.

### Objection 2
Happiness's essence is Divine Essence vision. Some consider it impossible for man to see the Divine Essence; therefore they desire it not. Therefore all men do not desire Happiness.

### Objection 3
"Happy is he who has all he desires, and desires nothing amiss." But all do not desire this; some desire certain things amiss yet wish to desire such things. Therefore all do not desire Happiness.

### On the Contrary
"If that actor had said: 'You all wish to be happy; you do not wish to be unhappy,' he would have said that which none would have failed to acknowledge in his will" (Augustine).

### I Answer That
Happiness admits two considerations. First, according to the general notion of Happiness: necessarily, every man desires Happiness. The general notion consists in perfect good. Since good is the will's object, man's perfect good entirely satisfies his will. Consequently, desiring Happiness is nothing else than desiring one's will's satisfaction, which everyone desires.

Second, we may speak of Happiness according to its specific notion regarding that in which it consists. Thus all do not know Happiness, because they know not in what thing the general Happiness notion is found. Consequently, in this respect, not all desire it.

### Reply to Objection 1
The reply to the first objection is clear from above.

### Reply to Objection 2
Since will follows intellect or reason's apprehension, just as distinction without real distinction can exist according to reason's consideration, the same thing is desired in one way and not desired in another. Happiness may be considered as final and perfect good—the general Happiness notion—and the will naturally and necessarily tends thereto. Again, considering it under other special aspects—operation itself, operating power, or object—the will does not tend thereto necessarily.

### Reply to Objection 3
The definition "Happy is the man that has all he desires" or "whose every wish is fulfilled" is good and adequate understood simply of all man desires by natural appetite. Thus he having all he desires naturally is happy, since nothing satisfies man's natural desire except perfect good—Happiness. But understanding it of things man desires by reason's apprehension, it does not belong to Happiness to have certain things man desires; rather it belongs to unhappiness, insofar as possessing such things hinders man from having all naturally desired things. Thus reason sometimes accepts as true things hindering truth-knowledge. Augustine added "desires nothing amiss" to include perfect Happiness, although "has all he desires" suffices if rightly understood.
