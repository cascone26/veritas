# Question 33: The Effects of Pleasure

## Article 1: Whether Expansion is an Effect of Pleasure?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Expansion appears to pertain more to love than pleasure. Scripture states "Our heart is enlarged" regarding love, and the commandment of charity is described as "exceeding broad." Since pleasure is distinct from love, expansion should not be attributed to pleasure.

**Objection 2:** When something expands, it becomes capable of receiving more. Reception pertains to desire for something not yet possessed. Therefore, expansion belongs to desire rather than pleasure.

**Objection 3:** Contraction opposes expansion. Yet contraction seems proper to pleasure, as the hand closes firmly on what we wish to grasp. Thus expansion does not pertain to pleasure.

### On the Contrary

Scripture expresses joy through expansion: "Thou shall see and abound, thy heart shall wonder and be enlarged" (Isaiah 60:5). Additionally, pleasure derives its name from "dilatatio" (expansion).

### I Answer That

Breadth is metaphorically applied to emotional states. Expansion denotes movement toward breadth and belongs to pleasure through two aspects. First, the apprehensive power recognizes conjunction with suitable good, and the mind perceives attained perfection—a spiritual magnitude. Second, the appetitive power acquiesces in the pleasurable object, as though embracing it within itself, thus expanding affection.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Metaphorical language permits the same thing to apply to different subjects according to different likenesses. Love's expansion spreads affection to others' concerns; pleasure's expansion makes the thing itself more capacious.

**Reply 2:** Desire includes expansion from imagination of the desired object, which increases upon the object's presence. The mind surrenders itself more completely when already experiencing pleasure than when merely desiring something unpossessed.

**Reply 3:** One taking pleasure holds the thing fast through clinging to it, yet opens the heart to enjoy it perfectly.

## Article 2: Whether Pleasure Causes Thirst or Desire for Itself?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Movement ceases upon reaching repose. Pleasure constitutes a repose of desire's movement. Therefore, desire ceases when pleasure is reached, so pleasure does not cause desire.

**Objection 2:** A thing does not cause its contrary. Pleasure and desire are contrary: desire concerns unpossessed good; pleasure concerns possessed good. Therefore, pleasure does not cause desire.

**Objection 3:** Distaste excludes desire. Yet pleasure often causes distaste. Therefore, it does not cause desire.

### On the Contrary

Our Lord stated: "Whosoever drinketh of this water, shall thirst again" (John 4:13), where Augustine interprets water as bodily pleasures.

### I Answer That

Pleasure considered in reality does not of itself cause desire for itself, only accidentally. However, what is actually present may be imperfectly possessed—either the thing lacks simultaneity (requiring successive possession) or the possessor obtains it gradually. In bodily pleasures, one desires successive parts. Spiritual pleasures cause most intense desire, as they perfect rather than exceed nature, never becoming distasteful. When considered as existing in memory, pleasure naturally tends to cause thirst when one returns to that former disposition.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Perfect pleasure includes complete rest, ceasing desire's movement. Imperfect pleasure allows continued desire for unpossessed remainder.

**Reply 2:** What is imperfectly possessed is simultaneously possessed and unpossessed, hence capable of being both desired and enjoyed.

**Reply 3:** Pleasures cause distaste one way and desire another, as explained above.

## Article 3: Whether Pleasure Hinders the Use of Reason?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Repose facilitates reason's due use. The Philosopher states that while resting, the soul inclines to knowledge and prudence. Pleasure is a kind of repose. Therefore, it helps rather than hinders reason.

**Objection 2:** Things not in the same subject do not hinder one another. Pleasure is in the appetitive faculty; reason's use is in the apprehensive power. Therefore, pleasure does not hinder reason.

**Objection 3:** What is hindered seems moved by the hindrance. But apprehensive power moves pleasure, not vice versa. Therefore, pleasure does not hinder reason.

### On the Contrary

The Philosopher states: "pleasure destroys the estimate of prudence" (Ethic. vi, 5).

### I Answer That

Appropriate pleasures increase their activities; others impede them. Pleasure taken in the act of reason itself (contemplation, reasoning) does not hinder but helps, as attention to pleasurable activity strengthens it. Bodily pleasures hinder reason threefold: by distracting attention, by contradicting reason's order, and by causing bodily alteration that fetters reason, as seen in drunkards.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Bodily pleasure implies appetite's repose in the object, contrary to reason, and always involves bodily alteration that hinders reason.

**Reply 2:** Though appetitive and apprehensive powers are distinct, they belong to one soul. When intent on one part's action, the soul cannot attend to contrary action of another part.

**Reply 3:** Reason requires proper use of imagination and sensitive powers, which function through bodily organs. Bodily alteration hinders these, thereby hindering reason.

## Article 4: Whether Pleasure Perfects Operation?

### Objections

**Objection 1:** Human operation depends on reason's use. But pleasure hinders reason. Therefore, pleasure weakens rather than perfects operation.

**Objection 2:** Nothing perfects itself or its cause. Pleasure is an operation. Therefore, it does not perfect operation.

**Objection 3:** If pleasure perfects operation, it does so as end, form, or agent. Not as end, since operation is not sought for pleasure's sake. Not as agent, since operation causes pleasure. Not as form, since pleasure does not perfect as habit does.

### On the Contrary

The Philosopher states: "pleasure perfects operation" (Ethic. x, 4).

### I Answer That

Pleasure perfects operation two ways. First, as an end—not the end "on account of which" something exists, but a good completing it. Thus pleasure is added good to operation's good. Second, as agent, indirectly: the agent through taking pleasure becomes more eagerly intent on action and executes it more carefully. Thus appropriate pleasures increase their activities.

### Replies to Objections

**Reply 1:** Bodily pleasure hinders reason's act, but pleasure arising from reason's act strengthens it.

**Reply 2:** Two things can cause each other—one as efficient cause, another as final cause. Operation efficiently causes pleasure; pleasure perfects operation as final cause.

**Reply 3:** [The reply follows from the answer above.]
