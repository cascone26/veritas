# Question 1: Man's Last End

## Article 1: Whether it belongs to man to act for an end?

### Objection 1
It seems man does not act for an end, since a cause is naturally first, but an end is by definition last. Therefore an end is not a cause, yet that for which man acts should be a cause of his action.

### Objection 2
Further, what is itself the last end is not for an end. But sometimes the last end is an action itself. Therefore man does not do everything for an end.

### Objection 3
Further, man seems to act for an end when he acts deliberately. But man does many things without deliberation, such as moving his foot or hand while attending to something else. Therefore man does not do everything for an end.

### On the contrary
All things in a genus derive from that genus's principle. The end is the principle in human operations. Therefore it belongs to man to do everything for an end.

### I answer that
Of actions done by man, only those properly called "human" are those proper to man as man. Man differs from irrational animals in being master of his actions through reason and will. Therefore properly human actions proceed from deliberate will. The object of the will is the end and the good. Thus all human actions must be for an end.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although the end is last in execution, it is first in the agent's intention. It is in this way that it functions as a cause.

### Reply to Objection 2
If a human action is the last end, it must be voluntary. An action can be voluntary in two ways: commanded by the will (like walking) or elicited by the will (the act of willing itself). The act elicited by the will cannot be the last end, since the will's object is the end, as sight's object is color. Therefore if an action is the last end, it must be commanded by the will, meaning some act of willing is for the end.

### Reply to Objection 3
Actions performed without deliberation are not properly human, since they don't proceed from reason's deliberation. They have only an imaginary end, not one fixed by reason.

## Article 2: Whether it is proper to the rational nature to act for an end?

### Objection 1
It seems proper to rational nature to act for an end. Man, who acts for an end, never acts for an unknown end. But many things lack knowledge of an end—either completely, like insensible creatures, or they don't grasp the idea of end as such, like irrational animals. Therefore acting for an end is proper to rational nature.

### Objection 2
Further, to act for an end means ordering one's action to an end. This is reason's work. Therefore it doesn't belong to things lacking reason.

### Objection 3
Further, the good and end is the object of the will. The will is in reason. Therefore acting for an end belongs only to rational nature.

### On the contrary
The Philosopher proves that not only mind but also nature acts for an end.

### I answer that
Every agent necessarily acts for an end. When causes are ordered to one another, removing the first removes the others necessarily. The first of all causes is the final cause. Matter doesn't receive form except insofar as moved by an agent, and an agent doesn't move except by intention for an end. Unless the agent were determined to some particular effect, it would not do one thing rather than another. For this determination, rational nature uses rational appetite (the will), while other things use natural inclination (natural appetite).

However, things tend to an end in two ways: first, by moving themselves to the end (as man does), and second, by being moved by another to the end (as an arrow by the archer). Rational beings move themselves to an end through free will and reason. Irrational things tend to an end by natural inclination, being moved by another, since they don't know the nature of end as such and cannot ordain anything to an end—they can only be ordained to an end by another. The entire irrational nature relates to God as an instrument to the principal agent.

Therefore, it is proper to rational nature to tend to an end as directing and leading itself to the end, while irrational nature tends to an end as directed or led by another.

### Reply to Objection 1
When a man acts of himself for an end, he knows the end. But when directed by another—through command or compulsion—he need not know the end. This is how irrational creatures operate.

### Reply to Objection 2
To ordain toward an end belongs to what directs itself to an end. To be ordained to an end belongs to what is directed by another. This can belong to irrational nature through one possessed of reason.

### Reply to Objection 3
The will's object is the good and end in universal. Therefore only rational beings with intellect can have will, since they alone can apprehend the universal. Irrational creatures have only natural or sensitive appetite, determined to particular goods. Particular causes are moved by universal causes—as a city governor intending the common good moves particular departments through commands. Therefore all things lacking reason are necessarily moved to their particular ends by some rational will extending to universal good, namely the Divine will.

## Article 3: Whether human acts are specified by their end?

### Objection 1
Human acts are not specified by their end. The end is an extrinsic cause, but everything is specified by intrinsic principles. Therefore human acts are not specified by their end.

### Objection 2
Further, what gives a thing its species should exist before it. But the end comes into existence afterwards. Therefore a human act doesn't derive its species from the end.

### Objection 3
Further, one thing cannot be in more than one species. But one and the same act may be ordained to various ends. Therefore the end doesn't give species to human acts.

### On the contrary
Augustine says: "According as their end is worthy of blame or praise so are our deeds worthy of blame or praise."

### I answer that
Each thing receives its species in respect of act, not potentiality. Things composed of matter and form are established in their species by their forms. The same applies to movements: action receives its species from the act which is the principle of acting, and passion from the act which is the movement's terminus. Heating as action is movement from heat, while heating as passion is movement toward heat.

Human acts, whether considered as actions or passions, receive their species from the end. Man moves himself and is moved by himself. Acts are called human insofar as they proceed from deliberate will. The will's object is the good and end. Therefore the principle of human acts, insofar as they are human, is the end. Similarly, it is their terminus: the human act terminates at what the will intends as the end, just as in natural agents the form of the thing generated conforms to the generator's form. Since morality is proper to man, moral acts properly receive their species from the end, and moral acts are the same as human acts.

### Reply to Objection 1
The end is not altogether extrinsic to an act, since it relates to the act as principle or terminus. This is essential to an act: to proceed from something (in action) and proceed toward something (in passion).

### Reply to Objection 2
The end, insofar as it pre-exists in intention, pertains to the will. In this way it gives species to human or moral acts.

### Reply to Objection 3
One and the same act, in one proceeding from the agent, is ordained to only one proximate end, from which it has its species. However, it can be ordained to several remote ends, with one being the end of another. It's possible that an act which is one in natural species be ordained to several ends of the will. For instance, "killing a man," one act in natural species, can be ordained to safeguarding justice or satisfying anger—making it several acts in different species of morality: in one way an act of virtue, in another of vice. A movement doesn't receive species from its terminus accidentally, but only from its per se terminus. Moral ends are accidental to a natural thing, and conversely, relation to a natural end is accidental to morality. Therefore acts identical in natural species can be diverse in moral species, and conversely.

## Article 4: Whether there is one last end of human life?

### Objection 1
There is no last end of human life but we proceed infinitely. Good is essentially diffusive. Consequently, if what proceeds from good is itself good, it must diffuse some other good, so good's diffusion proceeds indefinitely. Good has the nature of an end. Therefore there is an indefinite series of ends.

### Objection 2
Further, things pertaining to reason can be multiplied infinitely—mathematical quantities have no limit. Numbers are infinite since, given any number, reason can think of one greater. Desire of the end follows apprehension of reason. Therefore there is also an infinite series of ends.

### Objection 3
Further, the good and end is the object of the will. The will can react on itself infinitely: I can will something, and will to will it, and so on indefinitely. Therefore there is an infinite series of ends of the human will, and there is no last end.

### On the contrary
The Philosopher says: "To suppose a thing to be indefinite is to deny that it is good." Good has the nature of end. Therefore it is contrary to end's nature to proceed indefinitely. It is necessary to fix one last end.

### I answer that
Absolutely speaking, it's impossible to proceed indefinitely in matters of ends from any perspective. Wherever there is essential order of one thing to another, if the first is removed, those ordained to the first must necessarily be removed also. The Philosopher proves we cannot proceed infinitely in causes of movement—there would be no first mover, without which others cannot move, since they move only through being moved by the first mover.

There is a twofold order in ends: the order of intention and the order of execution. There must be something first in either order. What is first in intention is the principle moving appetite. If you remove this principle, nothing moves the appetite. The principle in execution is wherein operation begins. If this principle is removed, no one will begin work. The principle in intention is the last end; the principle in execution is the first of things ordained to the end. Consequently, proceeding to infinity is impossible on either side, since if there were no last end, nothing would be desired, no action would have its term, and the agent's intention would not rest. If there is no first thing among what is ordained to the end, none would begin work at anything, and counsel would continue indefinitely.

However, nothing prevents infinity in things ordained to one another not essentially but accidentally. Accidental causes are indeterminate. In this way there is accidental infinity of ends and of things ordained to the end.

### Reply to Objection 1
Good's nature is that something flows from it, not that it flows from something else. Since good has the nature of end, and the first good is the last end, this argument doesn't prove there's no last end. Rather, from the end already supposed, we may proceed downward indefinitely toward those things ordained to the end. This would be true considering the First Good's infinite power. But since the First Good diffuses itself according to intellect, which flows into effects according to a certain fixed form, there is a certain measure to good things' flow from the First Good. Consequently the diffusion of goods doesn't proceed indefinitely but, as written, God "disposes all things in number, weight and measure."

### Reply to Objection 2
In things which are of themselves, reason begins from naturally known principles and advances to some term. The Philosopher proves there's no infinite process in demonstrations because there we find things having essential, not accidental, connection. But in things accidentally connected, nothing prevents reason from proceeding indefinitely. To a stated quantity or number, as such, it's accidental that quantity or unity be added. Therefore reason can proceed indefinitely in such things.

### Reply to Objection 3
This multiplication of will acts reacting on itself is accidental to the order of ends. This is clear because regarding one and the same end, the will reacts on itself indifferently once or several times.

## Article 5: Whether one man can have several last ends?

### Objection 1
One man's will can be directed at the same time to several things as last ends. Augustine says some held man's last end to consist in four things: pleasure, repose, gifts of nature, and virtue. These are clearly more than one. Therefore one man can place his last end in many things.

### Objection 2
Further, things not in opposition don't exclude one another. Many things are not in opposition. Therefore the supposition that one thing is the last end doesn't exclude others.

### Objection 3
Further, by placing its last end in one thing, the will doesn't lose its freedom. Before placing it in that thing (pleasure), it could place it in something else (riches). Therefore even after placing his last end in pleasure, a man can simultaneously place his last end in riches. Therefore one man's will can be directed at the same time to several things as last ends.

### On the contrary
That in which a man rests as his last end is master of his affections, since he takes therefrom his entire life rule. Thus of gluttons it is written: "Whose god is their belly"—because they place their last end in belly pleasures. According to Matthew, "No man can serve two masters" not ordained to one another. Therefore it's impossible for one man to have several last ends not ordained to one another.

### I answer that
It's impossible for one man's will to be directed simultaneously to diverse things as last ends. Three reasons explain this.

First, because everything desires its own perfection, a man desires for his ultimate end that which he desires as his perfect and crowning good. Augustine states: "In speaking of the good's end we mean not that it passes away so as to be no more, but that it is perfected so as to be complete." The last end must so fill man's appetite that nothing besides remains for him to desire. This is impossible if something else is required for his perfection. Consequently the appetite cannot tend to two things as though each were its perfect good.

Second, just as in reasoning the principle is naturally known, so in rational appetite (the will) the principle must be naturally desired. This must be one, since nature tends to one thing only. The principle in rational appetite is the last end. Therefore that to which the will tends as to its last end is one.

Third, since voluntary actions receive their species from the end, they must receive their genus from the last end common to them all, just as natural things are placed in a genus according to a common form. Since all things that can be desired by the will belong to one genus, the last end must be one. Moreover, in every genus there is one first principle, and the last end has the nature of a first principle. Now as man's last end, simply as man, is to the whole human race, so is any individual man's last end to that individual. Therefore, just as all men naturally have one last end, so an individual man's will must be fixed on one last end.

### Reply to Objection 1
All these several objects were considered as one perfect good resulting from them by those placing their last end in them.

### Reply to Objection 2
Although it's possible to find several things not in opposition, it's contrary to a thing's perfect good that anything besides be required for that thing's perfection.

### Reply to Objection 3
The will's power doesn't extend to making opposites exist simultaneously. This would be the case were it to tend to several diverse objects as last ends, as shown above.

## Article 6: Whether man will all, whatsoever he wills, for the last end?

### Objection 1
Man doesn't will all he wills for the last end. Things ordained to the last end are called serious matter, being useful. But jests are foreign to serious matter. Therefore what man does in jest, he ordains not to the last end.

### Objection 2
Further, the Philosopher says at Metaphysics' beginning that speculative science is sought for its own sake. It cannot be said that each speculative science is the last end. Therefore man doesn't desire all he desires for the last end.

### Objection 3
Further, whoever ordains something to an end thinks of that end. But man doesn't always think of the last end in all he desires or does. Therefore man neither desires nor does all for the last end.

### On the contrary
Augustine says: "That is the end of our good, for the sake of which we love other things, whereas we love it for its own sake."

### I answer that
Man must necessarily desire all he desires for the last end. Two reasons establish this.

First, whatever man desires, he desires it under the aspect of good. If he doesn't desire it as his perfect good (the last end), he must necessarily desire it as tending to the perfect good. Every beginning is ordained to its completion, which is evident in effects of both nature and art. Therefore every perfection's beginning is ordained to complete perfection achieved through the last end.

Second, the last end stands in the same relation moving appetite as the first mover in other movements. Secondary moving causes move only insofar as moved by the first mover. Therefore secondary objects of appetite move appetite only as ordained to the first object of appetite, which is the last end.

### Reply to Objection 1
Actions done jestingly aren't directed to any external end, but merely to the jester's good, insofar as they afford him pleasure or relaxation. But man's consummate good is his last end.

### Reply to Objection 2
The same applies to speculative science, desired as the scientist's good, included in complete and perfect good, which is the ultimate end.

### Reply to Objection 3
One need not always think of the last end when desiring or doing something. But the virtue of the first intention, which was in respect of the last end, remains in every desire directed to any object, even though one's thoughts aren't actually directed to the last end. Thus while walking the road one needn't think of the end at every step.

## Article 7: Whether all men have the same last end?

### Objection 1
All men don't have the same last end. The unchangeable good seems before all else to be man's last end. But some turn from the unchangeable good by sinning. Therefore all men don't have the same last end.

### Objection 2
Further, man's entire life is ruled according to his last end. If all men had the same last end, they wouldn't have various pursuits in life. This is evidently false.

### Objection 3
Further, the end is the term of action. Actions are of individuals. Although men agree in specific nature, they differ in things pertaining to individuals. Therefore all men don't have the same last end.

### On the contrary
Augustine says all men agree in desiring the last end, which is happiness.

### I answer that
We can speak of the last end in two ways: first, considering only the aspect of last end; second, considering the thing in which the aspect is realized.

As to the aspect of last end, all agree in desiring it. All desire the fulfillment of their perfection, which is precisely wherein the last end consists. But as to the thing in which this aspect is realized, all men aren't agreed: some desire riches as their consummate good, some pleasure, others something else. Thus to every taste the sweet is pleasant, but to some wine's sweetness is most pleasant, to others honey's or something similar's. Yet that good is most complete which the well-disposed man desires for his last end.

### Reply to Objection 1
Those who sin turn from that in which their last end really consists, but they don't turn from the intention of the last end, which intention they mistakenly seek in other things.

### Reply to Objection 2
Various pursuits in life exist among men because of the various things in which men seek to find their last end.

### Reply to Objection 3
Although actions are of individuals, their first principle of action is nature, which tends to one thing, as stated above.

## Article 8: Whether other creatures concur in that last end?

### Objection 1
All other creatures concur in man's last end. The end corresponds to the beginning. Man's beginning (God) is also all else's beginning. Therefore all other things concur in man's last end.

### Objection 2
Further, Dionysius says God "turns all things to Himself as to their last end." But He is also man's last end because He alone is to be enjoyed by man, as Augustine says. Therefore other things too concur in man's last end.

### Objection 3
Further, man's last end is the will's object. The will's object is the universal good, which is all things' end. Therefore other things too concur in man's last end.

### On the contrary
Man's last end is happiness. All men desire happiness, as Augustine says. But "happiness is not possible for animals bereft of reason," as Augustine says. Therefore other things don't concur in man's last end.

### I answer that
The Philosopher says the end is twofold: the end "for which" and the end "by which"—the thing itself in which is found the good's aspect, and the use or acquisition of that thing. Thus we say a weighty body's movement's end is either a lower place as "thing," or being in a lower place as "use"; and the miser's end is money as "thing," or money's possession as "use."

If we speak of man's last end as the thing which is the end, thus all other things concur in man's last end, since God is man's last end and all other things' last end. If we speak of man's last end as the acquisition of the end, then irrational creatures don't concur with man in this end. Man and other rational creatures attain their last end by knowing and loving God. This isn't possible for other creatures, which acquire their last end insofar as they share in Divine likeness through being, living, or even knowing.

Hence it's evident how the objections are solved, since happiness means the acquisition of the last end.
