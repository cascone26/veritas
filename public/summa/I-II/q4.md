# Question 4: Things That Are Required for Happiness

## Article 1: Whether delight is required for happiness?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "vision is the entire reward of faith." Since happiness is the prize of virtue, nothing besides vision seems necessary for happiness.

### Objection 2
Happiness represents "the most self-sufficient of all goods." If happiness's essence consists in seeing God, then delight appears unnecessary since it would suggest a need for something external.

### Objection 3
The "operation of bliss should be unhindered." Yet delight hinders intellectual operation by destroying prudential judgment, making it unnecessary for happiness.

### On the Contrary
Augustine declares that happiness is "joy in truth."

### I Answer That
Delight is necessary for happiness in the manner that something attends upon or accompanies a primary good. Since happiness consists in attaining the Sovereign Good, and since the appetite naturally finds rest in the good obtained, delight must necessarily accompany happiness. It results from the will's repose in the good achieved.

### Reply to Objection 1
When a reward is given, the recipient's will achieves rest, and this constitutes delight. Therefore, delight is intrinsic to the concept of reward itself.

### Reply to Objection 2
The very sight of God produces delight as its natural consequence. Therefore, one who beholds God cannot lack this accompanying joy.

### Reply to Objection 3
Delight attending intellectual operation perfects rather than hinders it, as we pursue with greater care what brings us joy. Only extraneous delight, through distraction or opposition to reason, impedes intellectual work.

## Article 2: Whether in happiness vision ranks before delight?

### Objection 1
Delight perfects operation, and perfection surpasses what is perfected. Therefore delight ranks before the intellectual vision of God.

### Objection 2
We desire operations for the delight they afford. Nature itself has arranged delight to accompany necessary operations. Thus delight seems primary to intellectual vision.

### Objection 3
Vision corresponds to faith while delight corresponds to charity. Since the Apostle declares charity surpasses faith, delight must rank before vision.

### On the Contrary
The cause exceeds its effect. Vision causes delight, therefore vision ranks before delight.

### I Answer That
Vision necessarily ranks before delight. Delight constitutes the will's repose, which occurs only because of the goodness present in what the will embraces. The will does not seek good for the sake of repose but rather seeks repose in good because that good is truly good. Consequently, the operation in which the will reposes must precede the will's repose within it.

### Reply to Objection 1
Delight perfects operation "as vigor perfects youth," being a result rather than a constitutive element. It represents an attendant perfection rather than one that makes vision itself more perfect in its essential nature.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sensory apprehension grasps particular goods, so animals naturally seek operations for resulting delight. But intellect apprehends universal good itself, so our rational appetite properly directs toward good rather than delight. Divine nature adjusted delights to operations, not operations to delights.

### Reply to Objection 3
Charity's essential end is vision through which the beloved good becomes present. Delight results as charity's consequence, not as its primary object.

## Article 3: Whether comprehension is necessary for happiness?

### Objection 1
Augustine states: "To reach God with the mind is happiness, to comprehend Him is impossible." Therefore happiness exists without comprehension.

### Objection 2
Happiness perfects only the intellect and will. The intellect achieves perfection through seeing God; the will through enjoying Him. Comprehension seems superfluous as a third element.

### Objection 3
Operations depend on their objects: the true and the good. Vision corresponds to truth, delight to good. No third element appears necessary.

### On the Contrary
The Apostle writes: "So run that you may comprehend." Happiness represents the spiritual race's goal, requiring comprehension for completion.

### I Answer That
Man approaches an intelligible end partly through intellect and partly through will. Intellection provides imperfect prior knowledge; the will acts through love initially, then through three relations toward the beloved: presence (when obtained), impossibility (when unattainable), or hopeful yearning (when achievable but difficult). These correspond to three happiness elements: vision (perfect knowledge), comprehension (presence of the end), and delight (lover's repose). All three must concur in happiness.

### Reply to Objection 1
Comprehension has two meanings. First, finite containment of the comprehended—impossible for God. Second, merely grasping what is present—this comprehension is necessary for happiness, like catching something pursued.

### Reply to Objection 2
Both comprehension and delight pertain to the will, since the same faculty that loves also possesses and finds repose in what is attained.

### Reply to Objection 3
Comprehension constitutes no distinct operation from vision but rather expresses a relation to the already-gained end, making the seen object itself present to comprehension.

## Article 4: Whether rectitude of the will is necessary for happiness?

### Objection 1
Happiness essentially consists in intellectual operation. Augustine rejected the notion that cleanness of heart alone permits knowing truth, observing that many unclean persons know truths. Thus will-rectitude seems unnecessary.

### Objection 2
Intellection precedes volition, so happiness (perfect intellection) should not depend on will-rectitude.

### Objection 3
Virtue, which includes will-rectitude, aims at happiness as its end. Once the end is gained, the means become unnecessary, as a ship no longer needs navigation after reaching port.

### On the Contrary
Scripture teaches: "Blessed are the clean of heart; for they shall see God" and "without holiness no man shall see God."

### I Answer That
Will-rectitude is necessary for happiness both antecedently and concomitantly. Antecedently, since proper ordering toward the final end resembles form's relation to matter—matter cannot receive form without proper disposition, so nothing achieves an end without rightful ordering toward it. Concomitantly, since perfect happiness consists in beholding God's Essence (which is Goodness itself), whoever sees God necessarily loves all things as subordinated to God—this itself constitutes a right will.

### Reply to Objection 1
Every act of will is preceded by some intellection, yet certain acts of will precede certain acts of intellection. The will tends toward the final intellectual act constituting happiness, so right will-inclination is antecedently required.

### Reply to Objection 2
Not everything ordained to an end ceases upon its achievement—only what involves imperfection. Movement ceases but proper ordering endures as necessary.

### Reply to Objection 3
Virtue as the means to happiness involves movement toward the end; this movement ceases upon arrival. But right ordering, which virtue produces, endures in happiness itself.

## Article 5: Whether the body is necessary for man's happiness?

### Objection 1
Virtue's and grace's perfection presupposes nature's perfection. Since the soul without body lacks natural perfection (being naturally part of human nature), the soul cannot be happy without the body.

### Objection 2
Perfect operation follows perfect being. The soul separated from body lacks perfect being like a separated part. Thus the soul cannot be happy without the body.

### Objection 3
Happiness perfects man. But the separated soul is not man, so happiness cannot exist in it.

### Objection 4
The Philosopher states that bliss's operation is "unhindered." Yet the separated soul is hindered because, as Augustine notes, it naturally desires governing the body, holding it from completely tending toward divine vision.

### Objection 5
Happiness sufficiently satisfies desire. But the separated soul still desires union with the body, so it lacks the self-sufficiency happiness requires.

### Objection 6
In happiness man equals angels. Yet the separated soul does not equal angels, according to Augustine.

### On the Contrary
Scripture declares: "Happy are the dead who die in the Lord."

### I Answer That
Happiness divides into imperfect (this-life) and perfect (God's vision) forms. The body is necessary for earthly happiness since intellectual operation in this life requires phantasms dependent on bodily organs. But perfect happiness, consisting in divine essence-vision, does not require the body.

Some maintained that souls separated from bodies cannot achieve this perfect happiness until resurrection. However, scriptural authority and reason contradict this. Paul writes that while embodied we walk by faith, absent from the Lord, but the saints' separated souls are present with the Lord, walking by sight—seeing God's Essence wherein true happiness resides.

Reasonably, the intellect needs the body only for phantasms through which it grasps intelligible truth. Since God's Essence cannot be seen through phantasms, and since man's perfect happiness consists in this vision, the body is unnecessary. The separated soul can achieve happiness.

Something may perfect a thing two ways: constituting its essence or constituting its well-being. The body is unnecessary the first way but necessary the second way. A more perfect soul-nature operates more perfectly, and happiness increases in extent (though not intensity) upon resurrection when the body, now spiritual rather than corruptible, fully serves the spirit.

### Reply to Objection 1
Happiness perfects the soul regarding its intellective aspect, through which it transcends bodily organs—not regarding its role as body's natural form.

### Reply to Objection 2
The soul's relation to being differs from other parts'. While typical separated parts lose being, the human soul retains the composite's being and subsists in its own being, thus possessing perfect being enabling perfect operations.

### Reply to Objection 3
Happiness belongs to man regarding his intellect. Since intellect remains, happiness remains possible—like extracted teeth retaining their whiteness.

### Reply to Objection 4
One thing hinders another either through opposition (repugnant to happiness) or through deficiency (preventing complete perfection but not incompatible with happiness). The separated soul's hindrance is the second type. The soul desires enjoyment overflowing into the body so that it too participates. Possessing God, its appetite rests in what it has while still wishing the body to attain its share.

### Reply to Objection 5
The separated soul's desire entirely rests regarding the desired thing—it possesses sufficing goodness. Its desire is not wholly at rest regarding the desirer itself since it cannot possess that good in every wished way. Upon resurrection, happiness increases in extent rather than intensity.

### Reply to Objection 6
Augustine's statement about souls not seeing God as angels do refers to inequality of proportion, not quantity. The blessed souls eventually rise to higher angel-orders, seeing more clearly than lower angels. However, separated souls lack every perfection-element that blessed souls eventually possess.

## Article 6: Whether perfection of the body is necessary for happiness?

### Objection 1
Bodily perfection constitutes a bodily good. Since happiness excludes bodily goods, no bodily perfection is necessary.

### Objection 2
Happiness consists in divine essence-vision. The body participates in no such operation, so bodily disposition is unnecessary.

### Objection 3
The intellect understands more perfectly when abstracted from the body. Since happiness represents the intellect's most perfect operation, complete body-abstraction should occur, making bodily disposition unnecessary.

### On the Contrary
Scripture promises the blessed: "You shall see and your heart shall rejoice, and your bones shall flourish like a herb," indicating bodily perfection belongs to the reward.

### I Answer That
For earthly happiness, a well-disposed body is clearly necessary, as it enables virtuous operation. For perfect happiness, some claimed bodily disposition is unnecessary—even that the soul must entirely separate from the body. Porphyry stated: "for the soul to be happy, it must be severed from everything corporeal."

This reasoning fails. Since union with the body is natural to the soul, the soul's perfection cannot exclude this natural perfection. Therefore, perfect bodily disposition is necessary both antecedently and consequently for complete happiness.

Antecedently: Augustine explains that a corruptible, burdensome body diverts the mind from supreme vision. Once "this body will no longer be animal but spiritual," equaling angels' glory, it ceases being a burden. Consequently: From the soul's happiness-fullness, incorruption's vigor overflows into the lower nature, perfecting the body.

### Reply to Objection 1
Happiness does not consist in bodily good as its object, but bodily good can add charm and perfection to happiness.

### Reply to Objection 2
Although the body participates in no divine-essence-seeing operation, a corrupted body hinders the mind's elevation. Perfect bodily condition is therefore necessary to prevent obstruction.

### Reply to Objection 3
Perfect intellection requires abstraction from this corruptible body weighing on the soul—not from the spiritual body wholly subject to spirit in the blessed state.

## Article 7: Whether any external goods are necessary for happiness?

### Objection 1
External goods are promised to saints as reward: food, drink, wealth, and kingdom. Therefore external goods belong to happiness.

### Objection 2
According to Boethius, happiness represents "a state made perfect by the aggregate of all good things." Since man possesses some external goods (though least important), they too are necessary for happiness.

### Objection 3
Our Lord says: "Your reward is very great in heaven." Being in heaven implies occupying place, so at minimum spatial location is necessary for happiness.

### On the Contrary
Scripture declares: "For what have I in heaven? and besides Thee what do I desire upon earth?"—meaning nothing but God suffices for happiness.

### I Answer That
For imperfect earthly happiness, external goods are necessary as instruments enabling virtue's operation, not as happiness's essence. Humans need bodily necessaries and equipment for both contemplative and active virtue.

For perfect happiness—God's vision—such goods are wholly unnecessary. All external goods serve either animal-body support or operations involving the animal body. Perfect happiness exists either in the soul separated from the body or in the soul united to the spiritual (not animal) body. Therefore external goods are unnecessary, especially since contemplative felicity—approaching perfect happiness more closely—requires fewer such goods than active felicity.

### Reply to Objection 1
Scripture's material promises express spiritual realities through corporeal language to elevate desire from known to unknown things. Thus food signifies happiness's delight; wealth signifies God's sufficiency; kingdom signifies union with God.

### Reply to Objection 2
These animal-life-serving goods conflict with the spiritual life constituting perfect happiness. Nevertheless, that happiness encompasses all good-things because we possess all genuine goodness in the Supreme Good's fountain.

### Reply to Objection 3
Augustine explains that saints' reward is "heaven" not in material sense but raised in spiritual goods. However, the empyrean heaven will be appointed to the Blessed as fitting adornment rather than happiness-necessity.

## Article 8: Whether the fellowship of friends is necessary for happiness?

### Objection 1
Scripture frequently designates future happiness as "glory." Glory consists in one's good becoming known widely. Therefore friendship-fellowship is necessary.

### Objection 2
Boethius states: "there is no delight in possessing any good whatever, without someone to share it with us." Since delight is necessary for happiness, friendship is also necessary.

### Objection 3
Charity, perfected in happiness, includes both God-love and neighbor-love. Therefore friendship-fellowship is necessary for happiness.

### On the Contrary
Scripture states: "All good things came to me together with her," referring to divine wisdom and God-contemplation. Thus nothing further is necessary.

### I Answer That
For earthly happiness, the happy person needs friends—not for self-sufficiency (already possessed) nor for delight-finding (already present in virtue's operation), but for good-doing: performing good works for friends, delighting in their virtue's growth, and receiving their assistance in good pursuits.

For perfect heavenly happiness, friendship-fellowship is not essential since man possesses complete perfection in God. Nevertheless, it conduces to happiness's well-being. Augustine observes that spiritual creatures receive happiness's interior aid from the Creator's eternity, truth, and charity, but external aid comes from seeing one another and rejoicing in God together.

### Reply to Objection 1
Happiness's essential glory exists with God rather than with men.

### Reply to Objection 2
This saying applies to good-possession that fails to fully satisfy—not applicable here, as man possesses every good's sufficiency in God.

### Reply to Objection 3
Charity's perfection is essential regarding God-love but not neighbor-love. A single soul enjoying God would be happy though lacking neighbors to love. Should neighbors exist, perfect God-love produces neighbor-love as consequence, making friendship happiness's concomitant rather than essential element.
