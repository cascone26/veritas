# Question 76: The Union of Body and Soul

## Article 1: Whether the intellectual principle is united to the body as its form?

### Objection 1
The Philosopher states that the intellect is "separate" and not the act of any body, therefore it cannot be united to the body as its form.

### Objection 2
If the intellect were the body's form, it would be determined by the nature of matter. But then the intellect could not know all things.

### Objection 3
Whatever receptive power acts through a body receives forms materially and individually. But the intellect receives forms immaterially and universally.

### Objection 4
Intellectual action is not bodily action. Therefore intellectual faculty cannot be a power of the body.

### Objection 5
The intellectual principle has per se existence and is subsistent, so it is not the body's form.

### Objection 6
What belongs to a form by nature exists in it always. But the intellectual principle remains separate after death.

### On the contrary
According to the Philosopher, difference derives from form. Man's distinguishing difference is "rational," which applies through the intellectual principle. Therefore the intellectual principle is man's form.

### I answer that
The intellect, being the principle of intellectual operation, must be the form of the human body. That which primarily causes action is a form of the thing acting. The soul is the primary principle of life and vital operations. Therefore the intellect, the principle by which we understand, is the body's form.

If someone denies the intellectual soul is the body's form, they must explain how understanding belongs to this particular man. Each person knows that he himself understands. The Commentator claimed union occurs through intelligible species having two subjects—in the possible intellect and in corporeal phantasms. But this doesn't adequately explain why Socrates' intellectual act is Socrates' act.

Some maintained the intellect unites as the body's motor. This is absurd: the intellect moves only through appetite, whose operation presupposes understanding. Therefore, Aristotle's explanation alone suffices: this particular man understands because the intellectual principle is his form.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellectual power is separate because it doesn't belong to a corporeal organ. Yet the soul, to which this power belongs, is the body's form.

### Reply to Objection 2
For man to understand all things, it suffices that the intellectual power not be the body's act.

### Reply to Objection 3
The human soul, by its perfection, is not a form merged in matter entirely embraced by it. Therefore some power can be not the body's act.

### Reply to Objection 4
The human soul, though essentially the body's form, has some power that is not the body's act.

### Reply to Objection 5
The soul communicates its subsistent existence to corporeal matter, producing unified existence of the composite. The human soul retains its own existence after bodily dissolution, unlike other forms.

### Reply to Objection 6
To be united to the body belongs to the soul through itself. The human soul, when separated, retains natural inclination and aptitude to rejoin the body.

## Article 2: Whether the intellectual principle is multiplied according to the number of bodies?

### Objection 1
An immaterial substance isn't multiplied in number within one species. Therefore there is one intellect for all men.

### Objection 2
If souls were multiplied by bodies' number, then with bodies gone, all souls would become one.

### Objection 3
If my intellect differs from yours, species would be received individually, contrary to intellect's nature.

### On the contrary
The Philosopher says the relation of universal causes to universals is like particular causes to individuals. One individual intellectual soul cannot belong to several individuals.

### I answer that
It is absolutely impossible for one intellect to belong to all men. If Socrates and Plato shared one intellect, they would be one man. The intellect is a power of the soul which is man's form. Many distinct individuals cannot have one form, as they cannot have one existence.

Moreover, if one intellect served all men, all would be one "understander" with one act of understanding—clearly absurd. Different phantasms in different people wouldn't cause different intellectual operations, since the intelligible species abstracted from phantasms is what determines understanding, and one species yields one understanding of a nature.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellectual soul, unlike an angel, is a matter's form. Therefore, according to matter's division, many souls of one species exist.

### Reply to Objection 2
After bodies dissolve, souls retain their multiplied being, since each retains its own existence.

### Reply to Objection 3
The knower's individuality doesn't exclude understanding universals, as the species whereby it knows is abstracted from individual matter's conditions.

## Article 3: Whether besides the intellectual soul there is another soul?

### Objection 1
The intellectual soul is incorruptible; the sensitive and nutritive souls are corruptible. Therefore they cannot be identical.

### Objection 2
If the sensitive soul in man were incorruptible, "animal" would not be one genus common to man and other animals.

### Objection 3
The embryo is an animal before it is a man. This would be impossible if the sensitive and intellectual souls were identical.

### On the contrary
De Ecclesiasticis Dogmatibus states: "We say it is one and the same soul in man, both giving life to the body by uniting to it and ordering itself by its own reasoning."

### I answer that
If the soul unites to body as its form, several essentially different souls in one body is impossible. First, an animal would not be absolutely one having several souls—nothing is absolutely one except by one form. Second, "animal" is predicated of man essentially, not accidentally—this requires the same form making something animal and man. Third, when one soul operation is intense, it impedes another, which requires an essentially one principle.

The intellectual soul virtually contains whatever belongs to the sensitive and nutritive souls, as a pentagon contains a tetragon. Therefore by one and the same soul, Socrates is both animal and man.

### Reply to Objection 1
The sensitive soul is incorruptible not through being sensitive but through being intellectual.

### Reply to Objection 2
Not forms but composites are classified generically. Man is corruptible like other animals.

### Reply to Objection 3
The embryo first has a merely sensitive soul. When this is removed, a more perfect soul—both sensitive and intellectual—supplants it.

## Article 4: Whether in man there is another form besides the intellectual soul?

### Objection 1
The soul is "the act of a physical body which has life potentially." Therefore some other substantial form precedes the soul.

### Objection 2
Man moves himself. The moving part is the soul, the other part must be movable. Primary matter cannot be moved. Therefore another substantial form constitutes the body.

### On the contrary
Of one thing there is but one substantial being. The soul is man's substantial form. Therefore no other substantial form exists in man.

### I answer that
If the intellectual soul is united to body as its substantial form, another substantial form cannot exist in man. The substantial form gives being simply; by its coming a thing is generated simply; by its removal, corrupted simply. If another substantial form pre-existed, the soul wouldn't give being simply and wouldn't be the substantial form.

The intellectual soul virtually contains the sensitive and nutritive souls and all inferior forms. It alone does whatever imperfect forms do in other things.

### Reply to Objection 1
When the soul is called the act, the soul itself is included—by the soul, a body is a body, organic, and has life potentially.

### Reply to Objection 2
The soul moves the body through its motive power, not through its essence as form. The animate body is the part moved.

## Article 5: Whether the intellectual soul is properly united to such a body?

### Objection 1
The intellectual soul is incorruptible. Therefore it should not unite to a corruptible body.

### Objection 2
The intellectual soul is perfectly immaterial. Therefore it should unite to a most subtle body, not a mixed body.

### On the contrary
The Philosopher says the soul is "the act of a physical organic body having life potentially."

### I answer that
Since form is not for matter but matter for form, the intellectual soul needed sensing power as well as understanding power. Senses' action requires corporeal instruments. Touch's organ requires a medium between contraries. Therefore the body to which the intellectual soul unites should be a mixed body, reduced to the most equable complexion. Among animals, man has the best touch sense. Among men, those with the best touch sense have the best intelligence.

### Reply to Objection 1
The equable-complexioned body is corruptible by force of its matter, though chosen for its suitability to the form. God provided a remedy against death through grace's gift.

### Reply to Objection 2
The intellectual soul needed an equable-temperament organ for sense, not a simple element.

## Article 6: Whether the intellectual soul is united to the body through accidental dispositions?

### Objection 1
Dispositions to a form are accidents preceding the substantial form.

### On the contrary
Accident is posterior to substance. No accidental form can exist in matter before the soul.

### I answer that
If the intellectual soul unites to body as the substantial form, no accidental disposition can come between body and soul. Since matter is in potentiality to all acts in order, what is first among acts—existence—must be first in matter. Matter has actual existence through the substantial form. Therefore no accidental dispositions can pre-exist before the substantial form.

## Article 7: Whether the soul is united to the animal body by means of a body?

### Objection 1
Augustine says the soul "administers the body by light" and "by air."

### On the contrary
The Philosopher says: "We need not ask if the soul and body are one, as neither do we ask if wax and its shape are one."

### I answer that
If the soul unites to body as its form, it is impossible for another body to intervene. The form through itself makes a thing actual since it is itself essentially an act. It doesn't give existence through something else. All theories of intermediate bodies between soul and body—incorruptible bodies, corporeal spirit, or light—are fictitious.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine speaks of the soul as it moves the body. It moves grosser parts through more subtle parts. The motive power's first instrument is a kind of spirit.

## Article 8: Whether the soul is in each part of the body?

### Objection 1
The Philosopher says "It is not necessary for the soul to be in each body part."

### On the contrary
Augustine says: "In each body the whole soul is in the whole body, and in each part is entire."

### I answer that
Since the soul unites to body as its form, it must be in the whole body and in each part. The substantial form perfects not only the whole but each part. Upon the soul's withdrawal, no body part retains its proper action.

The whole soul is in each body part by totality of perfection and essence, but not by totality of power. Regarding sight, it is in the eye; regarding hearing, in the ear.
