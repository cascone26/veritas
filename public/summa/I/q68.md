# Question 68: The Work of the Second Day

## Article 1: Whether the firmament was made on the second day?

### Objection 1
It appears the firmament was not made on the second day. Genesis states that "God called the firmament heaven," yet heaven existed before the days, as confirmed by "In the beginning God created heaven and earth." Therefore, the firmament could not have been made on the second day.

### Objection 2
The work of six days follows Divine wisdom's order. Yet it would be improper for Divine wisdom to create later what is naturally prior. Though the firmament naturally precedes earth and waters, these are mentioned before light's formation on the first day. Therefore, the firmament was not made on the second day.

### Objection 3
All things made in six days were formed from matter created before days began. However, the firmament cannot have been formed from pre-existing matter, as this would make it susceptible to generation and corruption. Therefore, the firmament was not made on the second day.

### On the contrary
Genesis states: "God said: let there be a firmament," and "the evening and morning were the second day."

### I answer that
Augustine teaches two rules for such questions: first, hold Scripture's truth without wavering; second, since Scripture admits multiple interpretations, adhere to particular explanations only until proven false with certainty, lest Scripture face ridicule.

The firmament's creation on the second day admits two understandings. First, regarding the starry firmament, philosophical opinions differ. Some believed it composed of elements (Empedocles); others held it as a simple element like fire (Plato); still others considered it a fifth body beyond the four elements (Aristotle).

According to the first opinion, the firmament's substance could strictly be granted as made on the second day. Creation produces elemental substance, while distinction and adornment give forms to pre-existing elements.

However, Plato's view—that the firmament's creation implies producing fire—conflicts with this, as elemental production belongs to creation itself, particularly if formlessness preceded formation temporally.

Aristotle's opinion is even less compatible. Since days denote temporal succession, and the incorruptible firmament cannot change form, it could not have been made from antecedent matter. Thus, producing the firmament's substance belongs to creation, though its formation partly belongs to the second day according to both opinions. Dionysius notes the sun's light remained formless during the first three days, receiving form on the fourth.

If these days denote sequence in natural order rather than temporal succession, as Augustine holds, then any philosophical opinion permits saying the firmament's substantial formation belongs to the second day.

Another explanation understands the second day's firmament not as the starry heaven but as the atmospheric region where clouds gather, called firmament for its density. Basil notes that "a body is called firm, that is dense and solid, thereby differing from a mathematical body." This explanation contradicts none of these opinions. Augustine commends this view: "I consider this worthy of all commendation, as neither contrary to faith nor difficult to prove and believe."

### Reply to Objection 1
Chrysostom explains that Moses prefaces his account collectively—"In the beginning God created heaven and earth"—then explains the works individually, like describing house construction first, then explaining the foundation, walls, and roof separately. Thus, we need not hold that different heavens are meant in these passages.

Alternatively, the heaven created initially differs from that made on the second day. Augustine says the first heaven is formless spiritual nature; the second is corporeal heaven. Bede and Strabus hold the first day's heaven is the empyrean; the second day's firmament is the starry heaven. Damascene explains that the first day's heaven was spherical and starless—what philosophers call the ninth sphere or primary movable body with diurnal motion—while the second day's firmament is the starry heaven.

Another theory, mentioned by Augustine, holds that the first day's heaven was the starry heaven, and the second day's firmament was the air region where clouds gather, also called heaven but equivocally. The text says "God called the firmament heaven" to show equivocal usage, just as it says "God called the light day" (day also denoting twenty-four hours). Similar instances appear elsewhere, as Rabbi Moses notes.

### Reply to Objections 2 and 3
These objections are sufficiently answered by what has already been said.

## Article 2: Whether there are waters above the firmament?

### Objection 1
Waters above the firmament appear not to exist. Water is naturally heavy and tends downward, not upward. Therefore, waters do not exist above the firmament.

### Objection 2
Water is naturally fluid, and fluids cannot rest on a sphere, as experience demonstrates. Since the firmament is spherical, water cannot exist above it.

### Objection 3
Water is an element appointed for generating composite bodies, relating to them as imperfect to perfect. Composite bodies have their place upon earth, not above the firmament, making water useless there. Since God's works are not useless, waters do not exist above the firmament.

### On the contrary
Genesis states: "God divided the waters that were under the firmament from those that were above the firmament."

### I answer that
Augustine teaches: "These words of Scripture have more authority than the most exalted human intellect. Hence, whatever these waters are, we cannot doubt that they are there." Regarding their nature, opinions differ.

Origen says waters above the firmament are "spiritual substances," supported by Psalm 148:4: "Let the waters that are above the heavens praise the name of the Lord." Basil responds that these words do not mean these waters are rational creatures, but rather that "thoughtful contemplation of them by understanding persons fulfills the Creator's glory." Similarly, fire and hail are invoked without attributing reason to them.

These waters must be material. Their exact nature depends on firmament interpretations. If the firmament is the starry heaven of elemental nature, the waters above may be of the same elemental nature. If the starry heaven is not of elemental nature, the waters above differ in nature from elemental waters. Just as one heaven is called empyrean (fiery) for its splendor alone, another is called aqueous for its transparence, existing above the starry heaven.

If the firmament is the air region where clouds gather, then waters above represent vapors resolved from water, raised above atmosphere portions, producing rain. However, saying vapors are lifted above the starry heaven is absurd. The firmament's solid nature, the intervening fire region consuming all vapor, light bodies' tendency to drift beneath the moon's vault, and vapors' failure to rise even to mountain tops all demonstrate this impossibility.

### Reply to Objection 1
Some attempt solving this by supposing Divine power keeps water above the firmament despite its natural gravity. Augustine rejects this: "It is our business here to inquire how God has constituted the natures of His creatures, not how far it pleased Him to work by miracle." According to the last two firmament opinions, the solution emerges from what has been said.

According to the first opinion, the elements must be ordered differently from Aristotle's account: waters surrounding earth are dense; those around the firmament are rarer, proportionate to earth's and heaven's respective density. Alternatively, water signifies formless matter.

### Reply to Objection 2
The solution appears clearly from the last two opinions. According to the first opinion, Basil offers two replies. First, a body appearing concave beneath need not be rounded or convex above. Second, waters above the firmament are not fluid but exist externally in solid state, like ice, constituting the crystalline heaven of some writers.

### Reply to Objection 3
According to the third opinion, waters above the firmament exist as raised vapors, providing rain. According to the second opinion, they exist above the wholly transparent starless heaven. Some consider this the primary mobile, causing daily heavenly revolution, securing generation's continuance. Similarly, the starry heaven, through zodiacal movement, causes generation or corruption of bodies through stellar rising, setting, and various influences.

According to the first opinion, these waters temper celestial bodies' heat, as Basil supposes. Augustine notes some consider this proved by Saturn's extreme cold, owing to its nearness to waters above the firmament.

## Article 3: Whether the firmament divides waters from waters?

### Objection 1
The firmament does not divide waters from waters. Bodies of the same species naturally occupy the same place. The Philosopher states "all water is the same species." Therefore, water cannot be distinguished from water by place.

### Objection 2
If waters above the firmament differ specifically from those beneath, things differing specifically require nothing else to distinguish them. If these waters differ specifically, the firmament does not distinguish them.

### Objection 3
What divides waters from waters must contact them on either side, like a wall in a river. It is evident that waters below do not reach up to the firmament. Therefore, the firmament does not divide waters from waters.

### On the contrary
Genesis states: "Let there be a firmament made amidst the waters; and let it divide the waters from the waters."

### I answer that
Genesis, considered superficially, might suggest a theory like that of ancient philosophers who taught water was an infinite body and the primary element of all bodies. The "deep" in "Darkness was upon the face of the deep" might denote this infinite water mass. These philosophers taught that corporeal things are not confined beneath the perceived heaven but that an infinite water body exists above it. On this view, the firmament divides outer waters from inner—all bodies under heaven, since they considered water their principle.

However, this theory is demonstrably false and cannot represent Scripture's meaning. Rather, Moses spoke to ignorant people, condescending to weakness by presenting only sensory-apparent things. Even the uneducated perceive earth and water as corporeal, but air's corporeality is not evident to all, as some philosophers called air-filled space a vacuum.

Moses expressly mentions water and earth but not air by name, avoiding presenting the ignorant with unknowns. Yet to express truth to the capable, he implies air's existence in "Darkness was upon the face of the deep," suggesting a transparent body extended over water—the subject of light and darkness, namely air.

Whether the firmament is the starry heaven or the cloudy air region, it truly divides waters from waters, taking water to denote formless matter or any transparent body fittingly designated as water. The starry heaven divides lower transparent bodies from higher ones; the cloudy region divides that air region's higher part, where rain is generated, from the lower part connected with water and included under that name.

### Reply to Objection 1
If the firmament is the starry heaven, the waters above are not the same species as those beneath. If the firmament is the cloudy air region, both waters are the same species, though assigned different places—the higher being their generation place, the lower their resting place.

### Reply to Objection 2
If waters differ specifically, the firmament cannot divide them as their destruction's cause but only as each one's boundary.

### Reply to Objection 3
Because air and similar bodies are invisible, Moses includes all such bodies under water's name, thus making evident that waters exist on each side of the firmament, whatever sense the word bears.

## Article 4: Whether there is more than one heaven?

### Objection 1
Only one heaven exists. Heaven is contrasted with earth in "In the beginning God created heaven and earth." But there is only one earth. Therefore, only one heaven exists.

### Objection 2
What consists of its entire matter's sum must be one; such is heaven, as the Philosopher proves. Therefore, only one heaven exists.

### Objection 3
What is predicated univocally of many things applies according to some common notion. If multiple heavens existed, they would be called such univocally, for if equivocally, they could not properly be called many. If they are many, some common notion by which each is called heaven must exist, but this cannot be assigned. Therefore, more than one heaven cannot exist.

### On the contrary
Psalm 148:4 states: "Praise Him, ye heavens of heavens."

### I answer that
Basil and Chrysostom seem to differ here. Chrysostom says only one heaven exists, claiming "heavens of heavens" merely translates Hebrew idiom where the plural is always used, like Latin nouns lacking singular forms. Basil, followed by Damascene, says many heavens exist.

However, their difference is more nominal than real. Chrysostom's "one heaven" means the entire body above earth and water, for which reason flying birds are called "birds of heaven." Since this body contains many distinct parts, Basil said more heavens exist.

To understand heaven distinctions, note that Scripture uses "heaven" threefold. Sometimes it denotes that luminous (actually or potentially) and incorruptible-by-nature body on high. This body contains three heavens: first, the empyrean, wholly luminous; second, the aqueous or crystalline, wholly transparent; third, the starry heaven, partly transparent and partly actually luminous, divided into eight spheres—one for fixed stars and seven for planets, called the seven heavens.

Second, "heaven" applies to bodies participating in heavenly properties like sublimity and luminosity (actual or potential). Damascene calls all space between waters and the moon's orb the aerial heaven. Thus, there are three heavens: aerial, starry, and one higher, of which Paul speaks when saying he was "rapt to the third heaven."

Since this space contains fire and air elements, each having higher and lower regions, Rabanus subdivides it into four heavens. The fire's higher region is the fiery heaven; the lower, the Olympian, from a lofty mountain: the air's higher region is the ethereal heaven; the lower, the aerial. Adding these four to the three above yields seven corporeal heavens in Rabanus's opinion.

Third, metaphorical uses apply "heaven" to the Blessed Trinity, the Light and Most High Spirit. Some explain "I will ascend into heaven" thus, showing the evil spirit seeking equality with God. Spiritual blessings, the Saints' recompense, being highest goods, are signified by heaven and are so signified, according to Augustine, in "Your reward is very great in heaven."

Three supernatural vision kinds—bodily, imaginative, and intellectual—are sometimes called so many heavens, by reference to which Augustine explains Paul's rapture "to the third heaven."

### Reply to Objection 1
Earth stands to heaven as a circle's center to its circumference. Just as one center may have many circumferences, though earth is one, many heavens may exist.

### Reply to Objection 2
This argument holds for heaven insofar as it denotes the entire corporeal creation's sum, in which sense it is one.

### Reply to Objection 3
All heavens share sublimity and some luminosity degree, as what has been said demonstrates.
