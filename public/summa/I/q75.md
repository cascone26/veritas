# Question 75: Man Composed of Spiritual and Corporeal Substance

## Article 1: Whether the Soul is a Body?

### Objection 1
The soul moves the body, and "nothing can move unless it is itself moved." What is not hot cannot give heat. Therefore the soul, being a mover moved, must be a body.

### Objection 2
All knowledge requires likeness between knower and known. Since the soul must know corporeal things, it cannot be incorporeal.

### Objection 3
Movement requires contact between mover and moved. Contact occurs only between bodies. Therefore the soul must be a body.

### On the Contrary
Augustine states the soul "is simple in comparison with the body, inasmuch as it does not occupy space by its bulk."

### I Answer That
The soul is defined as the first principle of life in living things. Ancient philosophers, unable to transcend imagination, supposed only bodies were real. However, this is false.

Not every vital principle is a soul—the eye is a principle of vision but not the soul itself. The soul is the first principle. While a body like the heart may be vital, no body can be the first principle of life. If bodies were living as such, all bodies would be living. Rather, a body becomes living through some principle called its act. Therefore, the soul is not a body but "the act of a body; thus heat, which is the principle of calefaction, is not a body, but an act of a body."

### Reply to Objection 1
Not every mover is moved. Some movers are "altogether immovable" and cause unchanging movement. Others are "moved accidentally" (like the soul). Only bodies are "moved essentially." Ancient philosophers, believing only bodies existed, wrongly concluded the soul must be moved.

### Reply to Objection 2
A likeness of known things need not actually exist in the knower's nature, only potentially. Color exists potentially (not actually) in the pupil. Ancient philosophers failed to distinguish actuality from potentiality.

### Reply to Objection 3
Two kinds of contact exist: "of quantity" (body to body only) and "of power" (incorporeal substances can move bodies through power).

## Article 2: Whether the Human Soul is Something Subsistent?

### Objection 1
"This particular thing" applies to composites of soul and body, not to soul alone. Therefore the soul is not subsistent.

### Objection 2
Everything subsistent operates. The soul does not operate—understanding belongs to the composite, not the soul alone. Therefore the soul is not subsistent.

### Objection 3
If the soul were subsistent, it would operate apart from the body. But understanding requires phantasms, which cannot exist without the body. Therefore the human soul is not subsistent.

### On the Contrary
Augustine affirms that understanding "the nature of the soul is that of a substance and not that of a body" proves the human intellect is not only incorporeal but "a substance, that is, something subsistent."

### I Answer That
The principle of intellectual operation (the soul) must be both incorporeal and subsistent. Through intellect, humans know all corporeal things. Whatever knows certain things cannot contain those things in its nature—a sick person's tongue vitiated by bitter humor cannot taste sweetness.

If the intellectual principle were a body, it would have a determinate nature preventing knowledge of all bodies. Similarly, understanding through a bodily organ would fail—just as liquid in a colored glass appears that color.

The intellectual principle has operation per se apart from the body. Only what subsists can operate per se—we say what is hot gives heat, not heat gives heat. Therefore, the human soul (intellect/mind) is incorporeal and subsistent.

### Reply to Objection 1
"This particular thing" has two senses: (1) anything subsistent, or (2) what subsists completely in a specific nature. The soul qualifies as (1) but not (2), since it is part of human nature. What is composed of body and soul is "this particular thing" in sense (2).

### Reply to Objection 2
To operate per se requires existing per se—something either not inherent (like accidents) or not part of anything else. Eyes and hands don't subsist per se and thus don't operate per se. The soul understands as the eye sees; more correctly, humans understand through the soul.

### Reply to Objection 3
The body is necessary for intellect's action regarding objects (phantasms relate to intellect as color to sight), not as origin of action. Such dependence doesn't prove non-subsistence.

## Article 3: Whether the Souls of Brute Animals are Subsistent?

### Objection 1
Humans are the same genus as other animals. Human souls are subsistent. Therefore, animal souls are subsistent.

### Objection 2
The sensitive faculty relates to sensible objects as intellect to intelligible objects. Intellect apprehends apart from the body. Therefore sensitive faculty perceives apart from the body. Animal souls are sensitive, so they are subsistent.

### Objection 3
The soul of brute animals moves the body. The body is moved, not a mover. Therefore the animal soul has operation apart from the body.

### On the Contrary
The book De Ecclesiasticis Dogmatibus states: "Man alone we believe to have a subsistent soul: whereas the souls of animals are not subsistent."

### I Answer That
Ancient philosophers conflated sense and intellect, attributing both to corporeal principles. Plato distinguished them but deemed both incorporeal. Aristotle held that only understanding operates without a corporeal organ.

Sensation involves bodily change—vision affects the pupil through color reflection. The sensitive soul lacks per se operation; all sensitive soul operations belong to the composite. Since operation follows mode of being, souls with no per se operations are not subsistent. Therefore, animal souls are not subsistent.

### Reply to Objection 1
Though humans share the same genus with other animals, they differ in species. Species differences derive from form differences, which don't necessarily indicate genus diversity.

### Reply to Objection 2
The sensitive and intellectual faculties relate differently to objects. Both are potential to their objects, but sensible impressions involve bodily change—excessive sensible strength corrupts sensation. Intellectual understanding of highest objects enhances subsequent lower understanding. Bodily weariness in intellectual work occurs accidentally.

### Reply to Objection 3
Two motive powers exist: (1) appetitive power commanding motion (in sensitive soul, accompanied by bodily change), and (2) power executing motion by adapting members to appetite. Therefore, moving is not an act of the sensitive soul without the body.

## Article 4: Whether the Soul is Man?

### Objection 1
Paul writes: "Though our outward man is corrupted, yet the inward man is renewed day by day." The inward man is the soul. Therefore the soul is man.

### Objection 2
The human soul is substance, not universal substance. Therefore it is particular substance, thus a hypostasis or person—a human person. Therefore the soul is man.

### On the Contrary
Augustine commends Varro for holding "that man is not a mere soul, nor a mere body; but both soul and body."

### I Answer That
The statement "the soul is man" can mean two things:

First interpretation: Man is a soul (meaning the form belongs to species, while matter belongs to individual). This is false—natural things' definitions include form and matter. Just as this particular man comprises this soul, this flesh, these bones, so "man" comprises soul, flesh, and bones. Common matter belongs to the species' substance.

Second interpretation: This soul is this man (if sensation were proper to soul alone, since whatever performs a thing's proper operations is that thing). However, sensation is not the soul's operation alone. Since sensation is man's operation but not unique to him, man is not soul only but something composed of soul and body. Plato supposed sensation proper to soul and thus maintained man is soul using the body.

### Reply to Objection 1
According to Aristotle, what is principal in something is said to be that thing. What is principal in man is sometimes the intellectual part (the "inward man"), sometimes the sensitive part with body (the "outward man").

### Reply to Objection 2
Not every particular substance is a hypostasis or person, but what has the complete nature of its species. A hand or foot is not called hypostasis or person; neither is the soul alone, since it is human species' part.

## Article 5: Whether the Soul is Composed of Matter and Form?

### Objection 1
Potentiality opposes actuality. Whatever exists actually participates the First Act (God). Whatever exists potentially should participate first potentiality (prime matter). Since the human soul is sometimes potentially understanding, the soul must participate prime matter.

### Objection 2
Matter's properties are being a subject and being changed. The soul exhibits these: it is subject to science and virtue; it changes from ignorance to knowledge, vice to virtue. Therefore matter is in the soul.

### Objection 3
Things without matter have no cause of existence. The soul has a cause (created by God). Therefore the soul has matter.

### Objection 4
What lacks matter and is form only is pure act and infinite. This belongs to God alone. Therefore the soul has matter.

### On the Contrary
Augustine proves the soul was made neither of corporeal nor of spiritual matter.

### I Answer That
The soul has no matter. Consider this two ways:

From the soul's general notion: The soul is a body's form—either by virtue of itself entirely, or by virtue of some part. If entirely, no part can be matter (pure potentiality), since form is act and potentiality opposes actuality. If by a part, that part is the soul; the matter it actualizes is the "primary animate."

From the human soul's specific notion: The intellectual soul knows things in their absolute nature. A stone is known absolutely as a stone; the stone's form (as to proper formal idea) exists in the intellectual soul. Therefore, the intellectual soul itself is absolute form, not matter-form composite. If composed of matter and form, forms would be received as individuals, yielding only individual knowledge (as sensitive powers in corporeal organs). Matter individuates forms. Therefore, the intellectual soul and every intellectual substance knowing forms absolutely is exempt from matter-form composition.

### Reply to Objection 1
The First Act (infinite, virtually precontaining all things) is participated not as part of recipients but through diffusion of its processions. Potentiality must be proportionate to act. The intellectual soul's receptive potentiality differs from prime matter's.

### Reply to Objection 2
Being subject and being changed belong to matter through potentiality. The intelligence's potentiality differs from prime matter's—each has different subjection and change reasons.

### Reply to Objection 3
Form causes matter to be; so does the agent. A subsistent form doesn't owe existence to formal principle, nor has cause transmuting from potentiality to act.

### Reply to Objection 4
Every participated form must have existence by participation. Even a subsistent created form has participated existence. Only God, His own existence, is pure act and infinite. In intellectual substances, composition exists not of matter-form but of form and participated existence.

## Article 6: Whether the Human Soul is Incorruptible?

### Objection 1
Things with like beginnings and processes have like ends. Humans and animals begin alike and live alike. As animals' souls are corruptible, so are humans' souls.

### Objection 2
Whatever originates from nothing can return to nothing. The soul is created from nothing. Therefore it can be reduced to nothing.

### Objection 3
Nothing lacks its proper operation. The soul's proper operation—understanding through phantasm—cannot exist without the body. Therefore the soul cannot survive bodily dissolution.

### On the Contrary
Dionysius states human souls owe to Divine goodness that they are "intellectual" and possess "an incorruptible substantial life."

### I Answer That
The intellectual principle (human soul) is incorruptible. Corruption occurs two ways: per se and accidentally. No substance can be generated or corrupted accidentally. What has per se existence can only be corrupted per se—impossible for anything subsistent that is form alone. What belongs to something by virtue of itself is inseparable; existence belongs to form (an act) by virtue of itself. Therefore, a subsistent form cannot cease existing.

Even if the soul were matter-form composite, it would still be incorruptible. Corruption requires contrariety. No contrariety exists in the intellectual soul—it receives according to its mode of existence; contrary notions themselves are not contrary in the mind.

Moreover, all things naturally aspire to existence after their manner. Intellect apprehends existence absolutely and timelessly. Whatever has intellect naturally desires always to exist. Natural desire cannot be vain. Therefore every intellectual substance is incorruptible.

### Reply to Objection 1
Solomon argues in the foolish person's voice. That humans and animals begin alike applies to the body, not the soul. Animal souls are produced by bodily power; the human soul is produced by God.

### Reply to Objection 2
A thing is corruptible because it possesses potentiality to non-existence. Creation from nothing results from the Creator's active potentiality, not creature potentiality to non-existence.

### Reply to Objection 3
Understanding through phantasm is the soul's proper operation by virtue of union with the body. Separated from the body, it will understand differently.

## Article 7: Whether the Soul is of the Same Species as an Angel?

### Objection 1
The soul and angel share the same end—eternal happiness. Therefore they are the same species.

### Objection 2
The ultimate specific difference is noblest. The noblest in soul and angel is their intellectual nature. Therefore they share the same species.

### Objection 3
The soul differs from an angel seemingly only through union with the body. Since the body is outside the soul's essence, they are the same species.

### On the Contrary
Dionysius states angels possess "simple and blessed intelligence" not gathering Divine knowledge from visible things, contrary to the soul. Therefore soul and angel are not the same species.

### I Answer That
Origen held human souls and angels are the same species, supposing degree differences are accidental. This cannot be true. In incorporeal substances, number diversity without species and nature inequality is impossible. These substances are not matter-form composed but subsistent forms. A separate form can only be one of a single species. Species diversity always accompanies nature diversity.

Even if these substances were matter-form composed: if one's matter differs from another's, either the form principles the matter distinction (yielding species inequality), or matter principles form distinction. But one matter cannot differ from another except by quantity—having no place in incorporeal substances. Therefore angel and soul cannot be the same species.

### Reply to Objection 1
This argument proceeds from the ultimate supernatural end, not the proximate natural end.

### Reply to Objection 2
The intellectual faculty is not noblest because it is indeterminate and common to many intellectuality degrees. Not all intellectual things are one species.

### Reply to Objection 3
The body is not of the soul's essence; yet the soul by nature can unite to the body. The very fact the soul requires the body for operation proves it possesses an intellectuality grade inferior to angels.
