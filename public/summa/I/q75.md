# Question 75: Man Who Is Composed of a Spiritual and a Corporeal Substance: The Essence of the Soul

## Article 1: Whether the soul is a body?

### Objection 1
It appears the soul must be corporeal. Since the soul moves the body, and "nothing gives what it has not," only something already moving can cause motion. Furthermore, an unmoved mover produces eternal, unchanging movement—not the variable movement observed in animals. Therefore, the soul must be "a mover moved," and every such mover is a body.

### Objection 2
All knowledge arises through likeness between knower and known. An incorporeal thing cannot resemble a body. If the soul were not corporeal, it could not possess knowledge of corporeal things.

### Objection 3
Contact between mover and moved is necessary. Only bodies can touch bodies. Since the soul moves the body, the soul must itself be corporeal.

### On the contrary
Augustine teaches that the soul "is simple in comparison with the body, inasmuch as it does not occupy space by its bulk."

### I answer that
The soul's nature becomes clear through understanding that it is defined as "the first principle of life of those things which live." Life manifests itself primarily through knowledge and movement. Ancient philosophers, bound by imagination, assumed only bodies are real and therefore claimed the soul must be corporeal.

However, this is demonstrably false. Not every principle of vital action constitutes a soul—the eye enables vision but is not itself a soul. The soul is the first principle of life. If corporeality were sufficient for life, every body would live. Rather, a body becomes living through some principle called its "act." Therefore, the soul—the first life principle—is not itself a body but rather the act of a body, comparable to heat as the principle of warmth.

### Reply to Objection 1
Not every mover must itself be moved; this chain cannot regress infinitely. The soul, though not essentially moved, is moved accidentally and thus causes variable animal movement. Ancient philosophers, believing only bodies existed, wrongly concluded all movers must be bodies.

### Reply to Objection 2
A thing known need not actually exist within the knower's nature; rather, it exists potentially. The ancient philosophers failed to distinguish between actual and potential existence.

### Reply to Objection 3
Two contact types exist: "of quantity" (body-to-body only) and "of power" (where incorporeal things move bodies through power).

## Article 2: Whether the human soul is something subsistent?

### Objection 1
What subsists is called "this particular thing." Yet we attribute "this particular thing" not to the soul alone but to the soul-body composite. Therefore, the soul is not subsistent.

### Objection 2
Everything subsistent operates. The soul does not operate independently; as Aristotle notes, saying "the soul understands" resembles saying "the soul weaves"—absurd attributions. Therefore, the soul is not subsistent.

### Objection 3
If the soul subsisted, it would operate apart from the body. Yet understanding requires phantasms, which cannot exist without the body. Therefore, the human soul is not subsistent.

### On the contrary
Augustine declares: "Who understands that the nature of the soul is that of a substance and not that of a body, will see that those who maintain the corporeal nature of the soul, are led astray through associating with the soul those things without which they are unable to think of any nature—i.e. imaginary pictures of corporeal things."

### I answer that
The intellectual principle called the soul must necessarily be both incorporeal and subsistent. Through intellect, humans know all corporeal things. Whatever knows certain things cannot possess them within its own nature; such possession would impede knowledge of other things. The intellectual mind therefore cannot itself be a body, nor understand through bodily organs. Since the intellectual principle has operations per se independent of the body, and only subsistent things can operate independently, we conclude the human soul—called intellect or mind—is incorporeal and subsistent.

### Reply to Objection 1
"This particular thing" has two meanings: anything subsistent, or what subsists completely within a specific nature. The soul qualifies in the first sense as a subsistent entity, but not the second, as the complete thing is the soul-body composite.

### Reply to Objection 2
The soul understands, though more precisely, the human understands through the soul.

### Reply to Objection 3
The body is necessary for intellectual action not as its source but regarding the object; phantasms relate to intellect as colors to sight. Such dependence doesn't prove non-subsistence.

## Article 3: Whether the souls of brute animals are subsistent?

### Objection 1
Humans and other animals share the same genus. Since human souls are subsistent, so too must be animal souls.

### Objection 2
The sensitive faculty relates to sensible objects as the intellectual faculty relates to intelligible objects. Therefore, sensation apprehends sensible objects apart from the body, making brute souls subsistent.

### Objection 3
Animal souls move bodies. Therefore, animal souls operate independently of bodies.

### On the contrary
The book De Ecclesiasticis Dogmatibus states: "Man alone we believe to have a subsistent soul: whereas the souls of animals are not subsistent."

### I answer that
Ancient philosophers confused sense and intellect. Plato distinguished them but attributed both to an incorporeal principle, concluding animal souls subsist. Aristotle, however, held that only understanding occurs without corporeal organs. Sensation necessarily involves bodily change. Consequently, the sensitive soul has no independent operations; all sensitive operations belong to the composite being. Therefore, brute animal souls are not subsistent.

### Reply to Objection 1
Though humans and animals share genus, humans represent a different species. Species-differences derive from form-differences.

### Reply to Objection 2
The sensitive and intellectual faculties differ because sense-impression involves bodily change while intellect does not.

### Reply to Objection 3
The sensitive soul's appetitive operation necessarily involves bodily change. Moving is not the sensitive soul's independent act.

## Article 4: Whether the soul is man?

### Objection 1
Scripture states: "Though our outward man is corrupted, yet the inward man is renewed day by day." The inward aspect is the soul, so the soul is man.

### Objection 2
The human soul is substance—not universal substance, therefore particular substance, which constitutes a person. Therefore, the soul is man.

### On the contrary
Augustine commends Varro for holding "that man is not a mere soul, nor a mere body; but both soul and body."

### I answer that
"The soul is man" admits two interpretations. First: man's nature is soul alone. This cannot be true; definition's meaning includes both form and matter in natural things. Second: this soul is this man. Since sensation is human operation but not exclusively soul's operation, man is not soul alone but soul-body composite.

### Reply to Objection 1
What is principal in a thing is sometimes called that thing. The intellectual part is the "inward man" in truth; the sensitive part with body is the "outward man."

### Reply to Objection 2
Not every particular substance is a hypostasis or person—only what possesses its species' complete nature. The soul alone, being part of human species, is not a person.

## Article 5: Whether the soul is composed of matter and form?

### Objection 1
Potentiality opposes actuality. Since the human soul sometimes understands potentially, it must participate in primary matter.

### Objection 2
Matter's properties appear in the soul: being a subject and changing from ignorance to knowledge.

### Objection 3
Things without matter have no cause for existence. The soul has existence-cause—God. Therefore, the soul has matter.

### Objection 4
What lacks matter and is form only is pure act and infinite—God's exclusive possession.

### On the contrary
Augustine demonstrates that the soul was created neither of corporeal nor spiritual matter.

### I answer that
The soul contains no matter. The soul is a body's form. Either it is form entirely by itself, or by some part. If entirely, then no part can be matter. If form only through some part, that part is the soul.

Consider the human soul's intellectual nature specifically. Whatever is received into something is received according to that recipient's condition. The intellectual soul knows things in their nature absolutely. If the intellectual soul were matter-form composite, things would be received individually, permitting only individual knowledge. Therefore, the intellectual soul is exempt from matter-form composition.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellectual soul's receptive potentiality differs from first matter's potentiality. Primary matter receives individual forms; intelligence receives absolute forms.

### Reply to Objection 2
The intelligence's potentiality differs from primary matter's potentiality. Intelligence is subject to knowledge through potentiality regarding intelligible species.

### Reply to Objection 3
A subsistent form doesn't owe existence to formal principles in the way matter-form composites do.

### Reply to Objection 4
Participated existence is limited by participator-capacity. Only God, being His existence, is pure act and infinite. Intellectual substances comprise actuality-potentiality composition—not matter-form composition but form with participated existence.

## Article 6: Whether the human soul is incorruptible?

### Objection 1
Humans and animals share similar generation. Brute souls are corruptible; therefore, human souls are corruptible.

### Objection 2
What comes from nothing returns to nothing.

### Objection 3
The soul's operation—understanding through phantasms—requires the body. Therefore, the soul cannot survive body's dissolution.

### On the contrary
Dionysius declares that human souls possess "an incorruptible substantial life."

### I answer that
The intellectual principle must be incorruptible. Corruption occurs per se or accidentally. The human soul, being self-subsistent, cannot corrupt accidentally (as non-subsistent forms do when composites corrupt). It cannot corrupt per se because existence belongs to form intrinsically and inseparably—a subsistent form cannot cease existing.

Furthermore, the intellectual soul contains no contraries; received things lack contraries within it. Finally, everything naturally aspires toward existence after its manner. Intellect apprehends existence absolutely and perpetually. Therefore, everything having intellect naturally desires always to exist. Natural desire cannot be in vain. Thus, every intellectual substance is incorruptible.

### Reply to Objection 1
Humans and animals have similar generation regarding bodies but not souls. Brute souls result from bodily power; the human soul is God-produced.

### Reply to Objection 2
Creation from nothing doesn't imply creature-potentiality for non-existence but only the Creator's power to cease sustaining existence.

### Reply to Objection 3
After body-separation, the soul possesses another understanding-mode, similar to other body-separated substances.

## Article 7: Whether the soul is of the same species as an angel?

### Objection 1
Soul and angel share the same end—eternal happiness. Therefore, they are the same species.

### Objection 2
Nothing is nobler than intellectual nature in either. Therefore, they agree in ultimate specific difference.

### Objection 3
Soul and angel differ only through body-union, which lies outside the soul's essence.

### On the contrary
Things with different natural operations belong to different species. Soul and angel have different natural operations.

### I answer that
Origen held human souls and angels of identical species. This cannot be correct; in incorporeal substances, numerical diversity necessitates species and nature diversity. A separate form can exist only as single-species. Therefore, angels and souls cannot be the same species.

### Reply to Objection 1
This argument proceeds from proximate natural ends. Eternal happiness is the ultimate supernatural end.

### Reply to Objection 2
The intellectual faculty is not noblest because it is indeterminate and common to many intellectuality-degrees.

### Reply to Objection 3
The soul through its essence-nature can unite with body. That the soul requires body for operations proves it possesses inferior intellectuality-grade compared to angels.
