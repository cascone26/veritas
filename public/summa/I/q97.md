# Question 97: The Preservation of the Individual in the Primitive State

## Article 1: Whether Man Would Have Been Immortal in the State of Innocence

### Objection 1
"Mortal" is part of humanity's definition. Man could never have been immortal.

### Objection 2
Corruptible and incorruptible belong to different categories. Things cannot change categories.

### Objection 3
Immortality must come from nature or grace. Not nature (it doesn't change within species). Not grace (Adam recovered grace but not immortality).

### Objection 4
Immortality is promised as future reward. Man was created to earn rewards.

### On the contrary
"By sin death came into the world" (Romans 5:12).

### I answer that
Man possessed immortality through God's preserving power as efficient cause—not through inherent nature or glory. His body remained indissoluble through "supernatural force given by God to the soul," preserving the body while the soul remained obedient to God.

### Reply to Objections 1 and 2
These concern natural immortality, which man didn't possess.

### Reply to Objection 3
This preserving power was grace. Though Adam recovered grace through repentance, immortality's restoration was reserved for Christ.

### Reply to Objection 4
The promised immortality of glory differs from preliminary immortality in innocence.

## Article 2: Whether Man Would Have Been Impassible in the State of Innocence

### Objection 1
Sensation is a passion. Man was sensitive, so passible.

### Objection 2
Adam slept (Genesis 2:21), demonstrating passibility.

### Objection 3
God removed Adam's rib, demonstrating bodily alteration.

### Objection 4
Adam's soft body would naturally suffer from hard contact.

### On the contrary
If passible, man would be corruptible.

### I answer that
Man was passible broadly (experiencing sensation and sleep) but impassible strictly (not altered from natural state). He could curb harmful passions while avoiding sin.

### Reply to Objection 3
The rib was the principle of the human race, comparable to semen. Its removal caused no deterioration.

### Reply to Objection 4
Reason and divine providence protected from unexpected dangers.

## Article 3: Whether Man Required Food in the State of Innocence

### Objection 1
Adam's incorruptible body lost nothing, needing no food.

### Objection 2
Nourishment involves passibility.

### Objection 3
Adam would have lived without food had he not sinned.

### Objection 4
Food consumption produces waste unsuitable to innocence.

### On the contrary
"Of every tree in paradise ye shall eat" (Genesis 2:16).

### I answer that
In innocence, man possessed animal life requiring food. Natural heat causes humidity loss, requiring food as replacement. After resurrection, the soul will communicate spiritual properties instead, making food unnecessary.

### Reply to Objection 1
Though supernaturally immortal, Adam's body experienced natural heat causing humidity loss.

### Reply to Objection 2
Food undergoes transformation, not the eater.

### Reply to Objection 3
Refusing food would constitute sin, as God commanded eating.

### Reply to Objection 4
Excess material was voided fittingly by divine providence.

## Article 4: Whether Man Would Have Acquired Immortality Through the Tree of Life

### Objection 1
The corruptible tree couldn't grant incorruptibility.

### Objection 2
The tree's effects would be merely natural.

### Objection 3
This resembles pagan myths about food granting immortality.

### On the contrary
God feared man would eat and "live for ever" (Genesis 3:22). Augustine states it "warded off corruption."

### I answer that
The tree was partly but not absolutely causative of immortality. Two defects threatened life: humidity loss (remedied by ordinary food) and weakening of species-power. The tree strengthened this power, preventing age-related decline. Yet it couldn't absolutely cause immortality—bodily power is finite.
