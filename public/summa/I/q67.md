# Question 67: The Work of Distinction in Itself

## Article 1: Whether the word "light" is used in its proper sense in speaking of spiritual things?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "in spiritual things light is better and surer" and that Christ is called Light literally, not metaphorically like He is called the Stone.

### Objection 2
Dionysius includes Light among the intellectual names of God, and such names are used properly in spiritual matters.

### Objection 3
The Apostle says "All that is made manifest is light," and manifestation belongs more properly to spiritual than corporeal things.

### On the contrary
Ambrose says "Splendor" is said of God metaphorically.

### I answer that
Words can be used in their original application or extended meaning. "Sight" originally applies to sense perception but extends to all knowledge and even intellectual understanding. Similarly, light primarily signifies what makes things visible to the senses, then extends to what makes things manifest to cognition generally. When taken strictly, light applied to spiritual things is metaphorical. When taken in extended use regarding manifestation of any kind, it may properly apply to spiritual matters.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine refers to light as a luminous body in act—essentially fire, the noblest element.

### Reply to Objection 2
Aristotle describes light as fire in its proper matter, just as flame is fire in aerial matter. His logical examples should not receive excessive weight.

### Reply to Objection 3
These properties are assigned to light metaphorically, as local movement terminology extends to all alterations and changes.

## Article 2: Whether light is a body?

### Objection 1
Augustine states "light takes the first place among bodies," suggesting light is corporeal.

### Objection 2
The Philosopher says "light is a species of fire," and fire is a body.

### Objection 3
Movement, intersection, and reflection belong to bodies, and light exhibits these properties. Rays unite and separate, suggesting corporeal nature.

### On the contrary
Two bodies cannot occupy the same place simultaneously, yet light and air do.

### I answer that
Light cannot be a body for three reasons. First, regarding place: bodies require distinct locations; two bodies cannot naturally exist simultaneously in the same space. Second, regarding movement: if light were corporeal, its diffusion would be local movement, which cannot be instantaneous. Yet sunlight illuminates both hemispheres instantly. Bodies also have determinate natural movements, whereas light moves equally in all directions. Third, regarding generation and corruption: if light were a body, darkness would require a contrary body, and enormous quantities of matter would require daily generation and corruption, which is absurd.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine understands light as a luminous body in act—fire itself.

### Reply to Objection 2
Aristotle describes light as fire existing in its proper matter, similar to flame in aerial matter, but this philosophical categorization should not be overemphasized.

### Reply to Objection 3
These properties apply to light metaphorically. Local movement terminology extends to all types of alteration and change by analogy.

## Article 3: Whether light is a quality?

### Objection 1
Qualities remain in subjects when their causes cease, as heat remains in removed water. Light disappears when its source withdraws, so it is not a quality.

### Objection 2
Sensible qualities have contraries, but darkness is merely light's privation, not its opposite.

### Objection 3
Causes are more potent than effects. Light causes substantial forms and makes colors visible, suggesting light is substantial or spiritual rather than a sensible quality.

### On the contrary
Damascene states that light is a species of quality.

### I answer that
Light must have natural being, not merely intentional being as a similitude. First, light gives a name to air, making it luminous, whereas color does not color the air. Second, light produces natural effects, warming bodies through solar rays, which mere intentions cannot accomplish. Light also cannot be the sun's substantial form because substantial forms are not themselves sensible objects, while light is visible. Furthermore, substantial forms constitute species permanently, but light is not the permanent substantial form of air. Therefore, light is an active quality consequent upon the substantial form of the sun or other luminous bodies, similar to how heat follows from fire's substantial form.

### Reply to Objection 1
Quality's permanence depends on how substantially formed matter receives it. When matter receives substantial form perfectly, consequent qualities endure. When substantial form is received imperfectly, qualities are temporary. Light results not from matter's transmutation but from an active cause's presence, so it disappears when that cause vanishes.

### Reply to Objection 2
Light's lack of a contrary is accidental, since it is the natural quality of the first corporeal cause of change, which itself transcends contrariety.

### Reply to Objection 3
Light acts instrumentally by virtue of heavenly bodies toward producing substantial forms and rendering colors visible as a quality of the first sensible body.

## Article 4: Whether the production of light is fittingly assigned to the first day?

### Objection 1
Light is a quality and accident, requiring subordinate rather than first place. Its production should not be assigned to the first day.

### Objection 2
Light distinguishes night from day through the sun, made on the fourth day, not the first.

### Objection 3
Night and day result from a luminous body's circular movement, an attribute of the firmament, made on the second day.

### Objection 4
If spiritual light is meant, no spiritual darkness existed initially, since demons were originally good. Thus light's production should not be assigned to the first day.

### On the contrary
Without light there can be no day, so light must have been made on the first day.

### I answer that
Augustine suggests that "heaven" in "In the beginning God created heaven and earth" refers to spiritual nature in formless state, while "earth" means formless corporeal matter. Spiritual nature, being more dignified, received form first through enlightenment adhering to the Word of God, signified by light's production. Other writers hold Moses intentionally omitted spiritual creation. Basil notes Moses began with sensible time, omitting angelic creation as preceding temporally. Chrysostom suggests Moses addressed ignorant people prone to idolatry and would have worshipped spiritual natures. Formlessness required removal first through light, which is a quality of the first body and most common quality, needed before other distinctions. Light also precedes all other things in manifestation. Day requires light's existence. With Dionysius, the light was the sun's light, formless but already solar substance possessing illuminative power, later receiving special determinative power. This produced triple distinction: by cause (sun produces light, earth produces darkness), by place (light in one hemisphere, darkness in another), and by time (day and night alternation).

### Reply to Objection 1
According to those holding that matter's formlessness preceded its formation in duration, matter was created with substantial forms, afterward receiving accidental forms, with light foremost.

### Reply to Objection 2
Some held light was a luminous nebula returning to matter when the sun was made, but this contradicts Scripture's institution of enduring natural order. Others suggest the nebula remains but attaches inseparably to the sun, making it superfluous. Still others propose the sun's body formed from this nebula, but this contradicts the sun's immutable nature among those believing it differs from the four elements. Dionysius suggests the light was the sun's unformed light, already solar substance with illuminative power, later receiving determinative power for specific effects.

### Reply to Objection 3
Basil suggests day and night resulted from light's expansion and contraction rather than movement. Augustine objects that this vicissitude lacked necessity before humans and animals existed, and luminous bodies naturally cannot withdraw light while present. Heavenly movement is twofold: one common to entire heaven causing day-night distinction, beginning on the first day; another varying by body, causing days, months, and years' succession, beginning on the fourth day.

### Reply to Objection 4
Augustine teaches formlessness did not precede forms in duration, so light's production signifies spiritual creatures' formation with grace's perfection (not glory's), which they possessed from creation. Light-darkness distinction denotes spiritual creatures' distinction from other creation still unformed. Alternatively, if all creation received form simultaneously, darkness represents spiritual darkness of the wicked, not existing originally but foreknown by God.
