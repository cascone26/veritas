# Question 67: The Work of Distinction in Itself

## Article 1: Whether the word "light" is used in its proper sense in speaking of spiritual things?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "in spiritual things light is better and surer" and that Christ is not called Light in the same sense as Stone; the former is literal, the latter metaphorical.

### Objection 2
Dionysius includes Light among the intellectual names of God. Such names are used properly in spiritual matters, so light should be understood literally in spiritual contexts.

### Objection 3
The Apostle says in Ephesians 5:13: "All that is made manifest is light." Manifestation belongs more properly to spiritual than corporeal things, so light does likewise.

### On the contrary
Ambrose states that "Splendor" is said of God metaphorically.

### I answer that
Words can be used either in their original application or extended meaning. "Sight" originally denotes sensory perception but extends to all knowledge types and intellectual understanding. Similarly, light primarily signifies what makes manifest to physical sight, then extends to manifestation of any kind. When taken strictly, light applied to spiritual things is metaphorical. When understood in its extended sense as denoting any manifestation, it properly applies to spiritual matters.

### Reply to Objection 1
The distinction between literal and metaphorical application depends on whether "light" is taken strictly or broadly.

### Reply to Objection 2
Intellectual divine names may be used properly when extended beyond sensory meaning.

### Reply to Objection 3
Manifestation in the extended sense supports proper application to spiritual things.

## Article 2: Whether light is a body?

### Objection 1
Augustine states "light takes the first place among bodies," suggesting light is corporeal.

### Objection 2
Aristotle calls light "a species of fire." Fire is a body, therefore so is light.

### Objection 3
Movement, intersection, and reflection belong properly to bodies. Light exhibits these properties. Dionysius notes rays unite and separate, impossible unless corporeal.

### On the contrary
Two bodies cannot simultaneously occupy the same place. Light and air do occupy the same place. Therefore light is not a body.

### I answer that
Light cannot be a body for three reasons:

First, regarding place: Different bodies require different locations. No two bodies naturally exist simultaneously in the same space, as contiguity requires spatial distinction.

Second, regarding movement: If light were corporeal, its diffusion would constitute local body movement. All local motion requires passing through intervening space progressively. Yet light diffuses instantaneously—even across vast distances like East to West, the entire hemisphere illuminates immediately upon sunrise. Additionally, light's directional indifference (radiating equally circularly or straight) differs from bodies' determinate natural movements.

Third, regarding generation and corruption: If light were body, darkening would corrupt it, with matter receiving new form. Darkness is not itself a body. No apparent matter exists to daily generate such massive light quantities. It would be absurd that a body of such magnitude corrupts from mere luminary absence. If light moved with the sun, why does obscuring a candle darken an entire room without brightening around the candle?

These conclusions contradict both reason and common sense. Light cannot be corporeal.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine understands light as a luminous body in actuality—that is, fire, the noblest element.

### Reply to Objection 2
Aristotle considers light as fire existing in its proper matter, like flame in air or burning coal in earth. His logical examples represent probable opinions rather than definitive claims.

### Reply to Objection 3
These properties apply metaphorically to light. Local movement properties extend metaphorically to all alteration and change, as "distance" derives from spatial remoteness and extends to contraries.

## Article 3: Whether light is a quality?

### Objection 1
Every quality persists when its active cause withdraws—heat remains in water removed from fire. Light disappears when its source withdraws. Therefore light is not a quality.

### Objection 2
Every sensible quality has opposites: cold opposes heat, blackness opposes whiteness. Darkness merely privates light, lacking true contrariety. Light is therefore not a sensible quality.

### Objection 3
Causes exceed effects. Heavenly light causes substantial forms in earthly bodies and renders colors actually visible by bestowing immaterial being. Light constitutes a substantial or spiritual form rather than sensible quality.

### On the contrary
Damascene states light is "a species of quality."

### I answer that
Some held light possesses only intentional being—a color similitude in air—but this fails for two reasons. First, light names the air itself (making it luminous), whereas color does not render air "colored." Second, light produces natural effects: sunrays warm bodies, and mere intentions cannot effect natural changes.

Others claimed light is the sun's substantial form, but this also fails. Substantial forms are not themselves sensory objects; the intellect grasps essence, yet light is visible itself. Impossible also that one thing's substantial form becomes another thing's accidental form, since substantial forms constitute species inherently and everywhere accompany them. Light is not air's substantial form—air persists when light withdraws—so it cannot be the sun's substantial form.

Light must be an active quality consequent upon the sun's substantial form, as heat follows from fire's form. Evidence appears in how different stars' rays produce different effects according to bodies' diverse natures.

### Reply to Objection 1
Quality's manner of reception differs as substantial form reception differs. When matter receives form perfectly, consequent qualities become firm and enduring. When substantial form reception occurs imperfectly—as becoming impressed rather than fully impressed—resulting quality temporarily persists then fades, like heated water returning naturally to coolness.

Light is not produced by matter's transmutation receiving substantial form, nor represents substantial form's inception. Rather, light disappears with its active cause's removal.

### Reply to Objection 2
Light's lacking a contrary is accidental, being the natural quality of the first corporeal change's cause, which itself transcends contrariety.

### Reply to Objection 3
As heat acts instrumentally perfecting fire's form through virtue of substantial form, light acts instrumentally through heavenly bodies' virtue toward producing substantial forms and rendering colors actually visible as the primary sensible body's quality.

## Article 4: Whether the production of light is fittingly assigned to the first day?

### Objection 1
Light, being a quality and thus accident, should receive subordinate rather than first place. Production of light should not occupy the first day.

### Objection 2
Light distinguishes night from day through the sun, made on the fourth day. Therefore light production could not be first day.

### Objection 3
Night and day result from a luminous body's circular movement, attributed to the firmament created second day. Thus light production should not be assigned first day.

### Objection 4
If spiritual light is meant, it dispels darkness. Yet spiritual darkness did not initially exist—demons were originally good. Light production should not be first day work.

### On the contrary
What necessitates day must be made first day. Light is necessary for day's existence. Therefore light was made first day.

### I answer that
Two opinions exist regarding light's production:

Augustine's view: Moses necessarily could not omit spiritual creatures. Therefore "heaven" in Genesis's opening denotes as-yet formless spiritual nature, while "earth" denotes formless corporeal matter. Spiritual nature, being higher dignity, formed first. This spiritual nature's forming is signified by spiritual light's production. Spiritual nature receives form through enlightenment adhering to God's Word.

Other writers' view: Moses purposely omitted spiritual creatures' production.

Basil argues Moses begins from sensible things' temporal beginning while spiritual creation, being pre-existent, is omitted.

Chrysostom suggests Moses addressed an ignorant people attracted only to material things, whom he withdrew from idol service. Speaking of spiritual natures nobler than corporeal creatures would tempt them toward idolatry, as they worshipped even celestial bodies.

Corporeal creation shows multiple formlessness types: "void and empty" earth and "darkness" upon the deep. Two reasons require removing darkness first through light's production:

First: Light is the primary body's quality, so through light the world fittingly received form initially.

Second: Light is a common quality for terrestrial and celestial bodies. Just as knowledge proceeds from general principles, work proceeds universally: living things generate before animals, animals before humans. Divinely wisely, among distinction works, light production holds first place—being the primary body's form and common quality.

Basil adds a third reason: light manifests all other things. A fourth already mentioned: day cannot exist without light, hence first day's appropriate place.

### Reply to Objection 1
Those holding matter's formlessness preceded form temporally must hold matter created initially with substantial forms, later receiving accidental forms, with light holding first place.

### Reply to Objection 2
Some claimed the light was luminous nebula, later returning to matter upon sun's creation. This cannot hold because Genesis records the enduring nature order. What was made then cannot subsequently cease existing.

Others held the nebula continues, closely attached to the sun, becoming indistinguishable. This makes it superfluous, but God's works lack superfluity. Some held the sun's body was formed from this nebula. This impossibly contradicts those believing the sun naturally differs from four elements and is incorruptible—its matter cannot adopt another form.

With Dionysius, light was the sun's formless light—already solar substance possessing illuminative power generally, receiving later special determinative power producing determinate effects. Three distinctions resulted between light and darkness:

First, regarding cause: The sun's substance causes light; earth's opaque nature causes darkness.

Second, regarding place: One hemisphere held light, the other darkness.

Third, regarding time: One hemisphere received light while the other darkness; hence "He called the light day, and the darkness night."

### Reply to Objection 3
Basil argues day and night resulted then from light's expansion and contraction rather than movement. Augustine objects: no reason existed for such vicissitude without humans or animals requiring it. A luminous body's nature seems not to admit light withdrawal while the body remains actually present, except miraculously.

Augustine further notes that in founding nature's original order, we should not seek miracles but natural operation. Heaven's movement is twofold: one movement common to entire heaven causes day and night, beginning first day. Another movement varies with different bodies, causing successive days', months', and years' succession through proper movements.

First day accounts only for the day-night distinction from common celestial movement. Fourth day's further distinction into successive days, seasons, and years from the words "let them be for seasons, and for days, and years" comes from proper movements.

### Reply to Objection 4
Augustine teaches formlessness did not precede forms temporally. Light's production signifies spiritual creatures' formation, not with glory's perfection—in which they were not created—but with grace's perfection, possessed from creation. Light and darkness division denotes spiritual creatures' distinction from other as-yet-formless created things.

If all created things received form simultaneously, darkness means the wicked's spiritual darkness—not existing initially but divinely foreseen as existing—rather than an original condition.
