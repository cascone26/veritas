# Question 105: The Change of Creatures by God

## Article 1: Whether God Can Move Matter Immediately to Form

### Objection 1

The Philosopher proves that "nothing can bring a form into matter except that form which is in matter." Since God is not a form in matter, He cannot cause a form in matter.

### Objection 2

A universal cause must be determined to particular effects by some other cause. But Divine power is the universal cause of all things, so it cannot produce particular forms without intermediate agents.

### Objection 3

Determinate being depends on determinate particular causes. Since a thing's form provides its determinate being, forms must be produced through particular causes, not directly by God.

### On the contrary

Genesis 2:7 states: "God formed man of the slime of the earth," indicating direct divine action.

### I answer that

God can move matter immediately to form because Divine power extends over matter as produced by God. Whatever exists in passive potentiality can be reduced to act by active power extending over that potentiality. A form is simply matter in act, so God's power to reduce matter to act means He can imprint forms directly.

### Reply to Objection 1

An effect resembles its active cause either by species (as man generates man) or by virtual containment (as the sun produces animals). God's power extends to both matter and form, so composite things can be produced by Him directly.

### Reply to Objection 2

This objection assumes God acts by natural necessity. However, God acts through will and intellect, which know particular forms, enabling Him to imprint determinate forms on matter.

### Reply to Objection 3

God ordains secondary causes to determinate effects; therefore He can also produce certain effects directly without other causes.

## Article 2: Whether God Can Move a Body Immediately

### Objection 1

The mover and moved must exist simultaneously with contact between them. Since Dionysius states "There is no contact with God," God cannot move bodies immediately.

### Objection 2

God moves as an unmoved mover and object of desire. Only intellect apprehends Him, not bodies. Therefore God cannot move bodies immediately.

### Objection 3

The Philosopher proves infinite power moves instantaneously, yet bodies cannot move in one instant. Therefore infinite power cannot move bodies immediately.

### On the contrary

God produced bodily movements during the six days of creation, as Genesis 1:9 shows: "Let the waters be gathered together."

### I answer that

God can move any body immediately. Whatever determines effects in creatures can be produced directly by God. Since bodily movements result from forms or tend toward forms, and God can imprint forms directly, He can produce all bodily movements.

### Reply to Objection 1

God touches creatures through virtual contact by moving them, though no creature's natural power reaches Him. Dionysius means God Himself is not touched by creatures.

### Reply to Objection 2

God moves not necessarily as desired and known by what He moves—but as desired and known by Himself, since He acts for His own goodness.

### Reply to Objection 3

The Philosopher proves the first mover's power is not "of bulk," not that instantaneous motion occurs. An intelligent power operates according to fitness, and bodily motion cannot fittingly occur without time.

## Article 3: Whether God Moves the Created Intellect Immediately

### Objection 1

Intellectual action is governed by its subject since it doesn't pass into external matter. Movement by another means the action proceeds from the mover, not the moved. Therefore the intellect is not moved by another.

### Objection 2

The natural intellectual light is sufficient for understanding, so the intellect is not moved by another.

### Objection 3

God exceeds the capacity of our intellect and is not intelligible to us. Therefore God cannot move our intellect.

### On the contrary

Psalm 93:10 states God "teaches man knowledge," and teachers move the intellects of those taught.

### I answer that

As the corporeal mover gives the form that is the principle of movement, that which moves the intellect gives the form that is the principle of intellectual operation. Two principles of intellectual operation exist: the intellectual power itself and the likeness of the thing understood.

God moves the created intellect in both ways. As the First immaterial Being, He is the First intelligent Being, so all intellectual power proceeds from Him. Moreover, since all beings pre-exist in God as First Cause, they exist intelligibly in Him according to His nature. As intelligible types proceed from God to other intellects so they understand, they likewise proceed to creatures for subsistence. Therefore God moves the created intellect by giving intellectual power and impressing intelligible species, while maintaining both in existence.

### Reply to Objection 1

Intellectual operation is performed by the intellect as secondary cause but proceeds from God as first cause, who gives the power to understand.

### Reply to Objection 2

The intellectual light with the likeness of the thing understood is sufficient as a secondary principle but depends on the First Principle.

### Reply to Objection 3

Likenesses God impresses do not suffice for the created intellect to understand His Essence. Yet He moves the created intellect without being fully intelligible to it.

## Article 4: Whether God Can Move the Created Will

### Objection 1

Whatever is moved from without is forced. The will cannot be forced. Therefore it cannot be moved by God.

### Objection 2

God cannot make contradictories true simultaneously. To be voluntarily moved means being moved from within, not by another. Therefore God cannot move the will.

### Objection 3

Movement is attributed to the mover rather than the moved. If God moves the will, voluntary actions would not be imputed to humans for reward or blame.

### On the contrary

Philippians 2:13 states: "It is God who worketh in us both to will and to accomplish."

### I answer that

As the intellect is moved by its object and by the giver of intellectual power, the will is moved by its object (good) and by God who creates the power of willing. The will's potentiality extends to universal good, and only God is the universal good, sufficing to move the will. Only God's power equals the will's capacity extending to universal good.

Moreover, the power of willing is caused by God alone. To will is to incline toward universal good, which belongs to the First Mover to whom the ultimate end is proportionate. Therefore God moves the will both as its end and as the giver of willing power.

### Reply to Objection 1

A thing moved against its natural inclination is forced. But God moving the will does not force it because He gives the will its own natural inclination.

### Reply to Objection 2

To be moved voluntarily means being moved from within by an interior principle. Yet this interior principle may be caused by an exterior principle, so being moved from within does not exclude being moved by another.

### Reply to Objection 3

Since being moved by another does not prevent being moved from within, the act does not lose its basis for merit or demerit.

## Article 5: Whether God Works in Every Agent

### Objection 1

If God works sufficiently in every agent, the creature's operation would be superfluous.

### Objection 2

One action cannot proceed from two sources simultaneously. If the creature's operation is from God operating in it, it cannot simultaneously proceed from the creature itself.

### Objection 3

The maker causes the operation of the made thing by giving it the form whereby it operates. This occurs at creation. Therefore God does not operate further in the operating creature.

### On the contrary

Isaiah 26:12 states: "Lord, Thou hast wrought all our works in us."

### I answer that

Some falsely claim God alone is the ultimate cause of all effects, so fire does not give heat but God does. This is impossible because it removes the order of cause and effect from creatures, implying lack of power in the Creator. Active powers would be bestowed purposelessly if they wrought nothing.

God works in every worker in three ways. First, as the end: since every operation aims at some good, and nothing is truly good except by participating in the Supreme Good, God is the cause of every operation as its end. Second, as the mover of secondary agents: where several agents exist in order, the second acts by virtue of the first. All agents act by virtue of God, making Him the cause of action in every agent. Third, as giver and preserver: God not only moves agents to operation but also gives creatures their forms and preserves them in being. Since the form is within the thing and approaches the First Universal Cause, God is the cause of universal being innermost in all things, working intimately in all.

### Reply to Objection 1

God works sufficiently as First Agent, but this does not make secondary agent operation superfluous.

### Reply to Objection 2

One action cannot proceed from two agents of the same order, but the same action may proceed from a primary and secondary agent.

### Reply to Objection 3

God not only gives things their form but preserves them in existence, applies them to act, and is the end of every action.

## Article 6: Whether God Can Act Outside the Established Natural Order

### Objection 1

Augustine states God "does nothing against nature." What is outside the natural order seems against nature. Therefore God cannot act outside it.

### Objection 2

As the order of justice is from God, so is the order of nature. God cannot act against justice; therefore He cannot act outside the natural order.

### Objection 3

If God acts outside the natural order He established, He appears changeable, which is impossible.

### On the contrary

Augustine states God "sometimes does things contrary to the ordinary course of nature."

### I answer that

Each cause produces a certain order to its effects. A higher cause is not subject to a lower order; rather, the lower is subject to the higher. Considering the order depending on the First Cause, God cannot act against it, as this would oppose His foreknowledge, will, or goodness. But considering the order depending on secondary causes, God can act outside such order. He is not subject to secondary causes' order; rather, this order proceeds from Him by His will's choice. He could have created another order. Augustine says: "God acts against the wonted course of nature, but by no means against the supreme law."

### Reply to Objection 1

Something may happen outside the natural order in two ways. It may result from an agent not giving the natural inclination, which would be against nature. Or it results from the agent on whom the inclination depends, which is not against nature. Since God gave things their natural order, His acting outside it is not against nature.

### Reply to Objection 2

The order of justice arises by relation to the First Cause, the rule of all justice. Therefore God cannot act against such order.

### Reply to Objection 3

God fixed the natural order while reserving to Himself to act otherwise when He chooses. His acting outside this order does not change Him.

## Article 7: Whether Whatever God Does Outside the Natural Order Is Miraculous

### Objection 1

Creation, creation of souls, and justification of the unrighteous are done outside the natural order but are not called miracles.

### Objection 2

Augustine defines miracles as difficult, rare, surpassing nature's faculty, and beyond hope. But recovery from sickness is not arduous and occurs frequently. Therefore not all things outside nature's order are miracles.

### Objection 3

"Miracle" derives from admiration concerning manifest things. Some things outside nature's order are not manifest to senses. Therefore not all such things are miraculous.

### On the contrary

Augustine states: "Where God does anything against that order of nature which we know and are accustomed to observe, we call it a miracle."

### I answer that

"Miracle" derives from admiration arising when an effect is manifest but its cause is hidden. A cause may be known to some but unknown to others—an eclipse amazes a rustic but not an astronomer. A miracle has a cause absolutely hidden from all: God. Therefore those things God does outside causes we know are called miracles.

### Reply to Objection 1

Creation and justification are not properly miracles because they are not of a nature to proceed from any other cause, so they do not occur outside the natural order's scope.

### Reply to Objection 2

A thing is called arduous because it surpasses nature's faculty. It is called unusual because it falls outside the usual natural course. A miracle goes beyond hope "of nature," not "of grace."

### Reply to Objection 3

The apostles' knowledge, though not manifest in itself, was made manifest in its effects, showing it to be wonderful.

## Article 8: Whether One Miracle Is Greater Than Another

### Objection 1

Augustine states: "In miraculous deeds, the whole measure of the deed is the power of the doer." Since all miracles are done by God's power, one is not greater than another.

### Objection 2

God's power is infinite. The infinite exceeds the finite beyond all proportion, so no greater reason exists to wonder at one effect rather than another.

### On the contrary

Christ states (John 14:12): "Greater than these shall he do."

### I answer that

A miracle is called great not by comparison with Divine power but by comparison with nature's power which it surpasses. The more nature's power is surpassed, the greater the miracle.

Nature's power is surpassed in three ways. First, in the deed's substance: two bodies occupying the same place, the sun moving backward, a human body glorified—things nature absolutely cannot do. These rank highest.

Second, not in the deed but in what receives it: raising the dead, giving sight to the blind. Nature can give life but not to the dead. These rank second.

Third, in the measure and order: sudden healing without treatment, air suddenly condensing into rain at prayer. These rank lowest. Each kind has various degrees according to how nature's power is surpassed.
