# Question 66: The Order of Creation Towards Distinction

## Article 1: Whether formlessness of created matter preceded in time its formation?

### Objection 1
Genesis 1:2 states "The earth was void and empty," which Augustine interprets as signifying the formlessness of matter. Therefore, matter existed in a formless state before receiving form.

### Objection 2
Nature in its operations imitates God as a secondary cause imitates a first cause. In natural processes, formlessness precedes form temporally. Thus, this must also occur in God's creative work.

### Objection 3
God can cause accidents to exist without substance, as in the Eucharist. Since matter is superior to accidents, God could similarly cause matter to exist without form.

### On the contrary
An imperfect effect demonstrates imperfection in the agent. God is absolutely perfect, and His works are perfect (Deuteronomy 32:4). Therefore creation was never formless. Furthermore, formation involves distinction, which opposes confusion and formlessness. If formlessness preceded temporally, chaos would have existed initially in creation.

### I answer that
Holy writers disagree on this question. Augustine maintains that formlessness preceded only in origin or the order of nature, not in time. Basil, Ambrose, and Chrysostom hold that formlessness temporally preceded formation. However, these opinions differ less than they appear.

Augustine understands formlessness as the complete absence of all form. If formless matter preceded in duration, it would already exist; yet duration implies actual being, and act itself is form. This creates a logical contradiction.

Moreover, matter could not have possessed some common form with different forms supervening later—this was the error of ancient natural philosophers who treated prime matter as some actual corporeal thing (fire, air, or water). Such a view would reduce creation to mere alteration, not generation.

Therefore, prime matter was not created entirely formless or under any single common form, but under distinct forms. If formlessness refers to the condition of prime matter in itself, this formlessness preceded only in origin and nature, not in time—as potentiality precedes act and parts precede wholes.

Other holy writers understand formlessness differently: not as absence of all form, but as absence of the beauty and comeliness now apparent in creation. They say corporeal matter's formlessness temporally preceded its formation. From Genesis, three kinds of beauty were wanting: (1) light was absent from the transparent body called heaven, indicated by "darkness was upon the face of the deep"; (2) earth lacked beauty both from being concealed beneath water ("the earth was void") and from lacking adornment by plants ("empty" or "shapeless").

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine and other writers interpret "earth" differently. Augustine holds that "earth" and "water" signify primary matter itself, which cannot be made intelligible to ignorant people except through similitude to familiar objects. Matter resembles earth in being susceptible to form and water in its adaptability to various forms. The earth is called "void and empty" because matter is "known by means of form; hence, considered in itself, it is called 'invisible'" or "void."

Other holy writers understand "earth" as the element of earth itself, which they hold was formless as described.

### Reply to Objection 2
Nature produces actual effects from potential being; consequently, in natural operations, potentiality must temporally precede act and formlessness must precede form. However, God produces actual being from nothing and can therefore produce a perfect thing instantaneously according to His infinite power.

### Reply to Objection 3
Accident, as a form, is a type of act, whereas matter is essentially potential being. Therefore, it is more repugnant that matter exist in act without form than for accident to exist without subject.

## Article 2: Whether the formless matter of all corporeal things is the same?

### Objection 1
Augustine states: "I find two things Thou hast made, one formed, the other formless," referring to the invisible and shapeless earth as designating the matter of all corporeal things. Therefore, all corporeal matter is identical.

### Objection 2
The Philosopher states that things of one genus are one in matter. All corporeal things belong to the same genus of body. Therefore, all bodies share identical matter.

### Objection 3
Different acts befit different potentialities, while the same act befits the same potentiality. All bodies possess the same form of corporeity. Therefore, all bodies possess identical matter.

### Objection 4
Matter considered in itself exists only in potentiality. Distinction derives from form. Therefore, matter considered in itself is the same in all corporeal things.

### On the contrary
Things whose matter is identical are mutually interchangeable and mutually active or passive. However, heavenly and earthly bodies do not mutually act or suffer. Therefore, their matter is not identical.

### I answer that
Philosophers have disagreed on this question. Plato and pre-Aristotelian philosophers held that all bodies consist of four elements sharing one common matter, evident from their mutual generation and corruption. However, incorruptibility was attributed by Plato not to matter's condition but to God's will, which sustains heavenly bodies against their natural dissolution.

Aristotle disproved this by demonstrating that heavenly bodies possess natural movements different from elements. Circular motion, proper to heavenly bodies, involves no contrariety, whereas elemental movements are mutually opposite (upward and downward). Since generation and corruption arise from contraries, elements are corruptible while heavenly bodies are incorruptible, indicating their different natures.

Despite this difference, Avicebron taught unity of matter in all bodies, arguing from unity of form. If corporeity were one form with other forms supervening, this argument would hold necessarily—this form would inhere immutably, making all bodies incorruptible. Corruption would then be merely accidental. But if no form remains subsisting beneath generation and corruption in corruptible bodies, it necessarily follows that corruptible and incorruptible bodies lack identical matter.

Matter in itself exists in potentiality toward form. When receiving any single form, it remains in potentiality toward all others—even when forms are hierarchically perfect. Potentiality itself is indifferent to perfection and imperfection; under an imperfect form, matter remains potential to perfect forms and vice versa. Therefore, if matter existed under an incorruptible body's form while remaining potential to a corruptible body's form (which it does not actually possess), it would simultaneously possess form and privation of form. Such a condition implies corruptibility.

Thus, bodies naturally corruptible and those naturally incorruptible cannot possess identical matter.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine follows Plato's opinion, which does not admit a fifth essence. Alternatively, formless matter is one with the unity of order, as all bodies form one order of corporeal creatures.

### Reply to Objection 2
In the physical sense, corruptible and incorruptible things are not in the same genus due to their different modes of potentiality. However, logically considered, all bodies form one genus since they share the notion of corporeity.

### Reply to Objection 3
The form of corporeity is not identical in all bodies but consists of the various forms distinguishing them.

### Reply to Objection 4
As potentiality is directed toward act, potential beings are differentiated by their different acts. Therefore, celestial matter differs from elemental matter because celestial matter is not in potentiality to elemental form.

## Article 3: Whether the empyrean heaven was created contemporaneously with formless matter?

### Objection 1
If the empyrean heaven exists at all, it must be a sensible body. All sensible bodies are movable, yet the empyrean heaven is not movable—its movement would be ascertained by visible bodies' movements, which does not occur. Therefore, the empyrean heaven was not created with formless matter.

### Objection 2
Augustine states that lower bodies are governed by higher bodies in a certain order. If the empyrean heaven is the highest body, it must exercise influence on inferior bodies. This seems impossible, especially if it lacks movement; one body cannot move another without itself being moved. Therefore, the empyrean heaven was not created simultaneously with formless matter.

### Objection 3
If the empyrean heaven is the place of contemplation rather than ordained to natural effects, Augustine teaches that "in apprehending eternal things, we are not of this world," showing contemplation lifts the mind above worldly things. Therefore, corporeal place cannot be the seat of contemplation.

### Objection 4
Among heavenly bodies exists one partly transparent and partly luminous (the sidereal heaven) and another wholly transparent (the crystalline heaven). If a still higher heaven existed, it must be wholly luminous. But then air would be constantly illuminated and darkness would not exist. Therefore, the empyrean heaven was not created with formless matter.

### On the contrary
Strabus says that in "In the beginning God created heaven and earth," heaven denotes not the visible firmament but the empyrean or fiery heaven.

### I answer that
The empyrean heaven rests on the authority of Strabus, Bede, and Basil, who agree that it is the place of the blessed. Strabus and Bede hold that angels filled it immediately upon its creation; Basil states that just as the lost are driven into darkness, worthy deeds receive reward in light beyond this world where the just obtain rest. However, they differ in their reasoning.

A better reason derives from the future state of glory. Two-fold glory is expected: spiritual and corporeal, not only in the glorified human body but in the renewed universe. Spiritual glory began with the world through the angels' blessedness. It was fitting that from the beginning some corporeal glory commence in something corporeal, free from the start from corruption's servitude and change, wholly luminous—as the whole bodily creation after resurrection is expected to be. The empyrean is called "fiery" from its brightness, not heat.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sensible corporeal things are currently movable because bodily creatures' multiplication through movement maintains the elements. When glory is finally consummated, bodily movement ceases. Such was the empyrean's condition from the beginning.

### Reply to Objection 2
It is probable that the empyrean heaven, having glory as its ordained end, does not influence inferior bodies of another order directed only to natural ends. Yet it seems more probable it influences moved bodies while remaining motionless—as the Church's highest-ranking angels influence lower-ranking angels who serve as messengers, though themselves unsent, as Dionysius teaches.

### Reply to Objection 3
Corporeal place is assigned to contemplation as congruous, not necessary, so outward splendor corresponds to inner splendor. Hence Basil states: "The ministering spirit could not live in darkness but made his habitual dwelling in light and joy."

### Reply to Objection 4
The empyrean may possess light not condensed like the sun's rays but of subtler nature. Or it may possess glory's brightness, differing from mere natural brightness.

## Article 4: Whether time was created simultaneously with formless matter?

### Objection 1
Augustine states: "I find two things that Thou didst create before time was, the primary corporeal matter, and the angelic nature." Therefore, time was not created with formless matter.

### Objection 2
Time is divided by day and night. In the beginning, neither existed—these began when "God divided the light from the darkness." Therefore, time did not initially exist.

### Objection 3
Time measures the firmament's movement, and the firmament was made on the second day. Therefore, time did not exist initially.

### Objection 4
Movement precedes time and should therefore be numbered among first-created things rather than time itself.

### Objection 5
As time is the extrinsic measure of created things, so is place. Therefore, place must equally be reckoned among first-created things.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "Both spiritual and corporeal creatures were created at the beginning of time."

### I answer that
It is commonly said that first-created things were four: angelic nature, the empyrean heaven, formless corporeal matter, and time. However, this is not Augustine's opinion. He specifies only two as first-created: angelic nature and corporeal matter, mentioning no empyrean heaven. These two precede formation by nature only, not by duration; therefore, as they precede formation, they precede movement and time. Thus, time cannot be included among them.

The enumeration above represents other holy writers who hold that formlessness temporally preceded formation, requiring time's existence as duration's measure; otherwise, no such measure would exist.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine's teaching rests on the opinion that angelic nature and formless matter precede time by origin or nature.

### Reply to Objection 2
As some holy writers hold that matter was somewhat formless before receiving its full form, time was in some manner formless before fully formed and distinguished into day and night.

### Reply to Objection 3
If the firmament's movement did not commence immediately from the beginning, preceding time would measure not the firmament's movement but the first movement of any kind. It is accidental to time to measure the firmament's movement, insofar as this is the first movement. If another movement was first, time would measure it—everything being measured by the first of its kind.

### Reply to Objection 4
Among first-created things are those having general relationship to all things. Therefore, time must be included as having the nature of a common measure, but not movement, which relates only to the movable subject.

### Reply to Objection 5
Place is implied as existing in the empyrean heaven, the universe's boundary. Since place pertains to permanent things, it was created at once in its totality. However, time, being non-permanent, was created in its beginning—as we cannot grasp any part of time except the "now."
