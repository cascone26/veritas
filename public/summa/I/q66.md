# Question 66: The Order of Creation Towards Distinction

## Article 1: Whether formlessness of created matter preceded in time its formation?

### Objection 1
Genesis 1:2 describes the earth as "void and empty" or "invisible and shapeless," which Augustine interprets as the formlessness of matter. Therefore, matter existed formless before receiving form.

### Objection 2
Nature imitates God's working as a secondary cause imitates a first cause. In nature's operations, formlessness precedes form in time, so it should do likewise in Divine creation.

### Objection 3
Matter ranks higher than accident since matter is part of substance. God can cause accidents to exist without substance (as in the Eucharist), so He could cause matter to exist without form.

### On the contrary
An imperfect effect proves imperfection in the agent. God is absolutely perfect, and Scripture states "The works of God are perfect." Therefore creation was never formless. Additionally, formation of corporeal creatures occurred through distinction, which opposes the confusion implied by formlessness.

### I answer that
Holy writers differ on this question. Augustine believes formlessness preceded only in origin or nature, not in time. Others like Basil, Ambrose, and Chrysostom hold formlessness preceded temporally. These positions differ less than they appear.

Augustine understands formlessness as complete absence of form. If formless matter preceded in duration, it already existed; yet existence implies act, and act itself is form. To claim matter existed without form contradicts terms. Matter could not possess a common form with subsequent particular forms supervening, as this would suggest matter was itself some corporeal thing in act—the error of ancient philosophers claiming matter was fire, air, or water.

If matter possessed any preceding form conferring substantial being, subsequent forms would produce only particular beings, not being itself, making them merely accidental. This yields alteration, not generation.

Therefore, primary matter was created not formless nor under one common form, but under distinct forms. Formlessness—understood as primary matter's intrinsic condition—did not precede temporally its formation or distinction, but only in origin and nature, as Augustine teaches. Potentiality precedes actuality similarly; parts precede wholes.

Other holy writers understand formlessness differently: not as absence of all form, but as lacking the beauty and comeliness now apparent in creation. By this meaning, formlessness preceded formation in duration. Thus Augustine agrees with them in certain respects, disagreeing in others.

Scripture indicates three deficiencies in corporeal creatures' beauty: First, heavenly bodies lacked light, signified by "darkness upon the face of the deep." Second, earth lacked beauty from its watery covering hiding it ("the earth was void"). Third, earth lacked beauty from vegetation and plants ("empty" or "unadorned"). The heaven's formlessness is indicated by darkness; the earth's by void and emptiness.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine and others interpret "earth" differently in this passage. Augustine holds "earth" and "water" signify primary matter itself, since Moses had to convey this concept through familiar objects. Matter resembles earth as susceptible to form and water for adaptability to varied forms. Thus "void and empty" means matter is known only through form; considered alone, it appears "invisible" or "void," with potentiality completed by form.

Other writers understand "earth" as the element itself, interpreting formlessness according to their different understanding.

### Reply to Objection 2
Nature produces actual effects from potential being; consequently in natural operations, potentiality must precede actuality temporally, and formlessness must precede form. God, however, produces being in act from nothing and can thereby create perfect things instantaneously according to His power.

### Reply to Objection 3
Accident, as form, constitutes a kind of act, whereas matter is essentially being in potentiality. Therefore, matter existing in act without form is more contradictory than accident existing without subject.

## Article 2: Whether the formless matter of all corporeal things is the same?

### Objection 1
Augustine states: "I find two things that Thou didst create before time was, the primary corporeal matter, and the angelic nature." Therefore, all corporeal matter is identical.

### Objection 2
The Philosopher teaches: "Things that are one in genus are one in matter." All corporeal things belong to the same genus of body. Therefore, all bodies share identical matter.

### Objection 3
Different acts befit different potentialities; the same act befits the same potentiality. All bodies possess the same form, corporeity. Therefore, all bodies possess identical matter.

### Objection 4
Matter, considered in itself, exists only in potentiality. Distinction derives from form. Therefore matter itself is identical in all corporeal things.

### On the contrary
Things with identical matter are mutually interchangeable and mutually active or passive. Heavenly and earthly bodies do not act upon each other mutually. Therefore their matter is not identical.

### I answer that
Philosophers have held different opinions. Plato and those preceding Aristotle maintained all bodies derive from the four elements' nature. Since the four elements possess common matter—proved by their mutual generation and corruption—all bodies' matter is identical. Incorruptibility of certain bodies Plato attributed not to matter's condition but to God's will, declaring heavenly bodies subject to dissolution by nature yet indissoluble by His will.

Aristotle disproves this through bodies' natural movements. Heavenly bodies move circularly, differing from elemental movements (upward and downward), implying different natures. Circular motion lacks contraries, whereas elemental movements oppose each other. Since generation and corruption arise from contraries, elements are corruptible while heavenly bodies are incorruptible.

Despite this distinction, Avicebron taught matter's unity in all bodies, arguing from form's unity. If corporeity were one form with other distinguishing forms supervening, this argument would hold necessarily; that form would inhere immutably in matter, making all bodies incorruptible. Corruption would be merely accidental through successive forms' disappearance—partial corruption, not absolute, since actual being would subsist beneath transient form. This repeats ancient philosophers' error.

However, supposing no form subsists beneath generation and corruption in corruptible bodies, corruptible and incorruptible bodies' matter necessarily differs. Matter itself exists in potentiality to form. Considered in itself, it remains in potentiality to all forms it commonly receives. Upon receiving any form, it becomes actual regarding that form only, remaining in potentiality to all others—true even when some forms are more perfect and contain others virtually within themselves. Potentiality itself is indifferent to perfection and imperfection, so under an imperfect form it remains in potentiality to perfect forms and vice versa.

Matter existing under incorruptible body form would remain in potentiality to corruptible body form; not actually possessing the latter, it would possess both form and form's privation. Privation constitutes want of form in what is in potentiality thereto. This condition implies corruptibility. Therefore, bodies naturally corruptible and naturally incorruptible cannot possess identical matter.

Neither can we say, as Averroes imagines, that a heavenly body itself is the heaven's matter—in potentiality regarding place though not being—with its form as a separate substance united as motive force. No being in act exists unless it is wholly act and form or possesses act or form. Setting aside the separate substance as motive power, if the heavenly body lacks form (composition of form and form's subject), it must be entirely form and act. Every such thing is actually understood, which heavenly bodies are not—they are sensible. Therefore, heavenly bodies' matter, considered in itself, is in potentiality to that form alone which it actually possesses. Whether this is soul or otherwise matters not. That form perfects this matter such that no potentiality to being remains—only to place, as Aristotle teaches. Thus heavenly bodies' and elements' matter differs, except by analogy, agreeing only in potentiality's character.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine follows Plato's opinion rejecting a fifth essence. Alternatively, formless matter possesses unity of order, as all bodies unite in corporeal creatures' order.

### Reply to Objection 2
In physical sense, corruptible and incorruptible things lack common genus due to different potentiality modes. Logically, however, one genus encompasses all bodies, included in corporeity's single notion.

### Reply to Objection 3
Corporeity's form is not identical in all bodies; it is nothing other than the various forms distinguishing bodies, as stated previously.

### Reply to Objection 4
Potentiality directs toward act; potential beings are differentiated by their different acts, as sight by color, hearing by sound. Therefore, celestial bodies' matter differs from elemental matter because celestial matter is not in potentiality to elemental form.

## Article 3: Whether the empyrean heaven was created contemporaneously with formless matter?

### Objection 1
If the empyrean heaven exists, it must be a sensible body. All sensible bodies are movable; the empyrean heaven is not movable, since its movement would be evident from visible bodies' movement—which is not the case. Therefore, the empyrean heaven was not created contemporaneously with formless matter.

### Objection 2
Augustine states: "The lower bodies are governed by the higher in a certain order." If the empyrean heaven is highest, it must influence bodies below it. Yet this seems untrue, especially since it presumably lacks movement; one body cannot move another unless itself moved. Therefore, the empyrean heaven was not created with formless matter.

### Objection 3
If the empyrean heaven is contemplation's place rather than ordained to natural effects, yet Augustine teaches: "In so far as we mentally apprehend eternal things, so far are we not of this world." Contemplation elevates the mind above worldly things. Therefore, corporeal place cannot be contemplation's seat.

### Objection 4
Among heavenly bodies exists a partly transparent, partly luminous body—the sidereal heaven. There exists a wholly transparent body—the aqueous or crystalline heaven. If a higher heaven exists, it must be wholly luminous. But then air would be constantly illuminated; there would be no night. Therefore, the empyrean heaven was not created with formless matter.

### On the contrary
Strabus states that in "In the beginning God created heaven and earth," heaven denotes not the visible firmament but the empyrean or fiery heaven.

### I answer that
The empyrean heaven rests on Strabus's, Bede's, and Basil's authority, all agreeing it is the blessed's place. Strabus and Bede say it was filled with angels immediately upon creation; Basil states: "The ministering spirit could not live in darkness but made his habitual dwelling in light and joy."

They differ in reasoning. Strabus and Bede teach an empyrean heaven exists because the firmament (understood as the sidereal heaven) was made on the second day, not the beginning. Basil's reason is that otherwise God would seem to make darkness His first work, falsely as the Manicheans claim, calling the Old Testament God the God of darkness.

These reasons lack cogency. The firmament question finds different solutions in Augustine and other writers. Augustine explains darkness as formlessness preceding form by origin, not duration. Others note darkness is no creature but light's privation, proving Divine wisdom that creatures from nothing were produced first imperfectly, then brought to perfection.

A better reason derives from future glory's state. Two-fold glory is awaited: spiritual and corporeal, not only in glorified human bodies but in the renewed world. Spiritual glory began with the world in the angels' blessedness, equality with which is promised saints. Fittingly, from the beginning some corporeal glory's beginning should exist—something corporeal, free from corruption and change's servitude, wholly luminous, as the entire bodily creation after the Resurrection is expected to be. That heaven is called empyrean (fiery) from brightness, not heat.

Augustine notes Porphyry distinguished demons from angels by placing demons in air and angels in ether or empyrean. Porphyry, a Platonist, held the sidereal heaven as fiery, calling it empyrean or ethereal, taking ethereal to denote flame's burning, not as Aristotle understands it (movement's swiftness). This clarification prevents supposing Augustine maintained an empyrean heaven as modern writers understand it.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sensible corporeal things are presently movable; through corporeal creatures' movement, element multiplication is secured. When glory is finally consummated, bodies' movement ceases. Such must have been the empyrean heaven's condition from the beginning.

### Reply to Objection 2
Probably, as some assert, the empyrean heaven, ordained toward glory's state, does not influence inferior bodies of another order directed only to natural ends. Yet it seems more probable that it influences bodies moved though itself motionless, as highest-ranking angels influence lower-ranking who act as messengers though not sent, as Dionysius teaches. Thus the empyrean's influence upon the first heaven (moved) produces therein not something transient from movement but something fixed and stable—like conservation power or causation, pertaining to dignity.

### Reply to Objection 3
Corporeal place is assigned to contemplation as congruous, not necessary, that external splendor correspond to internal state. Hence Basil states: "The ministering spirit could not live in darkness but made his habitual dwelling in light and joy."

### Reply to Objection 4
Basil teaches: "It is certain the heaven was created spherical, of dense body, sufficiently strong to separate what is outside it from what it encloses. Therefore it darkens the external region, with the light by which itself is lit shut out from that region."

Since the firmament's body, though solid, is transparent (not excluding light, as we see stars through intervening heavens), the empyrean may possess light—not condensed emitting rays like the sun, but of subtler nature. Or it may possess glory's brightness, differing from mere natural brightness.

## Article 4: Whether time was created simultaneously with formless matter?

### Objection 1
Augustine states: "I find two things that Thou didst create before time was, the primary corporeal matter, and the angelic nature." Therefore, time was not created with formless matter.

### Objection 2
Time is divided by day and night. In the beginning there was neither, for these began when "God divided the light from the darkness." Therefore, time did not exist initially.

### Objection 3
Time measures the firmament's movement; the firmament was made on the second day. Therefore, time did not exist initially.

### Objection 4
Movement precedes time and should therefore be reckoned among first-created things rather than time.

### Objection 5
As time is created things' extrinsic measure, so is place. Therefore place, as truly as time, should be reckoned among first-created things.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "Both spiritual and corporeal creatures were created at the beginning of time."

### I answer that
Commonly, four things are said to be first-created: the angelic nature, the empyrean heaven, formless corporeal matter, and time. Augustine's opinion differs. He specifies only two things as first-created: the angelic nature and corporeal matter, mentioning no empyrean heaven. These two precede formation by nature only, not duration; therefore, as they precede formation, they precede movement and time. Time cannot be included among them.

The enumeration above represents other holy writers holding that matter's formlessness preceded its form in duration, this view necessitating time's existence as duration's measure; otherwise no such measure would exist.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine's teaching rests on the opinion that angelic nature and formless matter precede time by origin or nature.

### Reply to Objection 2
As matter was somewhat formless before receiving full form in some writers' opinion, time was somewhat formless before being fully formed and distinguished into day and night.

### Reply to Objection 3
If the firmament's movement did not begin immediately from the beginning, preceding time would measure not the firmament's movement but whatever first movement existed. It is accidental to time to measure the firmament's movement, occurring only as this is the first movement. If another movement was first, time would measure it, since everything is measured by its kind's first. Moreover, from the beginning some movement must be granted—at least in the angelic mind's concepts and affections' succession. Movement without time cannot be conceived, since time is "the measure of priority and succession in movement."

### Reply to Objection 4
Among first-created things are reckoned those having general relationship to things. Time must therefore be included, possessing a common measure's nature; not movement, which relates only to movable subjects.

### Reply to Objection 5
Place is implied as existing in the empyrean heaven, the universe's boundary. Since place concerns permanent things, it was created entirely at once. Time, being non-permanent, was created in its beginning: as now, we lay hold of only time's "now."
