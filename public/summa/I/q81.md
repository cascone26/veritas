# Question 81: The Power of Sensuality

## Article 1: Whether Sensuality is Only Appetitive

### Objection 1
Augustine suggests that sensual movement directed toward bodily senses appears cognitive since the senses themselves are apprehensive powers, implying sensuality involves cognition.

### Objection 2
Augustine divides sensuality against higher and lower reason, which are knowledge faculties. This suggests sensuality belongs to the same category as knowledge.

### Objection 3
In human temptation, sensuality represents the serpent, which presented information and proposed sin—functions of cognitive powers.

### On the contrary
"Sensuality is defined as the appetite of things belonging to the body."

### I answer that
Sensuality derives its name from sensual movement. While apprehension completes itself when the object exists within the one apprehending, appetite's operation consists in the desiring subject being drawn toward the desirable thing. Apprehension resembles rest; appetite resembles movement. Therefore, sensuality properly names the sensitive appetite, not cognitive capacity.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sensual movement is directed toward bodily senses in the sense that it inclines toward objects perceived through sensation. The bodily senses serve as preliminary to sensuality rather than comprising it.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sensuality shares with reason the quality of being motive powers. Both apprehensive and appetitive faculties move the soul, allowing their common division.

### Reply to Objection 3
The serpent not only informed but incited toward sin's commission. Sensuality specifically corresponds to this incitement aspect.

## Article 2: Whether Sensitive Appetite Divides into Irascible and Concupiscible as Distinct Powers

### Objection 1
A single power regards both sides of contraries—sight perceives both black and white. Since suitable and harmful are contrary, and concupiscible addresses the suitable while irascible addresses the harmful, these must be one power.

### Objection 2
Sensitive appetite concerns only sensible suitability, which is the concupiscible's object. Therefore, no distinct appetite differs from the concupiscible.

### Objection 3
Hatred belongs to the irascible part, yet hatred opposes love, which resides in the concupiscible. Therefore these powers are identical.

### On the contrary
Gregory of Nyssa and Damascene assign two distinct parts to sensitive appetite: the irascible and concupiscible.

### I answer that
Sensitive appetite constitutes one generic power called sensuality but divides into two species: irascible and concupiscible. Natural corruptible things require not merely inclination toward suitable goods and away from harmful ones, but also resistance against destructive contrary forces hindering acquisition of suitable things.

The concupiscible simply inclines the soul toward sensually suitable things and away from harmful ones. The irascible power enables resistance against attacks hindering suitability or inflicting harm, addressing arduous objects requiring overcoming obstacles.

These powers cannot reduce to one principle. The soul sometimes pursues unpleasant things contrary to concupiscible appetite's inclination, following irascible appetite's impulse to combat obstacles. Consequently, irascible and concupiscible passions counteract each other: aroused concupiscence diminishes anger; aroused anger diminishes concupiscence. The irascible serves as defender of the concupiscible, rising against what hinders desired acquisition or causes harm. All irascible passions originate from concupiscible passions and terminate in them.

### Reply to Objection 1
Concupiscible power addresses both suitable and unsuitable things. Irascible power specifically addresses resisting onslaught from unsuitable things.

### Reply to Objection 2
As the apprehensive part contains estimative power perceiving imperceptible things, sensitive appetite contains an appetitive power regarding things as suitable for self-defense rather than sensible pleasure. This is the irascible power.

### Reply to Objection 3
Hatred simply belongs to concupiscible appetite. Through resulting strife from hatred, it may pertain to irascible appetite.

## Article 3: Whether Irascible and Concupiscible Appetites Obey Reason

### Objection 1
Irascible and concupiscible constitute sensuality, which does not obey reason—Augustine identifies it with the serpent.

### Objection 2
What obeys something does not resist it. Yet these appetites resist reason, as Paul declares: "I see another law in my members fighting against the law of my mind."

### Objection 3
As appetitive power is inferior to reason, so is sensitive power. Sensitive faculties disobey reason—we cannot see or hear at will. Similarly, sensitive appetitive powers disobey reason.

### On the contrary
Damascene states that "the part of the soul which is obedient and amenable to reason is divided into concupiscence and anger."

### I answer that
Irascible and concupiscible powers obey the higher part containing intellect, reason, and will in two ways: regarding reason and regarding will.

Concerning reason: in animals, sensitive appetite naturally responds to estimative power. In humans, the cogitative power replaces estimation, functioning as particular reason comparing individual intentions. Human sensitive appetite thus naturally responds to particular reason, which itself naturally follows universal reason. Therefore universal reason directs sensitive appetite. One experiences this internally: applying universal considerations modifies or excites anger, fear, and similar responses.

Regarding will: sensitive appetite subjects itself to will in execution through motive power. In animals, movement immediately follows concupiscible and irascible appetites. Humans, however, await the will's command. Where multiple motive powers exist hierarchically, the second moves only by the first's virtue.

The Philosopher distinguishes despotic and politic rule. The soul dominates the body despotically—its members cannot resist. The intellect rules appetite through politic and royal power over free subjects who can resist. This resistance demonstrates the politic nature of reason's rule, not absence of obedience.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sensuality represents the serpent regarding its proper sensitive character. However, irascible and concupiscible denominate sensitive appetite regarding its acts, toward which reason directs them.

### Reply to Objection 2
Body parts cannot resist the soul; sensitive appetite can resist reason because it responds naturally to imagination and sensation alongside reason's guidance.

### Reply to Objection 3
External senses require external sensible objects for action, whose presence reason does not govern. Interior appetitive and apprehensive powers require no exterior objects and thus subject themselves to reason's command.
