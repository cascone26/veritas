# Question 107: The Speech of the Angels

## Article 1: Whether One Angel Speaks to Another

### Objection 1

Gregory states that in the resurrection "each one's body will not hide his mind from his fellows." Since angels' minds are not hidden from each other, speech seems unnecessary among them.

### Objection 2

Speech comes in two forms: interior and exterior. Exterior speech requires sensible signs like voice or gesture, which cannot apply to incorporeal angels.

### Objection 3

A speaker must draw the listener's attention through sensible signs. Since angels lack such means, one angel cannot speak to another.

### On the contrary

The Apostle states: "If I speak with the tongues of men and of angels" (1 Corinthians 13:1).

### I answer that

Angels do speak, though not through bodily speech. When the will directs an intelligible object from habitual knowledge to actual consideration, a person speaks to himself. An angel speaks to another when, through his will, he orders his mental concept to become known to another. This ordering constitutes angelic speech: making one's intellectual concept known to another.

### Reply to Objection 1

Our mental concepts are hidden by two obstacles. First, the will can retain or direct the concept outwardly—only God can see another's mind directly (1 Corinthians 2:11). Second, the body prevents knowledge even when the will directs the concept externally. Angels face no bodily impediments and can immediately manifest their concepts to one another.

### Reply to Objection 2

External voiced speech is necessary only because of bodily obstacles. Angels require only interior speech, which includes directing the mental concept to another's knowledge through the will.

### Reply to Objection 3

Good angels always see each other in the Word and thus perceive what is directed toward them. The intellect can be aroused by intelligible objects just as sensation is aroused by sensible objects.

## Article 2: Whether the Inferior Angel Speaks to the Superior

### Objection 1

A gloss on 1 Corinthians 13:1 states that angelic speech is "an enlightenment whereby the superior enlightens the inferior." Since inferiors never enlighten superiors, they cannot speak to superiors.

### Objection 2

To enlighten means to acquaint one with what another knows, which is identical to speaking. Therefore, if inferiors cannot enlighten superiors, neither can they speak to them.

### Objection 3

Gregory teaches that "God speaks to the angels by the very fact that He shows to their hearts His hidden and invisible things," which is enlightenment. Every angelic speech would thus be enlightenment.

### On the contrary

According to Dionysius's exposition, inferior angels said to superior ones: "Who is this King of Glory?"

### I answer that

All angelic enlightenment constitutes speech, but not all speech constitutes enlightenment. A mental concept may derive from two principles: God (primal truth) or the created will. Manifestation depending on divine truth is both speech and enlightenment. Manifestation depending on created will is speech but not enlightenment. The created will, not being light or truth's rule, cannot enlighten.

Regarding the will as principle, the one willing is supreme. Thus both superiors speak to inferiors and inferiors speak to superiors.

### Reply to Objection 1

These objections are answered by the distinction between enlightenment and non-enlightening speech.

### Reply to Objection 2

Same as above.

### Reply to Objection 3

All God's speech to angels is enlightenment because God's will is truth's rule. The same does not apply to angels' wills.

## Article 3: Whether an Angel Speaks to God

### Objection 1

Speech makes something known to another. Angels cannot make anything known to God, who knows all things. Therefore, angels do not speak to God.

### Objection 2

To speak is to order the mental concept to another. Angels eternally order their concepts to God. If they speak to Him, they would always speak to God, which seems contradictory.

### On the contrary

Zechariah 1:12 records the angel saying: "O Lord of hosts, how long wilt Thou not have mercy on Jerusalem?"

### I answer that

Angels speak to God not by giving anything but by ordering themselves to receive from Him—consulting the Divine will about duties or marveling at Divine excellence beyond comprehension. Gregory states that "the angels speak to God, when by contemplating what is above themselves they rise to emotions of admiration."

### Reply to Objection 1

Speech does not always make something known to another; sometimes it serves to manifest something to the speaker himself, as when disciples consult a master.

### Reply to Objection 2

Angels perpetually praise and admire God in an ongoing sense. They speak to God by consulting Him about new works requiring enlightenment.

## Article 4: Whether Local Distance Influences Angelic Speech

### Objection 1

Damascene states: "An angel works where he is." Since speech is angelic operation, it seems limited by place.

### Objection 2

The Seraphim "cried one to another" (Isaiah 6:3), suggesting local distance affects angelic speech.

### On the contrary

The rich man in hell spoke to Abraham despite local distance (Luke 16:24).

### I answer that

Angelic speech consists in intellectual operation, which abstracts from "here and now." Even human intellectual operations abstract from spatial conditions except accidentally through phantasms, which angels lack entirely. Therefore, local distance presents no impediment to angelic speech.

### Reply to Objection 1

Angelic speech exists in the speaking angel and hence in his place. Yet as local distance does not prevent angels seeing one another, neither does it prevent perceiving another's directed speech.

### Reply to Objection 2

The cry is not a bodily voice raised by distance but signifies the magnitude of what is said or the intensity of affection. Gregory observes: "The less one desires, the less one cries out."

## Article 5: Whether All Angels Know What One Speaks to Another

### Objection 1

Since local distance does not affect angelic speech, all angels should know what any one angel says to another.

### Objection 2

All angels share the intellectual power. If one angel's directed mental concept becomes known to one, it should become known to all.

### Objection 3

Enlightenment is a kind of speech, and Dionysius states each heavenly being communicates what he learns to others. Therefore, one angel's speech extends to all.

### On the contrary

One human speaks to another alone in private. Angels possess even greater capacity for selective communication.

### I answer that

An angel's mental concept becomes known to another when the first angel refers it through his will to that recipient. A thing ordered through some cause to one recipient need not be ordered to another. Thus, one angel may know a second angel's speech while a third does not—not through local distance but through the will's direction.

### Reply to Objection 1

These objections are answered by the will's determining whom the speech addresses.

### Reply to Objection 2

Same as above.

### Reply to Objection 3

Enlightenment concerns truths emanating from truth's first rule, which is common to all angels. Speech may concern matters ordered to the created will, which is proper to each angel. Therefore, such speeches need not be common to all.
