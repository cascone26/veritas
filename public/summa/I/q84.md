# Question 84: How the Soul While United to the Body Understands Corporeal Things

## Article 1: Whether the Soul Knows Bodies Through the Intellect

### Objection 1
Augustine states that bodies cannot be understood by the intellect, only perceived through senses. Intellectual vision concerns things existing in the soul by their essence, which bodies are not.

### Objection 2
As sense relates to the intelligible, so intellect relates to the sensible. Since the soul cannot understand spiritual things through senses, it cannot know bodies through intellect.

### Objection 3
The intellect concerns necessary, unchangeable things. All bodies are mobile and changeable, so the soul cannot understand them intellectually.

### On the contrary
Science resides in the intellect. If intellect cannot know bodies, natural science (treating mobile bodies) would be impossible.

### I answer that
Early philosophers thought only bodies existed and remained in flux, preventing certain knowledge. Plato responded by positing separate, immaterial Forms, claiming intellectual knowledge concerns these transcendent Ideas rather than material things.

However, this view fails. First, knowledge of matter and movement would be excluded from natural science. Second, it seems ridiculous to invoke separate substances to explain our knowledge of manifest things.

Plato erred by assuming the form in the knower must exist identically to the form in the thing known. Yet the form exists differently in different receivers. The intellect receives material and mobile things' species under conditions of immateriality and immobility. Through the intellect, the soul knows bodies immaterially, universally, and necessarily.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine refers to the medium of intellectual knowledge, not its object. The intellect knows bodies through immaterial intelligible species.

### Reply to Objection 2
The higher power operates more excellently the lower power's objects; the lower power cannot reach the higher's domain.

### Reply to Objection 3
Movement presupposes something immovable. The various conditions of mutable things are themselves immutable. Therefore, immutable science of mobile things is possible.

## Article 2: Whether the Soul Understands Corporeal Things Through Its Essence

### Objection 1
Augustine says the soul collects images of bodies formed in and from the soul, giving them something of its substance.

### Objection 2
Aristotle states the soul is, in a way, everything. Like is known by like, so the soul knows corporeal things through itself.

### Objection 3
The soul surpasses corporeal creatures. Lower things exist more eminently in higher things. Therefore, corporeal creatures exist more excellently in the soul.

### On the contrary
Augustine says the mind gathers knowledge of corporeal things through bodily senses, not through itself.

### I answer that
Ancient philosophers held the soul knows bodies through its essence, accepting that "like is known by like." The Platonists disagreed: Plato held the intellectual soul is immaterial and knows through immaterial Forms. Earlier natural philosophers held they must exist materially in the knowing soul.

This fails. First, in a material principle, results exist only potentially, not actually. Things are known through actuality, not potentiality. Second, if material existence in the knower were necessary, external material things should have knowledge—but fire outside the soul doesn't know fire.

Material things must exist in the knower immaterially. Only God, whose Essence comprises all things immaterially as effects pre-exist in their cause, understands all through His Essence. Neither human souls nor angels can.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine discusses imaginary vision through bodily images. The soul gives itself as a subject to be informed by images.

### Reply to Objection 2
Aristotle didn't claim the soul is actually composed of all things. The soul is all things "in a way"—potentially through senses to sensibles, through intellect to intelligibles.

### Reply to Objection 3
Every creature has finite, determinate essence. Only God's Essence perfectly resembles all created things, being the universal principle.

## Article 3: Whether the Soul Understands All Things Through Innate Species

### Objection 1
Gregory says humans share understanding with angels. Angels understand through innate species. Therefore, the soul has innate intelligible species.

### Objection 2
The intellectual soul excels corporeal primary matter. God created primary matter under forms it potentially receives. Much more would God create the intellectual soul under intelligible species.

### Objection 3
An untaught person answers truthfully to orderly questions, implying some knowledge before learning.

### On the contrary
Aristotle states the intellect is like "a tablet on which nothing is written."

### I answer that
Form is the action principle. Humans are sometimes only potential knowers, actualized through sensible objects and instruction. Therefore, the cognitive soul is potentially disposed to the principles of sensation and understanding.

Plato held that naturally the intellect is filled with intelligible species but is hindered by bodily union. This seems unreasonable. First, if the soul naturally knew all things, it seems impossible to forget such knowledge—union with the body is natural, so natural operation cannot be totally hindered by what belongs naturally. Second, if a sense is lacking, knowledge apprehended through it is absent—a blind person cannot know colors. This wouldn't occur if the soul possessed innate intelligible images.

### Reply to Objection 1
Humans share understanding with angels but not with equal perfection. The human intellect remains potentially disposed to such species.

### Reply to Objection 2
Primary matter has substantial being through form. The intellect doesn't receive substantial being through intelligible species, so the comparison doesn't hold.

### Reply to Objection 3
Orderly questioning proceeds from universal self-evident principles to particulars, producing knowledge in the learner for the first time.

## Article 4: Whether Intelligible Species Are Derived from Separate Forms

### Objection 1
The intellectual soul participates the understood thing. What is essentially actually understood (an immaterial form) causes the soul's actual understanding.

### Objection 2
The intelligible relates to intellect as the sensible to sense. Sensible species are caused by external objects. Therefore, intelligible species are caused by actually intelligible separate forms.

### Objection 3
The potential is reduced to actuality by the actual. The intellect requires an intellect always in act—a separate intellect.

### On the contrary
If this were true, senses wouldn't be necessary for understanding. Yet lacking a sense, we lack knowledge of its sensibles.

### I answer that
Plato claimed Forms subsist immaterially; our intellect participates them for knowledge. Avicenna held intelligible species pre-exist in separate intellects, flowing into souls. However, this opinion provides insufficient reason for the soul's union with the body. If the soul receives intelligible species only from separate principles and not from senses, it wouldn't need the body to understand.

Furthermore, if senses merely arouse the soul to turn to the active intellect for species, an individual lacking one sense could still receive species for missing sensibles. A blind person would know colors, which is manifestly false.

### Reply to Objection 1
Intelligible species reduce to God as first cause but proceed through sensible forms and material things.

### Reply to Objection 2
Material things are actually sensible but not actually intelligible. Sense and intellect don't compare equally.

### Reply to Objection 3
The passive intellect is reduced to actuality by the active intellect (the soul's power), not by a separate intelligence as proximate cause.

## Article 5: Whether the Intellectual Soul Knows Material Things in the Eternal Types

### Objection 1
The human soul doesn't know the eternal types in this life—it knows God as unknown. Therefore, the soul doesn't know all things in eternal types.

### Objection 2
God's invisible things are seen through created things, not conversely.

### Objection 3
Saying the soul knows things in eternal types returns to Plato's opinion.

### On the contrary
Augustine writes that unchangeable truth contains eternal types where we both see what is true.

### I answer that
Augustine, correcting Plato by faith, held that eternal types exist in God's mind—prototypes according to which all creatures are made and known. One knows something in another two ways. First, as the object itself known (as seeing mirror images)—only the blessed see this way. Second, as a principle of knowledge (as seeing by the sun)—the human soul knows all things in eternal types since by participating them we know all things.

However, besides intellectual light, intelligible species derived from things are required. Knowledge isn't solely from eternal types' participation.

### Reply to Objection 1
Resolved in the answer above.

### Reply to Objection 2
Resolved in the answer above.

### Reply to Objection 3
Resolved in the answer above.

## Article 6: Whether Intellectual Knowledge Is Derived from Sensible Things

### Objection 1
Augustine states we cannot expect truth's fullness from bodily senses. Bodies constantly change; what's never the same cannot be perceived.

### Objection 2
Augustine writes the body cannot impress the spirit since the agent is more excellent than the patient.

### Objection 3
Intellectual knowledge extends beyond sensibles—we understand imperceptible things.

### On the contrary
Aristotle states the knowledge principle is in the senses.

### I answer that
Democritus held all knowledge results from images flowing from bodies into souls. Plato denied sensibles affect the intellect, claiming separate intelligible forms are participated. Aristotle took a middle course: sensation results from sensible impression on the composite, but the active intellect (the soul's power) makes phantasms actually intelligible through abstraction.

On phantasms' part, intellectual knowledge is caused by senses. Since phantasms cannot affect the passive intellect without the active intellect making them actually intelligible, sensible knowledge isn't intellectual knowledge's total cause but rather its material cause.

### Reply to Objection 1
The active intellect's light is needed—through it we achieve unchangeable truth in changeable things.

### Reply to Objection 2
Augustine's argument supports the active intellect—the agent exceeds the patient.

### Reply to Objection 3
Sensible knowledge isn't intellectual knowledge's complete cause, so its greater extent is unsurprising.

## Article 7: Whether the Intellect Can Understand Without Turning to Phantasms

### Objection 1
The intellect is actualized by intelligible species. When actualized, it understands. Therefore, species suffice without phantasms.

### Objection 2
Imagination can imagine without sensibles present. Therefore, the intellect can understand without phantasms.

### Objection 3
No phantasms exist of incorporeal things. If intellect cannot understand without phantasms, it cannot understand God or angels.

### On the contrary
Aristotle states the soul understands nothing without a phantasm.

### I answer that
In the present life, the intellect cannot actually understand except by turning to phantasms. First, when imagination is hindered through corporeal organ lesion, intellectual understanding of previously known things is hindered. Second, experience shows that when understanding something, we form phantasms as examples.

The reason: the human intellect's proper object is quiddity existing in corporeal matter. Material nature exists individually in individual things. We apprehend individuals through senses and imagination. Therefore, intellect must turn to phantasms to perceive the universal nature existing in the individual.

### Reply to Objection 1
Species in the passive intellect exist habitually. For actual understanding, we must use them befitting the things they represent.

### Reply to Objection 2
Phantasm is an individual thing's likeness; imagination needs no further likeness. Intellect does.

### Reply to Objection 3
Incorporeal things are known through comparison with sensible bodies. Therefore, understanding them requires turning to bodily phantasms, though not phantasms of themselves.

## Article 8: Whether Intellect's Judgment Is Hindered by Suspension of Sensitive Powers

### Objection 1
The superior doesn't depend on the inferior. Intellect's judgment surpasses senses.

### Objection 2
People sometimes syllogize during sleep when senses are suspended.

### On the contrary
Acts contrary to moral law committed while asleep aren't imputed as sin, implying suspended senses hinder intellect.

### I answer that
The intellect's proper object is sensible nature. Perfect judgment requires knowing all pertaining to its nature, especially its term and end perceptible to senses. Everything we understand in this life is known through comparison to natural sensibles. Therefore, while senses are suspended, the intellect cannot form perfect judgment.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although intellect surpasses senses, it receives from them; its first principal objects are founded in sensibles.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sleep suspends senses variably. As sense and imagination are free, so intellect's judgment is unfettered. A sleeping person's syllogism always reveals a flaw upon waking.
