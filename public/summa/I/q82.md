# Question 82: The Will

## Article 1: Whether the Will Desires Something of Necessity

### Objection 1
Augustine claims that if something is necessary, it cannot be voluntary. Since the will's desires are voluntary, nothing the will desires can be necessary.

### Objection 2
Rational powers extend to opposite things. The will is rational, therefore it extends to opposites and is determined to nothing necessarily.

### Objection 3
We are masters of our own actions. But we cannot be masters of what is necessary. Therefore, the will's acts cannot be necessitated.

### On the contrary
Augustine states that all people desire happiness with one will. If this were contingent rather than necessary, exceptions would exist. Therefore, the will desires something necessarily.

### I answer that
"Necessity" operates in multiple ways. Natural necessity involves intrinsic principles (like corruptibility in composite things). Necessity of end occurs when something is required to achieve an objective. Necessity of coercion exists when an external agent forces compliance.

Coercion directly contradicts the will's nature, since the will is an inclination to something. However, necessity of end does not contradict the will—when a single means achieves an end, willing that end necessitates willing that means. Similarly, natural necessity aligns with the will. The intellect necessarily adheres to first principles; the will must necessarily adhere to happiness (the ultimate end).

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine speaks of coercive necessity. Natural necessity does not eliminate the will's liberty.

### Reply to Objection 2
When the will desires naturally, it corresponds more to the intellect regarding natural principles than to reason extending to opposites.

### Reply to Objection 3
We master our actions through choice, which concerns means rather than ends. The desire for the ultimate end falls outside actions we master.

## Article 2: Whether the Will Desires of Necessity Whatever It Desires

### Objection 1
Dionysius states that evil lies outside the will's scope. Therefore, the will necessarily tends toward proposed good.

### Objection 2
The will's object relates to it as a mover to the movable. The movable necessarily follows the mover's movement.

### Objection 3
The senses' object moves the sensitive appetite necessarily. The intellect's apprehended object should similarly move the will necessarily.

### On the contrary
Augustine says the will determines both sin and righteous living, extending to opposites. Therefore, the will does not necessarily desire all things it desires.

### I answer that
The intellect necessarily assents only to propositions necessarily connected with first principles. Similarly, the will necessarily adheres only to goods with necessary connection to happiness.

Individual goods without necessary connection to happiness do not necessitate the will's adherence—happiness remains possible without them. However, things genuinely connected to God (where true happiness resides) would necessarily attract the will—but only through the beatific vision's certainty. Without that vision, the will does not necessarily adhere to God or divine things.

A person beholding God's essence necessarily adheres to Him, just as we necessarily desire happiness now.

### Reply to Objection 1
The will can only tend toward good. Because good exists in multiple forms, the will is not determined necessarily to one.

### Reply to Objection 2
A mover necessarily causes movement when its power exceeds the movable's capacity entirely. The will's capacity embraces universal perfect good, so no individual good completely subjects it.

### Reply to Objection 3
Sensory powers apprehend only individual things, moving the sensitive appetite determinately. Reason compares multiple things, so the will remains unmoved necessarily by single objects.

## Article 3: Whether the Will Is a Higher Power Than the Intellect

### Objection 1
The will's object is good and the end. The end constitutes the first and highest cause. Therefore, the will is the first and highest power.

### Objection 2
Natural progress advances from imperfect to perfect things. The will's act follows the intellect's act naturally. Therefore, the will exceeds the intellect.

### Objection 3
Charity perfecting the will surpasses intellectual habits. Scripture affirms that knowledge without charity amounts to nothing.

### On the contrary
The Philosopher establishes the intellect as superior to the will.

### I answer that
Regarding themselves, the intellect surpasses the will. The intellect's object—the intelligible form of good itself—is simpler and more absolute than the will's object (the good as appetible). More abstract things are nobler intrinsically.

Relatively, the will may exceed the intellect when its object exists in something nobler than where the intellect's object resides. The intellect's act consists in the understood idea existing in the knower; the will's act consists in inclination toward the thing itself. When the good inheres in something nobler than the soul itself, the will becomes relatively superior.

Thus, loving God surpasses knowing God, but knowing bodily things surpasses loving them. Absolutely, the intellect remains nobler.

### Reply to Objection 1
Causality appears through comparative relations, making good appear nobler. Truth signifies something more absolute, extending even to the good's notion.

### Reply to Objection 2
The intellect precedes the will as the active power precedes the passive; understood good moves the will.

### Reply to Objection 3
This reasoning applies to the will compared with what surpasses the soul. Charity constitutes the virtue whereby we love God.

## Article 4: Whether the Will Moves the Intellect

### Objection 1
What moves exceeds what is moved. The intellect exceeds the will. Therefore, the will does not move the intellect.

### Objection 2
The intellect moves the will; the understood good moves without being moved. Therefore, the intellect is not moved by the will.

### Objection 3
We cannot will what we do not understand. If the will must move for understanding to occur, infinite regress results.

### On the contrary
Damascene states learning an art remains within our power—we learn through intellect and exercise choice through will.

### I answer that
Movement operates two ways: as an end (when the end moves the agent) and as an efficient cause. The intellect moves the will as an end; the understood good moves the will as an end. The will moves the intellect as an efficient cause.

The will's object is good and the end universally; each power aims at suitable good proper to it. Therefore, the will as agent moves all soul powers to their acts (except natural vegetative powers not subject to our will).

Both intellect and will may be considered two ways. Compared by object universality, the intellect exceeds the will. Considering the will universally and the intellect as particular power, the will is superior and capable of moving the intellect. These powers reciprocally include each other's acts.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellect and will move each other differently, as explained.

### Reply to Objection 2
Same as above.

### Reply to Objection 3
No infinite regress occurs. Every will movement requires prior apprehension, but not every apprehension requires prior willing. The counseling and understanding principle is God, higher than our intellect.

## Article 5: Whether Irascible and Concupiscible Parts Exist in the Superior Appetite

### Objection 1
Intellectual concupiscence (like wisdom's desire) cannot belong to sensitive appetite. Intellectual anger (against vice) similarly cannot be sensitive. Therefore, irascible and concupiscible parts should be distinguished in the intellectual soul.

### Objection 2
Charity resides in the concupiscible and hope in the irascible. Their objects are intellectual. Therefore, irascible and concupiscible powers must be assigned to the intellectual part.

### Objection 3
The soul possesses irascible, concupiscible, and rational powers before union with the body. No sensitive power belongs to the soul alone.

### On the contrary
Gregory of Nyssa and Damascene divide the irrational soul part into desiderative and irascible. The Philosopher states the will is in reason, while concupiscence and anger are in the soul's irrational part.

### I answer that
Irascible and concupiscible powers do not differentiate the intellectual appetite (will). The sensitive appetite cannot apprehend universal good; senses do not grasp universals. Thus, sensitive appetite parts differentiate by particular good notions: concupiscible regards pleasant sensible good; irascible regards repelling harm. The will, regarding good under common notion universally, does not differentiate into distinct appetitive powers.

### Reply to Objection 1
Love, concupiscence, and similar terms function two ways. As passions (with soul commotion), they belong only to sensitive appetite. As simple affections without passion, they constitute will acts.

### Reply to Objection 2
The will itself can be called irascible in willing evil's repulsion through reason's judgment. Similarly, the will is concupiscible desiring good. Thus, charity and hope exist in the will ordered to such acts.

### Reply to Objection 3
The preceding answer clarifies this objection.
