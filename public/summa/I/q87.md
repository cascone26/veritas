# Question 87: How the Intellectual Soul Knows Itself and All Within Itself

## Article 1: Whether the Intellectual Soul Knows Itself by Its Essence

### Objection 1
Augustine states the mind knows itself because it is incorporeal, suggesting direct self-knowledge through essence.

### Objection 2
Angels understand themselves by their essence. Human souls, as intellectual substance, should similarly possess this capacity.

### Objection 3
Aristotle teaches that in immaterial things, intellect and the understood object are identical.

### On the contrary
Aristotle indicates the intellect understands itself as it understands other things—through likenesses rather than essences.

### I answer that
Things are knowable insofar as they exist in actuality. God, as pure act, knows Himself through His essence. Angels, as acts in the intelligible realm, understand themselves through essence but require likenesses for other things.

The human intellect functions as mere potentiality among intelligible beings. In this life, our intellect's proper object is material nature. Thus we know ourselves through acts—first singularly (when Socrates perceives his understanding), then universally (considering the nature of mind generally).

### Reply to Objection 1
The mind's self-presence makes it knowable to itself. Augustine's point concerns non-accidental knowability.

### Reply to Objection 2
An angel's essence constitutes both intellect and understood object. The human mind differs fundamentally—as passive intellect it is pure potentiality.

### Reply to Objection 3
The principle applies properly only to separate substances, not to all beings. For humans, the species abstracted from sensibles serves as the form of understanding.

## Article 2: Whether Our Intellect Knows the Habits of the Soul by Their Essence

### Objection 1
Augustine states faith is known with certainty through conscience's declaration, suggesting habits are known directly.

### Objection 2
Material things are known through likenesses; habits present themselves within the soul. Therefore habits should be known by their essence.

### Objection 3
Habits cause things to be known, so habits themselves should be supremely knowable.

### On the contrary
Habits function as principles of acts. Acts logically precede powers and habits.

### I answer that
A habit occupies a middle position between mere potentiality and complete actuality. Since nothing is known except as actual, habits become knowable only through their acts. One discovers a habit by performing its characteristic act.

### Reply to Objection 1
Faith becomes known through interior acts of belief. One cannot know faith without knowing one believes.

### Reply to Objection 2
Habits exist in intellect not as objects but as that by which understanding occurs.

### Reply to Objection 3
Habits belong not to the order of knowable objects but to the order of knowing dispositions.

## Article 3: Whether Our Intellect Knows Its Own Act

### Objection 1
The act differs from the object. The intellect cannot know its own act.

### Objection 2
If intellect knows its act, it does so through another act, producing infinite regress.

### Objection 3
Proper sense cannot perceive its own act; only common sense does.

### On the contrary
Augustine asserts, "I understand that I understand."

### I answer that
The intellect's supreme perfection consists in its operation, which remains within the agent as its act. Therefore the intellect primarily understands its own understanding.

Human intellect differs from divine and angelic: its primary object is material nature. What the human intellect primarily knows is such material object; secondarily it knows the act by which this object is known; through this act, the intellect itself becomes known.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellect's object comprises universal being and truth, encompassing the act of understanding itself.

### Reply to Objection 2
The human intellect's act of understanding differs from the material nature's act understood. The intellect can be potentially infinite.

### Reply to Objection 3
Proper sense perceives through material organ mutation. Intellect operates without material organ mutation; therefore no comparison obtains.

## Article 4: Whether the Intellect Understands the Act of the Will

### Objection 1
The will's act exists in the will, not the intellect.

### Objection 2
The will's object differs from the intellect's object.

### Objection 3
Augustine notes soul affections are known through certain notions, not images or presence.

### On the contrary
Augustine declares, "I understand that I will."

### I answer that
The will's act constitutes an intelligible inclination following upon grasped form. Whatever resides intelligibly in an intelligent subject is understood by that subject. Therefore the intellect understands the will's act—both that willing occurs and the act's nature.

### Reply to Objection 1
Both will and intellect root in the soul's substance. What exists in the will thereby exists intelligibly in the intellect.

### Reply to Objection 2
Good and true contain each other: the true is good and the good is true. Will and intellect objects fall within each other's scope.

### Reply to Objection 3
Soul affections exist in intellect as effects in their principle. The principle contains a notion of what it causes.
