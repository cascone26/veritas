# Question 72: The Work of the Sixth Day

## Article 1: Whether the work of the sixth day is fittingly described?

### Objection 1
Land animals possess living souls just as birds and fishes do, yet they are not themselves living souls. Therefore, the description should state "let the earth bring forth living four-footed creatures" rather than simply "living creature."

### Objection 2
Genus should not be opposed to its species. Since beasts and cattle are quadrupeds, quadrupeds ought not be enumerated as a separate class alongside them.

### Objection 3
Man, like animals, belongs to a determinate genus and species. Yet nothing about his genus or species is mentioned in his creation account, so similar details should not appear in the production of other animals.

### Objection 4
Land animals resemble man more closely than birds and fishes do. Since the latter received God's blessing, land animals should have received it with even greater reason.

### Objection 5
Certain animals generate from putrefaction, which represents corruption. Corruption contradicts the pristine founding of the world, so such animals should not have been produced initially.

### Objection 6
Some animals are poisonous and harmful to man. Nothing injurious should have existed before man's sin. Therefore, either God should not have created such animals at all, or at least not before man sinned.

### On the contrary
Scripture suffices as authority on this matter.

### I answer that
The sixth day, adorning the earth through land animals' production, corresponds to the third day, which also mentions earth. Augustine and other Church writers discuss whether this production was potential or actual.

The various grades of life in creatures appear through how Scripture describes them. Plants possess imperfect life—only generation is mentioned without explicit reference to life itself, since nutrition and growth subordinate to generation represent their vital activity. Land animals generally surpass birds and fishes in perfection because their limbs are more distinct and their generation superior (though some creatures like bees demonstrate remarkable intelligence). Scripture calls fishes "creeping creatures having life" but designates land animals as "living creatures," suggesting fishes are bodies with something of a soul, while land animals—from their superior life—seem to be living souls with bodies subject to them. Man's life, being most perfect, is not produced by earth or water but directly by God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Different animal types receive distinct designations. Cattle signify domesticated animals serving man; beasts denote wild creatures like bears and lions. Creeping things include legless serpents or short-legged creatures like lizards and tortoises unable to rise far from ground. Quadrupeds are added as a separate classification for animals like deer and goats fitting none of these categories.

### Reply to Objection 2
"Cattle" designates domesticated animals useful to man, while "beasts" indicates wild animals such as bears and lions. "Creeping things" describes animals either lacking feet or possessing insufficient length to elevate themselves substantially from the earth, as with serpents, lizards, and tortoises. The term "quadrupeds" functions as genus, with others as subordinate species, since even some reptiles possess four feet.

### Reply to Objection 3
Genus and species are mentioned for animals and plants to denote generation of like from like. This was unnecessary regarding man, as what applied to other creatures applied to him. Creatures are produced "according to their kinds" to signify their distance from the Divine image and likeness, whereas man is made "to the image and likeness of God."

### Reply to Objection 4
God's blessing grants multiplication power through generation. Having been mentioned for birds and fishes, this blessing extends to earthly beasts without repetition. However, the blessing is repeated for man since human generation relates specially to the number of the elect and to prevent anyone from suggesting sin exists in begetting. Plants, experiencing neither propagation desire nor generation sensation, deserve no formal blessing.

### Reply to Objection 5
Since one thing's generation constitutes another's corruption, the world's initial formation could involve more perfect creatures arising from less perfect ones' corruption. Animals generated from inanimate things or plants could be produced then. However, those generated from animal corruption could only have been produced potentially.

### Reply to Objection 6
Augustine explains: "An unskilled person entering an artificer's workshop sees many appliances whose use he doesn't understand, potentially considering them unnecessary. Carelessly falling into fire or wounding himself with sharp tools, he mistakenly judges many things harmful; the craftsman, knowing their use, laughs at his foolishness. Similarly, people fault many world things through ignorance. Though unnecessary for furnishing our house, these serve the universe's perfection. Since unfallen man would use earthly things conformably to their designed order, poisonous animals would not have injured him."
