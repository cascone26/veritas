# Question 72: The Work of the Sixth Day

## Article 1: Whether the work of the sixth day is fittingly described?

### Objection 1
Land animals possess living souls just as birds and fishes do. Therefore, the description should reference "living four-footed creatures" rather than "the living creature."

### Objection 2
Since beasts and cattle are types of quadrupeds, enumeration should not list quadrupeds alongside these species as a separate class.

### Objection 3
If man's genus and species were not mentioned at his creation, why should they be specified for other animals as "according to its genus" and "in its species"?

### Objection 4
Land animals resemble man more closely than birds and fishes do. Therefore, if birds and fishes received blessing, land animals should have received it with even greater reason.

### Objection 5
Some animals arise from putrefaction, which represents corruption. Such corruption contradicts the pristine founding of creation and should not have occurred then.

### Objection 6
Poisonous and injurious animals exist. Nothing harmful to man should have existed before his sin, yet God, being good, would not create such things—or at least not until after man's transgression.

### On the Contrary
Scripture's authority suffices as confirmation.

### I Answer That
The sixth day adorns the earth through land animal production, paralleling the third day when earth was first mentioned. Regarding production's nature, Augustine suggests it was potential while other holy writers affirm it was actual. The varying perfection grades among living creatures appear reflected in Scripture's varied terminology describing them, as Basil observes.

Plants demonstrate imperfect, difficult-to-discern life; only generation is noted since vital activity appears primarily there. Land animals generally exceed birds and fishes in perfection due to more distinct limbs and superior generation, despite some imperfect creatures like bees and ants possessing remarkable intelligence. Scripture calls fishes "creeping creatures having life" but designates land animals as "living creatures," suggesting fish are bodies containing something of soul, while land animals—from greater life perfection—are, as it were, living souls with subject bodies. Man's life, being most perfect, originates directly from God rather than earth or water.

### Reply to Objection 1
Scripture's varied language reveals life grades in creatures. Plants show imperfect life where "only in generation is a vital act observed." Land animals, possessing "more distinct limbs and their generation of a higher order," merit "living creatures" designation, while fishes are "creeping creatures having life."

### Reply to Objection 2
"Cattle" signifies domestic animals serving man; "beasts" designates wild creatures like bears and lions; "creeping things" refers to footless serpents or short-legged animals like lizards and tortoises. The term "quadrupeds" accommodates creatures like deer and goats fitting no other category, functioning perhaps as a genus with others as species.

### Reply to Objection 3
Genus and species mention in other creatures denotes "generation of like from like." This proved unnecessary for man since what applied to creatures might be understood of him. Such specification signifies creatures' "remoteness from the Divine image and likeness," whereas man alone is made "to the image and likeness of God."

### Reply to Objection 4
God's blessing grants multiplicative power through generation. Already mentioned for birds and fishes, it could be understood for earth beasts without repetition. The blessing repeats for man because "generation of children has a special relation to the number of the elect" and to prevent supposing sin in procreation. Plants, lacking propagation desire or generative sensation, deserve no formal blessing.

### Reply to Objection 5
Since one thing's generation constitutes another's corruption, the initial formation permitted generating more perfect creatures from less perfect corruption. Animals generated from inanimate or plant corruption could have been produced then. Those from animal corruption could only have been generated potentially.

### Reply to Objection 6
Augustine explains: an unskilled workshop visitor sees tools whose purpose he misunderstands, falsely deeming them harmful or unnecessary, while the craftsman knows their use. Similarly, people fault creation's aspects without grasping their necessity "for the perfection of the universe." Before sinning, man would use creation conformably to intended order, so poisonous animals would not have harmed him.
