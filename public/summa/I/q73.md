# Question 73: The Things That Belong to the Seventh Day

## Article 1: Whether the completion of the Divine works ought to be ascribed to the seventh day?

### Objection 1
The completion of Divine works should not be assigned to the seventh day. All worldly actions constitute Divine works, yet the world's consummation occurs at its end. Christ's incarnation represents completion, called "the fulness of time," and at death He proclaimed, "It is consummated." Therefore, seventh-day completion doesn't apply.

### Objection 2
Completion requires action, yet Scripture describes God resting rather than acting on the seventh day. Thus completion cannot belong to it.

### Objection 3
Things receiving additions afterward cannot be called complete unless those additions are superfluous. After the seventh day, individual creatures were produced, new species appeared, souls were created daily, the Incarnation occurred as "a new thing," miracles were performed, and ultimately "all things" will become new. Therefore seventh-day completion is inappropriate.

### On the contrary
Genesis states: "On the seventh day God ended His work which He had made."

### I answer that
Perfection divides into first and second types. First perfection involves substantial completeness through integral parts. Second perfection represents the end—either an operation or something achieved through operation. First perfection causes second perfection, as form principles operation.

The universe's ultimate perfection—Saints' beatitude at world's consummation—represents second perfection. The universe's initial completeness upon founding represents first perfection, which the seventh day expresses.

### Reply to Objection 1
First perfection causes second perfection. Beatitude requires nature and grace. Therefore: seventh day provided nature's perfection at world's founding; Christ's Incarnation provided grace's perfection; world's end provides glory's perfection. As stated, "Grace and truth came by Jesus Christ."

### Reply to Objection 2
God acted on the seventh day by directing creatures toward their proper functions, initiating "second" perfection. Scripture legitimately attributes completion to the seventh day—regarding parts' completeness to the sixth day, regarding their operation to the seventh. Since God could have created additional creatures but ceased doing so, the seventh day marks consummation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Nothing entirely new was subsequently created; all later things existed previously in some manner during the six days. Some things existed materially (Eve from Adam's rib); others in causes (individual creatures existing in their first kind); new species existed in active powers (animals from putrefaction possessed stellar and elemental powers); hybrid animals existed in their causes (mules from asses and mares); souls existed by similitude; the Incarnation was foreshadowed; spiritual glory was anticipated in angels; bodily glory in heaven. Scripture confirms: "Nothing under the sun is new, for it hath already gone before."

## Article 2: Whether God rested on the seventh day from all His work?

### Objection 1
God did not rest from all work on the seventh day. Scripture says: "My Father worketh until now, and I work." Therefore rest from all work didn't occur.

### Objection 2
Rest opposes movement or labor. Since God produced His work without movement and without labor, He cannot be said to have rested.

### Objection 3
If God rested by causing humans to rest, this contradicts the distinction between God's work and human work. "God created" cannot mean "God made humans create." Therefore God's resting cannot mean making humans rest.

### On the contrary
Genesis states: "God rested on the seventh day from all the work which He had done."

### I answer that
Rest properly opposes movement and resulting labor. Though movement strictly applies to bodies, it extends spiritually in two ways: operation as movement (Divine goodness moving toward its object through self-communication, as Dionysius states); and desire tending toward external objects. Correspondingly, rest means either ceasing from work or satisfying desire.

God rested on the seventh day in both senses. First, He ceased creating new creatures—nothing afterward existed that hadn't previously existed in some degree in the initial works. Second, God required nothing from His creations for His own happiness but rested in Himself, sufficing to Himself and fulfilling His own desire. Though eternally resting in Himself, the rest taken after finishing works belongs specifically to the seventh day, as Augustine explains.

### Reply to Objection 1
God "worketh until now" by preserving and providing for created creatures, not by making new ones.

### Reply to Objection 2
Rest here opposes not labor or movement, but production of new creatures and desire tending toward external objects.

### Reply to Objection 3
As God rests in Himself alone and finds happiness in enjoying Himself, our sole happiness lies in enjoying God. Thus He grants us rest in Himself from both His works and our own. Saying God rested by granting rest isn't unreasonable, though the first explanation remains primary.

## Article 3: Whether blessing and sanctifying are due to the seventh day?

### Objection 1
Blessing and sanctifying don't apply to the seventh day. Times become blessed or holy when good events occur or evil is avoided. Since God loses or gains nothing through working or resting, no special blessing befits the seventh day.

### Objection 2
"Blessing" derives from "goodness." Good characteristically spreads and communicates itself, as Dionysius states. Days of creature-production deserved blessing rather than the day when production ceased.

### Objection 3
Each creature received blessing—each work prompted "God saw that it was good." Therefore blessing the seventh day afterward was unnecessary.

### On the contrary
Genesis states: "God blessed the seventh day and sanctified it, because in it He had rested from all His work."

### I answer that
God's seventh-day rest functions in two ways: ceasing from producing new works while preserving existing creatures; and resting in Himself after completing all works.

According to the first meaning, blessing befits the seventh day. As explained elsewhere, blessing referred to multiplication increase; God commanded blessed creatures: "Increase and multiply." This increase occurs through Divine Providence securing generation of like from like. According to the second meaning, sanctification befits the seventh day, since every creature's special sanctification consists in resting in God. Things dedicated to God are called sanctified for this reason.

### Reply to Objection 1
The seventh day is sanctified not because anything accrues to or departs from God, but because creatures multiply and rest in God.

### Reply to Objection 2
In the six days, creatures were produced in their first causes. After production, they multiply and are preserved—work belonging also to Divine goodness. This goodness' perfection becomes most clear by recognizing that God alone finds rest in it, and we find ours in its fruition.

### Reply to Objection 3
The goodness mentioned in each day's works belongs to nature's first institution; the seventh day's blessing belongs to propagation.
