# Question 88: How the Human Soul Knows What Is Above Itself

## Article 1: Whether the Human Soul Can Understand Immaterial Substances in Themselves

### Objection 1
Augustine suggests the mind acquires knowledge of incorporeal things from itself. Therefore, the human mind understands immaterial substances.

### Objection 2
Since the human mind is itself immaterial, it should more readily understand immaterial than material things.

### Objection 3
Things of highest intelligibility should be most knowable to us, and immaterial substances possess greater intelligibility.

### Objection 4
Nature would be frustrated if we could not understand abstract substances, since nature produces nothing purposeless.

### Objection 5
As sight perceives all corporeal bodies, the intellect should understand all intelligible substances.

### On the contrary
Scripture asks: "The things that are in heaven, who shall search out?"

### I answer that
Plato held that immaterial Forms constitute the proper objects of intellect. However, Aristotle's view shows our intellect in the present life relates naturally to material things and understands only by turning toward images. Consequently, immaterial substances that lack sensory or imaginative representations cannot be known directly.

Averroes proposed that through union with the active intellect (a separate substance), humans might ultimately know immaterial substances. This theory fails: a separate substance cannot be our formal instrument of understanding; union occurs only through participated light; understanding all material things does not sufficiently unite the active intellect for knowing immaterial substances.

### Reply to Objection 1
The mind gains knowledge of incorporeal things from itself—though imperfectly.

### Reply to Objection 2
The passive intellect receives similitudes abstracted from images, enabling knowledge of material rather than immaterial substances.

### Reply to Objection 3
Immaterial substances remain disproportionate to our present intellect.

### Reply to Objection 4
Even if we do not understand separate substances, they remain intelligible to themselves. Being understood by us is not their end.

### Reply to Objection 5
We understand material and immaterial substances differently—material things through abstraction, immaterial things cannot be known this way.

## Article 2: Whether the Intellect Can Understand Immaterial Substances Through Knowledge of Material Things

### Objection 1
Dionysius states the human mind cannot ascend to immaterial contemplation except through material guidance.

### Objection 2
Theologians and philosophers define angels and treat immaterial substances scientifically.

### Objection 3
The human soul—an immaterial substance—can be understood through its acts. Similarly, other immaterial substances should be knowable through their effects.

### Objection 4
Only causes infinitely distant from effects remain incomprehensible through them. This belongs to God alone.

### On the contrary
Dionysius declares: "Intelligible things cannot be understood through sensible things."

### I answer that
Avempace suggested that by understanding natural substances, we abstract until reaching immaterial quiddity. This would work if immaterial substances were forms of material things, as Platonists believed. Since immaterial substances differ entirely from material quiddities, our intellect cannot approach immaterial substance through abstraction. We cannot perfectly understand immaterial substances through material ones.

### Reply to Objection 1
Material things raise us to some knowledge of immaterial things, but not perfect knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 2
We know immaterial substances scientifically by denial and relation to material things, not by grasping their quiddity.

### Reply to Objection 3
The human soul understands itself through its act. Immaterial substances' nature cannot be perfectly known through material things.

### Reply to Objection 4
Through material likenesses, we acquire some common positive knowledge of angels but not specific nature knowledge; we acquire no such knowledge of God.

## Article 3: Whether God Is the First Object Known by the Human Mind

### Objection 1
Whatever is known in all other objects and judges all others is first known. We know all things in God's light and judge through it.

### Objection 2
Whatever causes something to be such is itself more so. God causes all knowledge.

### Objection 3
What is first known in an image is its exemplar. Our mind is God's image.

### On the contrary
"No man hath seen God at any time."

### I answer that
Since the human intellect cannot understand even created immaterial substances, much less can it comprehend God's essence. We know God through creatures. Our knowledge's first object is the quiddity of material things.

### Reply to Objection 1
Our mind's light is merely the first truth's impression. As this light itself is not understood, God is not our first known object.

### Reply to Objection 2
Other things are known because of God—not as first known object, but as first cause of our knowing faculty.

### Reply to Objection 3
Our image is imperfect; therefore the argument fails.
