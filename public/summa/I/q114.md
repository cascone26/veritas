# Question 114: The Assaults of the Demons

## Article 1: Whether Men Are Assailed by the Demons

### Objection 1

Angels are sent by God to guard man, but demons are not sent by God since they intend the loss of souls. Therefore demons do not assail men.

### Objection 2

It would be unjust to permit weak men to be assailed by strong demons. God would not permit such unequal combat.

### Objection 3

The assaults of the flesh and world suffice for man's exercise. Demonic assault is unnecessary.

### On the contrary

The Apostle writes: "Our wrestling is not against flesh and blood; but against Principalities and Powers, against the rulers of the world of this darkness" (Ephesians 6:12).

### I answer that

The assault itself arises from the demons' malice and envy. However, God orders these assaults toward good ends. While demons attack through their own wickedness, God permits and arranges such assaults according to His just judgments.

### Reply to Objection 1

Demons assail in two ways. By inciting to sin—not sent by God but sometimes permitted. As punishment—sent by God, as the lying spirit was sent to punish King Achab (1 Kings 22:20).

### Reply to Objection 2

The fight remains balanced because man possesses the promised reward, obtained principally through God's grace and secondarily through angelic protection. As Eliseus said: "There are more with us than with them" (2 Kings 6:16).

### Reply to Objection 3

While flesh and world suffice to exercise human weakness, they do not counter demons' malice. By divine ordinance, even this contributes to the elect's glory.

## Article 2: Whether to Tempt Is Proper to the Devil

### Objection 1

God tempts Abraham (Genesis 22:1). Man is tempted by flesh and world. Therefore tempting is not peculiar to the devil.

### Objection 2

To tempt signifies ignorance. Demons know what occurs among men.

### Objection 3

Temptation leads to sin in the will. Demons cannot change man's will.

### On the contrary

"Lest perhaps he that tempteth should have tempted you" (1 Thessalonians 3:5), where the gloss identifies the devil as one "whose office it is to tempt."

### I answer that

To tempt properly means to make trial of something. The devil always tempts to harm by urging man toward sin—this constitutes his proper office. God tempts to make others know their quality. The flesh and world tempt as instruments through which one discovers inclinations toward desire and worldly attachment.

### Reply to Objection 1

Various beings tempt in different ways as explained above.

### Reply to Objection 2

Demons know outward affairs but only God knows man's inward disposition. The devil tempts to explore this condition and direct temptation toward the vice to which one is most inclined.

### Reply to Objection 3

Though a demon cannot alter the will directly, he can change inferior powers, which incline the will though they cannot force it.

## Article 3: Whether All Sins Are Due to Demonic Temptation

### Objection 1

Dionysius states "the multitude of demons is the cause of all evils." Damascene says "all malice has been devised by the devil."

### Objection 2

The Lord said of the Jews: "You are of your father the devil" (John 8:44). Every sinner can be said likewise.

### Objection 3

As angels' good suggestions produce good actions, demons' evil suggestions produce all evil actions.

### On the contrary

"Not all our evil thoughts are stirred up by the devil, but sometimes they arise from the movement of our free-will."

### I answer that

Indirectly, the devil is the occasional cause of all sins: he instigated the first man to sin, from which proneness to sin resulted in all humanity. Directly, however, the devil does not cause every sin. Not all sins involve demonic instigation; some stem from free will and flesh corruption. As Origen notes, even without a devil, men would desire food and similar pleasures; disorders arise from unrestrained desires unless reason curbs them.

### Reply to Objection 1

The initial statements refer to the devil's indirect causality through original sin.

### Reply to Objection 2

When man sins without demonic instigation, he becomes a devil's child by imitating him.

### Reply to Objection 3

Man can fall into sin independently but cannot advance in merit without divine assistance borne by angels. Yet no sin exists that is not sometimes due to demonic suggestion.

## Article 4: Whether Demons Can Lead Men Astray Through Real Miracles

### Objection 1

The Antichrist's coming is "according to the working of Satan, in all power, and signs, and lying wonders" (2 Thessalonians 2:9).

### Objection 2

Augustine states the human body cannot receive beast limbs by demonic power. Therefore demons cannot work real miracles.

### Objection 3

If demons work real miracles, miracles become useless for confirming faith's teaching.

### On the contrary

Augustine says miracles are sometimes wrought by magic art like those of God's servants.

### I answer that

Strictly, only God works miracles—deeds beyond all created nature's order. However, "miracle" sometimes means whatever exceeds human power and experience. Demons can work such things, causing astonishment.

Though demonic works are not true miracles, they sometimes involve real effects. Pharaoh's magicians produced actual serpents and frogs. Fire from heaven consuming Job's servants was Satan's work, not phantoms.

### Reply to Objection 1

Antichrist's works may be "lying wonders" because they deceive senses through phantoms, or because real prodigies lead believers into falsehood.

### Reply to Objection 2

Transformations exceeding natural power (changing humans into beasts, reviving the dead) cannot truly result from demonic operation. When such seems accomplished, it is semblance through working on imagination or externally clothing corporeal things with assumed forms.

### Reply to Objection 3

Augustine states: "When magicians do what holy men do, they do so for different ends and by different right." The former act for their own glory by private compacts; the latter for God's glory by His evident assistance.

## Article 5: Whether a Demon Overcome by Man Is Hindered from Further Assaults

### Objection 1

Christ overcame the tempter most effectively, yet the demon afterward assailed Him through persecution.

### Objection 2

Punishing the defeated combatant by inciting sharper attacks contradicts God's mercy.

### On the contrary

"Then the devil left Him" (Matthew 4:11), Christ having overcome.

### I answer that

A conquered demon cannot tempt that particular person for a definite time. Scripture states: "All temptation being ended, the devil departed from Him for a time" (Luke 4:13). Two reasons explain this. First, God's clemency: God orders the devil away due to our weakness. Second, the devil's astuteness: as Ambrose notes, "the devil is afraid of persisting, because he shrinks from frequent defeat." Nevertheless, the devil sometimes returns, as shown by: "I will return into my house from whence I came out" (Matthew 12:44).

### Reply to Objection 1

Addressed by the explanation above.

### Reply to Objection 2

Same as above.
