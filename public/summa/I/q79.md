# Question 79: The Intellectual Powers

## Article 1: Whether the Intellect is a Power of the Soul, or its Essence?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "Mind and spirit...denominate the essence" rather than being relative attributes. The intellect seems to be the soul's essence rather than a power.

### Objection 2
Different genera of soul's powers unite only in the essence itself. Yet appetite and intellect, though distinct genera, unite in the mind. Therefore the intellect belongs to the soul's essence.

### Objection 3
Gregory teaches that humans understand with angels, who are called minds and intellects. Thus the mind appears to be the soul itself, not merely a power.

### Objection 4
Substance becomes intellectual through immateriality. Since the soul is immaterial through its essence, it must be intellectual through its essence.

### On the contrary
Aristotle assigns the intellectual faculty as a power of the soul.

### I answer that
The intellect is a power of the soul, not its essence. Only in God does understanding constitute His being. In all other intellectual creatures, the intellect functions as a power—the immediate principle of operation distinct from being itself, as power relates to its act.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellectual soul is named after its chief power. Augustine uses "mind" and "essence" interchangeably because the intellectual soul is substantially identified with intellection as its primary function.

### Reply to Objection 2
Appetite and intellect, though distinct in kind, operate through different formal aspects. Augustine places will in mind because appetite follows apprehension, creating a functional unity despite categorical distinction.

### Reply to Objection 3
Angels possess only intellectual and volitional powers. Humans, possessing sensitive and nutritive powers additionally, cannot be simply identified with intellect as angels are.

### Reply to Objection 4
Immateriality grants the capacity for intelligence; it is not identical with intelligence itself. The created intelligent substance possesses immateriality as its nature and intelligence as its resulting power.

## Article 2: Whether the Intellect is a Passive Power?

### Objection 1
Passivity belongs to matter, activity to form. Since intellectual power results from immateriality, the intellect should be active, not passive.

### Objection 2
If the intellect were passive, it would be corruptible, yet we established it as incorruptible.

### Objection 3
The agent is nobler than the patient. Vegetative powers are entirely active yet lowest in the soul's hierarchy. Therefore intellectual powers should be entirely active.

### On the contrary
Aristotle states: "To understand is in a way to be passive."

### I answer that
Passivity admits three degrees. Most strictly, it means losing something belonging to one's nature. Less strictly, it means undergoing any alteration. Most broadly, it means transitioning from potentiality to actuality—receiving what one potentially was.

The human intellect, being the lowest in the intelligence hierarchy, exists in potentiality regarding intelligible things. Initially, we can only potentially understand; subsequently we understand actually. This progression constitutes passivity in the broadest sense—perfection of the receptive power.

### Reply to Objection 1
The objection applies to passivity in the strict sense, affecting primary matter. Passivity in the broad sense characterizes all beings receiving their perfection.

### Reply to Objection 2
The "passive intellect" (sensitive appetite or cogitative power) exhibits passivity in the strict senses, being acts of corporeal organs. The possible intellect exhibits passivity only in the broad sense, remaining incorruptible.

### Reply to Objection 3
The intellect is passive regarding universal being; vegetative power is active regarding a particular body. A passive power regarding universal things surpasses active power regarding particulars.

## Article 3: Whether there is an Active Intellect?

### Objection 1
Since the intellect stands to intelligibles as sense stands to sensibles, and sense needs no active principle, the intellect requires no active intellect.

### Objection 2
Light facilitates sight by illuminating the medium, not by actualizing colors. No comparable medium exists in intellection.

### Objection 3
The passive intellect's immaterial nature suffices for receiving forms immaterially. Forms become actually intelligible through immateriality itself.

### On the contrary
Aristotle teaches: "As in every nature, so in the soul is there something by which it becomes all things, and something by which it makes all things."

### I answer that
Plato held forms subsist independently of matter, already actually intelligible. Thus no active intellect was needed. Aristotle rejected this. Forms existing in matter are not actually intelligible. Since nothing transitions from potentiality to act except through something already actual, the soul requires a power to abstract intelligible species from material conditions. This power is the active intellect.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sensibles exist in actuality outside the soul; hence no active sense is needed. Intelligibles in sensible things are not actually intelligible; thus an active intellect becomes necessary.

### Reply to Objection 2
Whether light actualizes colors or illuminates the medium, it remains necessary for vision. Similarly, the active intellect remains necessary for understanding.

### Reply to Objection 3
If intelligibles existed actually in nature, the passive intellect's immateriality would suffice. Since they do not, the active intellect must actualize them through abstraction.

## Article 4: Whether the Active Intellect is Something in the Soul?

### Objection 1
The active intellect provides light for understanding. This exceeds the soul's capacity, being accomplished by "the true light that enlighteneth every man" (John 1:9).

### Objection 2
Aristotle states the active intellect "does not sometimes understand and sometimes not understand." But our soul alternates. Thus the active intellect transcends our soul.

### Objection 3
If both passive and active intellects belong to the soul, understanding would occur whenever one wished. Yet this is manifestly false.

### Objection 4
The active intellect is "substance in actual being" while the passive intellect exists in potentiality. One thing cannot simultaneously be in potentiality and act regarding the same thing.

### Objection 5
If the active intellect is a power in the soul, it must flow from the soul's essence. This contradicts the idea that it participates in a superior intellect.

### On the contrary
Aristotle declares: "It is necessary for these differences [passive and active intellect] to be in the soul."

### I answer that
The active intellect belongs to the human soul as a participatory power derived from a superior intellect. The human soul, being intellectual only by participation, imperfectly intellectual, and mutable in its understanding, necessarily depends upon a higher intellect. Just as each perfect natural thing possesses powers derived from universal causes, so the soul must possess an intrinsic power derived from the supreme intellect.

Experience confirms this: we perceive ourselves abstracting universal forms from particular conditions. No action belongs to anything except through a principle inherently formal. Therefore this abstractive power must inhere in the soul itself.

Aristotle compared the active intellect to light received in air; Plato compared the separate intellect to the sun. According to Christian doctrine, this superior intellect is God, the soul's Creator, from whom it derives intellectual illumination.

### Reply to Objection 1
The divine light acts universally; the human soul participates in a particular power.

### Reply to Objection 2
Our intermittent understanding results from the passive intellect's variability, not the active intellect's.

### Reply to Objection 3
The active intellect is not an object moving the passive intellect. Rather, it actuates objects for understanding. Besides the active intellect's presence, we require phantasms, proper sensory disposition, and practice.

### Reply to Objection 4
The soul, while actually immaterial, remains in potentiality regarding determinate species. Thus one power can actualize immaterial forms through abstraction while receiving them in potentiality.

### Reply to Objection 5
The soul's immaterial essence, created by the supreme intellect, naturally possesses the power of abstraction flowing from its own essence—derived from yet remaining intrinsic to the soul.

## Article 5: Whether the Active Intellect is One in All?

### Objection 1
What is separate from body is not multiplied by bodily number. The active intellect is separate; therefore it is one for all.

### Objection 2
The active intellect causes the universal, which is one in many. What causes unity must be more unified.

### Objection 3
All humans assent to first intellectual concepts. They assent through the active intellect. Therefore all share one active intellect.

### On the contrary
Aristotle compares the active intellect to light, which is not identical in various illuminated things.

### I answer that
If the active intellect were a separate substance, it would be one for all. But since it belongs to the soul as a power, and souls are multiplied according to humans' number, each human possesses his own active intellect.

### Reply to Objection 1
The active intellect is "separate" meaning not the act of a corporeal organ, not meaning a separate substance.

### Reply to Objection 2
The active intellect causes universals by abstracting from matter. It need not be numerically identical in all intelligences.

### Reply to Objection 3
All humans, sharing one species, enjoy in common the power enabling species-characteristic action. Yet this shared power need not be numerically identical, though derived from one principle—the separate intellect (God).

## Article 6: Whether Memory is in the Intellectual Part of the Soul?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that what is not common to humans and beasts belongs to the soul's higher part. Yet beasts possess memory.

### Objection 2
Memory pertains to the past, which involves fixed temporal condition. The intellect transcends "here" and "now."

### Objection 3
Memory preserves species not actually apprehended. But the intellect in act understands all things whose species it possesses.

### On the contrary
Augustine affirms: "Memory, understanding, and will are one mind."

### I answer that
Whether memory belongs to intellect depends on how we understand it. If memory means retaining species, it belongs to the intellectual part, since the intellect stably preserves intelligible species within itself. Avicenna incorrectly claimed the intellect cannot retain species apart from actual understanding.

However, if memory includes understanding the past as past—involving temporal conditions—it belongs only to the sensitive part. The intellect can understand its own acts of understanding, which occur in time; thus it grasps that it previously understood.

### Reply to Objection 1
Intellectual memory (as species-retention) is not common to beasts; it belongs uniquely to the intellect.

### Reply to Objection 2
The past is accidental to intellection's object but not to its act. The intellect understands itself understanding yesterday or tomorrow.

### Reply to Objection 3
Intelligible species exist in the intellect in three states: potentiality, habitual knowledge, and actualization. In the habitual state, the intellect retains species without actual understanding.

## Article 7: Whether Intellectual Memory Differs as a Power from the Intellect?

### Objection 1
Augustine assigns memory, understanding, and will as three powers. Since memory differs from will, it differs from intellect.

### Objection 2
Sensitive memory differs from sense as distinct powers. By parity, intellectual memory should differ from intellect.

### Objection 3
Augustine states memory, understanding, and will are equal and mutually flowing. This would be impossible if memory and intellect were identical.

### On the contrary
Aristotle attributes to the intellect the treasury-function belonging to memory. Therefore memory is not a distinct power.

### I answer that
Powers are distinguished by their objects' formal aspects. The intellect regards being universally. No difference of being differentiates it into multiple powers. Therefore memory is not a distinct power from intellect; retention belongs inherently to passive power's nature.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine does not mean three powers but rather: memory = soul's habit of retention; intelligence = intellect's act; will = will's act.

### Reply to Objection 2
Past and present differentiate sensitive powers but not intellectual powers.

### Reply to Objection 3
Intelligence flows from memory as act flows from habit—equal in significance but distinct as actualization versus potentiality, not as power versus power.

## Article 8: Whether Reason Differs as a Power from Intellect?

### Objection 1
One authority lists: sense, imagination, reason, then intellect—suggesting distinct powers.

### Objection 2
Boethius compares intellect to eternity and reason to time. Eternity and time are incompatible in one subject.

### Objection 3
Humans share intellect with angels and sense with beasts. Reason, proper to humans, should be distinct from intellect.

### On the contrary
Augustine identifies reason, mind, and intelligence as fundamentally the same.

### I answer that
Reason and intellect in humans cannot be distinct powers. To understand means simply apprehending intelligible truth. To reason means advancing from one understood thing to another. Angels apprehend truth directly; humans arrive at intelligible knowledge by advancing from one thing to another—hence called rational.

Reasoning relates to understanding as movement relates to rest. Since movement proceeds from immobility and terminates in rest, human reasoning advances from first principles (simply understood) and returns to them through analysis. Rest and movement belong to one nature; understanding and reasoning belong to one power.

### Reply to Objection 1
That enumeration orders actions, not powers.

### Reply to Objection 2
Boethius appropriately compared intellect to eternity and reason to time, illustrating their difference while confirming they belong to one power in different modes.

### Reply to Objection 3
Angelic knowledge is not generically different from human reason but represents the perfect form of the same cognitive type.

## Article 9: Whether Superior and Inferior Reason are Distinct Powers?

### Objection 1
Augustine locates the Trinity's image in superior reason, not inferior. Since soul-parts are powers, these are two powers.

### Objection 2
Nothing flows from itself. The inferior reason flows from and is ruled by the superior. Hence they are distinct powers.

### Objection 3
Aristotle distinguishes the scientific from the opinionative part. Necessary and contingent are generically different, suggesting distinct powers.

### Objection 4
Damascene describes a hierarchy: opinion from imagination, then mind judging, then intellect assenting.

### On the contrary
Augustine declares superior and inferior reason are "only distinct by their functions."

### I answer that
Superior and inferior reason cannot be two powers. Augustine defines superior reason as "intent on contemplating and consulting eternal things," and inferior reason as "intent on disposing temporal things." Through temporal things we ascend to eternal ones; from known eternals we judge temporal affairs.

Though principles and conclusions might belong to different habitual knowledge-types, reason's nature unites them. Therefore superior and inferior reason constitute one power distinguished by functional operations and various habits: wisdom characterizes the superior, science the inferior.

### Reply to Objection 1
"Parts" apply to any division. Superior and inferior reason are parts by functional division, not power-distinction.

### Reply to Objection 2
The inferior reason "flows from" the superior as principles drawn from the superior reason direct the inferior's application.

### Reply to Objection 3
The "scientific" part exceeds the inferior reason's scope. The intellect knows both necessary and contingent things under the common aspect of being and truth. Perfect and imperfect modes vary operations and habits, not the power itself.

### Reply to Objection 4
Damascene's distinction concerns operational varieties, not power-multiplicity.

## Article 10: Whether Intelligence Differs as a Power from Intellect?

### Objection 1
One source lists: sense, imagination, reason, intellect, then intelligence—suggesting intelligence is a distinct power.

### Objection 2
Boethius describes four distinct modes of considering humans. Since intellect equals reason, intelligence should be distinct.

### Objection 3
Intelligence represents an act distinct from others intellect performs.

### On the contrary
Aristotle teaches: "Intelligence is of indivisible things containing no falsity"—knowledge proper to intellect, not a separate power.

### I answer that
"Intelligence" properly designates the intellect's very act—understanding itself. Intelligence is not a distinct power from intellect but rather the distinction between act and potentiality. Philosophers sometimes enumerate four intellects: active, passive, intellect in habit, and actual intellect. The active and passive are truly different powers. The other three represent states of the passive intellect.

### Reply to Objection 1
"Intelligence" means the intellect's act, distinguished from intellect as potentiality—act versus power, not power versus power.

### Reply to Objection 2
Boethius means intelligence as the intellect's act transcending reason's act.

### Reply to Objection 3
Diverse acts belong to one intellectual power: first apprehension (intelligence); directing apprehension toward other knowledge (intention); pursuing what is intended (invention); examining findings by known-truths' light (wisdom/judgment); expressing findings (discourse). Diverse acts do not multiply powers unless incapable of reduction to one principle.

## Article 11: Whether Speculative and Practical Intellects are Distinct Powers?

### Objection 1
Apprehensive and motive powers differ fundamentally. Speculative intellect merely apprehends; practical intellect moves. Thus they are distinct.

### Objection 2
Speculative intellect's object is truth; practical intellect's is good—fundamentally different.

### Objection 3
In the sensitive part, estimative differs from imaginative. Likewise, practical intellect should differ from speculative.

### On the contrary
Aristotle teaches: "The speculative intellect becomes practical by extension." One power cannot transform into another.

### I answer that
Speculative and practical intellects are not distinct powers. Whether an apprehended thing is directed toward operation is accidental to the thing itself. The speculative intellect directs apprehension toward truth-contemplation; the practical intellect directs it toward operation. They differ in end, not in power itself.

### Reply to Objection 1
Practical intellect moves not by executing movement but by directing toward it—a mode of apprehension.

### Reply to Objection 2
Truth and good interpenetrate: truth is desirable good; good is intelligible truth. The practical intellect knows truth but directs it toward operation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Many differences differentiating sensitive powers do not differentiate intellectual powers.

## Article 12: Whether Synderesis is a Special Power Distinct from Others?

### Objection 1
Jerome divides synderesis against the irascible, concupiscible, and rational—which are powers. Thus synderesis is a power.

### Objection 2
Synderesis and sensuality oppose each other. Opposite things share a genus; thus synderesis is a power as sensuality is.

### Objection 3
Augustine describes unchangeable rules and virtue-seeds in natural judgment-power—this is synderesis.

### On the contrary
Synderesis regards only good, not opposites. If it were a rational power, it would regard opposites. Therefore synderesis is not a power.

### I answer that
Synderesis is not a power but a habit—specifically, a natural habit. Just as practical reason argues from discovered principles, it requires naturally bestowed practical principles. These first practical principles naturally known belong to a special natural habit called synderesis.

Synderesis "incites to good" and "murmurs against evil" through providing first principles enabling us to discover and judge our actions.

### Reply to Objection 1
Jerome's division concerns operational varieties, not power-distinctions.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sensuality and synderesis oppose each other as acts oppose, not as different genera-species.

### Reply to Objection 3
Unchangeable practical notions belong to reason as power and synderesis as habit.

## Article 13: Whether Conscience is a Power?

### Objection 1
Origen describes conscience as "a correcting, guiding spirit accompanying the soul." Since soul-spirit designates a power, conscience is a power.

### Objection 2
Only soul-powers are sin-subjects. Yet conscience is defiled by sin.

### Objection 3
Conscience must be act, habit, or power. It is not an act (not always existing). It is not one habit (many habits form conscience).

### On the contrary
Conscience can be set aside. Powers cannot be laid aside. Therefore conscience is not a power.

### I answer that
Conscience is properly an act, not a power. The very name "conscience" implies knowledge applied to something—"con-scientia" (knowledge-with). Applying knowledge to particular cases constitutes an act.

Conscience's attributed functions confirm this: witnessing, binding, inciting, accusing, tormenting, rebuking—all follow knowledge-application. This application occurs three ways: recognizing past action (witnessing); judging what should/shouldn't be done (binding/inciting); judging action as well/poorly done (excusing/accusing/tormenting).

However, since habit is action's principle, "conscience" sometimes names synderesis—the first natural habit from which conscience-acts flow.

### Reply to Objection 1
Conscience is called spirit as mind's pronouncement, not indicating a power.

### Reply to Objection 2
Conscience is defiled not as subject but as the thing known is within knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 3
Though acts don't persist intrinsically, they remain in their causes—power and habit. All conscience-forming habits gain efficacy from synderesis.
