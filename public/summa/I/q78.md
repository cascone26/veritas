# Question 78: The Specific Powers of the Soul

## Article 1: Whether Five Genera of Powers are Distinguished in the Soul

### Objection 1
The soul has only three parts—vegetative, sensitive, and rational—so only three power categories exist, not five.

### Objection 2
Life occurs in four ways according to Aristotle: intellect, sense, local movement, and nourishment. Therefore only four power genera should exist, excluding the appetitive.

### Objection 3
Desire is common to all soul powers. Each power naturally desires its appropriate object. Therefore, appetitive power should not constitute a distinct genus.

### Objection 4
Sense, intellect, and appetite are the moving principles in animals. Movement thus depends on existing powers and should not be distinguished separately.

### On the contrary
Aristotle identifies "five powers: vegetative, sensitive, appetitive, locomotion, and intellectual."

### I answer that
Five genera of powers exist, distinguished by how soul operations transcend corporeal nature. The rational soul's operation requires no bodily organ. The sensitive soul operates through organs but not through corporeal qualities alone. The vegetative soul operates through corporeal organs and qualities yet differs from mere physical bodies by possessing intrinsic motion.

Soul powers are distinguished by their objects. Vegetative powers act only upon the united body. Sensitive powers extend to all sensible bodies. Intellectual powers grasp universal being. External things relate to the soul twofold: as naturally capable of union through their likenesses (producing sensitive and intellectual powers); as objects of soul's inclination (producing appetitive and locomotive powers).

### Reply to Objection 1
The three soul types correspond to operations transcending corporeal nature in distinct ways. The five powers represent different operational principles.

### Reply to Objection 2
Four modes of living do not preclude five power genera. Appetitive power does not constitute a separate living mode.

### Reply to Objection 3
Natural appetite—each thing's inherent inclination toward what suits it—differs from animal appetite arising from apprehended forms. The latter requires a distinct power since desire operates upon things not merely for sensing them but for other purposes.

### Reply to Objection 4
Though sense and appetite move perfect animals, they require an additional motive power. Immobile animals possess sensation and appetite without locomotion.

## Article 2: Whether the Vegetative Soul's Powers are Nutritive, Augmentative, and Generative

### Objection 1
These forces are called natural forces, but soul powers exceed natural forces. Thus they should not be classed as soul powers.

### Objection 2
Generation occurs in both living and non-living things. Therefore, it should not be assigned as a soul power.

### Objection 3
The soul surpasses the body. If bodies give species and quantity through one force, the soul achieves both through one power. The augmentative power is not distinct from the generative.

### Objection 4
What preserves a thing is that by which it exists. The generative power produces life; therefore it preserves it. The nutritive force directs toward preservation, so these powers are indistinguishable.

### On the contrary
Aristotle cites three operations: "generation, use of food, and growth."

### I answer that
Three vegetative powers exist. The vegetative part requires three operations for its object—the living body. Generation provides existence; augmentation provides proper quantity; nutrition preserves existence and quantity.

These powers differ significantly: nutritive and augmentative powers effect change in the same united body; generative power effects change in another body, thus approaching the sensitive soul's dignity. The generative power possesses greater nobility, as the perfect thing produces its like. Augmentative and nutritive powers serve the generative power; nutrition serves augmentation.

### Reply to Objection 1
These forces are called natural because they produce effects similar to nature's operations and function instrumentally through active and passive qualities.

### Reply to Objection 2
Living generation differs from inanimate generation by occurring through something within the living thing—the semen containing productive principle.

### Reply to Objection 3
Living generation produces initially small organisms requiring augmentative power to reach proper size.

### Reply to Objection 4
The vegetative principle operates through heat, which consumes humidity. The nutritive power restores consumed moisture by converting food into body substance.

## Article 3: Whether Five Exterior Senses are Properly Distinguished

### Objection 1
Many accident types exist. If powers diversify by objects, senses multiply according to accident kinds, yielding more than five.

### Objection 2
Common sensibles (magnitude, shape) differ from proper sensibles. These require separate sensory powers more than color or sound warrant division.

### Objection 3
One sense grasps one contrariety. Touch grasps multiple contraries (hot/cold, wet/dry). Therefore, touch comprises multiple senses.

### Objection 4
Taste constitutes a type of touch, so it should not be classed as distinct.

### On the contrary
Aristotle states: "There is no other besides the five senses."

### I answer that
The distinction basis lies in what senses perceive per se: the exterior cause of sensory alteration. Immutation is natural or spiritual. Natural immutation receives the form according to natural existence; spiritual immutation receives it according to spiritual existence.

Sensing requires spiritual immutation whereby sensible form creates an intention in the sense organ. Some senses involve spiritual immutation only (sight); others involve both spiritual and natural immutation. Sound involves natural motion. Odor involves natural alteration. Touch and taste involve natural organ alteration.

Sight ranks highest—most spiritual, perfect, and universal. Hearing and smell follow, requiring natural object immutation. Touch and taste prove most material, involving organ immutation.

### Reply to Objection 1
Only third-species qualities cause alteration and serve as sense objects.

### Reply to Objection 2
Common sensibles (magnitude, shape, movement) reduce to quantity. They do not affect senses per se but through sensible qualities.

### Reply to Objection 3
Touch forms one generic sense distributed throughout the body. All contraries share a common unnamed genus and proximate unnamed genus.

### Reply to Objection 4
Taste exists only in the tongue and differs from distributed touch. Touch involves natural organ immutation; taste's organ immutation occurs through moisture—the preliminary quality—not through flavor itself.

## Article 4: Whether Interior Senses are Suitably Distinguished

### Objection 1
Common sense should not be numbered with proper senses, as common divides against proper.

### Objection 2
Proper exterior senses suffice for judgment and perceiving sensations. The common sense power is superfluous.

### Objection 3
Imagination and memory are passions of the first sense, not distinct powers.

### Objection 4
The intellect depends less on senses than sensitive powers yet knows nothing without them. Therefore, the estimative power seems less justified.

### Objection 5
Cogitative and reminiscent actions differ from estimative and memorative actions as much as estimative differs from imagination. Either these should be added as powers or estimative and memorative should not be distinct.

### Objection 6
Augustine describes three visions: corporeal (sense), spiritual (imagination/phantasy), and intellectual (intellect), leaving no interior power between sense and intellect except imagination.

### On the contrary
Avicenna identifies five interior sensitive powers: "common sense, phantasy, imagination, estimative and memorative powers."

### I answer that
Perfect animal life requires multiple sensitive operations. A soul power constitutes the proximate operation principle.

Perfect animals apprehend things absent from current sensation, enabling motion toward absent objects. The sensitive soul must both receive sensible species under actual sensation and retain them. Reception and retention reduce to distinct principles: moist things receive easily but retain poorly; dry things retain well but receive poorly.

Animals must perceive intentions the exterior sense does not perceive. The sheep flees the wolf not from color or shape but recognizing it as a natural enemy. The bird gathers straw not for sensible pleasure but for nest-building utility.

Thus four interior powers operate: (1) Common sense and proper sense receive sensible forms; (2) Phantasy/imagination stores received forms; (3) Estimative power apprehends non-sensible intentions; (4) Memorative power preserves such intentions.

In humans, the "cogitative" power perceives intentions through idea coalition, combining individual intentions like the intellect combines universals. Humans possess memory like animals (sudden past recollection) and reminiscence (syllogistic past seeking through individual intentions). Therefore, four interior sensitive powers suffice: common sense, imagination, estimative, and memorative powers.

### Reply to Objection 1
Common sense is termed "common" not through predication as genus but as the common root and principle of exterior senses.

### Reply to Objection 2
Proper senses discriminate their sensibles from others. However, no proper sense discriminates between different sense categories (white from sweet). The common sense provides common reference for all sensory apprehensions and perceives sensations themselves.

### Reply to Objection 3
As one soul power arises through another, the soul serves as subject for one power through another. Thus imagination and memory are called passions of the first sensitive power.

### Reply to Objection 4
Intellect's operations originate in senses yet apprehend many things senses cannot perceive. Similarly, estimative power apprehends beyond sense perception.

### Reply to Objection 5
Human cogitative and memorative powers excel not through distinctive sensitive properties but through affinity and proximity to universal reason overflowing into them. They remain the same powers, perfected beyond other animals.

### Reply to Objection 6
Augustine calls spiritual vision that effected by body images absent bodies—common to all interior apprehensions.
