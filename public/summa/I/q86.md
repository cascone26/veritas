# Question 86: What Our Intellect Knows in Material Things

## Article 1: Whether Our Intellect Knows Singulars

### Objection 1
The intellect must know singulars because it understands composition. When we form "Socrates is a man," we know the singular Socrates.

### Objection 2
The practical intellect directs action, which concerns singular things.

### Objection 3
The intellect understands itself, and it is singular.

### Objection 4
Superior powers can perform what inferior powers do. Sense knows singulars, so the intellect can.

### On the contrary
Aristotle states: "the universal is known by reason; and the singular is known by sense."

### I answer that
The intellect cannot directly know singulars in material things. Singularity derives from individual matter, but the intellect understands by abstracting intelligible species from matter. What is abstracted becomes universal.

The intellect directly knows universals through intelligible species. However, it indirectly knows singulars through reflection on phantasms. Even after abstraction, understanding requires turning toward phantasms. Thus "Socrates is a man" forms through universal knowledge and indirect singular apprehension.

### Reply to Objection 1
This clarifies how the intellect forms propositions about singulars.

### Reply to Objection 2
Universal principles move only through sensitive particular apprehension.

### Reply to Objection 3
An immaterial singular presents no barrier to being understood.

### Reply to Objection 4
The intellect knows the singular immaterially and abstractly—as universal—whereas sense knows it materially and concretely.

## Article 2: Whether Our Intellect Can Know the Infinite

### Objection 1
God exceeds all infinite things, yet the intellect knows God.

### Objection 2
Infinite species exist in some genera. Therefore, the intellect knows the infinite.

### Objection 3
Multiple intelligible species can coexist as habitual knowledge.

### Objection 4
The intellect is non-corporeal and thus infinite in power.

### On the contrary
Aristotle states: "the infinite, considered as such, is unknown."

### I answer that
In material things, the infinite exists only potentially, with one thing succeeding another. Thus infinity exists potentially in our mind through successive consideration.

The intellect cannot understand the infinite actually because actual understanding requires comprehension through one species. The infinite cannot be represented by one species. Habitually knowing the infinite requires having already considered the entire infinity—an impossibility.

### Reply to Objection 1
God is called infinite as form unlimited by matter. The material infinite is unknowable; the formal infinite (God) is self-known but unknown to us naturally.

### Reply to Objection 2
Our mind knows species only through phantasms—a potential, confused knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 3
Intelligible species enter the intellect successively; a definite, not infinite, number occupy the intellect.

### Reply to Objection 4
The intellect's infinite power knows the infinite through universals abstracted from individual matter, extending indifferently to infinite individuals.

## Article 3: Whether Our Intellect Can Know Contingent Things

### Objection 1
Understanding, wisdom, and knowledge concern "not contingent, but necessary things."

### Objection 2
The intellect abstracts from time and material conditions. Contingent things escape intellectual knowledge.

### On the contrary
Moral sciences concern contingent human actions subject to free will. Natural sciences address generated and corruptible things.

### I answer that
Contingent things can be viewed two ways: as contingent or containing necessity. Every contingent thing possesses some necessary element. Contingency arises from matter; necessity derives from form. Matter individualizes; universals come from abstracting form.

The contingent as such is known directly by sense and indirectly by intellect. Universal and necessary principles of contingent things are known by intellect alone. Some sciences address necessary matters, others contingent.

## Article 4: Whether Our Intellect Can Know the Future

### Objection 1
Intelligible species relate indifferently to all time. The intellect knows the present, so it can know the future.

### Objection 2
Humans in sleep and frenzy know future things. The intellect is freer from sense.

### Objection 3
Human knowledge surpasses brute knowledge. Animals foresee futures through signs.

### On the contrary
Scripture states man "is ignorant of things past; and things to come he cannot know by any messenger."

### I answer that
Future things as subject to time are singular; the human intellect knows them only indirectly. The future can be known in itself (only by God, who sees all time eternally) or in its cause. If the cause produces necessary effects, the future is known with scientific certainty. If the cause produces results more often than not, the future is known conjecturally.

### Reply to Objection 1
This concerns knowledge derived from universal causal principles.

### Reply to Objection 2
Knowing the future while withdrawn from senses comes from superior spiritual or corporeal causes—through angelic illumination, demonic influence, or heavenly bodies' influence generating images in sleep.

### Reply to Objection 3
Brutes lack reason regulating imagination, so they follow heavenly bodies' influence entirely. Humans predict futures through reason differently.
