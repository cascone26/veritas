# Question 64: The Punishment of the Demons

## Article 1: Whether the demons' intellect is darkened by privation of the knowledge of all truth?

### Objection 1
If demons knew any truth, they would especially know themselves as separated substances. But knowledge of separated substances belongs to great happiness, which seems contrary to their wretchedness. Therefore demons are deprived of all knowledge of truth.

### Objection 2
What is most manifest in its nature should especially be manifest to angels. God is most manifest yet demons cannot know Him because they lack the purity of heart required. Therefore they cannot know other things either.

### Objection 3
According to Augustine, angels possess twofold knowledge: morning and evening. Demons have no morning knowledge because they do not see things in the Word, nor evening knowledge because this knowledge refers things to God's praise. Therefore demons have no knowledge of things.

### Objection 4
All angels at creation knew the mystery of God's kingdom. But demons are deprived of such knowledge, for "if they had known it, they would never have crucified the Lord of glory" (1 Corinthians 2:8). Therefore they are deprived of all knowledge of truth.

### Objection 5
Truth is known either naturally, by learning, or by experience. Demons cannot know truth naturally because good angels are separated from them as light from darkness. They cannot learn from good angels because there is no fellowship of light with darkness. They cannot learn by experience because experience comes from senses. Therefore demons have no knowledge of truth.

### On the contrary
Dionysius states that "certain gifts were bestowed upon the demons which have not been changed at all, but remain entire and most brilliant." Knowledge of truth stands among those natural gifts. Consequently demons possess some knowledge of truth.

### I answer that
Knowledge of truth is threefold: natural knowledge, speculative knowledge from grace, and effective knowledge producing love of God (the gift of wisdom).

The first kind—natural knowledge—was neither taken away nor lessened in demons. Since an angel is by nature an intellect, and his substance is simple, nothing can be withdrawn from his natural powers as punishment. Therefore, as Dionysius says, natural gifts remain entire in demons, and their natural knowledge was not diminished.

The second kind—speculative knowledge from grace—has not been utterly taken away but lessened. Only so much Divine knowledge is revealed to demons as is necessary, through angels or temporal workings of Divine power, but not as fully as to holy angels.

Of the third kind, and likewise of charity, demons are utterly deprived.

### Reply to Objection 1
Happiness consists in self-application to something higher. While separated substances are above humans in the natural order, man's perfect happiness consists in knowing God. For one separate substance to know another is natural, just as it is natural for humans to know sensible natures. Thus the angel's happiness does not consist in knowing separated substances.

### Reply to Objection 2
What is most manifest in nature is hidden from us because it surpasses our intellect's capacity. God's substance surpasses not only human but also angelic intellect proportionally. Yet demons, possessing angelic intellect, can have a higher natural knowledge of God than humans can. Though lacking grace's purity, they possess natural purity sufficient for their natural knowledge of God.

### Reply to Objection 3
The creature is darkness compared to Divine light; therefore creatures' natural knowledge is called "evening" knowledge. When this knowledge is referred to God's praise, as in good angels, it retains something of Divine light. But when not referred to God, as with demons, it becomes "nocturnal" knowledge, for darkness itself is called night.

### Reply to Objection 4
All angels initially possessed some knowledge of God's kingdom mystery, though not equally. Demons understood it much less fully than holy angels. Augustine observes it was made known to demons through temporal effects to strike terror into them, not clearly as to holy angels. Had they fully and certainly known Christ's divinity and Passion's effect, they would never have procured His crucifixion.

### Reply to Objection 5
Demons know truth three ways: first, by their nature's subtlety, enlightened by intellectual nature despite lacking grace's light; second, by revelation from holy angels, accepting what they manifest through intellectual likeness; third, by experience—not from senses but when innate intelligible species complete in individual things, knowing some things as present that they previously did not foresee.

## Article 2: Whether the will of the demons is obstinate in evil?

### Objection 1
Free will belongs to the intellectual being's nature, which remains in demons. Free will is primarily ordained to good rather than evil. Therefore demons' will is not so obstinate in evil as to be unable to return to good.

### Objection 2
God's mercy is infinite while demons' malice is finite. No one returns from sin's malice to justice's goodness except through God's mercy. Therefore demons can likewise return from malice to justice.

### Objection 3
If demons' will were obstinate in evil, it would especially be obstinate in the pride whereby they fell. But this sin no longer motivates them since excellence no longer endures. Therefore the demon is not obstinate in malice.

### Objection 4
Gregory says man can be reinstated by another since he fell through another. Lower demons fell through the highest one. Therefore their fall can be repaired by another, so they are not obstinate in malice.

### Objection 5
Whoever is obstinate in malice never performs good works. But demons perform some: they confess truth, saying to Christ "I know Who Thou art, the holy one of God" (Mark 1:24). Demons "believe and tremble" (James 2:19). Dionysius observes they "desire what is good and best, which is, to be, to live, to understand." Therefore they are not obstinate in malice.

### On the contrary
Psalm 73:23 states: "The pride of them that hate Thee, ascendeth continually," understood of demons. Therefore they remain ever obstinate in their malice.

### I answer that
Origen's opinion that every creature's will can incline toward good and evil (except Christ's soul through the Word's union) deprives angels and saints of true beatitude, since everlasting stability belongs to beatitude's very nature—hence called "life everlasting." This contradicts Scripture, which declares demons and wicked men sent "into everlasting punishment" while the good enter "everlasting life." Such opinion is erroneous; Catholic Faith firmly holds that good angels' will is confirmed in good and demons' will is obstinate in evil.

The cause lies not in sin's gravity but in their nature's condition or state. As Damascene says, "death is to men, what the fall is to the angels." All human mortal sins, grave or less grave, are pardonable before death; after death, they are irremissible and endure forever.

The appetitive power is proportioned to the apprehensive power that moves it. Sensitive appetite seeks particular good while will seeks universal good. An angel apprehends immovably, as humans apprehend first principles immovably; whereas humans apprehend movably through reason, proceeding from one consideration to another, open to either opposite.

Consequently man's will adheres movably, with power to forsake and cling to opposites; the angel's will adheres fixedly and immovably. Man's free will is flexible to opposites before and after choice; the angel's is flexible to opposites before choice but not after. Therefore good angels adhering to justice were confirmed therein; wicked ones, sinning, are obstinate in sin.

### Reply to Objection 1
Good and wicked angels possess free will according to their state's manner and condition, as stated.

### Reply to Objection 2
God's mercy delivers from sin those who repent. But such as are incapable of repenting, clinging immovably to sin, are not delivered by Divine mercy.

### Reply to Objection 3
The devil's first sin remains in him according to desire, though not believing he can obtain what he desired. Even so, if a man believed he could murder and willed it, then power was taken from him, the will to murder can remain—he would wish he had done it or would do it if able.

### Reply to Objection 4
That man sinned from another's suggestion is not the whole cause of man's sin being pardonable. Therefore the argument does not hold.

### Reply to Objection 5
A demon's act is twofold. One comes from deliberate will, properly called his own act. Such acts by demons are always wicked; though they sometimes do something good, they do not do it well—telling truth to deceive, believing and confessing unwillingly, compelled by evidence. Another act is natural to demons and can be good, witnessing to nature's goodness. Yet demons abuse even such good acts to evil purpose.

## Article 3: Whether there is sorrow in the demons?

### Objection 1
Sorrow and joy are opposites and cannot coexist in the same subject. Augustine writes that the devil rejoices over sinners under his power. Therefore there is no sorrow in demons.

### Objection 2
Sorrow causes fear; things causing fear when future cause sorrow when present. But there is no fear in demons, per Job 41:24: "Who was made to fear no one." Therefore there is no grief in demons.

### Objection 3
It is good to be sorry for evil. But demons can do no good action. Therefore they cannot be sorry, especially for sin's evil—the conscience's worm.

### On the contrary
The demon's sin is graver than man's. Man is punished with sorrow for pleasure taken in sin, per Apocalypse 18:7: "As much as she hath glorified herself, and lived in delicacies, so much torment and sorrow give ye to her." Consequently the devil, glorifying himself especially, is much more punished with sorrow's grief.

### I answer that
Fear, sorrow, joy, and similar emotions, insofar as they are passions, cannot exist in demons, being proper to sensitive appetite in corporeal organs. However, insofar as they denote simple will acts, they can exist in demons.

Sorrow, denoting a simple will act, is nothing but the will's resistance to what is or what is not. Clearly demons wish many things not to exist that do exist, and others to exist that do not—out of envy wishing others' damnation. Consequently sorrow must be said to exist in them, especially since punishment's very notion requires repugnance to the will.

Moreover, demons are deprived of happiness they desire naturally; their wicked will is curbed in many respects.

### Reply to Objection 1
Joy and sorrow about the same thing are opposites, not about different things. Hence nothing prevents being sorry for one thing and joyful for another; especially insofar as sorrow and joy imply simple will acts—even in one thing, something we will and something we will not can coexist.

### Reply to Objection 2
As there is sorrow in demons over present evil, so there is fear of future evil. "He was made to fear no one" means fear of God restraining from sin. Elsewhere Scripture states "the devils believe and tremble" (James 2:19).

### Reply to Objection 3
Being sorry for sin's evil on sin's account witnesses to the will's goodness, to which sin's evil opposes. Being sorry for punishment's evil on sin's account witnesses to nature's goodness, to which punishment's evil opposes. Augustine states "sorrow for good lost by punishment, is the witness to a good nature."

Consequently, since demons possess perverse and obstinate will, they are not sorry for sin's evil.

## Article 4: Whether our atmosphere is the demons' place of punishment?

### Objection 1
A demon is a spiritual nature. Spiritual nature is not affected by place. Therefore there is no place of punishment for demons.

### Objection 2
Man's sin is not graver than demons'. Man's punishment place is hell. Much more should it be demons' punishment place, not the darksome atmosphere.

### Objection 3
Demons are punished with fire's pain. There is no fire in the darksome atmosphere. Therefore the darksome atmosphere is not demons' punishment place.

### On the contrary
Augustine states "the darksome atmosphere is as a prison to the demons until the judgment day."

### I answer that
Angels stand midway between God and humans in their nature. Divine providence orders that inferior orders' welfare proceeds through superior orders. Man's welfare is disposed by Divine providence two ways: first, directly, when brought to good and withheld from evil through good angels; second, indirectly, when assailed and exercised fighting opposition.

Fittingly, the wicked spirits procured man's welfare through opposition, lest they cease serving the natural order. Consequently twofold punishment is due demons: one by reason of their sin—hell; another, that they may tempt men—the darksome atmosphere.

Men's salvation prolonging until judgment day, the angels' ministry and wrestling with demons endure until then. Hence good angels are sent to us here until then; demons are in this dark atmosphere for our trial, though some are now in hell tormenting those they led astray, just as some good angels remain with holy souls in heaven.

After judgment day, all wicked ones—men and angels—will be in hell; the good in heaven.

### Reply to Objection 1
A place is not penal to angel or soul by affecting nature through changing it, but by affecting will through saddening it—the angel or soul apprehending it is in a place disagreeable to its will.

### Reply to Objection 2
One soul is not set over another in nature's order as demons are over humans in the natural order; consequently there is no parallel.

### Reply to Objection 3
Some maintained that sense pain for demons and souls is postponed until judgment day, and saints' beatitude likewise. But this is erroneous, contrary to the Apostle (2 Corinthians 5:1): "If our earthly house of this habitation be dissolved, we have a house in heaven." Others admit this of demons but not souls. But better to say the same judgment applies to wicked souls and angels as to good ones.

Consequently, though a heavenly place belongs to angels' glory, their glory is not lessened when visiting us—they consider that place their own, as a bishop's honor is not lessened when absent from his throne.

Similarly, though demons are not actually bound within hell's fire while in this dark atmosphere, their punishment is undiminished—they know such confinement is their due. Hence it is said in a gloss upon James 3:6: "They carry fire of hell with them wherever they go." This does not contradict Luke 8:31, where they beseech not to be cast into the abyss—they asked this deeming confinement there a punishment, preferring places where they could injure men. Hence Mark 5:10 states they besought Him not to expel them out of the country.
