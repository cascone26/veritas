# Question 70: The Work of Adornment, as Regards the Fourth Day

## Article 1: Whether the lights ought to have been produced on the fourth day?

### Objection 1
Heavenly luminaries possess incorruptible bodies by nature, meaning their matter cannot exist without form. Since their matter was produced during creation before any day existed, their forms must have been created then too. Therefore, the lights were not produced on the fourth day.

### Objection 2
The luminaries serve as vessels of light. Light was made on the first day, so luminaries should have been made then, not on the fourth day.

### Objection 3
Lights are fixed in the firmament as plants are fixed in earth. Scripture states God "set them in the firmament." Plants were produced when earth received its form, so lights should have been produced with the firmament on the second day.

### Objection 4
Plants are effects of the sun, moon, and heavenly bodies. Since causes precede effects in nature's order, the lights ought to have been produced before the third day, not on the fourth.

### Objection 5
Astronomers observe that many stars are larger than the moon. Therefore, calling only the sun and moon "two great lights" is incorrect.

### On the contrary
Scripture's authority suffices.

### I answer that
Divine works are threefold: creation (producing heaven and earth without form), distinction (perfecting them through substantial form or ordering), and adornment (producing movable things). The work of adornment parallels distinction's structure. As distinction occurred over three days—heaven on day one, waters separated on day two, earth divided on day three—adornment similarly unfolds: lights adorn heaven on the fourth day; birds and fishes adorn the intermediate element on the fifth day; animals adorn earth on the sixth day.

Augustine's view that lights were made actually (not merely virtually) differs from others only in interpretation. The firmament lacks power to produce lights as earth produces plants, hence Scripture says "Let the firmament produce lights" never appears.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine holds no temporal succession in these works, so the lights' matter need not pre-exist under another form. Those viewing heavenly bodies as elemental may say they formed from existing matter, like animals and plants. For those viewing heavenly bodies as naturally incorruptible and non-elemental, lights were substantially created initially but received determination of power (not substantial form) on the fourth day. Chrysostom explains why lights are not mentioned as existing from the beginning: protecting people from idolatry, since proven non-gods by their non-eternity.

### Reply to Objection 2
Following Augustine, the first day's light is spiritual; the fourth day's is corporeal. Alternatively, if both are corporeal, the first day produced light generally; the fourth day granted lights determinate power for specific effects. Dionysius states the sun's previously formless light received form on the fourth day.

### Reply to Objection 3
Ptolemy held heavenly luminaries were not fixed in spheres but possessed distinct movement. Chrysostom notes God "set them" in the firmament meaning He commanded them there, as He placed man in Paradise. Aristotle held stars fixed in orbits with only sphere movement, yet senses perceive luminaries' movement, not spheres'. Moses describes what sense perceives, accommodating popular ignorance. If higher and lower firmaments exist naturally (though indistinguishably), the lower was made on day two; stars were fixed in the higher firmament on day four.

### Reply to Objection 4
Basil explains plants were recorded before sun and moon to prevent idolatry, since those viewing heavenly bodies as gods attribute plant origin primarily to them. Chrysostom notes the sun, moon, and stars cooperate in production through movement, as husbandmen cooperate through labor.

### Reply to Objection 5
Chrysostom states the two lights are called great regarding influence and power, not dimensions. Though stars exceed the moon in bulk, the moon's influence is more perceptibly felt in this lower world, and its apparent size appears greater to senses.

## Article 2: Whether the cause assigned for the production of lights is reasonable?

### Objection 1
Jeremiah warns: "Be not afraid of the signs of heaven, which the heathens fear." Therefore heavenly lights were not made to be signs.

### Objection 2
Signs are contradistinguished from causes. Yet lights cause what occurs on earth. Therefore they are not signs.

### Objection 3
The distinction of seasons and days began from the first day. Therefore lights were not made "for seasons, and days, and years" to distinguish them.

### Objection 4
Nothing is made for what is inferior to itself, since "the end is better than the means." But lights are nobler than earth. Therefore they were not made "to enlighten it."

### Objection 5
The new moon cannot "rule the night." But probably it did when first made, since men count from new moon. Therefore the moon was not made "to rule the night."

### On the contrary
Scripture's authority suffices.

### I answer that
Corporeal creatures serve either for their proper acts, other creatures, the universe, or God's glory. Moses addresses only usefulness to man, withdrawing his people from idolatry. Deuteronomy explains God "created" heavenly bodies "for the service of all nations." Genesis describes this service as threefold.

First, regarding sight directing human work: "Let them shine in the firmament and give life to the earth."

Second, regarding seasonal changes preventing weariness, preserving health, and providing food necessities: "Let them be for seasons, and for days, and years."

Third, regarding work and business convenience, indicating weather favorable to occupations: "Let them be for signs."

### Reply to Objection 1
Heavenly lights are set as signs of corporeal changes, not those depending on free will.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sensible causes bring knowledge of hidden effects conversely. Hence sensible causes can be signs. "Signs" is said rather than "causes" to guard against idolatry.

### Reply to Objection 3
The general time division into day and night occurred on day one regarding diurnal movement common to all heaven. Particular distinctions—one day hotter than another, seasonal variations, yearly differences—result from stars' particular movements beginning on day four.

### Reply to Objection 4
Light serves man who, by his soul, is nobler than heavenly bodies. It is not untrue that higher creatures are made for lower ones considered as ordained to the universe's good.

### Reply to Objection 5
When full, the moon rises in evening and sets in morning, thus ruling night, and was probably made in full perfection as seed-yielding plants, animals, and man were. Though perfection develops from imperfection naturally, perfection must exist simply before imperfection. Augustine, however, denies this, saying God fittingly made imperfect things which He later perfected.

## Article 3: Whether the lights of heaven are living beings?

### Objection 1
Nobler bodies deserve nobler adornment. Bodies less noble than heaven are adorned with living beings—fish, birds, beasts. Therefore heaven's lights, adorning it, should be living beings.

### Objection 2
Nobler bodies require nobler forms. Sun, moon, and stars are nobler than plants or animals and must have nobler forms. The noblest form is soul, the first life principle. Augustine states: "Every living substance stands higher in nature's order." Therefore heaven's lights are living beings.

### Objection 3
Causes are nobler than effects. Sun, moon, and stars cause life, especially in animals generated from putrefaction receiving life from their power. Much more, heavenly bodies possess living souls.

### Objection 4
Heaven and heavenly bodies' movement is natural, from intrinsic principles. The movement principle is a substance capable of apprehension, moved as desire moves the desirous. Therefore the apprehending principle is intrinsic to heavenly bodies, making them living.

### Objection 5
Heaven is the first movable. Of endowed-with-movement things, the first moves itself, preceding what moves by another. Only living beings move themselves. Therefore heavenly bodies are living beings.

### On the contrary
Damascene states: "Let no one esteem the heavens or heavenly bodies to be living things, for they have neither life nor sense."

### I answer that
Philosophers disagreed on this. Anaxagoras, condemned by Athenians, taught the sun was fiery stone, neither god nor living. Platonists held heavenly bodies possessed life. Church Doctors also diverged: Origen and Jerome believed bodies were alive; Basil and Damascene maintained they were inanimate; Augustine left it uncertain without committing himself, though suggesting angelic natures if they possess souls.

Examining this where such diversity exists, consider that soul-body union exists for the soul's sake, not the body's; form exists not for matter but matter for form. Soul's nature and power are apprehended through operations—its end. Sensation and nutrition require bodies as necessary instruments; hence sensitive and nutritive souls must unite to bodies for function. Some operations transcend bodily mediation, though bodies minister to their production. The intellect uses sense-derived phantasms, depending on body while capable of existing apart.

Heavenly bodies cannot perform nutrition, growth, and generation—incompatible with naturally incorruptible bodies. Heavenly bodies cannot exercise sensitive soul functions; all senses depend on touch perceiving elemental qualities, and sensory organs require elemental proportion, whereas heavenly nature is non-elemental. Thus only understanding and moving operations remain attributable to heavenly bodies; appetite follows both sensitive and intellectual perception proportionally.

Intellect operations need bodies only for phantasm supply through senses. Sensitive soul operations cannot attribute to heavenly bodies. Therefore, soul-heavenly body union cannot purpose intellectual operations. Only movement remains: whether it demands souls as motive power, not uniting as form but contacting as power—a mover unites to moved.

Aristotle shows the first mover comprises moving and moved parts, demonstrating their union. This mutual contact occurs if both are bodies; if one is body and one is not, the immaterial one contacts the body. Platonists explain soul-body union identically: spiritual substances contact moved bodies as moving power. Heavenly bodies' movement by direct spiritual substance influence and contact, not by nature (which moves to fixed ends then rests), indicates they are moved by intellectual substances. Augustine similarly believes all corporeal things are ruled by God through life's spirit.

Therefore, heavenly bodies are not living beings as plants and animals are; if called such, it is equivocally. The opinion difference between affirming and denying heavenly body life is merely verbal, not substantial.

### Reply to Objection 1
Certain adornment elements belong to the universe through proper movement; heavenly luminaries agree with others adorning it, being moved by living substances.

### Reply to Objection 2
One being may be nobler absolutely than another, but not in particular respects. While heavenly body souls are not absolutely nobler than animal souls, they are superior regarding their respective forms, since their form perfectly perfects their matter (not in potentiality to other forms) whereas animal soul does not. Also, the power moving heavenly bodies is nobler regarding movement.

### Reply to Objection 3
Since heavenly bodies are moved movers, they are instruments acting in virtue of agents. Therefore, since this agent is a living substance, the heavenly body imparts life through that agent.

### Reply to Objection 4
Heavenly movements are natural regarding their passive principle—a natural aptitude for intelligent power movement—not their active principle.

### Reply to Objection 5
Heaven moves itself as composed of mover and moved, not uniting mover as form with moved as matter, but contacting motive power. The moving principle may be called intrinsic, making movement natural regarding that active principle, as voluntary movement is natural to animals.
