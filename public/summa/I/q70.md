# Question 70: The Work of Adornment, as Regards the Fourth Day

## Article 1: Whether the Lights Ought to Have Been Produced on the Fourth Day?

### Objection 1
Heavenly luminaries are incorruptible bodies by nature, so their matter cannot exist without form. Since their matter was produced during creation before any day existed, their forms must have been too. Therefore, the lights were not produced on the fourth day.

### Objection 2
Luminaries serve as vessels for light. Light was made on the first day, so luminaries should have been made then, not the fourth day.

### Objection 3
Lights are fixed in the firmament like plants are fixed in earth. Since plants appeared when earth received its form on day three, lights should have appeared when the firmament received its form on day two.

### Objection 4
Plants result from the sun, moon, and stars. Causes precede effects in the natural order, so these heavenly bodies should have appeared before day three.

### Objection 5
Astronomers confirm many stars exceed the moon in size, so calling only the sun and moon "two great lights" seems incorrect.

### On the Contrary
"The authority of Scripture suffices."

### I Answer That
Scripture describes God's work as threefold: creation (producing formless heaven and earth), distinction (perfecting them), and adornment (adding external embellishments). Just as distinction involved three days forming three things, adornment also spans three days. On day four—the first day of adornment—the lights were produced "to adorn the heaven by their movements." Day five brought forth birds and fishes to beautify air and water. Day six produced animals to ornament the earth.

Notably, Augustine's teaching differs from other Church writers regarding how lights were produced. Augustine claims they were made actually, not merely virtually, since "the firmament has not the power of producing lights, as the earth has of producing plants."

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine sees no difficulty since he rejects temporal succession in creation. Others holding heavenly bodies as elemental can say they formed from existing matter, like animals and plants. Those viewing heavenly bodies as naturally incorruptible and distinct from elements must hold that "the lights were substantially created at the beginning," receiving a determination of power on day four rather than their substantial form. Chrysostom explains their non-mention earlier as guarding against idolatry, proving they are "not gods, by the fact that they were not from the beginning."

### Reply to Objection 2
Augustine identifies the first day's light as spiritual and the fourth day's as corporeal. Alternatively, the first day's light appeared generally, while the fourth day brought "a definite power to produce determinate effects." Dionysius describes how "the sun's light which previously was without form, was formed on the fourth day."

### Reply to Objection 3
Ptolemy holds that luminaries have distinct movement from celestial spheres, not being immovably fixed. Chrysostom notes God "bade them to be there" rather than fixing them immovably. Aristotle believes stars are fixed in orbits, moved only by sphere motion, though senses perceive luminaries' movement. Moses describes "what is obvious to sense, out of condescension to popular ignorance." A natural distinction may exist between lower firmament (day two) and higher firmament (day four) containing stars, imperceptible to sense.

### Reply to Objection 4
Basil explains that plants preceded sun and moon "to prevent idolatry," since pagans believed heavenly bodies generate plants. Yet Chrysostom notes these bodies "cooperate in the work of production by their movements, as the husbandman cooperates by his labor."

### Reply to Objection 5
Chrysostom states the two lights are called "great, not so much with regard to their dimensions as to their influence and power." Though stars exceed the moon's bulk, "the influence of the moon is more perceptible to the senses in this lower world," and "its apparent size is greater."

## Article 2: Whether the Cause Assigned for the Production of the Lights is Reasonable?

### Objection 1
Jeremiah warns against fearing heavenly signs, suggesting lights were not made to be signs.

### Objection 2
Sign contradicts cause. Lights are causes of earthly events, so they cannot be signs.

### Objection 3
Day and night distinctions began on day one. Lights therefore were not made "for seasons, and days, and years."

### Objection 4
Nothing is made for inferior things, since "the end is better than the means." Yet lights were made "to enlighten" the inferior earth.

### Objection 5
The new moon cannot rule the night. Yet if newly created, it likely appeared new, contradicting Scripture's claim.

### On the Contrary
"The authority of Scripture suffices."

### I Answer That
Corporeal creatures exist for their proper acts, for other creatures, for the universe, or for God's glory. Moses emphasizes only their usefulness to man, "to withdraw his people from idolatry." Scripture teaches lights serve three purposes: illumination for sight directing work; seasonal changes preventing weariness and ensuring health; and signs indicating weather for various occupations.

### Reply to Objection 1
Heavenly lights signify "changes effected in corporeal creatures, but not of those changes which depend upon the free-will."

### Reply to Objection 2
We gain knowledge of hidden effects through sensible causes and vice versa. "Nothing prevents a sensible cause from being a sign," though "signs" rather than "causes" guard against idolatry.

### Reply to Objection 3
General day-night division occurred day one through common diurnal movement. "Particular distinctions of days and seasons and years" derive from particular stellar movements beginning day four.

### Reply to Objection 4
Light serves man, who through his soul "is nobler than the heavenly bodies." A higher creature may serve lower ones "considered not in itself, but as ordained to the good of the universe."

### Reply to Objection 5
The full moon "rises in the evening and sets in the morning, and thus it rules the night, and it was probably made in its full perfection" like mature plants, animals, and man. Augustine allows that God made imperfect things He later perfected.

## Article 3: Whether the Lights of Heaven are Living Beings?

### Objection 1
More noble bodies deserve more noble adornment. Since less noble bodies have living creatures adorning them, heavenly lights should be living beings.

### Objection 2
Nobler bodies require nobler forms. Heavenly bodies are nobler than plants or animals, needing nobler forms. The noblest form is soul, the life principle. Therefore, heavenly lights are living.

### Objection 3
Causes are nobler than effects. Sun, moon, and stars cause life—especially in spontaneously generated creatures—so they possess living souls themselves.

### Objection 4
Heavenly body movement is natural, requiring intrinsic principles. This principle comprehends and is moved by objects desired—an apprehending substance—making heavenly bodies living.

### Objection 5
The first movable is heaven. All self-moving things are primary. Only living beings move themselves. Therefore, heavenly bodies are living.

### On the Contrary
Damascene states: "Let no one esteem the heavens or the heavenly bodies to be living things, for they have neither life nor sense."

### I Answer That
Philosophers disagreed profoundly. Anaxagoras denied heavenly bodies were living, earning Athenian condemnation. Platonists held them alive. Church Fathers divided similarly—Origen and Jerome affirmed heavenly body life; Basil and Damascene denied it; Augustine remained uncertain, merely suggesting that if alive, their souls resemble "the angelic nature."

Examining this question must remember that "the union of soul and body exists for the sake of the soul and not of the body." Soul operations appear through their ends. Nutrition, sensation, and growth require bodily instruments. However, intellectual operations need not depend on body as instrument—though intellect uses phantasms from bodily senses.

Heavenly bodies cannot exercise nutritive functions—"such operations are incompatible with a body naturally incorruptible." Sensory functions also cannot belong to them since "all the senses depend on the sense of touch, which perceives elemental qualities," and heavenly bodies lack elemental nature. Only intellectual and moving operations remain possible.

However, uniting soul to heavenly body for intellectual operations "cannot be for the purpose of the operations of the intellect." Only movement consideration remains. The soul need not be the heavenly body's form but may contact it as a mover contacts what moves. Aristotle demonstrates the first mover comprises moving and moved parts, united through contact. Platonists explain soul-body union similarly—spiritual substances unite to bodies as moving powers.

Heavenly bodies' movement proves this: unlike natural movement toward fixed ends where the mover rests upon achieving them, heavenly movement continues perpetually. Therefore, "they are moved by some intellectual substances." Augustine seems to agree, believing "all corporeal things are ruled by God through the spirit of life."

Thus, heavenly bodies are not living like plants and animals. The disagreement between affirming and denying their life amounts to word choice rather than substance.

### Reply to Objection 1
Heavenly luminaries adorn the universe through proper movements—agreeing with other adorning things because "they are moved by a living substance."

### Reply to Objection 2
One being may surpass another absolutely while being inferior in particulars. Though not absolutely nobler, heavenly body souls are "superior to them with regard to their respective forms, since their form perfects their matter entirely," unlike animal souls. Regarding movement, heavenly body motive power proves "of a nobler kind."

### Reply to Objection 3
The heavenly body, being mover and moved, resembles an instrument "acting in virtue of the agent." Since the agent is living, "the heavenly body can impart life in virtue of that agent."

### Reply to Objection 4
Heavenly movements are natural "not on account of their active principle, but on account of their passive principle; that is to say, from a certain natural aptitude for being moved by an intelligent power."

### Reply to Objection 5
Heaven "moves itself" as compound of mover and moved—not through form-matter union but through motive power contact. Thus the moving principle seems "intrinsic, and consequently its movement natural with respect to that active principle," similar to how animal movement seems natural to the animal itself.
