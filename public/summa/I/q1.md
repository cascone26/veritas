# Question 1: The Nature and Extent of Sacred Doctrine

## Article 1: Whether, besides philosophy, any further doctrine is required?

### Objection 1
It seems that philosophical science alone suffices. Humans should not seek knowledge beyond reason, as Scripture states: "Seek not the things that are too high for thee" (Sirach 3:22). Whatever is not beyond reason receives full treatment in philosophy. Therefore additional knowledge beyond philosophy is unnecessary.

### Objection 2
Knowledge concerns only being, and all that exists is treated in philosophy—including God Himself, through what Aristotle calls divine science or theology. Hence no further knowledge is needed.

### On the contrary
Scripture declares: "All Scripture, inspired of God is profitable to teach, to reprove, to correct, to instruct in justice" (2 Timothy 3:16). Since Scripture derives from divine inspiration rather than human reason, additional knowledge beyond philosophy is beneficial.

### I answer that
Humans required divinely-revealed knowledge beyond philosophical science for salvation. First, humans are directed toward God as their ultimate end, which exceeds rational comprehension: "The eye hath not seen, O God, besides Thee, what things Thou hast prepared for them that wait for Thee" (Isaiah 64:4). The ultimate end must be known by those directing their actions toward it.

Second, truths about God discoverable by reason needed divine revelation because: they would be known only by the few, after long time, and mixed with errors. Since humanity's salvation depends on knowing God, divine revelation was necessary for more fitting and certain instruction in divine truths.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although humans cannot seek through reason what exceeds understanding, once God reveals such things, they must be accepted through faith. Scripture continues: "For many things are shown to thee above the understanding of man" (Sirach 3:25). This constitutes sacred science.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sciences differentiate according to their methods of knowledge. The astronomer and physicist both prove the earth is round, but through different means—mathematics and matter respectively. Similarly, sacred theology differs in kind from philosophical theology because it employs revelation rather than natural reason.

## Article 2: Whether sacred doctrine is a science?

### Objection 1
Sacred doctrine appears not to be science. Every science proceeds from self-evident principles. But sacred doctrine proceeds from articles of faith, which lack universal acceptance: "For all men have not faith" (2 Thessalonians 3:2). Therefore it is not science.

### Objection 2
Sciences do not address individual facts. Yet sacred doctrine treats individual events—the deeds of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Therefore it is not science.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "to this science alone belongs that whereby saving faith is begotten, nourished, protected and strengthened." This applies only to sacred doctrine. Therefore it is science.

### I answer that
Sacred doctrine is indeed science. Two types of science exist: those proceeding from principles known by natural intelligence (arithmetic, geometry); and those proceeding from principles established by a higher science. Perspective derives from geometry; music from arithmetic.

Similarly, sacred doctrine is science because it proceeds from principles established by higher science—the science of God and the blessed. As musicians accept mathematical principles on authority, sacred science is founded upon principles revealed by God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Science's principles are either self-evident or reducible to conclusions of higher science. Sacred doctrine's principles are of the latter kind.

### Reply to Objection 2
Individual facts appear in sacred doctrine not as principal subjects but as examples for living and to establish authority of those transmitting divine revelation—the saints and prophets.

## Article 3: Whether sacred doctrine is one science?

### Objection 1
Sacred doctrine does not seem unified. The Philosopher states science concerns one class of subjects. Creator and creatures cannot be grouped together. Therefore sacred doctrine is not one science.

### Objection 2
Sacred doctrine treats angels, corporeal creatures, and human morality—subjects of separate philosophical sciences. Therefore it cannot be one science.

### On the contrary
Holy Scripture speaks of it singularly: "Wisdom gave him the knowledge of holy things" (Wisdom 10:10).

### I answer that
Sacred doctrine is one science. A faculty's or habit's unity derives from its object's formal aspect, not material aspect. Man, ass, and stone share the formal aspect of being colored—color is sight's formal object.

Sacred Scripture considers all things under the formality of being divinely revealed. Whatever divine revelation includes possesses this single formal aspect and belongs to sacred doctrine as one science.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sacred doctrine treats God primarily and creatures only as referrable to God as beginning or end. This preserves scientific unity.

### Reply to Objection 2
Inferior faculties differentiate what falls within a higher faculty's universal formality. Common sense, one faculty, encompasses all sense objects. Similarly, objects of different philosophical sciences can be treated by sacred doctrine under one aspect—insofar as they fall within revelation—bearing the divine science's stamp of simplicity while extending to everything.

## Article 4: Whether sacred doctrine is a practical science?

### Objection 1
Sacred doctrine seems practical. Practical science ends in action. Sacred doctrine aims at action: "Be ye doers of the word, and not hearers only" (James 1:22). Therefore it is practical.

### Objection 2
Sacred doctrine divides into Old and New Law. Law implies moral science, which is practical. Therefore sacred doctrine is practical.

### On the contrary
Every practical science concerns human operations—moral science addresses human acts; architecture concerns buildings. Sacred doctrine chiefly concerns God, not human operations. Therefore it is speculative, not practical.

### I answer that
Sacred doctrine, being one, extends to what belongs to different philosophical sciences under the same formal aspect—divine revelation. Thus it encompasses both speculative and practical dimensions, as God knows Himself and His works through one science. Nevertheless, it is predominantly speculative because more concerned with divine things than human acts, though it treats human acts insofar as they direct humans toward perfect knowledge of God—eternal bliss.

## Article 5: Whether sacred doctrine is nobler than other sciences?

### Objection 1
Sacred doctrine seems not nobler. Science's nobility depends on certitude. Other sciences, with undoubtable principles, seem more certain. Articles of faith can be doubted. Therefore other sciences are nobler.

### Objection 2
It is lower to depend upon higher science—music depends on arithmetic. Sacred doctrine depends upon philosophy (Jerome notes ancient doctors enriched books with philosophical ideas). Therefore it is inferior.

### On the contrary
Other sciences are called handmaidens of sacred doctrine: "Wisdom sent her maids to invite to the tower" (Proverbs 9:3).

### I answer that
Sacred doctrine transcends all sciences—both speculative and practical. One speculative science surpasses another through greater certitude or higher subject-matter. Sacred doctrine excels in both respects.

Certitude: Other sciences derive certitude from natural human reason, which can err. Sacred doctrine derives certitude from divine knowledge, which cannot be mistaken.

Subject-matter: Sacred doctrine treats things transcending human reason by their sublimity, while other sciences address only reason-accessible matters.

Regarding practical sciences: the nobler science directs toward further purpose—political science exceeds military science because armies serve the state. Sacred doctrine's practical purpose is eternal bliss—the ultimate end directing all practical sciences' purposes. Therefore it is manifestly noblest.

### Reply to Objection 1
What is most certain may seem least certain to us due to intelligence's weakness, as owls are dazzled by sunlight. That some doubt articles of faith reflects human intelligence's weakness, not the truths' uncertainty. Even slender knowledge of highest things surpasses most certain knowledge of lesser things.

### Reply to Objection 2
Sacred doctrine can depend upon philosophy not from deficiency but to clarify teaching. It accepts principles immediately from God through revelation, not from other sciences. Therefore it does not depend on them as superiors but uses them as inferiors and handmaidens—as master sciences use supply sciences.

This dependence reflects human intelligence's limitation, more readily led by naturally-known things toward truths transcending reason.

## Article 6: Whether this doctrine is the same as wisdom?

### Objection 1
This doctrine does not seem wisdom. No doctrine borrowing principles deserves the wisdom name; the wise direct, not are directed (Metaph. i). This doctrine borrows principles. Therefore it is not wisdom.

### Objection 2
Wisdom proves other sciences' principles, making it chief among sciences (Ethic. vi). This doctrine does not prove other sciences' principles. Therefore it is not wisdom.

### Objection 3
This doctrine is acquired through study; wisdom is acquired through God's inspiration, numbered among the Holy Spirit's gifts (Isaiah 11:2). Therefore it is not wisdom.

### On the contrary
Scripture states: "This is your wisdom and understanding in the sight of nations" (Deuteronomy 4:6).

### I answer that
This doctrine is wisdom above all human wisdom—not merely in one order but absolutely. The wise person arranges and judges, considering the highest principle in each order. In building, the wise architect plans the house's form; in human life, the prudent person directs acts toward fitting ends.

Therefore, whoever considers the absolutely highest cause—God—of the whole universe is most properly called wise. Wisdom is knowledge of divine things. Sacred doctrine essentially treats God as the highest cause—not merely as philosophers knew Him through creatures: "That which is known of God is manifest in them" (Romans 1:19)—but as He is known to Himself and revealed to others.

Therefore sacred doctrine is especially called wisdom.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sacred doctrine derives principles not from human knowledge but from divine knowledge—the highest wisdom—through which all knowledge is ordered.

### Reply to Objection 2
Other sciences' principles either are self-evident or proven through other sciences. But this science's proper knowledge comes through revelation, not natural reason. Therefore it concerns itself not with proving other sciences' principles but with judging them. Whatever in other sciences contradicts this science's truth must be condemned as false: "Destroying counsels and every height that exalteth itself against the knowledge of God" (2 Corinthians 10:4-5).

### Reply to Objection 3
Wisdom's twofold manner of judging produces two wisdoms. One judges by inclination—virtuous persons judge rightly about virtue through inclination toward it. Another judges by knowledge—morally-educated persons might judge virtuously without possessing virtue.

The first manner belongs to wisdom among the Holy Ghost's gifts: "The spiritual man judgeth all things" (1 Corinthians 2:15). Dionysius notes: "Hierotheus is taught not by mere learning, but by experience of divine things."

The second manner belongs to this doctrine, acquired through study, though its principles come by revelation.

## Article 7: Whether God is the object of this science?

### Objection 1
God does not seem the object. Every science presupposes its object's nature. This science cannot presuppose God's essence, for Damascene states: "It is impossible to define the essence of God." Therefore God is not its object.

### Objection 2
Whatever conclusions a science reaches must fall under its object. But Holy Writ concludes about creatures and human morality, not only God. Therefore God is not its object.

### On the contrary
The science's object is what it principally treats. This science chiefly treats God; it is called theology, treating of God. Therefore God is its object.

### I answer that
God is this science's object. A science's relation to its object parallels a habit's or faculty's relation to its object. Properly, a faculty's or habit's object is what all things are referred to through that faculty or habit—as man and stone are referred to sight through being colored. Colored things are sight's proper objects.

In sacred science, all things are treated under the aspect of God: either as being God Himself or as referring to God as beginning and end. Therefore God is this science's very object. This appears also from its principles—articles of faith—which concern God. The object of principles and whole science must be identical since the science is virtually contained in its principles.

Some, considering what is treated rather than how it is treated, asserted the object is things and signs, works of salvation, or Christ as head and members. Truly, we treat these in this science, but insofar as they refer to God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although we cannot know God's essence, we employ His effects—natural or gracious—as definitions regarding whatever this science treats concerning God; just as some philosophical sciences demonstrate about causes from effects, using effects as cause definitions.

### Reply to Objection 2
Whatever other conclusions this sacred science reaches are comprehended under God not as parts, species, or accidents, but as somehow related to Him.

## Article 8: Whether sacred doctrine is a matter of argument?

### Objection 1
This doctrine does not seem argumentative. Ambrose states: "Put arguments aside where faith is sought." This doctrine especially seeks faith: "But these things are written that you may believe" (John 20:31). Therefore sacred doctrine is not argumentative.

### Objection 2
If argumentative, argument is either from authority or reason. From authority seems unbefitting its dignity—authority provides the weakest proof. From reason seems unbefitting its end; Gregory states: "faith has no merit in those things of which human reason brings its own experience." Therefore sacred doctrine is not argumentative.

### On the contrary
Scripture says a bishop should "embrace that faithful word which is according to doctrine, that he may be able to exhort in sound doctrine and to convince the gainsayers" (Titus 1:9).

### I answer that
Like other sciences not arguing for their principles but from them toward other truths, sacred doctrine does not argue for articles of faith but from them proves other things. The Apostle argues from Christ's resurrection to prove general resurrection (1 Corinthians 15).

However, regarding philosophical sciences: inferior sciences neither prove their principles nor dispute with those denying them, leaving this to higher science. The highest science—metaphysics—can dispute with those denying its principles if the opponent makes some concession, but cannot dispute with those conceding nothing, though it can answer objections.

Sacred Scripture, having no superior science, can dispute with those denying its principles only if they admit some truths from divine revelation. Thus we argue with heretics from Scripture texts and against those denying one article from another. If an opponent accepts nothing of divine revelation, no means of proving articles of faith through reasoning remains—only answering objections. Since faith rests on infallible truth and truth's contrary cannot be demonstrated, arguments against faith cannot be demonstrations but answerable difficulties.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although human reason cannot prove faith's content, this doctrine argues from articles of faith to other truths.

### Reply to Objection 2
This doctrine especially bases itself on authority arguments, since its principles come through revelation. We should believe those who received revelation. This does not diminish its dignity; while human-reason-based authority provides the weakest proof, divine-revelation-based authority provides the strongest.

Sacred doctrine uses even human reason—not to prove faith (which would destroy faith's merit) but to clarify other things presented in this doctrine. Since grace perfects nature without destroying it, natural reason should serve faith as natural will's bent serves charity. The Apostle says: "Bringing into captivity every understanding unto the obedience of Christ" (2 Corinthians 10:5).

Sacred doctrine employs philosophers' authority in questions where natural reason could grasp truth, as Paul quotes Aratus: "For we are also His offspring" (Acts 17:28). Nevertheless, it uses such authorities as extrinsic and probable arguments, but properly uses canonical Scripture as incontrovertible proof and Church doctors' authority as probable.

Our faith rests upon revelation given apostles and prophets writing canonical books, not on revelations to other doctors. Augustine says: "Only those books of Scripture which are called canonical have I learned to hold in such honor as to believe their authors have not erred in any way in writing them. But other authors I so read as not to deem everything in their works to be true, merely on account of their having so thought and written, whatever may have been their holiness and learning."

## Article 9: Whether Holy Scripture should use metaphors?

### Objection 1
Holy Scripture should not use metaphors. What belongs to the lowest science seems unfitting for this highest science. Poetry—least of sciences—properly employs figures and similitudes. Therefore this science should not use them.

### Objection 2
This doctrine aims at truth clarity. A reward awaits those manifesting it: "They that explain me shall have life everlasting" (Sirach 24:31). Similitudes obscure truth. Therefore presenting divine truths through corporeal comparisons unfits this science.

### Objection 3
Higher creatures more approach divine likeness. If creatures represent God, representation should come from higher creatures, not lower. Yet Scripture often uses lower creatures.

### On the contrary
Scripture states: "I have multiplied visions, and I have used similitudes by the ministry of the prophets" (Hosea 12:10). Using similitudes means employing metaphors. Therefore this sacred science may use metaphors.

### I answer that
Holy Scripture fittingly presents divine and spiritual truths through material comparisons. God provides for everything according to its nature. Humans naturally attain intellectual truths through sensible objects because all knowledge originates in sensation. Therefore, Holy Scripture fittingly teaches spiritual truths through material likenesses.

Dionysius says: "We cannot be enlightened by the divine rays except they be hidden within the covering of many sacred veils." It also fits Holy Scripture's universal accessibility—"To the wise and to the unwise I am a debtor" (Romans 1:14)—that spiritual truths be presented through figures from bodily things, enabling even the simple who cannot grasp intellectual things to understand.

### Reply to Objection 1
Poetry uses metaphors to produce representation, as representation pleases humans naturally. Sacred doctrine uses metaphors as both necessary and useful.

### Reply to Objection 2
Divine revelation's ray is not extinguished by sensible imagery veiling it, as Dionysius says. Its truth remains such that revelation's recipients' minds do not rest in metaphors but are raised to truths' knowledge. Through those receiving revelation, others also gain instruction.

Hence metaphorically-taught things elsewhere receive clearer teaching. Truth's concealment in figures benefits thoughtful minds and protects against impious ridicule: "Give not that which is holy to dogs" (Matthew 7:6).

### Reply to Objection 3
Dionysius says divine truths' exposition through less noble bodies' figures is more fitting than through nobler bodies, for three reasons:

First, it better preserves human minds from error. Then it appears these are not literal divine-truth descriptions, which might seem doubtful had they used nobler bodies, especially for those unable imagining anything nobler than bodies.

Second, it suits our current knowledge of God. What God is not appears clearer than what He is. Therefore similitudes from things farthest from God form truer estimates that God transcends whatever we say or think.

Third, divine truths thereby better hide from the unworthy.

## Article 10: Whether in Holy Scripture a word may have several senses?

### Objection 1
Holy Writ's words cannot have multiple senses—historical or literal, allegorical, tropological or moral, and anagogical. Multiple senses produce confusion and deception and destroy argument's force. Only fallacies, not arguments, derive from proposition-multiplicity. Holy Writ should state truth without fallacy. Therefore multiple word-senses are impossible.

### Objection 2
Augustine distinguishes the Old Testament fourfold: history, etiology, analogy, and allegory. These seem altogether different from the four divisions mentioned above. Therefore explaining the same Holy Writ word through these four different senses seems unfitting.

### Objection 3
Besides these senses, parabolical sense exists, not among these four.

### On the contrary
Gregory states: "Holy Writ by the manner of its speech transcends every science, because in one and the same sentence, while it describes a fact, it reveals a mystery."

### I answer that
Holy Writ's author is God, Who signifies His meaning not only through words (as humans do) but also through things themselves. While in every other science things are signified by words, this science possesses the property that things signified by words themselves possess signification.

Therefore the first signification—whereby words signify things—belongs to the historical or literal sense. The signification whereby things signified by words themselves signify other things is called the spiritual sense, based on and presupposing the literal sense. This spiritual sense divides threefold:

The Apostle states the Old Law is a figure of the New Law. Dionysius says the New Law itself is a figure of future glory. Again, in the New Law, whatever our Head has done is a type of what we ought to do.

Therefore: so far as Old Law things signify New Law things, there is the allegorical sense; so far as things done in Christ or things signifying Christ are types of what we ought to do, there is the moral sense; so far as they signify what relates to eternal glory, there is the anagogical sense.

Since the literal sense is what the author intends, and Holy Writ's author is God, Who by one act comprehends all things through His intellect, it is not unfitting, as Augustine says, if even according to the literal sense one word in Holy Writ should have several senses.

### Reply to Objection 1
These multiplied senses produce neither equivocation nor multiplicity because they arise not from one word signifying several things but from things signified by words themselves being types of other things. Thus Holy Writ shows no confusion, for all senses are founded on one—the literal—from which alone arguments derive, not from those intended in allegory, as Augustine says.

Nevertheless, nothing from Holy Scripture perishes, since whatever faith requires within spiritual sense is elsewhere stated by Scripture in literal sense.

### Reply to Objection 2
History, etiology, and analogy fall under the literal sense. It is called history when something is simply related; etiology when its cause is assigned—Our Lord giving the reason Moses permitted divorce because of human hardness; analogy when one Scripture text's truth is shown not contradicting another's truth.

Of these four, allegory alone represents the three spiritual senses. Thus Hugh of St. Victor includes the anagogical under the allegorical sense, determining three senses only—the historical, allegorical, and tropological.

### Reply to Objection 3
The parabolical sense is contained in the literal, for words signify things properly and figuratively. Neither the figure itself but what is figured constitutes the literal sense. When Scripture speaks of God's arm, the literal sense is not that God has such a member, but only what is signified—operative power.

Hence nothing false can ever underlie Holy Writ's literal sense.
