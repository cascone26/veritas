# Question 85: The Mode and Order of Understanding

## Article 1: Whether Our Intellect Understands Corporeal Things by Abstraction from Phantasms

### Objection 1
The intellect would be false if it understands objects otherwise than as they exist. Understanding through abstraction would produce error.

### Objection 2
Material things include matter in their definitions. Material things cannot be understood without matter, yet abstraction removes matter.

### Objection 3
The Philosopher compares phantasms to the intellect as color is to sight. Sight doesn't abstract from color. Therefore, understanding shouldn't occur through abstraction.

### Objection 4
The passive intellect receives abstracted species; the active intellect illuminates phantasms like light illuminates color. Neither seems capable of abstraction.

### Objection 5
The Philosopher states the intellect "understands the species in the phantasm," not through abstraction.

### On the contrary
"Things are intelligible in proportion as they are separate from matter."

### I answer that
Three grades of cognitive powers exist. Sense, being corporeal, knows only individuals. The angelic intellect knows forms existing apart from matter. The human intellect occupies a middle position: immaterial yet united to the body.

Humans know forms existing individually in corporeal matter but not as existing in this particular matter. This constitutes abstraction. Plato erred by considering only the intellect's immateriality while ignoring its union with the body.

### Reply to Objection 1
Abstraction occurs two ways: by composition/division (implying separation in fact) and by simple consideration (understanding one thing without reference to another). The second mode doesn't involve falsehood.

### Reply to Objection 2
Matter is twofold: common (flesh and bone) and individual (this flesh, these bones). The intellect abstracts from individual sensible matter but not from common sensible matter. Mathematical species abstract from sensible matter entirely. Concepts like "being" and "unity" abstract even from common intelligible matter.

### Reply to Objection 3
Colors have the same mode of existence as sight. Phantasms, being corporeal and individual, don't share the mode of human intellect. The active intellect creates an intelligible species from the phantasm's content.

### Reply to Objection 4
The active intellect does more than illuminate: it abstracts the intelligible species. By its power, we disregard conditions of individuality and consider the specific nature.

### Reply to Objection 5
The intellect both abstracts species from phantasms and understands these natures in phantasms, since it cannot understand abstracted things without turning to phantasms.

## Article 2: Whether the Intelligible Species Is What Is Understood, or That by Which We Understand

### Objection 1
The understood in act exists in the one understanding. Only the abstracted intelligible species exists in the actually understanding intellect, so this species must be what is understood.

### Objection 2
What is actually understood must exist somewhere—not outside the soul. Therefore, it exists in the intellect as the intelligible species.

### Objection 3
Words signify what we understand. Therefore, intelligible species are what is actually understood.

### On the contrary
The intelligible species relates to the intellect as sensible images relate to sense. Sensible images aren't what is perceived but that through which sense perceives.

### I answer that
Some wrongly claim cognitive faculties know only their own impressions. This is manifestly false. First, sciences concern objects outside the soul. Second, this view makes all knowledge subjective, leading to the claim that contradictory opinions are simultaneously true.

The intelligible species is that by which the intellect understands—the likeness of the thing understood. Since the intellect reflects on itself, it can understand both its act and the species. Thus the species is secondarily understood; what is primarily understood is the object.

### Reply to Objection 1
The thing understood exists in the intellect through its likeness. The species isn't what is actually understood but its likeness.

### Reply to Objection 2
"Thing actually understood" implies both the thing and its being understood. Humanity understood exists only in individuals, but that it be apprehended universally occurs through the intellect's likeness.

### Reply to Objection 3
Words signify not the intelligible species itself but what the intellect forms for judging external things.

## Article 3: Whether the More Universal Is First in Our Intellectual Cognition

### Objection 1
What is first in nature is secondarily known to us. Universals precede in nature. Therefore, universals are secondarily known.

### Objection 2
Composition precedes simplicity for us. Universals are simpler.

### Objection 3
The defined comes to knowledge before the definition's parts. The more universal is part of the less universal's definition.

### Objection 4
We know causes and principles through effects. Universals are principles.

### On the contrary
"We must proceed from the universal to the singular and individual."

### I answer that
Intellectual knowledge arises partly from sensible knowledge. Since sense concerns singular things and intellect concerns universals, knowledge of particulars precedes universal knowledge. Second, the intellect proceeds from potentiality to actuality—imperfect confused knowledge precedes perfect distinct knowledge.

Knowing something comprising many things without proper knowledge of each component is confused knowledge. The intellect knows "animal" before "man"; the more universal before the less universal. A child distinguishes "man" from "not-man" before distinguishing individual men.

### Reply to Objection 1
The universal's nature in individuals follows two natural orders: by generation (imperfect precedes perfect) and by perfection (perfect precedes imperfect). The more common precedes in generation.

### Reply to Objection 2
The more universal is like both whole and part relative to the less universal. As whole, it potentially contains the less universal.

### Reply to Objection 3
Parts are known two ways: absolutely in themselves (stones before house) and as belonging to a whole (house vaguely before distinct parts).

### Reply to Objection 4
The universal as understood with universality's intention is epistemically prior. But what's a knowledge principle isn't necessarily an existence principle.

## Article 4: Whether We Can Understand Many Things Simultaneously

### Objection 1
Intellect transcends time. Therefore it understands different things simultaneously.

### Objection 2
Non-opposing intelligible species can coexist in one subject.

### Objection 3
The intellect understands wholes simultaneously, and wholes contain many parts.

### Objection 4
We cannot know difference between things without knowing both simultaneously.

### On the contrary
"Understanding is of one thing only; knowledge is of many."

### I answer that
The intellect can understand many things as one but not as many. Whatever the intellect understands under one species, it understands simultaneously. Whatever it understands under different species, it doesn't understand simultaneously. One intellect cannot simultaneously be perfected by different intelligible species, just as one body cannot simultaneously have different colors.

### Reply to Objection 1
The multitude of intelligible species causes vicissitude of operations.

### Reply to Objection 2
Not only opposite forms but non-opposite forms of the same genus cannot coexist simultaneously.

### Reply to Objection 3
Parts understood confusedly in the whole are known together; distinctly, each through its own species, not simultaneously.

### Reply to Objection 4
The intellect knows both things in relation to their difference, like knowing parts in the whole.

## Article 5: Whether Our Intellect Understands by Composition and Division

### Objection 1
Composition and division involve many; the intellect cannot understand many simultaneously.

### Objection 2
Composition and division imply time. The intellect abstracts from time.

### Objection 3
The intellect understands by assimilating to things. Composition and division don't exist in things as in the intellect.

### On the contrary
Words contain composition and division in propositions.

### I answer that
The human intellect necessarily understands by composition and division. Since the intellect progresses from potentiality to actuality gradually, it first grasps something about the object—its quiddity—then understands properties, accidents, and various relations. Thus it necessarily compares things by composition or division, and from one proceeds to another, which is reasoning.

The angelic and divine intellects possess perfect knowledge simultaneously. Therefore, humans know by composition, division, and reasoning, while angels and God know through understanding the simple essence.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellect knows many through composition by knowing things' difference and comparison.

### Reply to Objection 2
The intellect turns toward phantasms, and composition involves time insofar as it does.

### Reply to Objection 3
Something in the thing corresponds to the intellect's composition: form with matter, and accident with subject. The intellect's composition signifies component identity in the thing.

## Article 6: Whether the Intellect Can Be False

### Objection 1
The Philosopher says truth and falsehood are in the mind.

### Objection 2
Opinion and reasoning belong to the intellect. Falsehood exists in both.

### Objection 3
Sin is in the intellectual faculty and involves falsehood.

### On the contrary
Augustine states "everyone deceived does not rightly understand." The Philosopher affirms "the intellect is always true."

### I answer that
Sense isn't deceived about its proper object but may be deceived regarding common and accidental sensibles. Similarly, the intellect doesn't err about its proper object—the material thing's quiddity. It may err regarding things surrounding the essence—comparing and relating things by composition or division, or in reasoning.

In first principles' propositions, the intellect cannot err, generating infallible scientific conclusions. The intellect may be accidentally deceived about composite things' quiddity when definitions are false.

## Article 7: Whether One Person Can Understand One and the Same Thing Better Than Another

### Objection 1
Whoever understands a thing otherwise than as it is doesn't truly understand it. Degrees of understanding don't exist.

### Objection 2
Truth isn't subject to degree; things cannot be more or less understood.

### Objection 3
Different understanding forms would cause different species.

### On the contrary
Experience shows some understand more profoundly.

### I answer that
"Understanding better" admits two senses. First, the "more" determines the act regarding the thing understood—one cannot understand the same thing differently without error. Second, the "more" determines the act on the understander's part—one may understand better through greater understanding power.

The intellect improves through better body disposition (better soul allotted to better body) and through better lower powers (imagination, cognition, memory).

### Reply to Objection 1
Truth of understanding consists in understanding a thing as it is.

### Reply to Objection 2
Form differences due only to matter's disposition cause numerical, not specific, difference.

### Reply to Objection 3
Clarified by Reply to Objection 1.

## Article 8: Whether the Intellect Understands the Indivisible Before the Divisible

### Objection 1
Principles are indivisible and known first. Therefore, the indivisible is known before the divisible.

### Objection 2
Points define lines; unity defines number. Therefore, the intellect understands indivisibles first.

### Objection 3
The indivisible resembles the intellect more since "the intellect is simple."

### On the contrary
"The indivisible is expressed as a privation." Privation is secondarily known.

### I answer that
The indivisible is threefold. (1) The continuous, actually undivided—known before division since confused knowledge precedes distinct knowledge. (2) The indivisible regarding species (as reason in humans)—understood before logical division. Both are understood as the intellect's proper object. (3) The altogether indivisible (point, unity)—secondarily known through privation. Point is "that which has no parts"; "one" is "indivisible."

### Reply to Objection 1
In knowledge acquisition, principles aren't always known first. In perfect knowledge, effects' knowledge depends on principles and elements.

### Reply to Objection 2
A point isn't included in lines generally. Unity measures number, hence appears in number's definition.

### Reply to Objection 3
A thing is known first from the power's aptitude for the object, not natural likeness to the cognitive power.
