# Question 77: The Powers of the Soul in General

## Article 1: Whether the Essence of the Soul Is Its Power

### Objection 1
Augustine states that mind, knowledge, and love exist in the soul substantially or essentially, and are "one life, one mind, one essence." This suggests the soul's essence comprises its powers.

### Objection 2
The soul surpasses primary matter in nobility. Since primary matter is its own potentiality, the soul must likewise be its own power.

### Objection 3
The substantial form is simpler than the accidental form and cannot be intensified or relaxed. If accidental forms possess their own power, a substantial form like the soul certainly does.

### Objection 4
According to Aristotle, the soul is "that by which we sense and understand primarily." Therefore the soul itself constitutes its power.

### Objection 5
Whatever lies outside the essence constitutes an accident. If soul-powers are distinct from essence, they become accidents—contradicting Augustine, who denies powers inhere in the soul like color in a body.

### Objection 6
A simple form cannot serve as a subject. Since the soul lacks matter-form composition, it cannot receive powers as accidents.

### Objection 7
Substantial differences derive from substantive principles, not accidents. Since "sensitive" and "rational" are substantial differences drawn from sense and reason, these powers cannot be accidents but must be essential.

### On the contrary
Dionysius teaches that heavenly spirits divide into essence, power, and operation. The soul similarly distinguishes essence from power.

### I answer that
The soul's essence and power must remain distinct. Two proofs establish this:

First, power and act divide being into distinct categories. Operations belong to accident-categories (except God's operation, which is His substance). Therefore, the powers producing non-substantial operations cannot themselves be substantial.

Second, the soul by its essence constitutes an act. If the essence itself were the immediate operative principle, everything possessing a soul would constantly perform vital operations. Yet we observe souls remain sometimes inactive. This demonstrates the soul's essence relates to potential operations through distinct powers, functioning as first act relative to second acts.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine addresses the mind as knowing and loving itself. Knowledge and love, relative to the soul as object, exist substantially in the soul's essence. This reflects how potential wholes relate to their parts—present entirely in essence though not entirely in power.

### Reply to Objection 2
Primary matter's potentiality equals its essence because its actualization constitutes the substantial form. The soul differs fundamentally.

### Reply to Objection 3
Action belongs to composites through their substantial form, which generates operative power—much as accidental heat-form derives from fire's substantial form.

### Reply to Objection 4
The soul functions as the first, not proximate, principle of sensation. Aristotle means the soul constitutes the foundational source, not the immediate operative faculty.

### Reply to Objection 5
Understanding "accident" as the category opposed to substance, powers must be accidents. However, understanding "accident" among five universals, powers occupy a middle position as natural properties—caused by essential principles but not constituting essence.

### Reply to Objection 6
Although uncompounded, the soul possesses potentiality-admixture, permitting it to receive accidents.

### Reply to Objection 7
"Rational" and "sensitive" derive from the soul's nature itself, not from sense and reason as powers. We substitute accidents for substantial differences because substantial forms remain naturally obscure to us.

## Article 2: Whether There Are Several Powers of the Soul

### Objection 1
The intellectual soul approaches God's likeness. God possesses one simple power, so the soul should likewise.

### Objection 2
Higher powers achieve greater unity. The intellectual soul exceeds all forms in excellence and thus possesses singular power.

### Objection 3
One essence produces one actual existence at various perfection-levels. Therefore one soul-power should accomplish diverse operations.

### On the contrary
Aristotle identifies multiple powers within the soul's structure.

### I answer that
Multiple powers necessarily exist in the soul. Aristotle observes that lower things achieve imperfect goodness through few operations; higher things accomplish perfect goodness through multiple operations; the highest achieve perfection without motion. Humans occupy the lowest rank among beatitude-capable beings, requiring extensive operations and powers for universal perfection.

Additionally, the soul occupies the boundary between spiritual and corporeal natures, combining both dimensions' operative capacities.

### Reply to Objection 1
The intellectual soul approaches divine likeness through capacity for perfect goodness, though requiring multiple means—falling short of more perfect beings.

### Reply to Objection 2
Unified power excels among equals, but multiform power surpasses it across numerous objects.

### Reply to Objection 3
One essence produces one substantial existence but permits multiple operations—just as one soul-essence generates several powers.

## Article 3: Whether Powers Distinguish Themselves by Acts and Objects

### Objection 1
Specification derives from prior intrinsic principles, not subsequent extrinsic factors. Acts follow powers; objects remain external. Therefore powers lack act-and-object distinction.

### Objection 2
If powers distinguish by objects, the same power cannot address contraries. Yet sight encompasses both white and black; taste encompasses both sweet and bitter.

### Objection 3
Removing causes removes effects. If object-difference produces power-difference, identical objects cannot fall under different powers—yet the same thing is both known and desired.

### Objection 4
Diverse lower powers' objects sometimes belong to single higher powers. Sound and color belong respectively to hearing and sight, yet both fall under common sense.

### On the contrary
Aristotle teaches that acts and operations precede powers according to reasoning, while objects precede operations.

### I answer that
Powers necessarily distinguish by acts and objects. Power directs toward act; understanding power requires understanding its directed act. Acts diversify according to object-diversity. Objects constitute either the moving cause (for passive powers) or the end (for active powers), both determining act-species. However, accidental variations cannot alter power-species. Sensory powers naturally address passive qualities like color and sound, creating genuinely distinct powers. But being a musician or large remains accidental to color; these variations create no new sensitive powers.

### Reply to Objection 1
Though sequential in existence, acts precede powers logically and intentionally, like ends preceding agents.

### Reply to Objection 2
Powers address not contraries individually but their common formality. Sight grasps white and black as colors, not contrary-natures.

### Reply to Objection 3
Coincident subjects can relate to different power-aspects, explaining their belonging to various powers.

### Reply to Objection 4
Higher powers address more universal object-formalities than lower powers. Multiple lower-power objects converge under one higher power's unified formality.

## Article 4: Whether Order Exists Among Soul-Powers

### Objection 1
Things divided into species possess no temporal precedence. Powers contradict each other; therefore they lack ordering.

### Objection 2
Soul-based order cannot derive from the soul's unity. Object-diversity prevents ordering.

### Objection 3
Where power-ordering exists, one power's operation depends on another's. Sight functions independently of hearing. Therefore powers lack ordering.

### On the contrary
Aristotle compares soul-parts to geometric figures. Figures order themselves; therefore powers do likewise.

### I answer that
Since the soul is one yet powers multiple, emanating from one source requires definite ordering. Three ordering-types appear:

First, according to nature's hierarchy—intellectual powers precede sensitive powers, which precede nutritive powers. Second, according to generation's order—nutritive powers precede sensitive powers developmentally; sensitive powers precede intellectual powers. Third, among certain sensitive powers themselves—sight ranks first; hearing follows; smell ranks last.

### Reply to Objection 1
Species within a genus possess before-and-after relationships by nature despite appearing simultaneous in genus-predication.

### Reply to Objection 2
Soul-power ordering appears both from the soul's essential aptitude for ordered acts and from object-ordering.

### Reply to Objection 3
This argument applies to powers lacking the first two ordering-types. Powers possessing natural and generational ordering display operational dependence.

## Article 5: Whether All Soul-Powers Inhere in the Soul

### Objection 1
Body-powers belong to the body; soul-powers belong to the soul. The soul functions as subject-analogously to the body's function.

### Objection 2
Soul-powers' operations attribute to body through the soul. The soul primarily senses and understands. Therefore powers inhere primarily in the soul.

### Objection 3
Augustine notes the soul senses certain things without the body—emotions, for instance. If sensitive powers existed elsewhere, the soul could not sense independently.

### On the contrary
Aristotle asserts sensation belongs "neither to soul nor body, but to the composite." Therefore the composite serves as sensation's subject.

### I answer that
The operative subject necessarily constitutes the power's subject. Some soul-operations require no corporeal organs: understanding and willing. These powers inhere in the soul itself. Other operations—sight, hearing, nutrition—employ corporeal organs. Powers enabling these operations inhere in the composite, not the soul alone.

### Reply to Objection 1
Powers belong to the soul as principle, not as subject.

### Reply to Objection 2
Powers remain primarily in the soul as principle compared to the composite, though not as subject.

### Reply to Objection 3
Plato (whom Augustine sometimes cites without affirming) considered sensation the soul's exclusive operation. Augustine's statements mean either sensation necessarily requires body-organs, or sensation addresses non-bodily objects existing only in the soul's apprehension.

## Article 6: Whether Soul-Powers Flow from Its Essence

### Objection 1
Multiple diverse things cannot proceed from one simple thing. The soul's essence is singular; its powers are multiple. Therefore powers cannot derive from essence.

### Objection 2
What proceeds from something constitutes its effect. The soul's essence cannot be termed the powers' cause under any causality-type.

### Objection 3
Emanation involves movement. Nothing moves itself. The soul cannot generate powers within itself.

### On the contrary
Soul-powers constitute its natural properties. Subjects cause their proper accidents.

### I answer that
Substantial and accidental forms both qualify as acts but differ fundamentally. Substantial forms make things exist absolutely; their subjects remain purely potential. Accidental forms make things be such-and-such; their subjects are already actual. Since powers subject either in the soul alone or in the composite, and the composite achieves actuality through the soul, all powers flow from the soul's essence as their principle.

### Reply to Objection 1
One simple essence generates multiple diverse things naturally in ordered succession or through recipient-diversity.

### Reply to Objection 2
The subject constitutes its proper accident's final, active, and materially receptive cause. The soul's essence causes all powers as their end, active principle, and partial receptive principle.

### Reply to Objection 3
Proper accidents emanate not through transmutation but through natural resultance—like color naturally resulting from light.

## Article 7: Whether One Soul-Power Arises from Another

### Objection 1
Things created simultaneously do not derive from each other. All soul-powers exist created with the soul simultaneously.

### Objection 2
Soul-powers arise from the soul as accidents from subjects. One power cannot be another power's subject.

### Objection 3
Opposites do not generate opposites. Soul-powers divide as opposite species, preventing inter-generation.

### On the contrary
Powers reveal themselves through actions. One power's action causes another power's action—imagination results from sensation.

### I answer that
Natural ordering establishes causality among proceeding things. Soul-powers exhibit ordering; therefore one power derives from the essence through another's mediation. Perfect powers precede imperfect ones as end and active cause. Intellect surpasses sense; sense surpasses nutrition. Yet receptively, imperfect powers precede developmentally.

### Reply to Objection 1
Powers flow from essence through natural resultance, simultaneity-preserving. One power's relation to another parallels this.

### Reply to Objection 2
Accidents cannot directly subject other accidents, yet one receives prior to another in substance. Similarly, soul-powers receive through another's mediation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Perfect-imperfect opposition permits generation because imperfect things naturally proceed from perfection.

## Article 8: Whether All Soul-Powers Remain After Body-Separation

### Objection 1
The book "De Spiritu et Anima" states the soul withdraws taking sense, imagination, reason, intelligence, and appetitive powers with it.

### Objection 2
Soul-powers constitute its natural properties. Properties never separate from their subjects.

### Objection 3
Sensitive powers weaken not through body-weakness but through the soul's unchanging virtual principle.

### Objection 4
Memory, a sensitive-soul power, remains in separated souls. Scripture tells the rich glutton: "Remember that thou didst receive good things during thy lifetime."

### Objection 5
Joy and sorrow inhere in the concupiscible appetite. Yet separated souls experience joy and grief from divine rewards or punishments.

### Objection 6
Augustine notes the soul, when body-senseless, perceives through imaginative vision. The imagination constitutes a sensitive power.

### On the contrary
Ecclesiastical doctrine states man comprises soul with reason and body with senses. Dead body destroying, sensitive powers vanish.

### I answer that
All soul-powers derive from the soul as principle. However, only intelligence and will—inhering in the soul alone as subject—necessarily remain. Other powers—sensitivity and nutrition—subject in the composite. Since accidents cannot persist after their subject's destruction, sensitive and nutritive powers cease actually when the composite perishes. Yet they remain virtually in the soul as their root-principle.

### Reply to Objection 1
That book lacks authority. The soul takes these powers virtually, not actually.

### Reply to Objection 2
These non-actual-remaining powers belong to the composite, not the soul alone.

### Reply to Objection 3
Powers escape weakening because the unchanging soul constitutes their virtual principle.

### Reply to Objection 4
Augustine places memory within the mind, not the sensitive-soul part—understanding it intellectually.

### Reply to Objection 5
Separated-soul joy and sorrow derive from the intellectual appetite, not the sensitive appetite—as with angels.

### Reply to Objection 6
Augustine posed this as inquiry, not assertion, later retracting certain statements therein.
