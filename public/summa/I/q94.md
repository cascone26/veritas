# Question 94: The State and Condition of the First Man as Regards His Intellect

## Article 1: Whether the First Man Saw God Through His Essence

### Objection 1
Man's happiness consists in seeing God's essence. Adam in paradise "led a life of happiness."

### Objection 2
Augustine states the first man "lacked nothing which his good-will might obtain."

### Objection 3
Adam saw God "immediately" without enigma, according to the Master of Sentences.

### On the contrary
"That was not first which is spiritual, but that which is natural" (1 Corinthians 15:46).

### I answer that
Adam did not see God through His essence. Whoever sees God's essence necessarily loves Him perfectly and cannot willfully sin. Since Adam did sin, he could not have possessed this vision. His knowledge occupied a middle position between our current understanding and beatific vision—contemplating intelligible effects of God with steadfast clarity.

### Reply to Objection 1
Adam experienced happiness but not the perfect happiness of seeing God's essence.

### Reply to Objection 2
A well-ordered will would not seek what was promised as future reward.

### Reply to Objection 3
"Medium" has two meanings. Adam knew God without demonstrative reasoning but still through created effects, not the Divine Essence itself.

## Article 2: Whether Adam Saw Angels Through Their Essence

### Objection 1
Gregory states Adam "enjoyed the company of good angels."

### Objection 2
Adam's body was not corruptible, removing impediment to perceiving angels.

### Objection 3
Knowing itself, the soul should know other separate substances.

### On the contrary
Adam's soul possessed the same nature as ours. Our souls cannot understand separate substances.

### I answer that
Adam's innocence represented integrity of nature, not change of natural existence. His natural understanding operated through phantasms. Adam could not see angels in their essence, yet he possessed superior knowledge of them compared to us through greater certainty and fixity regarding intelligible things.

### Reply to Objection 2
Adam's limitation resulted from his connatural object falling short of angelic excellence, not from bodily impediment.

### Reply to Objection 3
Even individual separate substances know others only within their capacity.

## Article 3: Whether the First Man Knew All Things

### Objection 1
Knowledge comes through acquired, connatural, or infused species—none fits Adam's situation.

### Objection 2
Others acquire knowledge gradually. Adam should likewise.

### Objection 3
Adam could advance in knowledge, implying he lacked complete knowledge initially.

### On the contrary
Adam named the animals. Names must correspond to natures, so Adam knew all animal natures.

### I answer that
God established Adam with knowledge of everything for which humans possess natural aptitude—whatever is virtually contained in self-evident principles. Additionally, sufficient supernatural knowledge for directing life was granted. However, men's thoughts, future contingents, and unnecessary particular facts remained unknown.

### Reply to Objection 1
Adam possessed divinely infused species, differing no more than eyes Christ gave the blind differed from nature-given eyes.

### Reply to Objection 2
Adam deserved perfection as the first man in a unique way.

### Reply to Objection 3
Adam would have advanced experientially without increasing things known. Knowledge advancement and merit advancement differ.

## Article 4: Whether Man in His First State Could Be Deceived

### Objection 1
Paul states the woman "being seduced was in the transgression."

### Objection 2
The woman thought the serpent received speech from God—this was false.

### Objection 3
Distance naturally makes things appear smaller. Adam experienced optical illusions.

### Objection 4
Adam would have slept and dreamed, experiencing deception.

### Objection 5
If told falsehoods about unknown matters, Adam would believe them.

### On the contrary
Augustine declares: "To regard what is true as false is not natural to man as created."

### I answer that
While sin remained absent, no evil could exist. Since falsehood is the intellect's evil, the human intellect couldn't assent to falsehood. The primitive state's rectitude ensured that while the soul remained subject to God, lower faculties obeyed higher ones. When natural judgment operates freely, images don't deceive us.

### Reply to Objection 1
The woman was deceived before outward sin but had already sinned inwardly through pride.

### Reply to Objection 2
The woman believed the serpent received this faculty through supernatural operation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Adam's reason would have enabled him to judge the truth correctly.

### Reply to Objection 4
Sleep suspends reason, rendering one unaccountable for dreams.

### Reply to Objection 5
Adam wouldn't have believed falsehoods as true but might have considered them possible. Divine guidance would have prevented deception in matters exceeding his knowledge.
