# Question 102: Man's Abode, Which is Paradise

## Article 1: Whether Paradise is a Corporeal Place

### Objection 1

Bede claims paradise "reaches to the lunar circle," yet no earthly location matches this description. Earth cannot naturally extend so high, and the region beneath the moon contains fire that would consume terrestrial matter. Therefore, paradise is not corporeal.

### Objection 2

Scripture mentions four rivers originating in paradise, yet these rivers have visible sources elsewhere according to natural philosophy. Thus paradise cannot be a corporeal place.

### Objection 3

Explorers have surveyed the entire habitable world without discovering paradise's location, suggesting it lacks corporeal existence.

### Objection 4

The tree of life grows in paradise, yet Scripture indicates it represents spiritual wisdom. Therefore, paradise itself must be spiritual rather than corporeal.

### Objection 5

Corporeal trees were created on day three, while paradise's trees were planted after the six days concluded. This temporal distinction suggests paradise was not corporeal.

### On the contrary

Augustine acknowledges three positions: some regard paradise as merely corporeal, others as purely spiritual, and still others (whose view he favors) as both corporeal and spiritual.

### I answer that

Augustine teaches that we may hold paradise as spiritual "so long as we believe in the truth of the events narrated as having there occurred." Scripture presents paradise as historical narrative, and we must maintain historical truth as the foundation for spiritual interpretation. Isidore defines paradise as "a place situated in the east, its name being the Greek for garden." This eastern location befits its excellence, since east represents the heavens' right hand—nobler than the left—making it fitting for God's earthly garden.

### Reply to Objection 1

Bede's claim, taken literally, is false. However, it may mean paradise reaches the moon figuratively, since the atmosphere there maintains "a continually even temperature," resembling celestial bodies lacking opposing elements. Alternatively, some suggest paradise extended to the moon—meaning the middle atmospheric region where weather phenomena originate—though this would render it unsuitable for human dwelling.

### Reply to Objection 2

Augustine proposes that the four rivers flowed underground before surfacing elsewhere, a phenomenon observed in various streams. Thus humanity may lack knowledge of paradise's specific location despite the rivers' documented sources.

### Reply to Objection 3

Paradise remains isolated from habitable regions by mountains, seas, or impassable torrid zones, explaining why topographical writers omit its location.

### Reply to Objection 4

The tree of life was materially real, called such because its fruit possessed life-preserving properties. Yet it carried spiritual significance, comparable to the desert rock signifying Christ. Similarly, the knowledge tree was material but signified spiritual truths regarding obedience versus rebellion.

### Reply to Objection 5

Augustine contends that day-three plants existed in seminal virtue, becoming actually produced after the six days. Other authorities maintain all plants, including paradise's trees, were actually created day three, with their paradise planting representing a recapitulation of that creation.

## Article 2: Whether Paradise Was Adapted as Man's Abode

### Objection 1

Angels and humans are similarly ordered toward beatitude. Angels inhabit the empyrean heaven from their creation's beginning. Therefore, man should have dwelt there initially.

### Objection 2

If man required a specific place, necessity would stem from either soul or body. Soul's nature suggests heaven; body needs no special provision beyond other animals' habitats. Thus paradise was unsuitable.

### Objection 3

Paradise became vacant after sin. If truly adapted for human habitation, God created it purposelessly.

### Objection 4

Paradise apparently located on the equator experiences extreme heat, as the sun passes vertically overhead. This contradicts man's need for even temperature, making it an unfit dwelling.

### On the contrary

Damascene teaches paradise was "a divinely ordered region, and worthy of him who was made to God's image."

### I answer that

Man possessed incorruptibility and immortality through a supernatural divine gift, not natural bodily disposition. A power within man's soul preserved his body from corruption. The human body corrupts internally through humoral consumption and age, remedied by nourishment, and externally through unequal atmospheric temperature. Paradise provided both conditions: Damascene describes it as "permeated with...a temperate, pure, and exquisite atmosphere, and decked with ever-flowering plants." Thus paradise perfectly suited man's original immortal state.

### Reply to Objection 1

The empyrean heaven, as highest corporeal place beyond change, suits angels by its elevation over entire corporeal creation. This condition also suits beatitude's supreme stability. Yet heaven doesn't suit man's nature—he lacks rulership over all corporeal creation. Paradise alone fitted man's bodily nature, though heaven awaited his final beatific state.

### Reply to Objection 2

No particular place is naturally suited to spiritual substances, though some locations possess certain fitness. Paradise adapted to man regarding both body and soul, since his soul's power preserved bodily incorruptibility. Other animals lack this capacity. Damascene confirms "no irrational animal inhabited paradise," though God brought animals to Adam, and the serpent entered through diabolic complicity.

### Reply to Objection 3

Paradise's vacancy after sin didn't render it useless, as immortality wasn't vainly bestowed. Rather, it demonstrates God's original kindness and man's loss through transgression. Some traditions maintain Enoch and Elijah still dwell there.

### Reply to Objection 4

Proponents of equatorial paradise argue unvarying day-night equality creates temperance, with sun proximity preventing extreme cold while its brief vertical passage prevents excessive heat. Yet Aristotle distinctly says such regions are uninhabitable from heat. The likely truth is that paradise possessed a most temperate situation, whether equatorial or elsewhere.

## Article 3: Whether Man Was Placed in Paradise to Dress and Keep It

### Objection 1

Soil cultivation became punishment for sin. Therefore, it couldn't have existed in paradise's innocent state, making man's placement there for cultivation unfitting.

### Objection 2

Keeping paradise requires fear of violent trespass, which didn't exist there. Thus man wasn't needed as keeper.

### Objection 3

If man was placed for paradise's sake, he was subordinate to it—seemingly false. Paradise should serve man, not conversely.

### On the contrary

Genesis states God "took man and placed in the paradise of pleasure, to dress and keep it."

### I answer that

Augustine identifies two interpretations. First, God placed man in paradise so that God Himself might work within and preserve man through sanctification and protection from corruption—work that ceases only when man relapses into darkness. Second, man would dress and keep paradise with labor unlike post-sin cultivation, but pleasant through practical knowledge of nature's powers. Man would keep paradise not against trespassers but to preserve it for himself lest sin cause loss. All this served man's benefit, making paradise ordered toward man's good rather than conversely.

## Article 4: Whether Man Was Created in Paradise

### Objection 1

Angels were created in their dwelling place—the empyrean heaven. Paradise fittingly housed man before sin. Therefore, man should have been created in paradise.

### Objection 2

Other animals remain where produced: fish in water, land animals on earth. Man would have remained in paradise; thus he was created there.

### Objection 3

Woman was made in paradise. Since man exceeds woman in dignity, he should certainly have been created there.

### On the contrary

Genesis states God "took man and placed him in paradise."

### I answer that

Paradise suited man's incorruptible primitive state, yet this incorruptibility resulted from supernatural divine gift, not human nature. To attribute it to God rather than nature, God created man outside paradise, subsequently placing him there for his animal life's duration. Upon achieving spiritual perfection, he would transfer to heaven.

### Reply to Objection 1

The empyrean heaven naturally befits angels' nature, so they were created there. The principle applies analogously to animals in their respective habitats.

### Reply to Objection 2

Same as Reply 1, applied to all creatures.

### Reply to Objection 3

Woman's creation in paradise reflected not her individual dignity but that of the body's principle—Adam—from which hers derived. Children would similarly be born in paradise where their parents dwelt.
