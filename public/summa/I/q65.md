# Question 65: The Work of Creation of Corporeal Creatures

## Article 1: Whether corporeal creatures are from God?

### Objection 1
It appears corporeal creatures do not originate from God. Scripture states that "all the works which God hath made, continue for ever" (Ecclesiastes 3:14), yet visible bodies are temporal rather than eternal. Therefore, God did not create visible bodies.

### Objection 2
God witnessed that "all things that He had made...were very good" (Genesis 1:31). However, corporeal creatures demonstrate evil through harmful effects—serpents, excessive heat, and similar things. Since harmful things are evil, corporeal creatures cannot be from God.

### Objection 3
What originates from God draws us toward Him rather than away. Yet corporeal creatures withdraw us from God, as the Apostle indicates: "While we look not at the things which are seen." Therefore, corporeal creatures are not from God.

### On the contrary
Scripture affirms: "Who made heaven and earth, the sea, and all things that are in them" (Psalm 145:6).

### I answer that
Certain heretics maintained that visible things were created by an evil principle rather than the good God. However, this position is untenable. When diverse things share something in common, that commonality must arise from some unified cause. Being is common to all things, however different their natures. There must therefore exist one principle of being from which all existing things—whether invisible and spiritual or visible and corporeal—derive their existence. The devil is called "god of this world" not because he created it, but because worldly people serve him, just as the Apostle says of some: "Whose god is their belly."

### Reply to Objection 1
God's creatures continue to exist perpetually, at least regarding their material substance, since created things are never annihilated. The closer a creature approaches God's immutability, the more immovable it becomes. Corruptible creatures endure eternally in their matter while changing in substantial form. The Apostle's statement concerns visible things as they function as temporal rewards, contrasting with eternal invisible rewards.

### Reply to Objection 2
Corporeal creatures possess goodness according to their nature, though this goodness is partial and limited rather than universal. This limitation produces certain opposition of contrary qualities, though each quality itself is good. Those judging things solely by personal benefit mistake what is harmful to themselves as simply evil, overlooking that what injures one person benefits another, and the same thing may be evil and good in different respects. This would be impossible if bodies were essentially evil.

### Reply to Objection 3
Creatures themselves do not withdraw us from God but lead us toward Him, since "the invisible things of God are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made" (Romans 1:20). If creatures withdraw people from God, the fault lies with those using them foolishly. Scripture notes: "Creatures are turned into a snare to the feet of the unwise." That creatures can mislead actually proves their divine origin, since they cannot lure the foolish away except through some goodness they possess from God.

## Article 2: Whether corporeal things were made on account of God's goodness?

### Objection 1
It appears corporeal creatures were not made because of God's goodness. Scripture states God "created all things that they might be" (Wisdom 1:14). Therefore, all things were created for their own being, not God's goodness.

### Objection 2
Goodness functions as an end; therefore greater good serves as the end of lesser good. Since spiritual creatures relate to corporeal ones as greater good to lesser, corporeal creatures must exist for spiritual creatures' sake, not God's goodness.

### Objection 3
Justice gives unequal things only to the unequal. Since God is just, inequality not created by God must precede all created inequality. Only free will produces uncreated inequality, so corporeal creatures' inequality versus spiritual creatures results from free-will movements, not God's goodness.

### On the contrary
Scripture declares: "The Lord hath made all things for Himself" (Proverbs 16:4).

### I answer that
Origen maintained that corporeal creatures were not made according to God's original purpose but as punishment for spiritual creatures' sin. He claimed God initially created only spiritual beings of equal nature, but some turned toward God (receiving higher or lower ranks) while others turned away (becoming bound to different bodies). This position is erroneous.

First, it contradicts Scripture. After describing each corporeal creature's creation, the text states God saw it was good, suggesting everything was brought into being because its existence was good. According to Origen's view, corporeal creatures existed not because their being was good but to punish evil elsewhere.

Second, this theory would make the world's present arrangement purely accidental. If the sun was made to punish specific spiritual sin, then multiple suns would exist if other spirits had sinned similarly. Such consequences are inadmissible.

The universe constitutes a whole composed of parts. When assigning purposes to wholes and their parts: first, each part exists for its proper function (the eye for seeing); second, less honorable parts exist for more honorable ones (senses for intellect, lungs for heart); third, all parts serve the whole's perfection. Furthermore, the whole human person exists for an extrinsic end—communion with God.

Similarly, in the universe's parts, every creature exists for its proper act and perfection, less noble creatures exist for nobler ones (creatures less noble than humanity exist for humanity), and every creature serves the entire universe's perfection. Finally, the entire universe with all parts is directed toward God as its end, imitating and manifesting divine goodness for God's glory. Rational creatures possess God as their end in a special, higher manner, since they can attain Him through knowing and loving.

Therefore, divine goodness constitutes the final end of all corporeal things.

### Reply to Objection 1
Any creature possessing being represents divine being and its goodness. That God created all things so they might have being does not exclude His creating them for His own goodness.

### Reply to Objection 2
The proximate end does not exclude the ultimate end. That corporeal creatures were made in some manner for spiritual creatures does not prevent their being made because of God's goodness.

### Reply to Objection 3
Justice regarding retribution requires equal rewards or punishments for equal merit or demerit. However, this applies differently to things at their initial institution. Just as an architect without injustice places identical stones in different building positions to achieve overall perfection, God from the beginning established creatures of various unequal natures to secure universal perfection, acting wisely without injustice, since no antecedent merit difference existed.

## Article 3: Whether corporeal creatures were produced by God through the medium of the angels?

### Objection 1
Corporeal creatures appear to be produced by God through angels. All things were made through divine wisdom, and "wisdom ordains" (Metaphysics i). In governing things, the lower is ruled by the higher in fitting order. Therefore, production should follow this pattern—the lower (corporeal) produced by the higher (spiritual).

### Objection 2
Different effects indicate different causes, since like produces like. If both spiritual and corporeal creatures came immediately from God, no diversity would exist, with no creature further from God than another. But this is clearly false; the Philosopher notes some things are corruptible because they are far removed from God.

### Objection 3
Infinite power is unnecessary for finite effects. Every corporeal thing is finite, so finite spiritual creatures' power could have produced it. No natural dignity befitting a nature is denied unless punishing a fault.

### On the contrary
Scripture states: "In the beginning God created heaven and earth" (Genesis 1:1), meaning corporeal creatures. These were produced immediately by God.

### I answer that
Some maintained creatures proceeded from God by degrees—the first creature from God immediately, then each producing another until corporeal creatures. This is untenable because corporeal creatures' first production is creation, producing matter itself. The imperfect must precede the perfect in coming-to-be, and only God can create.

Higher causes extend to more numerous objects. What underlies all things universally exceeds what informs and restricts it; being is more universal than living, living than understanding, matter than form. What most widely underlies everything proceeds most directly from the highest cause. Therefore, what underlies all things belongs properly to the supreme cause's causality. No secondary cause can produce anything unless the produced thing presupposes something caused by a higher cause.

Creation produces something in its entire substance with nothing presupposed—neither uncreated nor created. Therefore, only God, the first cause, can create. To demonstrate all bodies were created immediately by God, Moses stated: "In the beginning God created heaven and earth."

### Reply to Objection 1
Production involves order, but not such that one creature creates another—that is impossible. Rather, divine wisdom constitutes diverse grades among creatures.

### Reply to Objection 2
Though God is one, He knows many different things without compromising His nature's simplicity. By His wisdom, He causes diverse things as known by Him, as a craftsman produces diverse works by apprehending diverse forms.

### Reply to Objection 3
An agent's power is measured not only by the thing made but also by the making manner. One thing is made differently by higher versus lower power. However, finite things' production—where nothing exists beforehand—requires infinite power. Only infinite power possesses this characteristic, belonging to no creature.

## Article 4: Whether the forms of bodies are from the angels?

### Objection 1
Body forms appear to come from angels. Boethius states: "From forms that are without matter come the forms that are in matter." Forms without matter are spiritual substances, while forms in matter are body forms. Therefore, body forms derive from spiritual substances.

### Objection 2
What exists through participation is reduced to what exists essentially. Spiritual substances are essentially forms, while corporeal creatures possess forms through participation. Therefore, corporeal forms derive from spiritual substances.

### Objection 3
Spiritual substances possess more causative power than heavenly bodies. Yet heavenly bodies give form to lower things, causing generation and corruption. Therefore, material forms should derive even more from spiritual substances.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "We must not suppose that this corporeal matter serves the angels at their nod, but rather that it obeys God thus." Matter serves that from which it receives form. Therefore, corporeal forms are not from angels but from God.

### I answer that
Some held that all corporeal forms derive from spiritual substances (angels). Two versions of this theory exist.

Plato held that corporeal matter forms derive from immaterially subsisting forms through participation. Thus immaterial man, immaterial horse, etc. exist, and individual sensible things constitute themselves from corporal matter's impression from these separate forms. The Platonist order of forms corresponds to separate substances: one separate substance is horse and causes all horses; above this is separate life causing all life; above this is being itself causing all being.

Avicenna and others maintained that corporeal forms do not subsist per se in matter but in intellect only. Forms existing in spiritual creatures' intellect (called "intelligences" by them, "angels" by us) produce all corporeal matter forms, as forms in a craftsman's mind produce his work. This theory resembles certain modern heretics claiming God created all things, but the devil formed corporeal matter and differentiated it into species.

These opinions share a common error: seeking a form's cause as though form were generated in itself. However, Aristotle proves what is properly made is the composite. Corruptible things' forms exist at one time and not another without themselves generating or corrupting—through the composite's generation or corruption. Forms do not possess being; composites possess being through forms. According to a thing's being mode comes its generation mode. Since like produces like, corporeal forms' cause exists not in immaterial forms but in composites—this fire generates from that fire.

Corporeal forms are caused not as immaterial form emanations but by matter being brought from potentiality into act by a composite agent. Since composite agents (bodies) are moved by created spiritual substances, corporeal forms derive from spiritual substances—not emanating but as their movement's outcome. Further still, angelic intellect species (seminal types of corporeal forms) must be referred to God as the first cause. However, in corporeal creatures' first production, no potentiality-to-act transmutation occurred. Therefore, initially produced bodies' forms came immediately from God, whose command alone matter obeys as its proper cause. Moses signified this by prefacing each work with "God said, Let this...be," denoting all things' formation by God's Word, from whom Augustine affirms comes "all form and fitness and concord of parts."

### Reply to Objection 1
Boethius' "immaterial forms" means things' types in God's mind. Thus the Apostle says: "By faith we understand that the world was framed by the Word of God; that from invisible things visible things might be made." If "immaterial forms" means angels, material forms derive from them through motion, not emanation.

### Reply to Objection 2
Matter-received forms should be referred not to self-subsisting forms of the same type (as Platonists held) but either to angelic intellect's intelligible forms, proceeding through movement, or higher—to divine intellect's types, implanting form seeds in created things enabling movement into actuality.

### Reply to Objection 3
Heavenly bodies inform earthly ones through movement, not emanation.
