# Question 65: The Work of Creation of Corporeal Creatures

## Article 1: Whether Corporeal Creatures Are from God?

### Objection 1
It seems corporeal creatures are not from God. Scripture states that "all the works which God hath made, continue for ever," yet visible bodies do not endure eternally, as "the things which are seen are temporal." Therefore, God did not make visible bodies.

### Objection 2
Scripture declares God "saw all things that He had made, and they were very good." However, corporeal creatures appear evil since they cause harm—serpents injure, excessive heat damages. Things called evil are harmful by nature. Thus, corporeal creatures cannot be from God.

### Objection 3
What originates from God should lead us toward Him, not away. Yet corporeal creatures withdraw us from God, as the Apostle indicates: "While we look not at the things which are seen." Therefore, corporeal creatures do not come from God.

### On the Contrary
Scripture proclaims: "Who made heaven and earth, the sea, and all things that are in them."

### I Answer That
Certain heretics claimed visible things were created not by the good God but by an evil principle. However, this position is untenable. When diverse things share something in common, some cause must account for that agreement, since naturally different things cannot unite themselves. Being is found common to all things. Therefore, one principle of being must exist from which all things—whether invisible and spiritual or visible and corporeal—derive their existence. The devil is called "god of this world" not as creator, but because worldlings serve him.

### Reply to Objection 1
God's creatures continue forever in some respects, at least regarding matter, since what is created is never annihilated, even if corruptible. Creatures approaching God, Who is immovable, share greater immovability. The Apostle's statement about temporal visible things applies to them as rewards, not their intrinsic nature.

### Reply to Objection 2
Corporeal creatures are good according to their nature, though this goodness is partial and limited. Opposing qualities result, though each quality individually is good. Those judging things by personal benefit rather than nature perceive what harms themselves as simply evil. Yet what injures one creature benefits another. This could not occur if bodies were essentially evil.

### Reply to Objection 3
Creatures themselves do not withdraw us from God but lead toward Him, as "the invisible things of God are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made." If they withdraw us, this reflects human misuse. Scripture notes "creatures are turned into a snare to the feet of the unwise," proving they come from God, for they cannot mislead us except through some goodness received from Him.

## Article 2: Whether Corporeal Things Were Made on Account of God's Goodness?

### Objection 1
Scripture states God "created all things that they might be." Therefore, all things were created for their own being's sake, not for God's goodness.

### Objection 2
Good functions as an end; greater good ends lesser good. Spiritual creatures relate to corporeal creatures as greater good to lesser good. Thus, corporeal creatures were created for spiritual creatures, not for God's goodness.

### Objection 3
Justice gives unequal things only to the unequal. God is just; therefore, inequality not created by God must precede all created inequality. Uncreated inequality arises only from free will's movements. Since corporeal creatures are unequal to spiritual ones, the former were made through free-will movements, not God's goodness.

### On the Contrary
Scripture declares: "The Lord hath made all things for Himself."

### I Answer That
Origen incorrectly maintained that corporeal creatures were not made according to God's original purpose but as punishment for spiritual creatures' sin. Scripture contradicts this, stating after describing each corporeal creature: "God saw that it was good," implying everything was brought into being because its existence was good. Additionally, Origen's view would make the world's arrangement result from chance, which is absurd.

The universe constitutes a whole composed of parts. Each part exists for its proper act (as the eye for seeing); less noble parts exist for more noble ones (senses for intellect, lungs for heart); all parts perfect the whole. Humanity exists for fruition of God as an extrinsic end. Similarly, every creature exists for its proper perfection; less noble creatures exist for nobler ones, as creatures less noble than humans exist for humanity; each creature perfects the entire universe. The whole universe with all its parts is ordained toward God as its end, imitating and displaying Divine goodness to God's glory. Reasonable creatures especially have God as their end, since they attain Him through knowing and loving Him. Thus, Divine goodness is the end of all corporeal things.

### Reply to Objection 1
In possessing being, any creature represents Divine being and goodness. That God created all things so they might have being does not exclude their creation for His goodness.

### Reply to Objection 2
Proximate ends do not exclude ultimate ends. Corporeal creatures being made somewhat for spiritual creatures does not prevent their creation for God's goodness.

### Reply to Objection 3
Justice's equality applies to retribution, where equal rewards or punishments correspond to equal merit or demerit. This does not apply to things' original institution. An architect justly places identical stones in different building positions for structural perfection, not prior stone differences. Similarly, God originally established creatures of various unequal natures for universal perfection according to His wisdom, without injustice, since no prior merit diversity exists.

## Article 3: Whether Corporeal Creatures Were Produced by God Through the Medium of the Angels?

### Objection 1
As Divine wisdom governs all things, by it all things were made, according to Scripture: "Thou hast made all things in wisdom." Wisdom's function is ordering things. In governance, the lower is ruled by the higher in fitting order. Therefore, in production, the corporeal should be produced by the spiritual as lower by higher.

### Objection 2
Different effects indicate different causes; like always produces like. If God produced both spiritual and corporeal creatures immediately, no diversity would exist, for one would not be further removed from God than another. Yet this is false, as the Philosopher states some things are corruptible because far removed from God.

### Objection 3
Infinite power is unnecessary for finite effects. Every corporeal thing is finite. Spiritual creatures' finite power could produce it, especially since their nature admits no dignity denial except as fault punishment.

### On the Contrary
Scripture states: "In the beginning God created heaven and earth," understood as corporeal creatures. They were produced immediately by God.

### I Answer That
Some claimed creatures proceeded from God by degrees, with the first creature emanating directly from Him, then producing another, and so forth until corporeal creatures arose. This is untenable because corporeal creatures' first production is through creation, producing matter itself. In coming into being, the imperfect must precede the perfect. Only God can create.

The higher a cause, the more numerous objects its causation extends to. The underlying principle in things is always more universal than what informs and restricts it; being is more universal than living, living than understanding, matter than form. What underlies more things proceeds more directly from higher causes. What underlies all things belongs properly to the supreme cause's causality. Creation produces a thing's entire substance, with nothing presupposed, whether uncreated or created. Therefore, only God, the first cause, can create. To show that all bodies were created immediately by God, Moses said: "In the beginning God created heaven and earth."

### Reply to Objection 1
Production involves order, but not such that one creature creates another—that is impossible. Rather, Divine wisdom constitutes diverse grades in creatures.

### Reply to Objection 2
God, though one, knows many different things without detriment to His nature's simplicity. Through His wisdom, He causes diverse things as known by Him, as an artificer produces diverse artworks by apprehending diverse forms.

### Reply to Objection 3
An agent's power is measured not only by what is made but by the manner of making it. The same thing is made differently by higher versus lower power. Producing finite things where nothing pre-exists requires infinite power and can belong to no creature.

## Article 4: Whether the Forms of Bodies Are from the Angels?

### Objection 1
Boethius states: "From forms that are without matter come the forms that are in matter." Forms without matter are spiritual substances; forms in matter are corporeal forms. Therefore, corporeal forms derive from spiritual substances.

### Objection 2
All things such by participation reduce to what is such by essence. Spiritual substances are forms essentially; corporeal creatures have forms by participation. Therefore, corporeal forms derive from spiritual substances.

### Objection 3
Spiritual substances possess greater causative power than heavenly bodies. Yet heavenly bodies give form to earthly things, causing generation and corruption. Corporeal forms must derive from spiritual substances even more.

### On the Contrary
Augustine states: "We must not suppose that this corporeal matter serves the angels at their nod, but rather that it obeys God thus." Matter may be said to serve that from which it receives form. Thus, corporeal forms come not from angels but from God.

### I Answer That
Some held all corporeal forms derive from spiritual substances called angels, stated in two ways. Plato held corporeal matter's forms derive from and are formed by immaterially subsisting forms through participation. He posited an immaterial man, an immaterial horse, etc., from which individual sensible things are constituted as corporeal matter receives impressions from these separate forms through assimilation or "participation." Platonists believed the separate substance order matches the form order: a single separate substance is horse and causes all horses; above it is separate life causing all life; above this is being itself causing all being.

Avicenna and others maintained corporeal forms do not subsist per se in matter but in intellect only. They claim forms existing in spiritual creatures' intellect (called "intelligences" or "angels") produce all corporeal matter forms, as craftsmen's handiwork forms proceed from their minds. This resembles heretical modern claims that God created all things but the devil formed and differentiated corporeal matter into species.

These opinions share a common origin: all seek a form cause as though form were self-generated. However, Aristotle proves that what is properly made is the "composite." Corruptible forms exist and then cease existing, not through generation or corruption themselves, but through the composite's generation or corruption. Forms do not have being; composites have being through forms. A thing's being mode determines its coming-into-being mode. Since like produces like, the corporeal forms' cause should not be sought in immaterial forms but in something composite, as this fire is generated by that fire. Corporeal forms result not from immaterial form emanations but from matter being brought from potentiality to act by composite agents. Since a composite (bodily) agent is moved by a created spiritual substance, as Augustine states, corporeal forms derive from spiritual substances not by emanation but as the movement's term. The angelic intellect's species, being corporeal forms' seminal types, must be referred to God as the first cause. In corporeal creatures' first production, no potentiality-to-act transmutation occurred. Thus, corporeal forms that bodies initially possessed came immediately from God, whose bidding alone matter obeys as its proper cause. To signify this, Moses prefaces each work with "God said, Let this thing be," denoting all things' formation by God's Word, from Whom, according to Augustine, comes "all form and fitness and concord of parts."

### Reply to Objection 1
By immaterial forms, Boethius means things' types in God's mind. The Apostle states: "By faith we understand that the world was framed by the Word of God; that from invisible things visible things might be made." If immaterial forms means angels, material forms come from them not by emanation but by motion.

### Reply to Objection 2
Forms received in matter should be referred not to self-subsisting forms of the same type, as Platonists held, but either to angelic intellect's intelligible forms, from which they proceed by movement, or higher, to Divine intellect's types, by which form seeds are implanted in created things, enabling them to be brought by movement into act.

### Reply to Objection 3
Heavenly bodies inform earthly ones by movement, not by emanation.
