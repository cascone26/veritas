# Question 116: Fate

## Article 1: Whether There Be Such a Thing as Fate

### Objection 1

Gregory states in a homily that "Far be it from the hearts of the faithful to think that fate is anything real."

### Objection 2

Augustine derives "fate" from the verb meaning "to speak," suggesting things "fore-spoken" by divine decree. If things happen by fate, neither luck nor chance would exist in the world.

### On the contrary

Boethius defines fate as "a disposition inherent to changeable things, by which Providence connects each one with its proper order." What does not exist cannot be defined.

### I answer that

Events appear lucky or chance-like when compared to immediate causes, yet are directly intended by higher causes. Some philosophers denied fate entirely, refusing to reference higher causes for contingent events—this contradicts Providence doctrine. Others attributed all events to heavenly bodies, but human actions are not directly subject to stellar influence.

Rather, what happens accidentally must be reduced to a preordaining cause—Divine Providence. God's intellect alone can order human actions, which proceed from the will. Therefore, insofar as all events below are subject to Divine Providence as pre-ordained or "fore-spoken," we admit fate's existence. Church doctors avoided the term because some twisted it toward stellar determinism. Augustine advises: whoever ascribes human affairs to fate meaning God's will may hold this opinion but should remain silent about it.

### Reply to Objection 1

Gregory denies fate because the term was misapplied to stellar influence, not because truly understood fate lacks reality.

### Reply to Objection 2

Events may be lucky relative to proximate causes but not relative to Divine Providence, whereby "nothing happens at random in the world."

## Article 2: Whether Fate Is in Created Things

### Objection 1

Augustine states the "Divine will or power is called fate," which exists in God, not creatures.

### Objection 2

Only God directly causes accidental occurrences. Therefore fate exists in God alone.

### Objection 3

If fate inheres in creatures, it must be substance or accident, multiplied according to creature numbers. Since fate seems one thing, it cannot inhere in creatures.

### On the contrary

Boethius declares: "Fate is a disposition inherent to changeable things."

### I answer that

Divine Providence produces effects through mediate causes. The ordering of effects considered in God is called Providence; as existing in mediate causes ordered by God, it constitutes fate. Boethius explains: "Fate is worked out when Divine Providence is served by certain spirits; whether by the soul, or by all nature itself, whether by heavenly movements, whether by angelic power."

### Reply to Objection 1

Divine power can be called fate as its source, but essentially fate is the disposition or order of second causes.

### Reply to Objection 2

Fate possesses causality as much as the second causes whose ordering constitutes it.

### Reply to Objection 3

Disposition here signifies order, a relation. Considered relative to its principle, fate is one. Relative to its effects, it is multiple.

## Article 3: Whether Fate Is Unchangeable

### Objection 1

Boethius notes the "fickle chain of fate" versus Providence's "unwavering simplicity."

### Objection 2

"If we be moved, what is in us is moved." Since fate inheres in changeable things, fate itself is changeable.

### Objection 3

If fate is unchangeable, fated events occur necessarily, eliminating contingency.

### On the contrary

Boethius affirms that fate is "an unchangeable disposition."

### I answer that

Fate, considered regarding second causes, is changeable; but as subject to Divine Providence, it possesses conditional necessity. The conditional statement is true: "If God foreknew this would happen, it will happen." Thus Boethius, after calling fate's chain fickle, adds that since it derives from unchangeable Providence, it must itself be unchangeable.

### Reply to Objection 1

These objections resolve from understanding fate's dual aspect: changeable in itself, yet conditionally unchangeable as dependent on God's eternal knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 2

Same as above.

### Reply to Objection 3

Same as above.

## Article 4: Whether All Things Are Subject to Fate

### Objection 1

Boethius states the chain of fate moves heaven, stars, elements, and all born things. Nothing seems excluded.

### Objection 2

Augustine identifies fate with Divine will and power. Since God causes all things, all are subject to fate.

### Objection 3

Fate inheres in changeable things. All creatures are changeable. Therefore fate is in all things.

### On the contrary

Boethius affirms that "some things subject to Providence are above the ordering of fate."

### I answer that

Fate is the ordering of second causes to divinely foreseen effects. Whatever is subject to second causes is subject to fate. However, what God accomplishes directly—creation, glorification of spiritual substances—is not subject to second causes and therefore not subject to fate. Boethius observes: "Those things which are nigh to God have a state of immobility, and exceed the changeable order of fate."

### Reply to Objection 1

The processes Boethius describes occur through second causes and thus fall under fate. Not all creation does so.

### Reply to Objection 2

Whatever is subject to Divine will is not necessarily subject to fate.

### Reply to Objection 3

Though all creatures are changeable, not all proceed from changeable created causes.
