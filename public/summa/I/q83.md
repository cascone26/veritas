# Question 83: Free-Will

## Article 1: Whether Man Has Free-Will?

### Objection 1
Man lacks free-will because he often fails to do what he wills. As Paul writes, "the good which I will I do not, but the evil which I will not, that I do" (Romans 7:19).

### Objection 2
Free-will requires the power to will or not to will, yet Scripture states "It is not of him that willeth...nor of him that runneth" (Romans 9:16).

### Objection 3
Freedom requires being a cause of oneself. Since God moves the will—"The heart of the king is in the hand of the Lord" (Proverbs 21:1)—man cannot possess free-will.

### Objection 4
Jeremiah states "The way of a man is not his...neither is it in a man to walk" (Jeremiah 10:23).

### Objection 5
According to Aristotle, our perception of good depends on our nature, which comes from outside ourselves. Therefore, we necessarily follow particular ends.

### On the contrary
Scripture declares God "left him in the hand of his own counsel; that is of his free-will" (Sirach 15:14).

### I answer that
Man possesses free-will; otherwise counsel, commands, rewards, and punishments would be pointless. Some things act without judgment (stones falling), others from natural judgment without freedom (animals fleeing wolves by instinct). Humans, however, judge through reason rather than mere instinct. In contingent matters, reason can follow opposite courses. Since particular operations are contingent, human judgment remains undetermined to one course. Thus, man's rationality necessitates free-will.

### Reply to Objection 1
The sensitive appetite resists reason even when reason forbids something. The "good" man fails to achieve is "not desiring against reason."

### Reply to Objection 2
Paul's words don't deny that man wills freely, but rather that free-will alone suffices without God's moving and helping grace.

### Reply to Objection 3
Free-will is the cause of its own movement as man moves himself through it. However, liberty doesn't require being the first cause of oneself. God, as first cause, moves both natural and voluntary causes without removing their proper character.

### Reply to Objection 4
Man's "way" is beyond his control in execution, where impediments may arise. Yet choice itself remains within us, though presupposing God's help.

### Reply to Objection 5
Natural intellectual qualities dispose man toward happiness naturally, beyond free-will's scope. Bodily qualities and acquired habits remain subject to reason. Nothing here undermines free-will.

## Article 2: Whether Free-Will is a Power?

### Objection 1
Free-will denotes a free judgment, which is an act, not a power.

### Objection 2
Free-will is defined as "the faculty of the will and reason." Bernard calls it "the soul's habit of disposing of itself," not a power.

### Objection 3
Natural powers remain despite sin, yet free-will is forfeited through sin. Augustine states man "by abusing free-will, loses both it and himself."

### On the contrary
Only powers can properly be subjects of habits. Since free-will is the subject of grace, it must be a power.

### I answer that
Although "free-will" strictly denotes an act, common usage applies it to the principle of free judging. It is neither a habit nor a power-with-habit for two reasons.

First, free-will must be natural to man, yet no natural habit governs things subject to free-will. Second, habits dispose us toward good or bad, whereas free-will remains indifferent between good and evil choices. Therefore, it must be a power.

### Reply to Objection 1
Powers are commonly named from their acts. Free-will is named from the act of free judgment.

### Reply to Objection 2
"Faculty" sometimes denotes power ready for operation. Bernard means habit not as opposed to power, but as aptitude for action.

### Reply to Objection 3
Man loses free-will through sin regarding freedom from fault and unhappiness, not natural liberty from coercion.

## Article 3: Whether Free-Will is an Appetitive Power?

### Objection 1
Damascene states "free-will straightway accompanies the rational nature." Since reason is cognitive, free-will is cognitive.

### Objection 2
Free-will means free judgment, an act of cognitive power.

### Objection 3
Choice, free-will's principal function, involves comparing things—a cognitive act.

### On the contrary
Aristotle teaches choice is "the desire of those things which are in us." Desire belongs to appetitive power.

### I answer that
Free-will's proper act is choice. Two things concur in choice: cognitive counsel judges one thing preferable; appetitive acceptance follows that judgment. Aristotle ultimately describes choice as "desire proceeding from counsel." Since choice's proper object is means to ends—inherently good—and good is appetite's object, choice is principally appetitive. Thus free-will is an appetitive power.

### Reply to Objection 1
Appetitive powers accompany apprehensive powers, so free-will follows rational nature in this sense.

### Reply to Objection 2
Judgment concludes counsel, terminated first by reason's judgment, then appetite's acceptance.

### Reply to Objection 3
Comparison belongs to preceding counsel (reason's act). Though appetite doesn't compare, moved by apprehensive power, it resembles comparison by choosing one thing over another.

## Article 4: Whether Free-Will is Distinct from the Will?

### Objection 1
Damascene distinguishes thelesis (will) from boulesis (free-will as comparative willing). Therefore they are distinct powers.

### Objection 2
Choice (free-will's act) differs from willing: willing regards ends, choice regards means.

### Objection 3
Will is intellectual appetite. Intellect contains active and passive powers; similarly, intellectual appetite should have another power besides will.

### On the contrary
Damascene affirms "free-will is nothing else than the will."

### I answer that
Appetitive powers correspond to apprehensive powers. As intellect and reason are cognitive powers, will and free-will are appetitive powers. "Understanding" means simple acceptance (first principles); "reasoning" moves from principles to conclusions. Similarly, "willing" means simple appetite for ends; "choosing" means desiring means for ends' sake. Just as understanding and reasoning are one power, so willing and choosing belong to one power. Therefore, will and free-will are one power, not two.

### Reply to Objection 1
Boulesis and thelesis differ by distinct acts, not distinct powers.

### Reply to Objection 2
Choice and willing are different acts yet belong to the same power.

### Reply to Objection 3
Intellect moves will rather than acting like passive intellect. Thus no distinction of active and passive will is necessary.
