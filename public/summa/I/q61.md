# Question 61: The Production of the Angels in the Order of Natural Being

## Article 1: Whether the Angels Have a Cause of Their Existence

### Objection 1
It appears angels lack a cause of existence because Genesis treats of created things but makes no mention of angels, suggesting they weren't created by God.

### Objection 2
Aristotle states that immaterial substances existing as forms without matter "have being and unity of itself, and has no cause of its being." Since angels are immaterial forms, they lack a cause of being.

### Objection 3
Whatever is produced receives form from an agent. Angels, being forms themselves, cannot derive form from any agent, so they lack an active cause.

### On the Contrary
Scripture declares: "Praise ye Him, all His angels" and further states "For He spoke and they were made" (Psalm 148:2, 5).

### I Answer That
Angels and all existing things except God were made by Him. God alone is His own existence; in all else, essence differs from existence. Only God exists by His own essence; all other things possess existence through participation. Whatever exists by participation is caused by what exists essentially, as all ignited things are caused by fire. Therefore, angels necessarily were made by God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine explains that angels weren't omitted from Genesis but are designated by "heavens" or "light." They were either passed over or named after corporeal things because Moses addressed an uncultured people incapable of understanding incorporeal nature.

### Reply to Objection 2
Subsisting forms lack a formal cause of existence and unity, and lack an active cause producing effects through matter. However, they possess a cause productive of their entire substance.

### Reply to Objection 3
The solution to this objection is evident from the response to the second.

## Article 2: Whether the Angel Was Produced by God From Eternity

### Objection 1
God causes angels through His being, acting through no intermediary beyond His essence. Since His being is eternal, He produced angels from eternity.

### Objection 2
Whatever exists at one period but not another is subject to time. Angels transcend time as stated in the book De Causis, so they always exist rather than existing and not existing alternately.

### Objection 3
Augustine proves the soul's incorruptibility through its capacity for truth. Since truth is eternal, the intellectual nature of souls and angels is not merely incorruptible but eternal.

### On the Contrary
Wisdom declares: "The Lord possessed me in the beginning of His ways, before He made anything from the beginning" (Proverbs 8:22). Since angels were made by God, they were not at one time.

### I Answer That
God alone—Father, Son, and Holy Ghost—exists from eternity. Catholic faith holds this without doubt; anything contrary must be rejected as heretical. God produced creatures from nothing, meaning after they had not existed.

### Reply to Objection 1
God's being is His will. Although God produced angels by His being, He also made them by His will. God's will does not act by necessity in creating creatures; therefore, He produced such creatures as He willed, when He willed.

### Reply to Objection 2
Angels transcend the time measuring heavenly movement, being above corporeal motion. However, they do not transcend time measuring succession of their existence after non-existence, nor time measuring succession in their operations. Augustine states that "God moves the spiritual creature according to time."

### Reply to Objection 3
Angels and intelligent souls are incorruptible because they possess a nature capable of truth. However, they did not possess this nature from eternity; God bestowed it when He willed. Consequently, angels did not exist from eternity.

## Article 3: Whether the Angels Were Created Before the Corporeal World

### Objection 1
Jerome states that six thousand years haven't elapsed, yet asks how to measure the ages during which "Angels, Thrones, Dominations, and other orders served God." Damascene reports that some say angels were begotten before all creation, as Gregory the Theologian declares.

### Objection 2
The angelic nature stands between Divine and corporeal natures. The Divine nature is eternal; corporeal nature temporal. Therefore, angelic nature was produced before time yet after eternity.

### Objection 3
Angelic nature is more remote from corporeal nature than one corporeal nature from another. Yet one corporeal nature was made before another, as shown in Genesis's six days. Therefore, angelic nature preceded every corporeal nature.

### On the Contrary
Genesis states: "In the beginning God created heaven and earth" (Genesis 1:1). This would be untrue if anything preceded creation. Therefore, angels were not created before corporeal nature.

### I Answer That
Two paternal opinions exist on this matter. The more probable view holds that angels were created simultaneously with corporeal creatures. Angels form part of the universe rather than constituting it independently; both angels and corporeal natures unite in one universe. The mutual relationship of creatures comprises the universe's good, and no separated part achieves perfection. Therefore, it seems improbable that God, whose "works are perfect," created angels before other creatures. Yet the contrary view merits respect, especially given Gregory Nazianzen's authority in Christian doctrine.

### Reply to Objection 1
Jerome speaks according to Greek Fathers' teaching, all holding that angels were created before the corporeal world.

### Reply to Objection 2
God transcends the entire universe rather than being its part, possessing the universe's perfection eminently within Himself. An angel comprises part of the universe; therefore, the comparison fails.

### Reply to Objection 3
All corporeal creatures share one matter; angels lack matter in common with them. Creating corporeal matter thereby involves creating all things in a manner, whereas creating angels doesn't necessarily involve the universe's creation.

If the contrary view holds, "In the beginning" in Genesis must mean "In the Son" or "In the beginning of time," not "Before which nothing existed," unless applied solely to corporeal creatures.

## Article 4: Whether the Angels Were Created in the Empyrean Heaven

### Objection 1
Angels are incorporeal substances independent of bodies for their existence and creation. Therefore, they weren't created in any corporeal place.

### Objection 2
Augustine remarks that angels were created in the upper atmosphere, not the empyrean heaven.

### Objection 3
If angels were created in the highest heaven, they shouldn't mount to a still higher place. Yet Isaiah describes the sinning angel saying "I will ascend into heaven" (Isaiah 14:13), contradicting this.

### On the Contrary
Strabus, commenting on "In the beginning God created heaven and earth," explains that "heaven" refers to the empyrean—the fiery or intellectual firmament—which was filled with angels immediately upon creation.

### I Answer That
The universe comprises corporeal and spiritual creatures. Spiritual creatures were created bearing relationship to corporeal creatures and ruling over them. Therefore, angels fittingly were created in the highest corporeal place, presiding over all corporeal nature—whether called the empyrean heaven or otherwise. Isidore affirms that the highest heaven is the angels' heaven, explaining Deuteronomy's passage: "Behold heaven is the Lord's thy God, and the heaven of heaven."

### Reply to Objection 1
Angels were created in a corporeal place not from dependence on bodies regarding existence or creation—God could have created them before all corporeal creation, as many holy Doctors hold. Rather, their creation in a corporeal place demonstrates their relationship to corporeal nature and that their power touches bodies.

### Reply to Objection 2
By "uppermost atmosphere," Augustine possibly meant heaven's highest part, to which atmosphere relates through subtlety and transparency. Alternatively, he addressed only sinning angels, who perhaps belonged to inferior orders. Nothing prevents higher angels, possessing exalted universal power over corporeal things, from being created in the corporeal creature's highest place, while inferior angels were created among lower bodies.

### Reply to Objection 3
Isaiah addresses no corporeal heaven but the heaven of the Blessed Trinity. The sinning angel desired ascending to it, wishing equality with God, as later appears in the text.
