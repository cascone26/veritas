# Question 62: The Perfection of the Angels in the Order of Grace and of Glory

## Article 1: Whether the Angels Were Created in Beatitude

### Objection 1
Angels were created in beatitude, as stated in De Eccl. Dogm. xxix, which indicates "the angels who continue in the beatitude wherein they were created."

### Objection 2
The angelic nature surpasses corporeal creatures. Corporeal creation was made perfect instantly, without form lacking temporally but only naturally. Therefore, angels must have been created in beatitude.

### Objection 3
According to Augustine, things made during the six days were created simultaneously. The "morning" of angelic knowledge was their knowing the Word. Therefore, angels possessed beatitude from creation's beginning.

### On the Contrary
Beatitude requires establishment in goodness. Angels' fall proves they were not confirmed in good from creation. Therefore, they were not in beatitude initially.

### I Answer That
Beatitude represents ultimate perfection of rational or intellectual nature. Two ultimate perfections exist: one achievable through natural power (called happiness), another beyond nature—seeing God as He is.

As to the first beatitude achievable by natural power, angels were created blessed. They acquire such beatitude without progressive action, possessing it through natural dignity. However, ultimate beatitude surpassing nature was not given them initially, since it represents their end rather than their nature.

### Reply to Objection 1
Beatitude here means that natural perfection angels possessed in innocence.

### Reply to Objection 2
Corporeal creatures lack operative perfection instantly at creation. Augustine shows germinating power, not mature growth, was initially bestowed. Similarly, angels possessed natural perfection initially but not perfection achieved through operation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Angels possess twofold knowledge of the Word: natural knowledge through similitude in their nature, and glorious knowledge through His essence. The first was present from creation; the second came only through turning to God in beatitude.

## Article 2: Whether an Angel Needs Grace to Turn to God

### Objection 1
Angels need no grace for natural acts. Angels naturally love God. Therefore, grace is unnecessary for turning to God.

### Objection 2
Help is required only for difficult tasks. Turning to God presented no obstacle for angels. Therefore, grace was unnecessary.

### Objection 3
Turning to God disposes one for grace. If grace were needed to turn to God, infinite regress would follow.

### On the Contrary
Angels reached beatitude through turning to God. Grace is necessary for everlasting life, as stated in Romans 6:23.

### I Answer That
Angels required grace to turn toward God as beatitude's object. The will's natural inclination extends only to what accords with nature. Seeing God's essence exceeds all created intellect's nature. Therefore, the will cannot be inclined toward such beatitude except through supernatural assistance.

### Reply to Objection 1
Angels love God naturally as author of natural being. However, here we address turning to God as beatitude's source through essence vision.

### Reply to Objection 2
"Difficult" means beyond power either because surpassing natural capacity or through intervening hindrance. For angels, it is difficult only because supernatural.

### Reply to Objection 3
Three conversions to God exist: perfect love possessing Him (requiring consummated grace), merit deserving beatitude (requiring habitual grace), and self-disposition for grace (requiring only God's drawing operation). No infinite regress occurs.

## Article 3: Whether the Angels Were Created in Grace

### Objection 1
Augustine states angelic nature was first made without form, then received form through grace. Therefore, they were not created in grace.

### Objection 2
Grace turns rational creatures toward God. If angels possessed grace initially, none would have turned away.

### Objection 3
Grace lies between nature and glory. Angels were not beatified at creation. Therefore, they were created in nature only, receiving grace afterward, then beatification.

### On the Contrary
Augustine states: "Who wrought the good will of the angels? Who, save Him Who created them with His will, that is, with the pure love wherewith they cling to Him; at the same time building up their nature and bestowing grace on them?"

### I Answer That
Most probable and consistent with holy men's teachings: angels were created in sanctifying grace. All things produced through Divine Providence in time possess seedlike forms at initial creation. Sanctifying grace bears the same relation to beatitude as seedlike forms do to natural effects. Therefore, angels were created with grace from the beginning.

### Reply to Objection 1
Angelic formlessness can be understood either by comparison with glory-formation or grace-formation. Regarding grace, it preceded not temporally but by nature's order, like Augustine's position on corporeal things.

### Reply to Objection 2
Forms incline subjects according to the subject's nature. Intellectual nature inclines freely toward desired objects. Grace's movement does not impose necessity; possessing grace, one may neglect it and sin.

### Reply to Objection 3
Although grace lies between nature and glory in the order of nature, in created beings glory is not simultaneous with nature—it is operation's end. Grace functions as operation's principle, not as operation's end. Therefore, grace fittingly accompanied nature from creation.

## Article 4: Whether an Angel Merits His Beatitude

### Objection 1
Merit arises from difficulty in righteous action. Angels experienced no difficulty in acting rightly. Therefore, righteous action was unmeritorious.

### Objection 2
Merit does not arise from natural operations. Turning to God was natural for angels. Therefore, they did not merit beatitude.

### Objection 3
If angels merited beatitude, it was either before or after possession. Before: many deny angels possessed grace beforehand. After: then they merit it currently, which is false—lower angels could ascend, and grace-degrees would not remain permanent.

### On the Contrary
Apocalypse 21:17 states the angel's "measure" equals a man's measure in heavenly Jerusalem, indicating equivalent meritorious status.

### I Answer That
Perfect beatitude is natural only to God. Beatitude is creation's end, not its nature. Everything attains its last end through operation. Such operation either produces the end (when within the agent's power) or deserves it (when exceeding the agent's capacity).

Ultimate beatitude exceeds both angelic and human nature. Therefore, both merit their beatitude.

If angels possessed grace initially—without which merit is impossible—saying they merited presents no difficulty. If they lacked grace before beatitude, they would possess it without merit, contrary to beatitude's notion as virtue's reward.

Therefore: angels possessed grace before beatification and merited beatitude through that grace.

### Reply to Objection 1
Angel difficulty in righteous action stems not from power-contrariety but from the good work exceeding natural capacity.

### Reply to Objection 2
Angels did not merit beatitude through natural movement toward God but through charity arising from grace.

## Article 5: Whether the Angel Obtained Beatitude Immediately After One Act of Merit

### Objection 1
Righteous action is more difficult for humans than angels. Humans are not rewarded after one meritorious act. Therefore, neither were angels.

### Objection 2
Angels could act instantly from creation's first instant. If one meritorious act merited beatitude, angels merited it at creation's outset. Therefore, angels were beatified immediately.

### Objection 3
Things far apart require many intermediate intervals. Angelic beatitude is remote from natural condition; merit lies between. Therefore, angels must traverse many merit-stages.

### On the Contrary
Human souls and angels are alike ordained for beatitude. Saints are promised equality with angels. A separated soul possessing merit deserving beatitude enters beatitude immediately unless obstructed. Angels instantly possessed meritorious charity in their first act. Without internal obstacle, they entered beatitude through one meritorious act.

### I Answer That
The angel was beatified instantly after charity's first act meriting beatitude. Grace perfects nature according to nature's mode. Angelic nature receives perfection not progressively but immediately naturally. As angels are naturally inclined to natural perfection, by merit they are inclined to glory. Therefore, instantly after merit, the angel secured beatitude. Merit can arise from single acts; humans merit beatitude through every charity-informed act. Therefore, angels were beatified straightway after one charity act.

### Reply to Objection 1
Humans were not intended to secure ultimate perfection immediately like angels. A longer path was assigned to humanity.

### Reply to Objection 2
Angels transcend corporeal time; their instants concern succession of acts. Merit and beatitude-fruition cannot coexist—the former belongs to imperfect grace, the latter to consummate grace. Therefore, different instants obtain: one wherein angels merited, another wherein they were beatified.

### Reply to Objection 3
It is angelic nature to instantly attain ordained perfection. Consequently, only one meritorious act is required, which serves as the interval bringing angels to beatitude.

## Article 6: Whether the Angels Receive Grace and Glory According to the Degree of Their Natural Gifts

### Objection 1
Grace proceeds from God's absolute will. Therefore, grace-degree depends on God's will, not natural gifts' degree.

### Objection 2
Moral acts relate more closely to grace than nature does, as preparation for grace. Yet grace does not come "of works." Therefore, grace-degree depends even less on natural gifts.

### Objection 3
Humans and angels are alike ordained for beatitude. Humans do not receive more grace according to natural gifts' degree. Therefore, neither do angels.

### On the Contrary
The Master of the Sentences states: "Those angels created with subtler natures and keener intelligence in wisdom were likewise endowed with greater gifts of grace."

### I Answer That
Reasonably, grace and beatitude-perfection gifts were bestowed on angels according to their natural gifts' degree. Two sources support this:

First, from God's perspective: God established various degrees in angelic nature wisely. Angelic nature was made to attain grace and beatitude. Therefore, angelic nature's grades seem ordained for grace and glory's various degrees—like a builder preparing stones more artistically for more ornate house-parts.

Second, from the angel's perspective: Angels are not compound of conflicting natures. When nothing retards or thwarts motion, nature moves with full energy. Therefore, angels with higher nature were turned to God more mightily and efficaciously. Greater grace and glory are bestowed according to turning-to-God earnestness in humans similarly. Therefore, angels with greater natural powers possessed greater grace and glory.

### Reply to Objection 1
Grace comes from God's will alone; so does angelic nature. God's will ordained nature for grace and various nature-degrees to various grace-degrees.

### Reply to Objection 2
Creature acts come from creatures; nature comes directly from God. God's will ordained nature for grace more than ordained grace for works. Therefore, grace follows nature-degree rather than works.

### Reply to Objection 3
Specific difference (in angels) differs from numerical difference (in humans). Specific difference regards the end; numerical difference regards matter. Additionally, something in humans can thwart intellective nature's movement, but not in angels. Therefore, the argument differs for both.

## Article 7: Whether Natural Knowledge and Love Remain in the Beatified Angels

### Objection 1
First Corinthians 13:10 states: "When that which is perfect is come, then that which is in part shall be done away." Natural knowledge and love are imperfect compared to beatified knowledge and love. Therefore, they cease in beatitude.

### Objection 2
Where one suffices, another is superfluous. Glorious knowledge and love suffice for beatified angels. Therefore, natural knowledge and love remaining would be superfluous.

### Objection 3
One faculty cannot simultaneously perform two acts, as one line cannot terminate at two points simultaneously. Beatified angels perpetually exercise beatified knowledge and love (since happiness consists in act). Therefore, natural knowledge and love cannot remain.

### On the Contrary
So long as nature endures, its operation remains. Beatitude does not destroy nature but perfects it. Therefore, it does not remove natural knowledge and love.

### I Answer That
Natural knowledge and love remain in angels. Operation principles relate like operations themselves. Nature relates to beatitude as first to second—beatitude is nature's addition. The first must be preserved in the second. Therefore, nature must be preserved in beatitude, and nature's act must be preserved in beatitude's act.

### Reply to Objection 1
Perfection's advent removes opposite imperfection. Nature's imperfection is not opposed to beatitude's perfection but underlies it—as potency underlies form. Natural knowledge's imperfection is not opposed to glorious knowledge's perfection. Things can be known simultaneously through different mediums—probably and demonstratively. Similarly, an angel knows God through His essence (glorious knowledge) and through his own essence (natural knowledge) simultaneously.

### Reply to Objection 2
All beatitude-composing things are self-sufficient. However, for their existence, they presuppose natural gifts, since uncreated beatitude alone is self-subsisting.

### Reply to Objection 3
One faculty cannot simultaneously perform two operations unless one is ordained to the other. Natural knowledge and love are ordained to glorious knowledge and love. Therefore, nothing prevents their coexisting in the angel conjointly with glory's knowledge and love.

## Article 8: Whether a Beatified Angel Can Sin

### Objection 1
Beatitude does not eliminate nature. Created nature's very notion is that it can fail. Therefore, beatified angels can sin.

### Objection 2
Rational powers regard opposites, as the Philosopher observes. The angel's beatified will remains rational. Therefore, it inclines toward good and evil.

### Objection 3
Free will's liberty involves choosing good or evil. Beatified angels' will-freedom is not lessened. Therefore, they can sin.

### On the Contrary
Augustine states: "There is in the holy angels that nature which cannot sin."

### I Answer That
Beatified angels cannot sin. Their beatitude consists in seeing God through His essence. God's essence is goodness's very essence. Therefore, the angel beholding God is disposed toward God as anyone not seeing God is toward goodness's common form. No one can will or do anything except aiming at what is good, nor wish turning away from good precisely as such. Therefore, the beatified angel cannot will or act except aiming toward God. Whoever wills or acts thus cannot sin. Consequently, beatified angels cannot sin.

### Reply to Objection 1
Created good, considered alone, can fail. However, from its perfect union with uncreated good—beatitude's union—it becomes unable to sin for the stated reason.

### Reply to Objection 2
Rational powers regard opposites in things they are not naturally inclined toward. However, as to things whereunto they have natural tendency, they are not referred to opposites. The intellect cannot but assent to naturally known principles; the will cannot help clinging to good formally as good. The will is naturally ordained to good as its proper object. Therefore, angels' will regards opposites concerning many things done or undone. However, they have no tendency to opposites regarding God Himself, Whom they see as goodness's very nature. In all things their aim is toward God, which ever alternative they choose, that is not sinful.

### Reply to Objection 3
Free will in choosing means to an end is disposed as the intellect is to conclusions. The intellect can proceed to different conclusions according to given principles; but proceeding outside the principles' order comes of its defect. Therefore, perfect liberty for free will involves choosing opposite things while maintaining the end's order. Choosing by turning away from the end's order comes from liberty's defect—this is sin. Therefore, angels—who cannot sin—possess greater will-liberty than we do, who can sin.

## Article 9: Whether the Beatified Angels Advance in Beatitude

### Objection 1
Charity is merit's principle. Perfect charity exists in angels. Therefore, beatified angels can merit. As merit increases, beatitude's reward increases. Therefore, beatified angels can progress in beatitude.

### Objection 2
Augustine states God uses us for our gain and His goodness. Similarly for angels, whom He uses for spiritual ministrations. This would not profit them unless they merited and advanced in beatitude. Therefore, beatified angels can merit and advance in beatitude.

### Objection 3
For anyone not occupying the foremost place to be unable to advance argues imperfection. Angels are not in beatitude's highest degree. Therefore, if unable to ascend higher, imperfection would attach to them—inadmissible.

### On the Contrary
Merit and progress belong to life's present wayfaring condition. Angels are not wayfarers traveling toward beatitude; they already possess it. Consequently, beatified angels can neither merit nor advance in beatitude.

### I Answer That
In every movement, the mover's intention centers upon one determined end, to which he intends moving the movable subject. Intention looks toward the end, to which infinite progress is repugnant.

Clearly, the rational creature cannot through its own power attain beatitude consisting in God-vision. It therefore needs God's moving it toward beatitude. Therefore, one determined thing must be toward which every rational creature is directed as its last end.

This determined object cannot consist precisely in what is seen—the Supreme Truth is seen by all blessed in various degrees. Rather, it lies in the vision-mode that is fixed beforehand by Him directing toward the end. The supreme vision-mode—comprehension—belongs to God alone. However, since infinite efficacy is required for comprehending God while the creature's beholding efficacy is finite, and since every finite being is infinitely removed from the infinite, the rational creature understands God more or less clearly according to infinite degrees. As beatitude consists in vision, vision-degree lies in a determinate vision-mode.

Therefore, every rational creature is led by God to beatitude's end such that from God's predestination it is brought to a determinate beatitude-degree. Consequently, when that degree is secured, it cannot pass to a higher degree.

### Reply to Objection 1
Merit belongs to a subject moving toward its end. The rational creature moves toward its end not merely passively but actively. If the end lies within rational capacity, the action procures it. If the end exceeds its power and is looked for from another, the action is meritorious.

However, what already exists in the ultimate term is not said to be moved but to have been moved. Consequently, merit belongs to this life's imperfect charity; perfect charity does not merit but rather enjoys reward. As in acquired habits, the operation preceding the habit produces it, but the operation from an acquired habit is perfect and enjoyable. Similarly, perfect charity's act has no merit-quality but belongs to reward's perfection.

### Reply to Objection 2
A thing is useful in two ways:

First, as being on the way to an end—merit of beatitude is thus useful.

Second, as the part is useful to the whole—the wall to the house. Angelic ministerings are useful for beatified angels inasmuch as they are their beatitude's part. Pouring out acquired perfection upon others belongs to what is perfect, considered as perfect.

### Reply to Objection 3
Although a beatified angel is not absolutely in beatitude's highest degree, in his own regard he is in the highest degree according to Divine predestination. Nevertheless, angels' joy can increase regarding those saved by their ministrations, according to Luke 15:10: "There is joy before the angels of God upon one sinner doing penance." Such joy belongs to their accidental reward, increasable until judgment day. Hence some writers say they can merit as to accidental reward. However, it is better to say the Blessed cannot merit without simultaneously being a wayfarer and comprehensor—like Christ alone. The Blessed acquire such joy from their beatitude's virtue rather than merit it.
