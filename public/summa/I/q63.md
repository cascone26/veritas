# Question 63: The Malice of the Angels with Regard to Sin

## Article 1: Whether the evil of fault can be in the angels?

### Objection 1
Evil cannot exist in things lacking potentiality, only in beings in potentiality. Angels are subsisting forms without potentiality to natural existence. Therefore, evil cannot exist in them.

### Objection 2
Angels surpass heavenly bodies in nature. Philosophers deny evil exists in heavenly bodies. Thus evil cannot exist in angels either.

### Objection 3
Natural movement toward God through love is essential to angels. This natural inclination cannot be withdrawn. Therefore angels cannot sin.

### Objection 4
Only good or apparent good can be desired. Angels cannot experience apparent good that is not truly good, lacking error before guilt. Thus angels cannot sin through desire.

### On the contrary
Job 4:18 states: "In His angels He found wickedness."

### I answer that
Any rational creature, including angels, can sin by nature. Those incapable of sinning possess this immunity as a divine gift, not from nature itself. Sin consists in deviating from proper rectitude in action. Only God's will, which needs no external regulation, cannot sin. Every created will achieves rectitude only when regulated according to divine will, similar to how a subordinate's will must align with commanding authority. Therefore, sin is possible in any creature's will by its nature.

### Reply to Objection 1
Angels lack potentiality for natural existence, yet possess potentiality in their intellective part regarding inclination toward various objects. Evil can exist through this intellectual potentiality.

### Reply to Objection 2
Heavenly bodies operate only naturally, preventing both natural corruption and disordered natural action. Angels possess free will beyond natural operation, through which evil can occur.

### Reply to Objection 3
Angels naturally turn to God as their natural principle. However, turning to God as supernatural beatitude comes through infused love, from which sinning can turn them away.

### Reply to Objection 4
Mortal sin occurs two ways in free will. First, when something evil is chosen, requiring ignorance or error. This cannot occur in angels lacking passions or prior sinful habits. Second, sin occurs through choosing something good in itself but without proper measure or regulation. This requires no ignorance, only absent consideration. The angel sinned thus, seeking his own good from free will without proper subordination to divine regulation.

## Article 2: Whether only the sin of pride and envy can exist in an angel?

### Objection 1
Demons delight in obscenities of carnal sins, as Augustine notes. Therefore carnal sins can exist in demons.

### Objection 2
Pride and envy are spiritual sins. Sloth, avarice, and anger are equally spiritual. Therefore these can exist in angels as well.

### Objection 3
Gregory teaches many vices spring from pride and envy. If causes exist, effects follow. Therefore other vices can exist in demons.

### On the contrary
Augustine states the devil "is not a fornicator nor a drunkard, nor anything of the like sort; yet he is proud and envious."

### I answer that
Sin exists in demons two ways: by actual guilt and by affection. Regarding guilt, all sins exist in demons through inducing human sin. Regarding affection, only sins befitting spiritual nature can exist in demons. Spiritual nature cannot experience bodily pleasures but only spiritual ones. Sin requires either choosing evil itself or choosing good insubordinately. The first sin of angels could only be pride—not submitting to superiors when submission is due. Envy follows necessarily: the envious grieve over another's good deemed hindering their own excellence. The demon coveted singular excellence, so another's excellence threatened his aspiration. Thus after pride came envy, grieving over human good and divine excellence contrary to the demon's will.

### Reply to Objection 1
Demons delight in human fleshly sins through envy alone, not from carnal disposition. They take pleasure in all human sins as hindering human good.

### Reply to Objection 2
Avarice involves immoderate desire for temporal possessions serving human life. Demons have no inclination toward such goods, so proper avarice cannot exist in them, though excessive desire for any created good falls under pride. Anger and concupiscence imply passion, existing only metaphorically in demons. Sloth involves sadness making one sluggish in spiritual exercise through bodily weariness, inapplicable to demons. Thus pride and envy are the only spiritual sins finding residence in demons.

### Reply to Objection 3
All other sins derived from envy and pride in demons are comprised within these two primary sins.

## Article 3: Whether the devil desired to be as God?

### Objection 1
Creatures cannot desire their own equality with God, as this involves logical contradiction. For a finite being to equal infinity would make it infinite. Therefore angels could not desire such equality.

### Objection 2
Every creature naturally tends toward resemblance to God as its end. If the angel desired resemblance without equality, he would not sin.

### Objection 3
Angels possess greater wisdom than men. Yet no prudent man desires equality with angels, much less God, since choice concerns possible things requiring deliberation. Therefore angels would not sin by desiring godlikeness.

### On the contrary
Isaiah 14:13-14 records the devil saying: "I will ascend into heaven...I will be like the Most High." Augustine notes he wished "to be called God" through pride.

### I answer that
The devil certainly sinned in seeking to be as God. Two interpretations exist: equality or likeness. Equality is impossible—the angel knew this through natural knowledge and lacked conditions enabling impossible choice. Moreover, natural desire preserves one's own nature; becoming another nature means ceasing to exist. No lower creature seeks a higher nature's grade, as this destroys itself. Godlikeness interpreted as resemblance can occur rightly when properly ordered toward God. However, seeking such likeness independently rather than from God constitutes sin. One may also sin desiring godlike qualities inappropriate to creatures, such as creating heavens and earth. The devil sinned thus, desiring godlikeness in seeking final beatitude through his own nature rather than supernatural beatitude through divine grace. He sought resemblance by possessing beatitude's achievement through his own power alone, which belongs exclusively to God. Consequently, he sought dominion over others, perversely imitating divine governance.

### Reply to Objection 1
The response clarifies the distinction between desiring impossible equality and desiring likeness in inappropriate ways.

### Reply to Objection 2
The response explains proper and improper seeking of godlikeness through divine assistance versus independent power.

### Reply to Objection 3
The response notes angels lack error before guilt and passion clouding judgment, making impossible desires unlikely except through misapplied imagination.

## Article 4: Whether any demons are naturally wicked?

### Objection 1
Porphyry describes demons as "craftily natured," deceptive beings. Deception constitutes evil. Therefore some demons are naturally wicked.

### Objection 2
Men are created by God like angels. Yet some men possess natural malice as stated in Wisdom 12:10. Therefore angels may possess natural wickedness.

### Objection 3
Irrational animals possess natural vicious dispositions—foxes naturally sly, wolves naturally rapacious—yet God created them. Therefore demons, God's creatures, may be naturally wicked.

### On the contrary
Dionysius states "demons are not naturally wicked."

### I answer that
Everything existing, insofar as it exists with particular nature, naturally tends toward some good, proceeding from a good principle. Particular goods may involve associated evils; universal good connects with no evil. If nature inclines toward some particular good, natural inclination toward associated evil is possible but only accidentally as connected with good. If nature inclines toward good generally, it cannot naturally incline toward evil. Intellectual nature inclines toward good universally, which it apprehends as will's object. Since demons are intellectual substances, they possess no natural inclination toward any evil; therefore they cannot be naturally evil.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine rebukes Porphyry's view that demons are naturally deceitful. Demons deceive through their own will, not nature. Porphyry held demons possess sensitive rather than intellectual nature, capable of natural inclination toward evil only accidentally through goods connected with it.

### Reply to Objection 2
Human malice called "natural" stems either from custom becoming second nature or from sensitive nature's inclination toward inordinate passion, not from intellectual nature.

### Reply to Objection 3
Brute beasts possess natural sensitive inclination toward particular goods with connected evils. The fox's slyness seeking food involves natural skilled deception. Thus slyness is not evil in foxes as it is natural, just as fierceness is not evil in dogs.

## Article 5: Whether the devil was wicked by fault of his own will in the first instant of his creation?

### Objection 1
John 8:44 states the devil "was a murderer from the beginning," indicating immediate wickedness.

### Objection 2
Augustine teaches that form's lack in creatures precedes only by nature, not time. The "heaven" created initially signifies unformed angelic nature; "light made" signifies full angelic formation through turning to the Word. Thus both occurred in one instant; simultaneously some became blessed and others sinned.

### Objection 3
Sin opposes merit. Yet intellectual natures can merit in their first instant—Christ's soul and good angels did so. Therefore demons could similarly sin in their first instant.

### Objection 4
Intellectual natures surpass corporeal ones. Yet corporeal things begin operating in their first instant—fire moves upward immediately. Therefore angels could operate in their first instant. Either this operation was ordered or disordered. If ordered, with grace, the angel would merit beatitude immediately. Yet some sinned; therefore they acted disordinately in their first instant.

### On the contrary
Genesis 1:31 states: "God saw all the things that He had made, and they were very good." Demons were among these things. Therefore demons were good at some time.

### I answer that
Some maintained demons became wicked immediately in their first instant through free will sin, not natural evil. Augustine rejected this view as contradicting Scripture, which describes the devil's former perfection and paradise dwelling. Others argued angels could have sinned in the first instant but did not. Yet this view faces difficulty: when two operations follow one another, both cannot terminate in one instant. Creation's term is the angel's being; sin's term is wickedness. Two such terminations seem impossible in one instant.

However, this argument fails. It applies only to successive movements measured temporally. If changes are instantaneous, both can terminate simultaneously in one instant, as when sunlight illuminates the moon and moonlight illuminates earth simultaneously. Creation and free will movement are both instantaneous in angels, lacking discursive reasoning. Therefore their terms could exist in the same instant.

Yet this must be rejected. Whatever begins existing through an agent draws its operation from that agent's nature, as fire moves upward from its productive cause. If something derives nature from a defective cause capable of defective action, it can perform defective operation in its first instant, as a defective limb limps from birth through generation's defective principle. But God, causing angels' existence, cannot be sin's cause. Therefore the devil was not wicked in his creation's first instant.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine explains: when Scripture says the devil "sins from the beginning," this means from "sin's beginning," not "creation's beginning"—he never abandoned his sin.

### Reply to Objection 2
Light and darkness' distinction, signifying demons' sins through darkness, must be understood according to God's foreknowledge. Augustine notes that God alone could foreknow those who would fall before they fell.

### Reply to Objection 3
All merit comes from God; angels could merit in their first instant. Sin differs: God cannot cause it. Therefore the same reasoning does not apply.

### Reply to Objection 4
God did not distinguish angels before some turned from Him and others turned toward Him. All were created in grace and merited in their first instant. Some then placed beatitude barriers through sinning, destroying preceding merit and losing merited beatitude.

## Article 6: Whether there was any interval between the angel's creation and fall?

### Objection 1
Ezekiel 28:15 states: "Thou hast walked perfect in thy ways from the day of thy creation until iniquity was found in thee." Walking represents continuous movement requiring an interval. Therefore time elapsed between creation and fall.

### Objection 2
Origen teaches the ancient serpent did not initially walk on his breast and belly, referring to sin. Therefore the devil did not sin immediately after creation.

### Objection 3
Angels and men share capacity for sinning. Yet time elapsed between man's formation and sin. Therefore similarly, time should have elapsed for the devil.

### Objection 4
Creation's instant differs from sin's instant. Some time must exist between any two distinct instants. Therefore an interval separated creation and fall.

### On the contrary
Scripture states of the devil: "He stood not in the truth," understood as meaning he was in truth but did not remain there.

### I answer that
Two views exist on this matter. The more probable opinion, harmonizing with Saints' teachings, is that the devil sinned immediately after his first instant of creation. This follows if he elicited a free will act in his first creation instant and was created in grace. Since angels achieve beatitude through one meritorious act as stated previously, if the devil created in grace merited in the first instant, he would receive beatitude immediately afterward if not impeded by sin.

However, if the angel was not created in grace or could not elicit free will acts in his first instant, nothing prevents an interval between creation and fall.

### Reply to Objection 1
Scripture sometimes represents instantaneous spiritual movements through continuous corporeal movements. "Walking" signifies free will movement toward good.

### Reply to Objection 2
Origen means the serpent did not initially walk on breast and belly because he was not yet wicked in the first instant.

### Reply to Objection 3
Angels possess inflexible free will after choosing once. If not immediately barring beatitude after the first instant's natural movement toward good, he would be confirmed in goodness. This does not apply to men, so the argument fails.

### Reply to Objection 4
While time as continuous has middle moments between instants, angels, unsubject to heavenly movement's continuous time, experience time through mental act succession. The first instant corresponds to the evening knowledge operation wherein all angels introspected themselves—all were good. From such operation, some converted through morning knowledge to Word praise, while others absorbed in themselves became "night" through pride, as Augustine states. All were good in the first instant; the second instant separated good from wicked.

## Article 7: Whether the highest angel among those who sinned was the highest of all?

### Objection 1
Ezekiel 28:14 describes the devil as a cherub, not seraph. Dionysius ranks cherubim below seraphim. Therefore the highest fallen angel was not the highest among all.

### Objection 2
God made intellectual nature to achieve beatitude. If the highest angel sinned, divine ordinance was frustrated in the noblest creature—unfitting.

### Objection 3
Higher inclination toward something makes falling from it less possible. Higher angels incline more toward God. Therefore the sinning angel was not the highest, but lower ranked.

### On the contrary
Gregory states the chief sinning angel "being set over all the hosts of angels, surpassed them in brightness, and was by comparison the most illustrious among them."

### I answer that
Two considerations apply: proneness to sin and sinning's motive. Regarding proneness, higher angels were less likely to sin than lower ones. Damascene observes the highest fallen was set over terrestrial order, agreeing with Platonic philosophy which Augustine cites. All gods were good; some demons were good, others bad. "Gods" meant intellectual substances above the lunar sphere; "demons" meant intellectual substances below it yet higher than men. This opinion is not contrary to faith: God governs corporeal creation through angels, so lower angels might preside over lower bodies, higher over higher, the highest before God. In this sense, Damascene claims fallen angels were lower ranking, though some in that order remained good.

However, considering sinning's motive, it existed more in higher angels. The demons' sin was pride; pride's motive is excellence, greater in higher spirits. Thus Gregory says the sinning angel was the very highest. This view is more probable: angels' sin came not from proneness but free choice alone. The motive argument carries greater weight. Yet this does not exclude the alternative view; the lower angels' chief might have had sinning's motive.

### Reply to Objection 1
"Cherubim" means "fullness of knowledge"; "Seraphim" means "those on fire" or "setting on fire." Cherubim derives from knowledge compatible with mortal sin; Seraphim derives from charity's heat incompatible with mortal sin. Thus the sinning angel is called cherub, not seraph.

### Reply to Objection 2
Divine intention is not frustrated: God foreknew both sinners and saved ones and procures glory from both, saving through goodness and punishing through justice. However, the sinning intellectual creature fell from its due end. This unfits no exalted creature: intellectual creatures were made such that their will can turn to evil.

### Reply to Objection 3
However great the highest angel's inclination toward good, no necessity compelled him: he retained power not to follow it.

## Article 8: Whether the sin of the highest angel was the cause of the others sinning?

### Objection 1
Causes precede effects. Yet Damascene observes all demons sinned simultaneously. Therefore one's sin was not another's sinning cause.

### Objection 2
Angels' first sin was pride, seeking excellence. Subjection to an inferior opposes excellence more than subjection to a superior. Thus angels would not sin by desiring subjection to a higher angel rather than God. Yet the chief's sin caused others' sinning by making them his subjects. Therefore the highest angel's sin was not the others' sinning cause.

### Objection 3
Desiring subjection to another against God exceeds desiring dominion against God in sin's severity, having less sinning motive. If the chief's sin caused others' subjection to him, lower angels sinned more deeply than the highest—contradicting a Psalm 103:26 gloss stating the more excellent became greater in malice. Therefore the chief's sin was not the cause.

### On the contrary
Apocalypse 12:4 states the dragon "drew" with him "the third part of the stars of heaven."

### I answer that
The highest angel's sin caused others' sinning not through compulsion but through exhortation-like inducement. Evidence appears in all demons being his subjects, as Christ's words indicate: "Depart from Me, cursed, into everlasting fire prepared for the devil and his angels." Divine justice orders that whoever consents to another's evil suggestion becomes enslaved to him in punishment, per 2 Peter 2:19: "By whom a man is overcome, of the same also he is the slave."

### Reply to Objection 1
Although demons sinned simultaneously, one's sin could cause others' sinning. Angels require no time delay for choice, exhortation, or consent as men do, needing deliberation for choosing and speech for exhorting. In one instant the highest angel expressed his affection through intelligible speech, and others could consent, especially with primary concepts which everyone accepts directly upon hearing.

### Reply to Objection 2
Other things equal, the proud prefer superior to inferior subjection. Yet he chooses inferior subjection if it provides advantage unavailable under a superior. Thus demons wished the chief as prince and leader achieving beatitude through their own natural powers. This was not against their pride, especially since they remained naturally subject to the highest angel anyway.

### Reply to Objection 3
Angels have nothing retarding action; whatever moves them receives their whole might, good or bad. Since the highest angel possessed greater natural energy, he fell into sin with intenser energy, becoming greater in malice.

## Article 9: Whether those who sinned were as many as those who remained firm?

### Objection 1
The Philosopher teaches "evil is in many, but good is in few."

### Objection 2
Justice and sin exist similarly in angels and men. Yet more wicked men exist than good, as Ecclesiastes 1:15 states "the number of fools is infinite." Therefore similarly more angels sinned.

### Objection 3
Angels distinguish according to persons and orders. If more remained firm, those sinning would not represent all orders.

### On the contrary
2 Kings 6:16 states: "There are more with us than with them," explained as the good angels aiding us versus wicked spirits opposing us.

### I answer that
More angels remained firm than sinned. Sin opposes natural inclination; what opposes nature occurs less frequently. Nature produces effects either always or more often than not.

### Reply to Objection 1
The Philosopher speaks regarding men, in whom evil stems from seeking sensible pleasures known to most, forsaking reason's good known to few. Angels possess only intellectual nature; the argument does not apply.

### Reply to Objection 2
The response above suffices for this objection.

### Reply to Objection 3
Those holding the chief devil belonged to lower angels' order overseeing earthly matters find that only the lowest order fully fell, with some remaining in every order. Those maintaining the chief was highest probably hold some fell from every order, as men fill orders replacing angelic ruin. This better establishes free will liberty: at every creature-degree, will can turn to evil. Scripture notably does not attribute Seraphim and Thrones names to demons, derived from love's ardor and God's indwelling incompatible with mortal sin. Yet Cherubim, Powers, and Principalities names apply to demons, derived from knowledge and power common to good and bad.
