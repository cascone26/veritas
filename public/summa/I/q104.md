# Question 104: The Special Effects of Divine Government

## Article 1: Whether Creatures Need to Be Kept in Being by God

### Objection 1

Some creatures by their nature cannot fail to exist, particularly spiritual substances and heavenly bodies. Since being necessarily results from their form, they possess existence of themselves and require no divine preservation.

### Objection 2

God possesses greater power than created agents. Yet created agents preserve their effects even after ceasing to act—a house stands after the builder stops building. Therefore God can preserve creatures without continuous action.

### Objection 3

Tendency toward non-existence is violent and unnatural, requiring an active corrupting cause. Spiritual substances and heavenly bodies cannot be corrupted by anything, so they need not fear annihilation even if God withdrew His action.

### Objection 4

If God preserves creatures through action, that action must produce something in the effect. But preservation neither gives new being (the creature already exists) nor adds anything new continuously. Therefore creatures are not actively preserved.

### On the contrary

Scripture states: "Upholding all things by the word of His power" (Hebrews 1:3).

### I answer that

Both reason and faith establish that creatures are preserved by God. Preservation occurs two ways: indirectly (removing corruption's causes) and directly (when the preserved depends entirely on the preserver for existence). All creatures need direct preservation. Every effect depends on its cause insofar as it is a cause.

An agent may cause an effect's "becoming" without causing its "being." A builder causes a house's becoming through arrangement of materials, but the house's being depends on those materials' natural properties. However, agents producing effects of different species can cause form itself, thus causing both becoming and being.

Light illustrates this principle: water retains heat after fire ceases because water can receive heat as fire possesses it. Air loses light immediately when the sun ceases acting because air cannot receive light as the sun possesses it—light has no root in air.

Every creature relates to God as air relates to the sun. God alone possesses being by His essence; His existence is His essence. Every creature has being by participation; its essence is not its existence. As Augustine states: "If God's ruling power were withdrawn, creatures' nature would cease at once."

### Reply to Objection 1

Being naturally results from a creature's form given divine influence, just as light results from air's transparent nature given the sun's action. The potentiality to non-being in spiritual creatures rests in God's ability to withdraw His influence, not in their form or matter.

### Reply to Objection 2

God cannot grant creatures preservation after ceasing His influence, just as He cannot make them not receive being from Him. Created agents only cause becoming, not being.

### Reply to Objection 3

This argument concerns preservation through removing corruption, which not all creatures require.

### Reply to Objection 4

God's preservation continues the action whereby He gives existence—an action timeless and without motion. Light's preservation in air similarly results from the sun's continual influence.

## Article 2: Whether God Preserves Every Creature Immediately

### Objection 1

God creates and preserves through the same action. Since He created all things immediately, He preserves all immediately.

### Objection 2

A thing relates more closely to itself than to another. Creatures cannot preserve themselves, much less preserve others. Therefore God preserves all without intermediate causes.

### Objection 3

Effects are preserved by causes of their being, not merely becoming. All created causes only cause through motion, thus only cause becoming. Therefore they preserve nothing.

### On the contrary

God gives being through intermediate causes, therefore He preserves things through them.

### I answer that

As stated, preservation occurs indirectly and directly. Created things preserve others both ways. Many physical causes hinder corrupting agents—salt preserves meat from decay. Effects also depend on creatures for being in causal series: while the first cause principally preserves effects, intermediate causes preserve them secondarily. Higher causes nearer the first cause especially preserve things.

The Philosopher notes that the diurnal movement causes continuation of generated things, while the zodiacal movement causes diversity through generation and corruption. Thus God preserves certain things through intermediate causes.

### Reply to Objection 1

God created all immediately but established an order whereby some things depend on others for preservation, though He remains the principal cause.

### Reply to Objection 2

Since effects are preserved by proper causes on which they depend, just as no effect is its own cause, no effect possesses power of self-preservation, only of preserving another.

### Reply to Objection 3

Created nature cannot cause another's acquiring new form except through change. Yet after causing a form without fresh change, the cause preserves it—as light's preservation occurs without further change in air from the sun's presence.

## Article 3: Whether God Can Annihilate Anything

### Objection 1

Augustine states "God is not the cause of anything tending to non-existence." An annihilating God would be such a cause; therefore He cannot annihilate.

### Objection 2

God's goodness is why things exist. God cannot cease being good. Therefore He cannot cause things to cease existing.

### Objection 3

Annihilation would require God's action, but every action's term is existence. Therefore God cannot annihilate.

### On the contrary

Scripture states: "Correct me...not in Thy fury, lest Thou bring me to nothing" (Jeremiah 10:24).

### I answer that

Some held God acts from natural necessity in creating, so could not annihilate. This contradicts Catholic faith: God created freely according to Psalm 134:6: "Whatsoever the Lord pleased, He hath done." God's giving existence depends on His will. He preserves things by continually pouring out existence into them. Thus as He was free not to create before creation, He remains free not to continue existence after—this would be annihilation.

### Reply to Objection 1

Non-existence has no direct cause. God cannot directly cause things to tend toward non-existence, yet indirectly He causes this by withdrawing His action.

### Reply to Objection 2

God's goodness causes things not through natural necessity but through free will. As He might not have produced things, He might not preserve them without prejudice to His goodness.

### Reply to Objection 3

God's annihilation would not imply action but mere cessation of action.

## Article 4: Whether Anything Is Annihilated

### Objection 1

The end corresponds to the beginning. Initially only God existed; therefore all things must tend toward that state, becoming nothing.

### Objection 2

Every creature possesses finite power. The Philosopher proves finite power cannot move for infinite time. Therefore creatures cannot last infinitely and must eventually cease.

### Objection 3

Forms and accidents possess no matter as part of themselves yet cease to exist. Therefore they are reduced to nothing.

### On the contrary

Scripture states: "I have learned that all the works that God hath made continue for ever" (Ecclesiastes 3:14).

### I answer that

God acts on creatures according to nature or miraculously. Natural divine acts reflect creature natures; miraculous acts manifest grace. Creatures' natures show none undergo annihilation: immaterial beings have no potentiality to non-existence; material beings persist at least in matter, which is incorruptible as the subject of generation and corruption. Annihilation does not manifest grace; rather God's power and goodness show forth through preserving creatures.

### Reply to Objection 1

Creatures' emergence from non-existence manifests divine power, yet their reduction to nothing would impede this manifestation. God's power shows conspicuously in preserving all in existence.

### Reply to Objection 2

Creatures' potentiality to existence is merely receptive; active power belongs to God. The infinite duration of things follows from God's infinite power.

### Reply to Objection 3

Forms and accidents are incomplete beings, not subsisting independently. Yet they are not entirely reduced to nothing but remain in matter's or subject's potentiality.
