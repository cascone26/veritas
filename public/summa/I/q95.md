# Question 95: Things Pertaining to the First Man's Will—Grace and Righteousness

## Article 1: Whether the First Man Was Created in Grace

### Objection 1
The Apostle distinguishes Adam from Christ. Since only Christ's spirit is quickened by grace, Adam lacked grace.

### Objection 2
Augustine states Adam did not possess the Holy Ghost.

### Objection 3
Augustine indicates God first showed what creatures could do through free will alone, then by grace.

### Objection 4
The Master teaches God gave Adam help to stand but not advance. Only grace enables advancement.

### Objection 5
Grace requires the recipient's consent, which presupposes existence. Adam could not consent at creation.

### Objection 6
Nature precedes grace more than grace precedes glory.

### On the contrary
Both man and angel were ordained to grace. Augustine affirms angels were created with grace, so man was too.

### I answer that
The primitive state's rectitude—reason subjected to God, lower powers to reason, body to soul—necessarily stemmed from supernatural grace. After sin, this subjection dissolved only when grace was lost. Therefore, this subjection depended on grace. Augustine confirms that upon losing grace, they experienced shameful impulses.

### Reply to Objection 1
The Apostle means spiritual life originated in Christ for bodies, not denying Adam possessed spiritual life.

### Reply to Objection 2
Adam possessed the Holy Ghost as other just souls did, but not as the faithful now possess Him through admission to eternal happiness.

### Reply to Objection 3
Augustine shows God demonstrates what free will accomplishes, not that grace came afterward.

### Reply to Objection 4
Though created in grace, advancement came through grace, not created nature.

### Reply to Objection 5
Nothing prevents Adam's consent to grace even in his first moment.

### Reply to Objection 6
We merit glory through grace's act, not nature's act.

## Article 2: Whether Passions Existed in the First Man's Soul

### Objection 1
Passions cause flesh to lust against spirit. This opposition was absent in innocence.

### Objection 2
Adam's soul was nobler than his impassible body.

### Objection 3
Perfect moral virtues restrain passions entirely.

### On the contrary
Augustine affirms Adam possessed "undisturbed love of God" and other passions.

### I answer that
Since evil was neither present nor imminent in innocence, Adam lacked passions toward evil (fear, sorrow) and passions toward absent goods. However, passions regarding present good (joy, love) and properly timed future good (desire, hope) existed. His sensual appetite remained entirely subject to reason.

### Reply to Objection 1
Flesh lusts against spirit through passions rebelling against reason, impossible in innocence.

### Reply to Objection 2
The body was impassible regarding natural disruptions; the soul was impassible regarding disruptions to reason.

### Reply to Objection 3
Perfect virtue regulates rather than eliminates passions.

## Article 3: Whether Adam Had All Virtues

### Objection 1
Some virtues restrain excessive passions absent in innocence.

### Objection 2
Virtues addressing evil-passions were unnecessary.

### Objection 3
Penance and mercy had no objects in innocence.

### Objection 4
Adam lacked perseverance, proven by his sin.

### Objection 5
Faith involves obscurity incompatible with innocence.

### On the contrary
Augustine describes Adam as "adorned with modesty, restrained by temperance."

### I answer that
Adam possessed all virtues in some sense. Virtues involving no imperfection (charity, justice) existed absolutely. Virtues implying imperfection consistent with innocence (faith, hope) existed since Adam lacked divine vision. Virtues implying incompatible imperfection (penance, mercy) existed habitually but not actively.

### Reply to Objection 1
Restraining excess is accidental to temperance; moderating passions is essential.

### Reply to Objection 2
Adam could hate demonic malice. Virtues addressing passions in oneself existed habitually.

### Reply to Objection 4
Perseverance as habit existed. Perseverance as uninterrupted continuation did not.

## Article 4: Whether the First Man's Actions Were Less Meritorious Than Ours

### Objection 1
We need grace more, so receive it more copiously. Greater grace produces greater merit.

### Objection 2
We struggle more now, achieving greater merit.

### Objection 3
Adam gained no merit resisting temptation without grace.

### On the contrary
If true, man would be better off after sinning.

### I answer that
Merit's degree is measured by its root (grace and charity) and the action's degree (absolute or proportional). Adam's works were more meritorious considering grace's degree and absolute work degree. However, proportionally, greater merit now exists due to human weakness—small deeds exceed our capacity more than great deeds exceeded his ease.

### Reply to Objection 1
After sin, man needs grace for more things, not necessarily more grace. Post-sin, grace addresses sin's remission and weakness.

### Reply to Objection 2
Difficulty contributes to proportional merit. Easy deeds performed with equal promptness equal arduous deeds.

### Reply to Objection 3
Internal evil impulse was absent then, enabling resistance without grace's special aid.
