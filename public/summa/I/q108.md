# Question 108: The Angelic Degrees of Hierarchies and Orders

## Article 1: Whether All Angels Belong to One Hierarchy

### Objection 1

Since angels are supreme creatures, they must be ordered for the best. The Philosopher shows the best ordering is governance by one authority. Therefore all angels should belong to one hierarchy.

### Objection 2

Dionysius states hierarchy is order, knowledge, and action. All angels agree in one order toward God. Therefore all angels belong to one hierarchy.

### Objection 3

All men are of one hierarchy. Therefore likewise all angels are of one hierarchy.

### On the contrary

Dionysius distinguishes three hierarchies of angels.

### I answer that

Hierarchy means sacred principality. Since God is the sole Prince of all, there is one hierarchy in that respect. However, those governed in different ways belong to different principalities.

Men and angels receive Divine enlightenments differently: angels in intelligible purity, men under sensible signs. Therefore human and angelic hierarchies must be distinguished.

The superior angels possess more universal knowledge in three grades: as proceeding from God as universal principle (first hierarchy); as depending on universal created causes (second hierarchy); as applied to particular things (lowest hierarchy).

### Reply to Objection 1

Unity of rule does not necessitate unity of hierarchy when subjects cannot be governed in the same way.

### Reply to Objection 2

Regarding knowing God Himself in His essence, there is no hierarchical distinction. Distinction exists regarding knowledge of created things' types.

### Reply to Objection 3

All men are of one species with one connatural mode of understanding. Angels lack this uniformity.

## Article 2: Whether Several Orders Exist in One Hierarchy

### Objection 1

Dionysius says hierarchy is order. If there are many orders, there would be many hierarchies.

### Objection 2

Different orders constitute different grades. Yet "nothing is possessed individually" among angels. Therefore different orders do not exist.

### Objection 3

In the Church, orders are distinguished by cleansing, enlightening, and perfecting. But each angel cleanses, enlightens, and perfects. Therefore no distinction exists.

### On the contrary

The Apostle says God "has set the Man Christ above all principality and power, and virtue, and dominion"—various angelic orders belonging to one hierarchy.

### I answer that

One hierarchy constitutes one multitude ordered under a prince's rule. Every multitude requires a beginning, middle, and end, producing three orders in every hierarchy. The diversity of orders arises from diversity of offices and actions, reducing to three: supreme, middle, and base.

### Reply to Objection 1

Order has two meanings: comprehending different grades (hierarchy as an order) and denoting one grade within a hierarchy (several orders within one hierarchy).

### Reply to Objection 2

Each gift is possessed more excellently by some than others. The degree of perfection distinguishes grades and orders among angels.

### Reply to Objection 3

Heavenly orders are distinguished by different acts than the ecclesiastical hierarchy's distinctions.

## Article 3: Whether Many Angels Exist in One Order

### Objection 1

All angels are unequal. Since equals belong to one order, many angels cannot occupy one order.

### Objection 2

A single angel should execute its office adequately. If orders are distinguished by offices, several angels per order would be superfluous.

### Objection 3

The lowest of a superior order would be more akin to the highest of an inferior order. Thus he would not properly belong more to one order than the other.

### On the contrary

Scripture states "The Seraphim cried to one another," implying multiple Seraphim.

### I answer that

Our knowledge of angels is imperfect. We can only distinguish angelic offices and orders generally, placing many angels in one order. With perfect knowledge, we would recognize that each angel has his own office and order, more distinctly than any star.

### Reply to Objection 1

All angels of one order are equal in the common similitude placing them in that order, though not absolutely equal. Within one order exist those who are first, middle, and last.

### Reply to Objection 2

The special distinction wherein each angel has his own office and order is hidden from us.

### Reply to Objection 3

Angels on the boundary of two orders are more naturally akin than one is to others in its own order—yet less suited to similar offices.

## Article 4: Whether Distinction of Hierarchies and Orders Derives from Angelic Nature

### Objection 1

Sanctity and resemblance to God exist in angels by grace, not nature. Therefore hierarchical distinction derives from grace, not nature.

### Objection 2

Seraphim means "burning," pertaining to charity from grace. Therefore angelic orders derive from grace.

### Objection 3

The ecclesiastical hierarchy derives from grace, not nature. Therefore neither do angelic orders derive from nature.

### On the contrary

The Master states angelic order is a multitude likened by grace gifts, as they agree also in natural gifts.

### I answer that

The angelic order derives from its end, considered twofold. According to natural faculty, orders are distinguished by natural gifts. According to the supernatural end exceeding natural powers, orders are distinguished by grace's gifts. Since gratuitous gifts are given according to capacity of natural gifts, angelic orders are distinguished by both natural and grace gifts, while human orders are distinguished by grace gifts alone.

## Article 5: Whether Angelic Orders Are Properly Named

### Objection 1

Common names like "angels" and "virtues" should not be appropriated to individual orders.

### Objection 2

Only God is truly Lord. Therefore calling one order "Dominations" is improper.

### Objection 3

"Domination," "Principalities," and "Powers" all suggest government and should not apply to three distinct orders.

### Objection 4

Archangels are angel-princes. This name should belong solely to the Principalities order.

### Objection 5

"Seraphim" derives from charity; "Cherubim" from knowledge—both common to all angels.

### Objection 6

Thrones are seats. God sits in the rational creature through knowing and loving. Therefore no distinct Thrones order should exist besides Cherubim and Seraphim.

### On the contrary

Scripture names these orders throughout various books.

### I answer that

Each order's proper name expresses its property. All spiritual perfections are common to all angels but possessed more excellently by superior ones. Superior orders are denominated from superior perfections.

Dionysius explains names according to spiritual perfections. Gregory regards exterior ministrations. The lowest order lacks excellence in manifestation, making "Angel" especially proper to it. "Virtue" as excellence of strength is proper to one order.

### Reply to Objection 1

"Angel" and "Virtue" are common to all but especially proper to specific orders based on their distinctive characteristics.

### Reply to Objection 2

Dominion belongs to God by way of excess. Divine word gives heavenly princes the name Lord through participation.

### Reply to Objection 3

A lord prescribes what shall be done; a power constitutes ordination; a principality presides as first among executors.

### Reply to Objection 4

Archangels stand between Principalities and Angels—princes regarding Angels, angels regarding Principalities.

### Reply to Objection 5

"Seraphim" derives from ardor's excess: upward movement toward God, penetrating heat rousing subjects, and brightness enlightening others. "Cherubim" derives from knowledge's excess: perfect Divine vision and abundant pouring forth of knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 6

Thrones excel through immediate knowledge of Divine work-types in God, distinguished from Cherubim's knowledge-excellence and Seraphim's ardor-excellence. Thrones' properties include elevation, firmness, reception of God, and openness to serve.

## Article 6: Whether Angelic Order Grades Are Properly Assigned

### Objection 1

"Dominations," "Principalities," and "Powers" suggest prelacy and should be supreme.

### Objection 2

Thrones are nearest to God and should be highest.

### Objection 3

Knowledge precedes love, so Cherubim should surpass Seraphim.

### Objection 4

Gregory places Principalities above Powers, contrary to Dionysius.

### On the contrary

Dionysius places: first hierarchy—Seraphim, Cherubim, Thrones; middle—Dominations, Virtues, Powers; lowest—Principalities, Archangels, Angels.

### I answer that

Gregory and Dionysius agree on all orders except Principalities and Virtues. The highest hierarchy contemplates Divine ideas in God Himself; the second in universal causes; the third applies them to particular effects.

Dionysius places in the first hierarchy orders named for Divine relation. The middle comprises orders denoting common government. The lowest comprises orders denoting work execution.

Regarding the end: Thrones are raised as God's familiar recipients; Cherubim supereminently know Divine secrets; Seraphim excel in union with God Himself. Government comprises appointing what shall be done (Dominations), giving power to execute (Virtues), and ordering execution through others (Powers). In the lowest hierarchy, Principalities lead, Angels execute, Archangels hold the middle.

### Reply to Objection 1

Angels' subjection to God exceeds presiding over inferiors. Orders named from Divine nearness are highest.

### Reply to Objection 2

Throne-nearness belongs also to Cherubim and Seraphim in more excellent ways.

### Reply to Objection 3

Loving higher things—God above all—excels knowing them. Knowing lower things excels loving them.

### Reply to Objection 4

Little real difference exists between Dionysius's and Gregory's dispositions. Both find Apostolic support.

## Article 7: Whether Angelic Orders Outlast the Day of Judgment

### Objection 1

The Apostle says Christ will "bring to naught all principality and power." Therefore all orders will be abolished.

### Objection 2

After Judgment, angels cease advancing in knowledge. Therefore angelic orders remain purposeless.

### Objection 3

Angelic offices purpose leading men toward salvation until Judgment. Therefore orders cannot outlast Judgment.

### On the contrary

Scripture states angels remain "in their order and courses."

### I answer that

Grade distinctions derive from grace and nature differences, which remain perpetually. Office execution will partially cease (leading others toward the end) and partially remain (according with end-attainment). Military ranks retain different duties in both battle and triumph.

### Reply to Objection 1

Principalities and powers cease regarding leading others toward their end—when the end is reached.

### Reply to Objection 2

Inferior angels' knowledge depends on superior angels' light for preservation, not merely acquisition. Superior angels' enlightenment will continue.

### Reply to Objection 3

Angels will enlighten those already saved through angelic ministry.

## Article 8: Whether Men Are Taken Up Into Angelic Orders

### Objection 1

Angels from the lowest hierarchy never transfer to the middle or first. Therefore men don't transfer to angelic orders.

### Objection 2

Angelic offices include guarding, working miracles, coercing demons—tasks not appearing to belong to saints' souls.

### Objection 3

Chrysostom rejects saying bad men's souls become demons. Therefore saints won't transfer to angelic orders.

### On the contrary

Christ states "they will be as the angels of God."

### I answer that

Regarding nature's grade, men cannot be assumed into angelic orders—natural distinction always remains. However, through grace's gift, men merit glory equaling angels' degree in each order. Augustine states "there will not be two separate societies of men and angels, but only one; because all beatitude consists in cleaving to God alone."

### Reply to Objection 1

Men cannot transfer to nature grade but can ascend to grace grade.

### Reply to Objection 2

Holy men remain our nature and don't ordinarily administer human affairs. Still, special dispensation sometimes grants saints these offices.

### Reply to Objection 3

The error consists in claiming demons are merely dead souls' essence—which Chrysostom rejects. Transferring men to demons' penalty is not erroneous.
