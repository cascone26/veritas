# Question 103: The Government of Things in General

## Article 1: Whether the World is Governed by Anyone

### Objection 1

Natural things comprising most of the world lack knowledge of their ends, so they cannot be said to move or work toward an end. Therefore the world is not governed.

### Objection 2

Things requiring governance are directed toward an object, but the world exhibits stability in itself rather than being moved toward something. Thus it lacks governance.

### Objection 3

Things naturally determined to particular actions by their own nature require no external governing principle. Since the world's principal parts are thus determined, governance is unnecessary.

### On the contrary

Scripture states: "But Thou, O Father, governest all things by Thy Providence" (Wisdom 14:3). Boethius likewise declares that God governs the universe by eternal mandate.

### I answer that

Ancient philosophers denying world governance claimed all things happen by chance, but this can be refuted two ways. First, observation shows nature produces outcomes consistently for the best, which would not occur without providence directing nature toward good—this constitutes governance. Second, considering Divine goodness: since God's goodness caused all things' existence, and the best produces the best, God's supreme goodness must lead things to their perfection. A thing's ultimate perfection consists in attaining its end, so Divine governance belongs to the same goodness that brought things into being.

### Reply to Objection 1

Things move toward ends in two ways. Some, like rational creatures, move themselves through knowledge of ends and means. Others are moved by another, like an arrow directed toward its target by the archer who knows the end unknown to the arrow. Natural things' unvarying course despite lacking knowledge demonstrates they are governed by some reason.

### Reply to Objection 2

All created things possess stable elements (at minimum, primary matter) and something involving movement or operation. Both require governance because even stable elements, created from nothing, would return to nothingness without sustaining governance.

### Reply to Objection 3

Natural necessity in determined beings represents an impression from God directing them toward their end, similar to how an arrow's necessity to move toward a point comes from the archer, not the arrow. Creatures receive their nature from God, while natural things receive violent necessity from humans in addition to nature. Thus natural necessity demonstrates Divine Providence's governance.

## Article 2: Whether the End of Government is Something Outside the World

### Objection 1

Government's end consists in the good the thing governed attains—a good existing in the thing itself, not externally. A sick person is restored to health, a good within them, so the world's governance end must be internal, not external.

### Objection 2

The Philosopher distinguishes ends as either operations or works produced by operations. The universe cannot produce anything outside itself, and operations exist in agents. Therefore nothing extrinsic constitutes the governance end.

### Objection 3

The good of multitudes consists in order and peace, which Augustine calls "tranquility of order." The world comprises a multitude, so its governance end is peaceful internal order, not external good.

### On the contrary

Scripture declares: "The Lord hath made all things for Himself" (Proverbs 16:4). Since God stands outside the universe's entire order, all things' end is something extrinsic to them.

### I answer that

The end corresponds to the beginning. Since all things' beginning is something outside the universe—namely God—the end must likewise be external good. This follows from reason: good has the nature of an end; particular things' ends consist in particular goods, while all things' universal end is the Universal Good—God, who is good essentially and by nature. Every particular good participates in goodness. Therefore the end of the entire universe must be a good outside the universe.

### Reply to Objection 1

We acquire good in multiple ways: as a form existing in us (like health); as something done by us (like a builder achieving his end through building); or as something possessed and acquired by us (like a field buyer entering possession). Nothing prevents something outside the universe being the good toward which all things are directed.

### Reply to Objection 2

The Philosopher discusses various arts' ends—some consisting in operations themselves, others in products. Something extrinsic can be the end not only as made but also as possessed or acquired, as Hercules represents the statue made to represent him. Thus a good outside the universe can be governance's end as something possessed and represented, with all things tending toward participating in and assimilating to it.

### Reply to Objection 3

The universe's internal order—a good existing within it—constitutes an end, but not the ultimate end. Rather, it is ordered to external good as to its final end, as an army's order is ordered to the general.

## Article 3: Whether the World is Governed by One

### Objection 1

We judge causes by effects. The world exhibits non-uniform movements and operations—some contingent, some necessary, various in kinds. Therefore multiple governors, not one, rule the world.

### Objection 2

Things governed by one do not act against each other except through the ruler's incapacity—inapplicable to God. Yet created things oppose each other as contraries. Therefore the world is not governed by one.

### Objection 3

Nature always manifests the better. "It is better that two should be together than one" (Ecclesiastes 4:9). Therefore the world is governed by many, not one.

### On the contrary

We confess one God and one Lord according to the Apostle: "To us there is but one God, the Father...and one Lord" (1 Corinthians 8:6). Both pertain to governance, for dominion and providence belong to God.

### I answer that

The world must necessarily be governed by one. Since the world's governance end is essentially good—the greatest good—governance must be the best kind. The best governance is rule by one. Governance consists in directing governed things toward the end, which is some good. Unity belongs to goodness' very idea: all things desire good as they desire unity; without unity they cease existing. Therefore the ruler's intention over multitudes is unity or peace. One alone causes unity properly, for several cannot cause unity unless themselves united. What is one in itself better causes unity than several united things. Hence a multitude is better governed by one than several. Therefore the world's governance, being the best form, must be by one, as the Philosopher expresses: "Things refuse to be ill governed; multiplicity of authorities is bad" (Metaphysics xii).

### Reply to Objection 1

Movement dissimilarity results from diversity of moved things—diversity essential to the universe's perfection—not from governor diversity.

### Reply to Objection 2

Although contraries disagree in proximate ends, they agree in the ultimate end by being included in the universe's single order.

### Reply to Objection 3

Considering individual goods, two are better than one. But regarding essential good, no addition is possible.

## Article 4: Whether Government's Effect is One or Many

### Objection 1

Governance's effect is one: the good consisting in order, as in an army's example. Therefore world governance has but one effect.

### Objection 2

From one naturally proceeds one. The world is governed by one, so governance's effect is one.

### Objection 3

If governance's effect is not one through governor unity, it must be many through governed things' multiplicity. But these things are innumerable, so no definite number of governance effects can be assigned.

### On the contrary

Dionysius states: "God contains all and fills all by His providence and perfect goodness" (Divine Names xii). Providence belongs to governance, so definite Divine governance effects exist.

### I answer that

Actions' effects are judged from their ends; action achieves end attainment. The world's governance end is essential good, toward which all things tend through participation and similarity. Consequently, governance effects can be considered three ways.

First, regarding the end itself: there is one effect—assimilation to the supreme good.

Second, regarding things by which creatures become like God: there are two general effects. Creatures assimilate to God in two respects: first, that God is good (creatures become like Him by being good); second, that God causes goodness in others (creatures resemble God by moving others toward good). Thus there are two governance effects: preserving things in their goodness, and moving things toward good.

Third, considering individual governance effects: they are without number.

### Reply to Objection 1

The universe's order includes both preservation of God-created things and their movement. Order appears among these, with one superior to another and one moving another.

## Article 5: Whether All Things are Subject to Divine Government

### Objection 1

Scripture states: "I saw that under the sun the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong...but time and chance in all" (Ecclesiastes 9:11). Things under Divine governance are not ruled by chance. Therefore things under the sun escape Divine government.

### Objection 2

The Apostle says: "God hath no care for oxen" (1 Corinthians 9:9). Governors care for governed things. Therefore not all things are subject to Divine government.

### Objection 3

Rational creatures can govern themselves—they are masters of their actions. Therefore not all things require Divine governance.

### On the contrary

Augustine states: "Not only heaven and earth, not only man and angel, even the bowels of the lowest animal, the bird's wing, the plant's flower, the tree's leaf hath God endowed with fitting nature details" (City of God v, 11). Therefore all things are subject to His governance.

### I answer that

God rules things for the same reason He causes them, since the same principle gives existence and perfection—both belonging to governance. God is the cause of universal being itself, not merely some particular kind. Therefore, as nothing escapes God's creation, nothing escapes His governance. From governance's end perspective: a person's governance extends over things coming under that governance's end. The Divine governance end is Divine goodness. Since nothing fails to be ordered to Divine goodness as its end, nothing can escape Divine government.

Those claiming corruptible lower things, individual things, or human affairs escape Divine governance were foolish, representing those saying "God hath abandoned the earth" (Ezekiel 9:9).

### Reply to Objection 1

Things under the sun undergo generation and corruption containing chance elements—not that everything occurring is casual, but each thing involves some chance. This chance element actually proves these things undergo governance. Unless corruptible things were governed by a higher being, they would tend toward nothing definite, especially lacking knowledge. Thus chance existence in these things demonstrates higher-cause ordering governs them.

### Reply to Objection 2

By the single Divine governing art, various things are variously governed. Rational creatures are governed through inducement toward good and away from evil by precepts, prohibitions, rewards, and punishments. Irrational creatures are not thus governed. Hence the Apostle's statement means only that oxen are not governed as rational creatures are.

### Reply to Objection 3

Rational creatures govern themselves through intellect and will, both requiring governance and perfection by Divine intellect and will. Above self-governance, the rational creature requires Divine governance.

## Article 6: Whether All Things are Immediately Governed by God

### Objection 1

Gregory of Nyssa reproves Plato's division of providence into three parts. Therefore all things are immediately governed by God.

### Objection 2

It is better for one to accomplish something alone, if possible, than through many. God can govern all things without intermediary causes. Therefore He immediately governs all things.

### Objection 3

Nothing in God is defective or imperfect. An earthly king governing through ministers shows imperfection. Therefore God governs all things immediately.

### On the contrary

Augustine states: "Grosser bodies are ruled in orderly way by subtler, more powerful bodies; all bodies by the rational spirit of life; sinful spirits by good spirits; and this spirit by God Himself" (Trinity iii, 4).

### I answer that

In governance, two things require consideration: the design of governance (providence itself) and the execution of that design. Regarding design, God governs all things immediately; regarding execution, He governs some things through others.

The reason: God is goodness' very essence, so everything must be attributed to God in its highest degree. The highest goodness in governance design consists in knowing individuals acted upon. Therefore God has governance design over all things, even the very least.

Since governance should bring governed things to perfection, and greater perfection exists in being good in itself and causing goodness in others than being good alone, God governs such that He makes some things causes of others in governance, as a master imparts knowledge to pupils and gives them faculty to teach others.

### Reply to Objection 1

Plato's opinion merits rejection because he held that God did not immediately govern all things even in governance design.

### Reply to Objection 2

If God alone governed, things would be deprived of causality's perfection. What is effected by many would not be accomplished by one.

### Reply to Objection 3

That earthly kings have ministers executing their laws signifies not merely imperfection but also dignity: kingly power becomes evident through minister ordering.

## Article 7: Whether Anything Can Happen Outside Divine Government's Order

### Objection 1

Boethius says: "God disposes all for good." If nothing occurs outside Divine governance order, no evil exists.

### Objection 2

Nothing occurring according to a ruler's pre-ordination happens by chance. If nothing occurs outside Divine governance, nothing is fortuitous.

### Objection 3

Divine Providence's order is certain and unchangeable. If nothing occurs outside it, all things happen necessarily, and nothing is contingent—which is false.

### On the contrary

Scripture declares: "O Lord, Lord, almighty King, all things are in Thy power, and there is none that can resist Thy will" (Esther 13:9).

### I answer that

An effect can result outside some particular cause's order, but not outside the universal cause's order. No effect results outside a particular cause's order except through some impeding cause, which itself must be reduced to the first universal cause. Since God is the first universal cause of all being generally, nothing can occur outside Divine governance order. Yet from one perspective, something may seem to evade Divine Providence's order regarding one particular cause; necessarily, from another cause perspective, it returns to that order.

### Reply to Objection 1

Nothing is wholly evil in the world, for evil is ever founded on good (I:48:3). Something is called evil through escaping some particular good's order. If it wholly escaped Divine governance order, it would wholly cease existing.

### Reply to Objection 2

Things are called fortuitous regarding some particular cause from whose order they escape. But "nothing in the world happens by chance" regarding Divine Providence's order, as Augustine declares.

### Reply to Objection 3

Certain effects are contingent compared to their proximate causes, which may fail producing effects—not because anything occurs entirely outside Divine governance order.

## Article 8: Whether Anything Can Resist Divine Government's Order

### Objection 1

Scripture states: "Their tongue and their devices are against the Lord" (Isaiah 3:8), suggesting resistance to Divine order is possible.

### Objection 2

A king does not justly punish those not rebelling against his commands. If none rebel against God's commands, God cannot justly punish anyone.

### Objection 3

Everything is subject to Divine governance order. Yet some things oppose others. Therefore some things rebel against Divine governance order.

### On the contrary

Boethius declares: "There is nothing that can desire or is able to resist this sovereign good. It is this sovereign good therefore that ruleth all mightily and ordereth all sweetly."

### I answer that

Divine Providence's order can be considered two ways: generally, proceeding from the universal governing cause; or particularly, proceeding from some particular cause executing Divine governance order.

Considered generally, nothing can resist Divine governance order. First, the Divine governance order is wholly directed toward good, and everything through its operation tends toward good only—"no one acts intending evil," as Dionysius states. Second, every inclination, whether natural or voluntary, is merely an impression from the first mover, like the arrow's inclination toward a fixed point resulting from the archer's impulse. Wherefore every agent, natural or free, attains its divinely appointed end as though of its own accord. Thus God "ordereth all sweetly."

### Reply to Objection 1

Some are said to act against God not by entirely resisting Divine governance order—even sinners intend attaining some good—but by resisting some particular good belonging to their nature or state. Therefore they are justly punished by God.

### Reply to Objection 2

This follows clearly from the above.

### Reply to Objection 3

From one thing opposing another, it follows that some one thing resists a particular cause's order, but not the order depending on the universal cause of all things.
