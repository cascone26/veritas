# Question 80: The Appetitive Powers in General

## Article 1: Whether the Appetite is a Special Power of the Soul?

### Objection 1
Appetite should not be considered a special soul power since it is common to both animate and inanimate things. The Philosopher states that "all desire good," meaning appetite is universal rather than unique to the soul.

### Objection 2
Powers differentiate by their objects. Since what we desire equals what we know, the appetitive power is not distinct from the apprehensive power.

### Objection 3
Each soul power naturally desires its suitable object. Therefore, we should not assign a separate appetitive power for desirable things in general.

### On the contrary
The Philosopher distinguishes appetitive from other powers in the De Anima. Damascene similarly separates appetitive from cognitive powers.

### I answer that
The soul necessarily possesses an appetitive power. Every form carries inclination—fire rises by its nature. However, knowing beings possess forms more perfectly than non-knowing things. Non-knowing entities have only natural inclination, but knowing beings receive species of other things. The human soul becomes, in a way, "all things by sense and intellect," approaching God's nature wherein all things pre-exist.

Therefore, knowing beings require inclination surpassing natural appetite. This superior inclination belongs to the appetitive power, enabling animals to desire apprehended objects beyond mere natural inclination.

### Reply to Objection 1
Appetite in knowing things surpasses its common occurrence in all things, requiring a distinct soul power.

### Reply to Objection 2
The apprehended and desired are identical in reality but differ in aspect: apprehension considers sensible or intelligible qualities, while desire considers suitability or goodness. Diverse aspects demand distinct powers.

### Reply to Objection 3
Each soul power has natural inclination toward its suitable object. Animal appetite, following apprehension, desires what suits the animal generally, transcending particular power-specific inclinations.

## Article 2: Whether Sensitive and Intellectual Appetites are Distinct Powers?

### Objection 1
Powers differentiate by essential, not accidental, differences. Whether the appetible object is apprehended by sense or intellect is accidental to it, so sensitive and intellectual appetites are not distinct powers.

### Objection 2
Intellectual knowledge concerns universals while sensitive knowledge concerns individuals. Since appetite moves toward individual things, intellectual appetite cannot differ from sensitive appetite.

### Objection 3
Just as motive power following intellect in humans is not distinct from motive power following sense in animals, appetitive powers should similarly not differentiate.

### On the contrary
The Philosopher distinguishes a double appetite and states that the higher appetite moves the lower.

### I answer that
Intellectual appetite must be distinct from sensitive appetite. The appetitive power is passive, naturally moved by apprehended objects. Since passive and movable things differentiate according to corresponding active principles, and intellect and sense apprehend generically different objects, intellectual and sensitive appetites are distinct powers.

### Reply to Objection 1
The thing desired being apprehended by sense or intellect is not accidental but natural to it; the appetible moves appetite only through apprehension. Therefore, apprehension differences are appetible differences, making appetitive powers distinct by their objects.

### Reply to Objection 2
Intellectual appetite tends toward individual existing things but apprehends them as standing under universals—desiring something because it is good. Consequently, intellectual appetite can desire immaterial goods like knowledge and virtue, inaccessible to sense.

### Reply to Objection 3
Universal opinion moves only through particular opinion. Similarly, the higher appetite moves through the lower, so distinct motive powers following intellect and sense are unnecessary.
