# Question 74: All the Seven Days in Common

## Article 1: Whether these days are sufficiently enumerated?

### Objection 1
The work of creation appears distinct from distinction and adornment work. Since separate days are assigned to water and land distinction, creation should receive its own day as well.

### Objection 2
Fire and air are nobler than earth and water. Yet only water and land receive distinct days for their separation, suggesting fire and air warrant separate consideration.

### Objection 3
Fish differ from birds as much as birds differ from land animals. Man differs even more from other creatures. Yet fish, birds, and land animals receive only three days total, suggesting this enumeration is insufficient.

### Objection 4
Light relates to luminaries as accident to subject. Since subjects and their accidents typically arise together, light and luminaries should not occupy separate days.

### Objection 5
The seventh day involved no new creation. Therefore it should not be enumerated among the six working days.

### On the contrary
Scripture states: "The evening and the morning were the second day... the third day" (Genesis 1). Where there exists a second and third, more than one day exists.

### I answer that
The distinction of days reflects a principle: parts must first be distinguished, then adorned with inhabitants. The corporeal creation divides into three parts: heaven (highest), water (middle), and earth (lowest). The perfection of the Divine works corresponds to the perfection of the number six, which is the sum of its aliquot parts.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine teaches that creation—producing formless matter and spiritual nature—exists outside time. Works of distinction and adornment involve temporal change and thus occur "in a day," while creation occurs "in the beginning."

### Reply to Objection 2
Fire and air, being less evident to the uneducated, receive less distinct mention. Moses encompasses them within water (lower air) or heaven (upper air) categories.

### Reply to Objection 3
Animal production follows the parts of creation they adorn. Days separate or unite according to whether animals inhabit the same or different worldly regions.

### Reply to Objection 4
Light's nature in a subject was created on day one. The fourth day formation of luminaries signifies "that they then received a form that they had not before."

### Reply to Objection 5
The seventh day receives mention because "God rested in Himself from His works." The seventh day represents a distinct state where nothing new would be added.

## Article 2: Whether all these days are one day?

### Objection 1
Genesis 2:4-5 states God made "the heaven and the earth, and every plant of the field" in one day. Yet heaven and earth precede plants. Therefore the first and third days constitute one day.

### Objection 2
Sirach 18:1 declares: "He that liveth for ever, created all things together." Multiple days would contradict simultaneous creation.

### Objection 3
God ceased creating on the seventh day. If the seventh day is distinct, God apparently did not make it—an unacceptable conclusion.

### Objection 4
Each day's work completes with divine utterance and accomplishment. Delaying the next work to another day would leave time vacant—superfluous activity.

### On the contrary
Genesis 1 repeatedly distinguishes days: "evening and morning were the second day... third day." Multiple days clearly exist.

### I answer that
Augustine holds all seven days represent "one day represented in a sevenfold aspect." Other expositors maintain seven genuinely distinct days. Augustine understands "day" as angelic knowledge of divine works, not temporal succession. The two interpretations differ regarding temporal sequence but align regarding the actual mode of divine production.

### Reply to Objection 1
On the day of creation, God created "every plant of the field... potentially," before actual sprouting. Augustine assigns this to day three; other writers to creation's beginning.

### Reply to Objection 2
God created all things simultaneously "so far as regards their substance in some measure formless." Distinct formation—involving "distinction and adornment"—occurred progressively.

### Reply to Objection 3
God ceased from "making new things" on the seventh day but continued "providing for their increase," enabling successive days to follow.

### Reply to Objection 4
Things were not distinguished and adorned simultaneously "from a want of power" but "that due order might be observed in the instituting of the world."

## Article 3: Whether Scripture uses suitable words to express the work of the six days?

### Objection 1
The Word of God created all things (John 1:3). Yet the creation of heaven and earth lacks mention of the Word, unlike other works. This seems inconsistent.

### Objection 2
Water was created by God, yet no explicit creation narrative appears. The world's creation thus seems incompletely described.

### Objection 3
Genesis 1:31 states God "saw all the things that He had made, and they were very good." This approval should appear for every work, including creation and the second day.

### Objection 4
God's Spirit occupies no place and undergoes no movement. Yet Genesis describes it "moving over the waters"—language unbecoming to deity.

### Objection 5
What already exists requires no repetition. After "Let the firmament be made... and it was so," adding "God made the firmament" seems redundant.

### Objection 6
Evening and morning incompletely divide the day, which contains many parts. This division seems unsuitable.

### Objection 7
"First" (not "one") properly corresponds to "second" and "third." The text should read "the first day," not "one day."

### On the contrary
Scripture employs these precise formulations throughout Genesis 1, indicating divine intentionality in expression.

### I answer that
These formulations carry theological significance reflecting divine wisdom and the Trinity's manifestation.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine explains that the Word's mention differs between creation and formation. In creation, the Son appears as "the beginning, by the words, 'In the beginning God created.'" In formation, the Word receives explicit mention because "the giving form to every creature is by the word of God."

### Reply to Objection 2
Augustine interprets "heaven" as formless spiritual nature and "earth" as formless corporeal matter. Thus no creature is omitted. Basil notes that heaven and earth as extremes encompass all intervening elements.

### Reply to Objection 3
Augustine identifies three phrases denoting threefold creature being: "Let be made" (being in the Word), "It was done" (being in angelic mind), and "He made" (being in proper nature).

### Reply to Objection 4
Augustine and Basil interpret "Spirit of God" as the Holy Ghost, not wind or air. The motion signifies "pre-eminent power," not bodily movement.

### Reply to Objection 5
Augustine teaches these three phrases represent creature existence in the Word, in angelic knowledge, and in proper nature, explicitly refuting doctrines attributing creation to angels.

### Reply to Objection 6
Augustine understands "evening" and "morning knowledge" as angelic perception modes. Basil notes that "day" traditionally names the entire period from its more significant part.

### Reply to Objection 7
"One day" established the measure of a natural day—twenty-four hours. This also signifies day's completion through solar return or foreshadows the eighth day's identity with the first in weekly cycles.
