# Question 69: The Work of the Third Day

## Article 1: Whether it was fitting that the gathering together of the waters should take place on the third day?

### Objection 1
The work of the first and second days explicitly states that things were "made," yet the third day is distinguished from these. Therefore, the third day's work should similarly be described as a making rather than a gathering.

### Objection 2
The earth was completely covered by waters and thus invisible. There existed no dry place where waters could be gathered together.

### Objection 3
Things not in continuous contact cannot occupy one place. Not all waters maintain continuous contact, so gathering them into one place seems impossible.

### Objection 4
A gathering involves local movement. Waters naturally flow toward the sea, making a divine command for this unnecessary.

### Objection 5
The earth received its name at initial creation. Renaming it on the third day appears superfluous.

### On the contrary
"The authority of Scripture suffices."

### I answer that
Thomas presents two interpretive approaches. Augustine's view emphasizes that all works follow "an order of origin and nature" rather than temporal sequence. The gathering of waters represents the impression of substantial forms upon formless matter, not temporal movement of pre-existing waters.

Other authorities understand an actual temporal sequence where "the formlessness of matter precedes its formation." Heaven received form when darkness departed (being the light's source). Water's formlessness is called the "deep." Earth's formlessness appeared as "void" or "invisible" because waters covered it. On the third day, "the earth, the lowest body, received its form by the withdrawal of the waters."

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine explains that Scripture avoids saying the third day's work was "made" to signify that higher spiritual forms remain "perfect and stable in being," whereas inferior forms prove "imperfect and mutable." The gathering and appearing of land represent this instability, as "water glides and flows away, the earth abides."

### Reply to Objection 2
Augustine's interpretation needs no explanation: waters were produced within their very gathering, not gathered after formation. Others offer three solutions: waters heaped higher where gathered; waters were rarefied or nebulous before condensing; or earth contained hollows to receive the confluence. The first seems most probable.

### Reply to Objection 3
All waters flow toward the sea through visible or hidden channels, explaining why they are "gathered together into one place." Alternatively, "one place" should be understood as contrasted with the dry land's location, meaning waters gathered apart from land. The plural "Seas" suggests multiple locations.

### Reply to Objection 4
The divine command establishes bodies' natural movements, by which they "fulfill His word." Water's nature would completely cover earth as air surrounds both; yet withdrawal from part of earth proved necessary for plants and animals. Scripture attributes this to divine power, citing passages like Job 38:10 and Jeremiah 5:22.

### Reply to Objection 5
Augustine holds that "earth" initially meant primary matter but here means the element itself. Alternatively, Basil notes earth is first mentioned regarding its nature, here regarding its principal property—dryness. Or, with Rabbi Moses, "He called" denotes equivocal use: light initially called "Day" later designates twenty-four hours; "firmament" called "heaven" parallels the originally created "heaven"; similarly, "dry land" called "Earth" distinguishes it from sea.

## Article 2: Whether it was fitting that the production of plants should take place on the third day?

### Objection 1
Plants possess life like animals do. Since animal production belongs to the work of adornment rather than distinction, plant production should similarly belong to adornment, not the formative work of the third day.

### Objection 2
Production of plants that cursed the earth should be recorded separately from the earth's formation work. Genesis 3:17 states thorns and thistles resulted from the curse, so plants generally should not be included in the third day's formation.

### Objection 3
Stones and metals are as firmly fixed to earth as plants, yet they are not mentioned during formation. Plants therefore should not have been made on the third day.

### On the contrary
"The earth brought forth the green herb" after which "The evening and the morning were the third day."

### I answer that
The earth's formless state on the third day was twofold: covered by waters (invisible), and lacking the comeliness provided by plants that clothe it. Both aspects end on the third day—first through water-gathering and land appearing, second through vegetation production.

Augustine's position differs significantly from other commentators. Others hold plants were produced actually in their various species on the third day. Augustine maintains the earth then received the power to produce plants—the potential cause rather than actuality. He supports this through Genesis 2:4-5: plants existed "in their causes, within the earth" before sprouting from the surface.

This aligns with reason: God created all things in their origins or causes during six days, then rested. Subsequently, through governing creatures in "the work of propagation, He worketh until now." Plant production from earth constitutes propagation work, so actual production occurred only potentially on the third day.

Alternatively, establishing initial species belonged to the six days' work, but reproduction of like from like belongs to the universe's government. Scripture indicates this through "before it sprung up" and "before it grew"—before like produces like through seeds, as happens naturally now. The phrase "Let the earth bring forth...such as may seed" indicates production of perfect species from which others arise.

### Reply to Objection 1
Plant life remains hidden because they lack sensation and local movement—the chief means of distinguishing animate from inanimate. Being firmly rooted in earth, their production constitutes part of earth's formation.

### Reply to Objection 2
Thorns and thistles existed before the curse, either virtually or actually. Yet they were not produced as human punishment; rather, the earth which he cultivates for food produces unfruitful plants. Hence the text specifies "TO THEE"—directed toward mankind specifically.

### Reply to Objection 3
Moses presented only matters manifest to people's senses. Minerals generate in hidden ways within earth's bowels and lack specific distinction from earth itself, appearing as earth-species. Therefore, he omits them from discussion.
