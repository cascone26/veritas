# Question 6: The Cause of Faith

## Article 1: Whether Faith is Infused into Man by God?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "science begets faith in us, and nourishes, defends and strengthens it." Things begotten by science appear acquired rather than infused, so faith seems not divinely infused.

### Objection 2
Man attains belief through hearing and witnessing miracles. John 4:53 records a father believing after seeing his son healed, and Romans 10:17 states "faith is through hearing." Therefore, faith appears acquired by man.

### Objection 3
Augustine notes that "faith depends on the believer's will." What depends on human will can be acquired by humans, so faith should be acquirable by man.

### On the Contrary
Ephesians 2:8-9 states: "By grace you are saved through faith, and that not of yourselves...for it is the gift of God."

### I Answer That
Two elements are necessary for faith. First, matters of faith must be proposed to man, which is essential for explicit belief. Second, the believer must assent to what is proposed.

Regarding the first element, faith must originate from God because matters of faith surpass human reason and cannot be known unless God reveals them. Some receive direct revelation, while others receive teachings through sent preachers.

Regarding assent, two causes exist: external inducements like miracles or persuasion (insufficient alone, since witnesses divide between believers and non-believers), and an internal cause moving man inwardly to assent.

The Pelagians claimed free will alone constitutes this internal cause, making faith's beginning human and completion divine. However, this is false. Since assent to faith elevates man above his nature, this must proceed from a supernatural principle—God moving man inwardly through grace. Therefore, faith's assent—its chief act—comes from God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Science generates and nourishes faith through external persuasion, but faith's principal cause is what moves man inwardly to assent.

### Reply to Objection 2
This argument concerns the external cause proposing matters of faith or persuading through words and deeds.

### Reply to Objection 3
Belief depends on the believer's will, yet "man's will needs preparation by God with grace...to be raised to things above his nature."

## Article 2: Whether Lifeless Faith is a Gift of God?

### Objection 1
Deuteronomy 32:4 states "the works of God are perfect." Lifeless faith is imperfect, so it cannot be God's work.

### Objection 2
As deformed acts lack their due form, lifeless faith lacks its proper form. Deformed sinful acts are not from God, so neither is lifeless faith.

### Objection 3
God heals wholly, as John 7:23 suggests. Faith heals from unbelief, so whoever receives faith from God should be healed from all sins—which only living faith accomplishes. Therefore, only living faith is God's gift.

### On the Contrary
A gloss on 1 Corinthians 13:2 states that "the faith which lacks charity is a gift of God," and this constitutes lifeless faith.

### I Answer That
Lifelessness represents a privation. Some privations are essential to a thing's species (like humoral imbalance to sickness), while others supervene externally (like darkness to transparent bodies).

Since lifelessness is not essential to faith's species—faith is called lifeless through lacking an extrinsic form—the cause of lifeless faith is identical to faith's cause: God. Therefore, lifeless faith is a gift of God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Though lifeless faith lacks virtue's perfect form, it possesses sufficient perfection for faith's essential definition.

### Reply to Objection 2
Deformity in moral acts is essential to their species through intrinsic privation. God causes the act but not its deformity. Similarly, deformity relates to acts as falsehood relates to faith—God is not deformity's cause, though He causes good acts generically, even when unchristened by charity in sinners.

### Reply to Objection 3
Someone receiving faith without charity is partially healed—ceasing from previous unbelief without ceasing from other sins. Thus God sometimes grants belief without granting charity, just as prophecy is granted without charity.
