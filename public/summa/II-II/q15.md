# Question 15: The Vices Opposed to Knowledge and Understanding

## Article 1: Whether Blindness of Mind is a Sin

### Objection 1
Blindness of mind appears not to be sinful, since what excuses from sin is not itself sin. Scripture states: "If you were blind, you should not have sin" (John 9:41). Therefore, blindness of mind is not a sin.

### Objection 2
Punishment differs from guilt. Blindness of mind constitutes punishment, as shown in Isaiah 6:10. Since evil cannot come from God except as punishment, blindness of mind is punishment rather than sin.

### Objection 3
Every sin is voluntary, according to Augustine. Yet blindness of mind lacks voluntariness, since all naturally desire to know truth. Therefore, blindness of mind cannot be sinful.

### On the Contrary
Gregory lists blindness of mind among vices arising from lust.

### I Answer That
Just as bodily blindness privates the principle of physical sight, mental blindness privates the principle of intellectual sight. This principle has three aspects:

First, the light of natural reason, which pertains to the rational soul's essence and cannot be entirely forfeited, though its operation may be hindered by lower powers.

Second, a habitual light superadded to natural reason—the light of grace. Its privation constitutes blindness as punishment.

Third, an intelligible principle through which understanding occurs. A person may fail to attend to this principle in two ways: either by deliberately turning the will away from its consideration, or when the mind becomes preoccupied with preferred objects, hindered from considering this principle. In either case, blindness of mind constitutes sin.

### Reply to Objection 1
The blindness that excuses arises from natural defect in those unable to perceive.

### Reply to Objection 2
This argument addresses the second type of blindness, which functions as punishment.

### Reply to Objection 3
Understanding truth is inherently beloved; yet accidentally it may become hateful when it hinders pursuit of something more desired.

## Article 2: Whether Dullness of Sense Differs as a Distinct Sin from Blindness of Mind

### Objection 1
Dullness and blindness appear identical since one thing has one contrary. Gregory opposes both to the gift of understanding. Therefore, they constitute the same vice.

### Objection 2
Gregory describes dullness as "dullness of sense in respect of understanding," which seems identical to defective understanding—blindness of mind.

### Objection 3
If they differ, blindness would be voluntary while dullness constitutes natural defect. Natural defects are not sins, yet Gregory counts dullness among sins arising from gluttony.

### On the Contrary
Different causes produce different effects. Gregory attributes dullness to gluttony and blindness to lust—distinct vices. Therefore, these vices differ accordingly.

### I Answer That
"Dull" opposes "sharp." Sharpness involves piercing capacity; dullness means obtuseness and inability to penetrate. Bodily senses are acute when perceiving objects from distance or with penetrating detail; dull when requiring proximity or powerful stimuli.

By similitude, intellectual sense relates to reality's fundamentals as bodily senses apprehend sensible principles. A person possesses acute intellectual sense when apprehending a property or effect immediately reveals the nature and finest details. Dull sense means requiring extensive explanation to reach truth about something.

Thus dullness of sense denotes mental weakness regarding spiritual goods' consideration, while blindness implies complete privation of such knowledge. Both oppose the gift of understanding. Dullness becomes sinful through voluntariness—when affection for carnal things causes dislike or neglect of spiritual consideration.

### Reply to Objection 1
Both oppose understanding but differ in degree—one complete privation, one weakness.

### Reply to Objection 2
Dullness in understanding differs from complete blindness as weakness differs from privation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Dullness constitutes sin through voluntary neglect of spiritual matters, not through natural defect.

## Article 3: Whether Blindness of Mind and Dullness of Sense Arise from Fleshly Sins

### Objection 1
Augustine retracts his earlier statement that only the clean know truth, noting "many unclean persons know many truths." Therefore, blindness and dullness do not arise from fleshly sins.

### Objection 2
These vices affect the intellective soul-part; carnal sins corrupt the flesh. The flesh does not act upon the soul. Therefore, fleshly sins do not cause these intellectual defects.

### Objection 3
Spiritual vices are nearer the mind than carnal vices. Therefore, spiritual rather than carnal vices should cause blindness and dullness.

### On the Contrary
Gregory states dullness arises from gluttony and blindness from lust.

### I Answer That
Perfect human intellectual operation requires abstraction from sensible phantasms. The more the intellect frees itself from phantasms, the better it contemplates intelligible things. Pleasure fixes attention on its object. Carnal vices—gluttony and lust—involve the most impetuous bodily pleasures concerning touch. These vices intensely fix attention on corporeal things, weakening operation regarding intelligibles. Lust effects this more severely than gluttony, as sexual pleasure exceeds table pleasure's vehemence.

Therefore, lust produces blindness of mind, nearly excluding spiritual knowledge, while gluttony produces dullness of sense, weakening the same faculty. Conversely, abstinence and chastity dispose toward intellectual perfection.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although carnal vice-slaves occasionally achieve subtle intelligible considerations through natural genius or acquired habit, bodily pleasures necessarily frequently withdraw attention from such contemplation.

### Reply to Objection 2
The flesh impedes intellective faculties' operation without altering them directly.

### Reply to Objection 3
Carnal vices' remoteness from mind causes them to distract toward remote objects, thereby more hindering contemplation.
