# Question 91: Taking the divine name for the purpose of invoking it by means of praise

## Article 1: Whether God Should Be Praised with the Lips?

### Objection 1

The Philosopher states that the best of men receive "something greater" than praise. Since God transcends all things, He should receive more than praise and is described as being "above all praise."

### Objection 2

Divine praise constitutes an act of religion and worship. God should be worshiped with the mind rather than lips, as Christ cited Isaiah against those who "honor Me with their lips, but their heart is far from Me."

### Objection 3

People are praised to encourage improvement. However, God cannot be improved through human words, being unchangeable and supremely good.

### On the Contrary

Psalm 62:6 states: "My mouth shall praise Thee with joyful lips."

### I Answer That

Words serve different purposes when addressing God versus humans. When speaking to people, words communicate unknown thoughts. When addressing God, words serve not to inform Him—the searcher of hearts—but to arouse reverence in ourselves and others.

Praising God with our lips benefits ourselves by arousing devotion toward Him. As Psalm 49:23 indicates: "The sacrifice of praise shall glorify Me, and there is the way by which I will show him the salvation of God." Through praise, humans ascend in affection toward God and withdraw from opposing things.

Vocal praise also benefits others by inciting their affections toward God, as Psalm 33:2 states: "His praise shall always be in my mouth."

### Reply to Objection 1

God may be discussed in two ways. First, regarding His essence—wherein He is incomprehensible and ineffable, thus above all praise. We owe Him reverence and the honor of latria. Second, regarding His effects ordained for our good—wherein we owe Him praise, as Isaiah 63:7 expresses through remembering "the tender mercies of the Lord."

### Reply to Objection 2

Praising with lips proves worthless without heartfelt praise. The heart praises God by fervently recalling His glorious works. Nevertheless, outward lip praise arouses inward fervor in those praising and incites others to praise God.

### Reply to Objection 3

We praise God for our benefit, not His.

## Article 2: Whether God Should Be Praised with Song?

### Objection 1

Colossians 3:16 mentions "teaching and admonishing one another in psalms, hymns and spiritual canticles." Only what Scripture authorizes should appear in divine worship, suggesting spiritual rather than corporal canticles should be employed.

### Objection 2

Jerome, commenting on Ephesians 5:19, states: "God is to be sung not with the voice but with the heart." He warns against theatrical measures in church music.

### Objection 3

Psalm 33 indicates praise suits "little and great." Yet Gregory ordains that ministers of the sacred altar must not sing in his See.

### Objection 4

The Old Law employed musical instruments and human singing in God's praise. The Church avoids such instruments to avoid imitating Jews, so singing should similarly be avoided.

### Objection 5

Heart-praise surpasses lip-praise. Singing distracts singers from contemplating lyrics and hinders others' understanding compared to spoken recitation.

### On the Contrary

Blessed Ambrose established singing in the Church of Milan, as Augustine relates.

### I Answer That

As stated previously, vocal praise arouses human devotion toward God. Whatever conduces to this result belongs fittingly in divine praises. The human soul responds variously to different melodic sounds, as philosophers note. Music in divine praises constitutes a salutary institution enabling "faint-hearted" souls to rise toward devotion. Augustine observes: "by the delight of the ears the faint-hearted may rise to the feeling of devotion."

### Reply to Objection 1

"Spiritual canticle" designates both inwardly sung and outwardly voiced canticles that arouse spiritual devotion.

### Reply to Objection 2

Jerome does not condemn singing absolutely but reproves theatrical singing intended for show rather than devotion. Augustine notes: "When it befalls me to be more moved by the voice than by the words sung, I confess to have sinned penally."

### Reply to Objection 3

Teaching and preaching surpass singing in excellence for arousing devotion. Therefore, deacons and prelates, whose function involves preaching, should not be devoted to singing lest withdrawn from greater responsibilities.

### Reply to Objection 4

Teaching should employ only things "that make good hearers." Musical instruments move souls toward pleasure rather than creating good disposition. Old Testament instruments served because people were coarser and required earthly arousal, and such instruments prefigured spiritual realities.

### Reply to Objection 5

Chanting for pleasure distracts souls from sung content. However, chanting for devotion's sake increases attention, as singers linger thereon and each spiritual affection possesses "its own appropriate measure in the voice." Hearers similarly understand singing aims toward "God's glory," sufficient to arouse devotion.
