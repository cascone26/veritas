# Question 5: Those who have faith

## Article 1: Whether there was faith in the angels, or in man, in their original state?

### Objection 1
It seems there was no faith in angels or man in their original state. Hugh of St. Victor states that "man cannot see God or things that are in God, because he closes his eyes to contemplation." Angels in their original state saw things in the Word according to Augustine, and the first man in innocence knew his Creator through "inward inspiration" rather than by faith seeking an absent God. Therefore, there was no faith in angels and man originally.

### Objection 2
Further, the knowledge of faith is dark and obscure, according to 1 Corinthians 13:12: "We see now through a glass in a dark manner." Obscurity was not present in the original state of angels or man, as it is punishment for sin. Therefore, faith could not exist in them originally.

### Objection 3
Further, the Apostle says (Romans 10:17) that "faith comes by hearing." This could not apply to angels and man in their original state, as they could not hear from another. Therefore, there was no faith in them originally.

### On the contrary
It is written (Hebrews 11:6): "He that cometh to God, must believe." The original state of angels and man involved approach to God. Therefore they had need of faith.

### I answer that
Some claim there was no faith in angels before confirmation in grace or in man before sin, due to their manifest contemplation of Divine things. However, since "faith is the evidence of things that appear not" (Hebrews 11:2), and we "believe what we see not" according to Augustine, only manifestation of faith's principal object excludes faith itself.

The principal object of faith is the First Truth, whose vision constitutes heavenly happiness and supersedes faith. Since angels before confirmation and man before sin lacked the happiness whereby God is seen in His essence, their knowledge did not exclude faith.

If man and angels were created in a purely natural state, one might argue there was no faith before confirmation or sin, since such knowledge surpasses even angelic natural knowledge. However, we established that both were created with the gift of grace. Therefore, there must have been in them a certain beginning of hoped-for happiness through grace received but not consummated—begun in the will by hope and charity, and in the intellect by faith.

In faith's object, something formal exists (the First Truth surpassing all natural creature knowledge) and something material (things to which we assent while adhering to the First Truth). Regarding the formal aspect, before obtaining heavenly happiness, faith is common to all having knowledge of God by adhering to the First Truth. Regarding material objects of faith, some things are believed by one and known manifestly by another, even now. Thus angels before confirmation and man before sin possessed manifest knowledge about certain Divine mysteries that we can know only through belief.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although Hugh of St. Victor's words carry authority, the contemplation removing faith's necessity is heavenly contemplation—seeing supernatural truth in its essence. Angels did not possess this before confirmation, nor man before sin. Yet their contemplation was higher than ours: they approached God nearer and had manifest knowledge of more Divine effects and mysteries. Faith was not in them such that they sought an absent God as we seek Him; by wisdom's light, God was more present to them than to us, though not as present as to the Blessed in glory.

### Reply to Objection 2
There was no darkness of sin or punishment in man's and angels' original state, but there was natural obscurity in human and angelic intellect—every creature is darkness compared to Divine light's immensity. This obscurity suffices for faith.

### Reply to Objection 3
In the original state, there was no hearing from man speaking outwardly, but from God inspiring inwardly. Thus prophets heard, as expressed in Psalm 84:9: "I will hear what the Lord God will speak in me."

## Article 2: Whether in the demons there is faith?

### Objection 1
It seems demons have no faith. Augustine states that "faith depends on the believer's will," which is a good will—wishing to believe in God. Since no deliberate will of demons is good, as established earlier, it seems demons lack faith.

### Objection 2
Further, faith is "a gift of Divine grace" (Ephesians 2:8). According to a gloss on Hosea 3:1, demons lost their gifts of grace by sinning. Therefore, faith did not remain in demons after sin.

### Objection 3
Further, unbelief seems graver than other sins, as Augustine observes on John 15:22. If demons have faith, some men would be guilty of a sin graver than demons', which seems unreasonable. Therefore, demons lack faith.

### On the contrary
It is written (James 2:19): "The devils believe and tremble."

### I answer that
The believer's intellect assents to what is believed, not because of direct vision or resolution to self-evident principles, but because the will commands the intellect to assent. The will's movement of the intellect toward assent occurs through two causes: first, through the will being directed to the good—in this way, believing is praiseworthy. Second, because the intellect is convinced it ought to believe what is said, though without objective evidence. If a prophet preaching God's word were to foretell something and give a sign by raising the dead, a witness's intellect would be convinced to recognize that God, Who lies not, was speaking, although the foretold thing would not be evident in itself—and faith's essence would not be removed.

Therefore, faith is commended in the first sense in Christ's faithful; faith is not in demons in this way. Rather, demons have faith only in the second way, seeing many evident signs whereby they recognize the Church's teaching comes from God, although they do not see the things themselves the Church teaches—such as three Persons in God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Demons are, in a way, compelled to believe by signs' evidence, so their will deserves no praise for their belief.

### Reply to Objection 2
Faith, as a grace gift, inclines man to believe by giving him certain affection for the good, even when faith is lifeless. Demons' faith is not a grace gift; rather, they are compelled to believe through their natural intellectual acumen.

### Reply to Objection 3
The very fact that faith's signs are so evident that demons are compelled to believe displeases them, so their malice is by no means diminished by their belief.

## Article 3: Whether a man who disbelieves one article of faith, can have lifeless faith in the other articles?

### Objection 1
It seems a heretic disbelieving one faith article can have lifeless faith in other articles. A heretic's natural intellect is not more able than a Catholic's. A Catholic's intellect needs the gift of faith to believe any faith article. Therefore, heretics can believe faith articles without the gift of lifeless faith.

### Objection 2
Further, just as faith contains many articles, geometry contains many conclusions. A man can possess geometry regarding some conclusions while remaining ignorant of others. Therefore, a man can believe some faith articles without believing others.

### Objection 3
Further, just as man obeys God by believing faith articles, he also does by keeping the Law's commandments. Man can obey some commandments while disobeying others. Therefore, he can believe some articles while disbelieving others.

### On the contrary
Just as mortal sin contradicts charity, disbelief in one faith article contradicts faith. Charity does not remain after one mortal sin. Therefore, neither does faith after disbelieving one article.

### I answer that
Neither living nor lifeless faith remains in a heretic disbelieving one faith article.

The species of every habit depends on the formal aspect of its object; without this, the habit's species cannot remain. Faith's formal object is the First Truth as manifested in Holy Scripture and the Church's teaching, which proceeds from the First Truth. Consequently, whoever does not adhere to the Church's teaching—which proceeds from the First Truth manifested in Scripture—as to an infallible and Divine rule, lacks the faith habit but holds faith matters otherwise than by faith. Similarly, one whose mind holds a conclusion without knowing its proof has not scientific knowledge but merely opinion about it.

One adhering to the Church's teaching as an infallible rule assents to whatever the Church teaches. If, regarding Church-taught things, one holds what one chooses and rejects what one chooses, one no longer adheres to the Church's teaching as infallible but to one's own will. Thus, a heretic obstinately disbelieving one faith article is unprepared to follow the Church's teaching entirely. If not obstinate, one is in error, not heresy. Therefore, such a heretic regarding one article has no faith in other articles but only opinion according to personal will.

### Reply to Objection 1
A heretic does not hold other faith articles (in which they do not err) as a faithful person does—by adhering simply to Divine Truth, requiring the faith habit's help. Rather, heretics hold faith matters by their own will and judgment.

### Reply to Objection 2
Science's various conclusions have respective demonstration means; one may be known without another, allowing some conclusions to be known without others. Faith, however, adheres to all faith articles through one means—the First Truth proposed in Scripture according to the Church's teaching possessing right understanding. Whoever abandons this means entirely lacks faith.

### Reply to Objection 3
The Law's various precepts may be referred either to respective proximate motives (allowing one to be kept without another) or to their primary motive—perfect obedience to God. In the latter, a man fails whenever breaking one commandment (James 2:10): "Whosoever shall offend in one point is become guilty of all."

## Article 4: Whether faith can be greater in one man than in another?

### Objection 1
It seems faith cannot be greater in one man than another. A habit's quantity is taken from its object. Whoever has faith believes everything constituting faith; failing in one point loses faith entirely. Therefore, faith cannot be greater in one than another.

### Objection 2
Further, things consisting in something supreme cannot be "more" or "less." Faith consists in something supreme—man should adhere to the First Truth above all things. Therefore, faith cannot be "more" or "less."

### Objection 3
Further, faith relates to grace knowledge as principle understanding relates to natural knowledge, since faith articles are grace knowledge's first principles. Principle understanding is possessed equally by all men. Therefore, faith is possessed equally by all faithful.

### On the contrary
Wherever great and little are found, more or less exists. In faith matters, great and little are found. Our Lord said to Peter (Matthew 14:31): "O thou of little faith, why didst thou doubt?" And to the woman (Matthew 15:28): "O woman, great is thy faith!" Therefore, faith can be greater in one than another.

### I answer that
A habit's quantity may be considered from two viewpoints: first, regarding the object; second, regarding the subject's participation.

Faith's object may be considered in two ways: regarding its formal aspect, or regarding the material object proposed for belief. Faith's formal object is one and simple—the First Truth. Regarding this, there is no faith diversity among believers; it is specifically one in all. However, things proposed as our belief's matter are many and can be received more or less explicitly. In this respect, one man can believe more things explicitly than another, so faith can be greater regarding explicitness.

Considering faith from the subject's participation viewpoint: since faith's act proceeds from both intellect and will, a man's faith may be described as greater in one way regarding his intellect—through greater certitude and firmness—and in another way regarding his will—through greater promptitude, devotion, or confidence.

### Reply to Objection 1
One obstinately disbelieving faith matters lacks the faith habit. However, one not explicitly believing all while prepared to believe all possesses that habit. Regarding the object, one man has greater faith than another insofar as he believes more things.

### Reply to Objection 2
It is essential to faith that one gives the First Truth first place. But among those doing this, some submit with greater certitude and devotion than others; in this way faith is greater in one than another.

### Reply to Objection 3
Principle understanding results from man's nature, equally shared by all. Faith results from grace's gift, not equally in all, as explained earlier. Hence the comparison fails. Nevertheless, principles' truth is more known to one than another, according to greater intellect capacity.
