# Question 8: The Gift of Understanding

## Article 1: Whether Understanding is a Gift of the Holy Ghost

### Objection 1
Understanding should not be counted among the gifts of the Holy Ghost. The gifts of grace are distinct from natural gifts, yet understanding constitutes a natural intellectual habit whereby self-evident principles are known, as stated in Aristotle's Ethics.

### Objection 2
Divine gifts are shared by creatures according to their capacity. Human nature knows truth discursively through reason rather than simply through understanding. Therefore, the Divine knowledge bestowed on humans should be called a gift of reason, not understanding.

### Objection 3
Understanding is paired with the will as distinct powers of the soul. Since no gift of the Holy Ghost bears the name "will," neither should any be called "understanding."

### On the Contrary
Scripture states: "The Spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him, the Spirit of wisdom of understanding" (Isaiah 11:2).

### I Answer That
Understanding implies intimate knowledge, as "intelligere" means "intus legere" (to read inwardly). Intellectual knowledge penetrates to the very essence of things, whereas sensory knowledge concerns only external qualities.

Many hidden realities require human knowledge to penetrate within: the nature beneath accidents, meaning beneath words, truth beneath figures, and causes behind effects. Human knowledge begins externally, and its penetrative power depends on the strength of understanding's light. Natural understanding possesses finite power and reaches only a certain limit.

Therefore, humans need supernatural light to penetrate further and know what natural light cannot reach. This supernatural light bestowed on humans is called "the gift of understanding."

### Reply to Objection 1
Natural light manifests only general principles known naturally. Since humans are ordained to supernatural happiness, they must reach higher truths requiring the gift of understanding.

### Reply to Objection 2
Reasoning always begins from understanding and ends in understanding. When grace adds its light beyond nature's capacity, this addition is called "understanding" rather than "reason," as it functions relative to supernatural knowledge like natural light functions for naturally known things.

### Reply to Objection 3
"Will" denotes mere appetitive movement without indicating excellence, while "understanding" denotes knowledge's excellence penetrating to reality's heart. The supernatural gift is named after understanding rather than will.

## Article 2: Whether the Gift of Understanding is Compatible with Faith

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "the thing which is understood is bounded by the comprehension of him who understands it," whereas the thing believed is not comprehended. Therefore, faith and understanding seem incompatible in the same person.

### Objection 2
Whatever is understood is seen by understanding. Faith concerns things that appear not. Therefore, faith and understanding cannot coexist in the same subject.

### Objection 3
Understanding exceeds science in certainty. Since science and faith are incompatible, understanding and faith are even more so.

### On the Contrary
Gregory states: "Understanding enlightens the mind concerning the things it has heard." One with faith can be enlightened about what has been heard, as when "Our Lord opened the scriptures to His disciples, that they might understand them" (Luke 24:27-32).

### I Answer That
Two distinctions are necessary: one regarding faith, another regarding understanding.

On faith's side: Some things fall directly under faith, such as the mystery of the Trinity and the Incarnation. Other things fall under faith derivatively, being subordinate to these principal mysteries, such as all Divine Scripture's contents.

On understanding's side: Understanding operates two ways. First, we understand something perfectly when arriving at knowledge of its essence and the very truth of the proposition. During faith's state, we cannot perfectly understand things that are faith's direct object, though we can understand subordinate matters this way.

Second, we understand imperfectly when we do not know a thing's essence or a proposition's truth regarding its mode of being, yet we know that external appearances do not contradict the truth, and we understand that we ought not depart from faith's matters for externally apparent things. Even during faith's state, nothing prevents us from understanding faith's direct objects imperfectly.

### Reply to Objection 1
These objections argue regarding perfect understanding, while the contrary position concerns imperfect understanding of matters subordinate to faith.

### Reply to Objection 2
Same as above.

### Reply to Objection 3
Same as above.

## Article 3: Whether the Gift of Understanding is Merely Speculative or Also Practical

### Objection 1
According to Gregory, "understanding penetrates certain more exalted things," yet the practical intellect concerns inferior things and singulars involved in actions. Therefore, understanding as a gift is not practical.

### Objection 2
The gift of understanding exceeds the intellectual virtue of understanding. The intellectual virtue concerns only necessary things, according to Aristotle. Therefore, the gift concerns only necessary matters, not practical things concerning contingent matters arising from human activity.

### Objection 3
The gift of understanding enlightens the mind in matters surpassing natural reason. Human activities with which the practical intellect concerns itself do not surpass natural reason, which directs practical matters. Therefore, the gift is not practical.

### On the Contrary
Scripture says: "A good understanding to all that do it" (Psalm 110:10).

### I Answer That
As stated above, the gift of understanding concerns not only things directly under faith, but also things subordinate to faith. Good actions bear a certain relationship to faith, since "faith worketh through charity" (Galatians 5:6). Therefore, the gift of understanding extends also to certain actions—not as their principal object, but insofar as our actions' rule is the eternal law, to which the higher reason, perfected by understanding's gift, adheres by contemplating and consulting it.

### Reply to Objection 1
Things with which human actions concern themselves are not surpassingly exalted in themselves, but as referred to the eternal law's rule and the end of Divine happiness, they become exalted matters for understanding.

### Reply to Objection 2
The gift's excellence consists precisely in considering eternal or necessary matters—not merely as rules of human actions, but as the gift's object itself.

### Reply to Objection 3
Human actions' rule is human reason and the eternal law. The eternal law surpasses human reason, so knowing human actions as ruled by eternal law surpasses natural reason and requires the supernatural light of a gift.

## Article 4: Whether the Gift of Understanding is in All Who are in a State of Grace

### Objection 1
Gregory states the gift of understanding is given as a remedy against dulness of mind. Yet many in grace's state suffer dulness. Therefore, understanding's gift is not in all possessing grace.

### Objection 2
Only faith seems necessary for salvation among knowledge-related things, since through faith Christ dwells in hearts. Not all possessing faith have understanding's gift. Therefore, understanding's gift is not necessary for salvation and is not in all possessing grace.

### Objection 3
What is common to all in grace's state is never withdrawn from them. Yet understanding's grace sometimes withdraws profitably. Therefore, understanding's gift is not in all possessing grace.

### On the Contrary
Scripture declares: "They have not known or understood, they walk on in darkness" (Psalm 81:5). Yet no one in grace's state walks in darkness according to John 8:12. Therefore, none in grace's state lack understanding's gift.

### I Answer That
All in grace's state must possess the will's rectitude, since grace prepares the will for good. The will cannot be rightly directed to good without prior knowledge of truth, since the will's object is good understood. As the Holy Ghost directs humanity's will through charity's gift to move it directly toward supernatural good, so through understanding's gift He enlightens the human mind to know some supernatural truth, to which the right will must tend.

Therefore, just as charity's gift is in all possessing sanctifying grace, so is understanding's gift.

### Reply to Objection 1
Those with sanctifying grace may suffer dulness regarding things unnecessary for salvation; but regarding things necessary for salvation, the Holy Ghost sufficiently instructs them.

### Reply to Objection 2
Though not all with faith fully understand proposed beliefs, they understand that they ought to believe them and should nowise deviate from them.

### Reply to Objection 3
Regarding things necessary for salvation, understanding's gift never withdraws from holy persons. Yet regarding other matters, it withdraws profitably so pride has no incentive.

## Article 5: Whether the Gift of Understanding is Found in Those Without Sanctifying Grace

### Objection 1
Augustine suggests understanding flies ahead while humanity's will is weak and slow to follow. In all possessing sanctifying grace, the will is prompt through charity. Therefore, understanding's gift can exist without sanctifying grace.

### Objection 2
Prophecy requires understanding. Yet prophecy exists without sanctifying grace. Therefore, understanding's gift can exist without sanctifying grace.

### Objection 3
Understanding's gift corresponds to faith's virtue. Since faith can exist without sanctifying grace, so can understanding's gift.

### On the Contrary
Our Lord said: "Every one that hath heard of the Father, and hath learned, cometh to Me" (John 6:45). Whoever possesses understanding's gift comes to Christ, which is impossible without sanctifying grace. Therefore, understanding's gift cannot exist without sanctifying grace.

### I Answer That
The Holy Ghost's gifts perfect the soul according to its amenability to the Holy Ghost's motion. The intellectual light of grace is called understanding's gift insofar as human understanding is easily moved by the Holy Ghost, a movement's consideration depending on truly apprehending the end. Unless human intellect is moved by the Holy Ghost to have right estimation of the end, it has not yet obtained understanding's gift, however much the Holy Ghost may have enlightened it regarding other truths preambling faith.

To have right estimation about the last end, one must not err about the end and must adhere firmly to it as the greatest good. No one can do this without sanctifying grace. Therefore, no one possesses understanding's gift without sanctifying grace.

### Reply to Objection 1
By understanding, Augustine means any intellectual light not fulfilling all conditions of a gift unless the human mind is sufficiently perfected to have right estimation about the end.

### Reply to Objection 2
Understanding requisite for prophecy is mind enlightenment regarding revealed things, not enlightenment regarding right estimation about the last end, which belongs to understanding's gift.

### Reply to Objection 3
Faith implies merely assenting to what is proposed, while understanding implies certain truth perception. Except in those possessing sanctifying grace, such perception cannot regard the end.

## Article 6: Whether the Gift of Understanding is Distinct from the Other Gifts

### Objection 1
Things whose opposites are not distinct are not themselves distinct. Gregory states wisdom opposes folly, understanding opposes dulness, counsel opposes rashness, knowledge opposes ignorance. Yet folly, dulness, ignorance, and rashness seem undifferentiated.

### Objection 2
The intellectual virtue of understanding differs from other intellectual virtues in being about self-evident principles. Understanding's gift concerns no self-evident principles. Therefore, understanding's gift does not differ from other intellectual gifts.

### Objection 3
All intellectual knowledge is either speculative or practical. Understanding's gift relates to both. Therefore, it is not distinct from other intellectual gifts but comprises them all.

### On the Contrary
When several things are enumerated together, they must be distinct from one another. The gift of understanding is enumerated with other gifts (Isaiah 11:2). Therefore, it is distinct from the other gifts.

### I Answer That
The difference between understanding and piety, fortitude, and fear is evident: understanding belongs to the cognitive power while these three belong to the appetitive power.

The difference between understanding and wisdom, knowledge, and counsel is less evident. Understanding is concerned with grasping and penetrating things proposed, while wisdom concerns judgment about Divine things, knowledge about created things, and counsel about individual actions' application.

On faith's side, two things are requisite: first, that things be penetrated or grasped by intellect—this belongs to understanding's gift. Second, that humans judge rightly—this judgment regarding Divine things belongs to wisdom's gift, regarding created things to knowledge's gift, and regarding individual actions' application to counsel's gift.

### Reply to Objection 1
The distinction between those four gifts clearly aligns with Gregory's opposing defects: dulness contrary to sharpness, folly opposes wisdom, ignorance opposes knowledge, rashness opposes counsel.

### Reply to Objection 2
Understanding's gift concerns graceful knowledge's first principles, but differently than faith: faith's role is assenting to them, while understanding's gift is piercing mentally what is said.

### Reply to Objection 3
Understanding's gift relates to both kinds of knowledge—speculative and practical—not regarding judgment but regarding apprehension, by grasping what is said.

## Article 7: Whether the Sixth Beatitude "Blessed are the Clean of Heart" Responds to the Gift of Understanding

### Objection 1
Cleanness of heart seems chiefly belonging to appetite, yet understanding's gift belongs to the intellectual power. Therefore, this beatitude does not respond to understanding's gift.

### Objection 2
Scripture states: "Purifying their hearts by faith" (Acts 15:9). Cleanness comes through heart purification. Therefore, this beatitude relates to faith's virtue rather than understanding's gift.

### Objection 3
The Holy Ghost's gifts perfect humans in present life. Yet God's sight does not belong to present life but to the Blessed's happiness.

### On the Contrary
Augustine states: "The sixth work of the Holy Ghost which is understanding applies to the clean of heart, whose eye being purified, they can see what eye hath not seen."

### I Answer That
The sixth beatitude contains two elements: merit (cleanness of heart) and reward (God's sight), each responding somehow to understanding's gift.

Cleanness is twofold. First, as preamble and disposition to seeing God: the heart being cleansed of inordinate affections through virtues and gifts belonging to the appetitive power. Second, as complement to God's sight: the mind's cleanness purged of phantasms and errors, receiving proposed truths about God no longer through corporeal phantasms or heretical misrepresentations. This cleanness results from understanding's gift.

God's sight is also twofold. Perfect sight sees God's Essence; imperfect sight sees what God is not, understanding increasingly that He surpasses all the mind comprehends. Both visions belong to understanding's gift: the first to understanding in its perfection in heaven; the second to understanding in its inchoation in the present life.

### Reply to Objection 1
The first objection refers to the first kind of cleanness.

### Reply to Objection 2
The second objection refers to the first kind of cleanness as well.

### Reply to Objection 3
Gifts perfect us in this life inchoatively and will be fulfilled in the next.

## Article 8: Whether Faith, Among the Fruits, Responds to the Gift of Understanding

### Objection 1
Understanding is faith's fruit, as Isaiah 7:9 states: "If you will not believe you shall not understand." Therefore, faith is not understanding's fruit.

### Objection 2
What precedes is not what follows's fruit. Faith seems preceding understanding, as it is the spiritual edifice's foundation. Therefore, faith is not understanding's fruit.

### Objection 3
More gifts pertain to intellect than appetite. Yet among fruits, only faith pertains to intellect while others pertain to appetite.

### On the Contrary
Understanding's gift seems ordained chiefly to faith's certitude, which is reckoned a fruit. A gloss on Galatians 5:22 states: "The faith which is a fruit is certitude about the unseen."

### I Answer That
Fruits of the Spirit are something ultimate and delightful produced in us by the Holy Ghost's power. The ultimate and delightful has an end's nature, the will's proper object. Consequently, what is ultimate and delightful regarding the will must be, after a fashion, all other things' fruit pertaining to other powers.

Accordingly, to the gift of virtue perfecting a power, we may distinguish a double fruit: one belonging to the same power; the other, last of all as it were, belonging to the will. Therefore, the fruit properly responding to understanding's gift is faith—namely, faith's certitude—while the fruit responding to it last of all is joy, belonging to the will.

### Reply to Objection 1
Understanding is faith's fruit, taken as a virtue. Here, we take faith not in this sense but for a kind of faith's certitude, which humans attain through understanding's gift.

### Reply to Objection 2
Faith cannot altogether precede understanding, for it would be impossible assenting by believing what is proposed without understanding it somehow. However, understanding's perfection follows faith's virtue: which understanding's perfection is itself followed by a kind of faith's certainty.

### Reply to Objection 3
Practical knowledge's fruit cannot consist in that very knowledge, since such knowledge is known not for its own sake but for something else. Speculative knowledge's fruit consists in itself, which fruit is the thing known's certitude. Therefore, counsel's gift, belonging only to practical knowledge, has no corresponding fruit; while wisdom, understanding, and knowledge's gifts, which can belong also to speculative knowledge, have but one corresponding fruit, which is certainly denoted by faith's name.
