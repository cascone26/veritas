# Question 3: The Outward Act of Faith

## Article 1: Whether Confession Is an Act of Faith

### Objection 1
Confession appears not to be an act of faith, since the same act cannot belong to different virtues. Confession is part of penance, so it belongs to that virtue rather than to faith.

### Objection 2
People are sometimes prevented by fear or shame from confessing their faith. The Apostle asks for prayers "with confidence, to make known the mystery of the gospel" (Ephesians 6:19). Since fortitude moderates fear and daring, confession seems to belong to fortitude rather than faith.

### Objection 3
Faith working through charity produces external good works generally. Yet not all external works are considered acts of faith. Therefore, confession should not be classified as an act of faith.

### On the Contrary
A gloss on 2 Thessalonians 1:11 explains "the work of faith in power" as referring to "confession which is a work proper to faith."

### I Answer That
Outward actions properly belong to the virtue toward whose end they are specifically directed. Fasting, for example, is an act of abstinence because it aims at taming the flesh.

Confession of faith matters is specifically referred to faith's end. Scripture confirms this: "Having the same spirit of faith... we believe, and therefore we speak also" (2 Corinthians 4:13). The outward utterance expresses the inward thought. Therefore, just as internal belief is an act of faith, so is external confession.

### Reply to Objection 1
Scripture commends three types of confession. First, confession of faith matters is a proper act of faith directed to faith's end. Second, confession of thanksgiving or praise serves the virtue of latria by giving outward honor to God. Third, confession of sins aims at sin's removal and belongs to penance.

### Reply to Objection 2
Fortitude removes obstacles to faith confession by overcoming fear or shame, functioning as an indirect rather than direct cause. The principal cause of confession remains faith itself.

### Reply to Objection 3
Inward faith, with charity's aid, commands all outward virtuous acts through other virtues, but produces confession as its own proper act without requiring another virtue's assistance.

## Article 2: Whether Confession of Faith Is Necessary for Salvation

### Objection 1
Confession does not appear necessary for salvation. Faith's proper end—uniting the mind to divine truth—can occur without outward confession. Therefore, confession is not required for salvation.

### Objection 2
Outward confession reveals faith to others, but this seems necessary only for those teaching others. Simple believers apparently are not bound to confess faith.

### Objection 3
Whatever tends to scandalize or disturb others is not necessary for salvation. The Apostle says: "Be without offense to the Jews and to the gentiles and to the Church of God" (1 Corinthians 10:32). Public faith confession sometimes disturbs unbelievers, so it is not necessary.

### On the Contrary
The Apostle states: "With the heart we believe unto justice; but with the mouth, confession is made unto salvation" (Romans 10:10).

### I Answer That
Things necessary for salvation fall under divine law's precepts. Since faith confession is affirmative, it falls under affirmative precepts. Affirmative precepts bind always but not for all times and places—they apply according to circumstances fitting virtuous human acts.

Therefore, confessing faith is not always necessary but becomes so in specific circumstances: when silence would deprive God of due honor or harm our neighbor's spiritual welfare. For instance, if questioned about faith, remaining silent to suggest faithlessness or falsity, or to turn others from faith, makes confession necessary for salvation.

### Reply to Objection 1
Faith's end, like other virtues' ends, must serve charity's end—love of God and neighbor. When God's honor and neighbor's good demand it, one must confess faith outwardly rather than merely maintain internal union with truth.

### Reply to Objection 2
In necessity situations where faith faces danger, everyone must proclaim faith to encourage the faithful or counter unbelievers' attacks. Otherwise, not all believers must instruct others in faith.

### Reply to Objection 3
Public faith confession causing unbeliever disturbance without benefiting faith or the faithful lacks value. Christ taught: "Give not that which is holy to dogs" (Matthew 7:6). However, if faith profit or urgency exists, one should confess publicly despite unbeliever upset, as Christ indicated regarding Pharisee scandal.
