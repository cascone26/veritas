# Question 14: Blasphemy against the Holy Ghost

## Article 1: Whether the sin against the Holy Ghost is the same as the sin committed through certain malice?

### Objection 1
According to Matthew 12:32, sin against the Holy Ghost involves blasphemy, yet not every malicious sin constitutes blasphemy. Therefore, these appear to be different categories.

### Objection 2
Sin through deliberate malice is distinguished from sin through ignorance and weakness, whereas sin against the Holy Ghost is distinguished from sin against the Son of Man. Since their contraries differ, the sins themselves must differ.

### Objection 3
Sin against the Holy Ghost appears as a specific genus of sin with its own determinate type. However, deliberate malice represents not a special sin category but rather a general condition affecting any sin. Therefore, these cannot be identical.

### On the contrary
The Master states that "to sin against the Holy Ghost is to take pleasure in the malice of sin for its own sake," which constitutes sinning through deliberate malice.

### I answer that
Three interpretations have been offered regarding sin against the Holy Ghost.

The earlier Church Fathers—including Athanasius, Hilary, Ambrose, Jerome, and Chrysostom—understood this as literal blasphemy against the Holy Spirit. They interpret the distinction in Matthew 12:32 as follows: Christ performed actions regarding His human nature and actions regarding His divinity. The Jews initially blasphemed against the Son of Man by calling Him "a glutton...a wine drinker," but subsequently blasphemed against the Holy Ghost when they attributed His divine works to the devil's power.

Augustine, however, understands sin against the Holy Ghost as final impenitence—persisting in mortal sin until death. This sin extends beyond mere words to include thoughts and deeds. It is called sin against the Holy Ghost because it opposes remission of sins, which constitutes the Holy Ghost's work.

Others interpret sin against the Holy Ghost as sin against the good appropriated to the Holy Ghost. Since goodness is appropriated to the Holy Ghost (power to the Father, wisdom to the Son), sins through weakness constitute sins "against the Father," sins through ignorance constitute sins "against the Son," and sins through deliberate malice constitute sins "against the Holy Ghost."

This malicious choosing occurs in two ways. First, through the inclination of a vicious habit—in this sense, sinning through malice does not constitute sin against the Holy Ghost specifically. Second, through contempt that removes whatever might prevent choosing evil—hope is removed by despair, fear by presumption, and so forth. Since these preventative factors are effects of the Holy Ghost in us, sinning through such deliberate malice in this sense constitutes sin against the Holy Ghost.

### Reply to Objection 1
Just as faith confession involves not only words but also deeds, blasphemy against the Holy Ghost can be expressed through word, thought, and deed.

### Reply to Objection 2
According to the third interpretation, blasphemy against the Holy Ghost is distinguished from blasphemy against the Son of Man insofar as He is also Son of God. Thus, sin against the Son of Man involves sin through ignorance or weakness.

### Reply to Objection 3
Sin through deliberate malice, insofar as it results from a habit's inclination, does not constitute a special sin. However, insofar as it results from special contempt of an effect of the Holy Ghost in us, it carries the character of a special sin.

## Article 2: Whether it is fitting to distinguish six kinds of sin against the Holy Ghost?

### Objection 1
The Master assigns six kinds: despair, presumption, impenitence, obstinacy, resistance of known truth, and envy of a brother's spiritual good. However, despair and presumption seem to constitute unbelief rather than sin against the Holy Ghost.

### Objection 2
Impenitence concerns past sins while obstinacy concerns future sins. Yet temporal distinctions do not diversify virtue or vice species.

### Objection 3
Resistance to known truth and envy of a brother's spiritual good appear to constitute blasphemy against the Son rather than the Holy Ghost.

### Objection 4
Bernard states that "to refuse to obey is to resist the Holy Ghost." A gloss indicates that "feigned repentance is blasphemy against the Holy Ghost." Therefore, the enumeration seems incomplete.

### On the contrary
Augustine and other authorities confirm these six species.

### I answer that
These six species fittingly apply to sin against the Holy Ghost, as they involve contempt of things preventing deliberate sin. These preventative factors relate to God's judgment, His gifts, or sin itself.

Regarding Divine Judgment: Humans are deterred from deliberate sin through hope arising from considering divine mercy—removed by despair—and through fear arising from considering divine justice—removed by presumption.

Regarding God's Gifts: Two gifts withdraw humans from sin. Acknowledgment of truth—against which stands "resistance of known truth." Inward grace—against which stands "envy of a brother's spiritual good."

Regarding Sin Itself: Two considerations withdraw humans from sin. The inordinateness and shamefulness of the act, which arouses repentance—against which stands "impenitence." The smallness of the good sought in sin—removed by "obstinacy," whereby humans harden their purpose by clinging to sin.

### Reply to Objection 1
Despair and presumption do not consist in disbelieving God's justice and mercy but in contemptuously rejecting them.

### Reply to Objection 2
Obstinacy and impenitence differ not merely in temporal respect but in formal aspects, given diverse considerations of matters present in sin.

### Reply to Objection 3
Grace and truth were Christ's work through gifts of the Holy Ghost that He gave to humans.

### Reply to Objection 4
Refusing obedience belongs to obstinacy; feigned repentance belongs to impenitence; schism belongs to envy of a brother's spiritual good.

## Article 3: Whether the sin against the Holy Ghost can be forgiven?

### Objection 1
Augustine states: "We should despair of no man, so long as Our Lord's patience brings him back to repentance." Therefore, sin against the Holy Ghost can be forgiven.

### Objection 2
"No disease is incurable to an all-powerful physician." Therefore, sin against the Holy Ghost can be forgiven.

### Objection 3
Free will remains indifferent to good or evil. Therefore, humans can return from any sin to justice.

### On the contrary
Matthew 12:32 states: "He that shall speak against the Holy Ghost, it shall not be forgiven him, neither in this world, nor in the world to come."

### I answer that
Various interpretations yield different understandings of unforgivability.

If understood as final impenitence: This represents unpardonable sin in an absolute sense. A mortal sin wherein one persists until death receives no forgiveness in the afterlife.

According to the other two interpretations: Such sin is called unpardonable not because it receives no forgiveness whatsoever, but because, considered in itself, it deserves no pardon. This operates in two ways.

First, regarding punishment: Those sinning through ignorance or weakness deserve lesser punishment, while those sinning through deliberate malice offer no excuse mitigating punishment.

Second, regarding guilt: A disease is incurable by its nature when it removes whatever constitutes cure. Similarly, sin against the Holy Ghost is unpardonable by nature insofar as it removes means toward sin's pardon. However, this does not close forgiveness's path to an all-powerful, merciful God, Who sometimes, through miracle as it were, restores spiritual health to such persons.

### Reply to Objection 1
We should despair of no human in this life, considering God's omnipotence and mercy.

### Reply to Objection 2
This argument considers the matter from God's omnipotence's perspective, not from sin's circumstances.

### Reply to Objection 3
While free will remains subject to change in this life, it sometimes rejects that whereby it could turn toward good. Considered intrinsically, this sin is unpardonable, although God can pardon it.

## Article 4: Whether a man can sin first of all against the Holy Ghost?

### Objection 1
Natural order requires progression from imperfection to perfection. Since sin against the Holy Ghost constitutes the most grievous sin, humans must reach it through committing lesser sins.

### Objection 2
Sinning through deliberate malice requires choice, which develops after many sins.

### Objection 3
Repentance and impenitence concern the same object. Impenitence, being a species of sin against the Holy Ghost, similarly presupposes prior sins.

### On the contrary
"It is easy in the eyes of God on a sudden to make a poor man rich" (Sirach 11:23). Therefore, conversely, a man can be led to commit the most grievous sin against the Holy Ghost.

### I answer that
Sinning against the Holy Ghost involves sinning through deliberate malice in two ways.

First, through a habit's inclination: One does not properly sin against the Holy Ghost in this sense, nor does this occur immediately, since sinful acts must precede to cause the habit inducing sin.

Second, through contemptuously rejecting preventative things: This properly constitutes sin against the Holy Ghost. It typically presupposes other sins. However, it remains possible for humans to sin against the Holy Ghost in their first sinful act through contempt, given free will's capacity, many previous dispositions, and vehement movement toward evil coupled with feeble attachment to good.

Therefore, it scarcely or never happens that the perfect sin all at once against the Holy Ghost. Origen notes: "I do not think that anyone who stands on the highest step of perfection can fail suddenly; this can only happen by degrees and bit by bit."

### Reply to Objection 1
Movement in both good and evil progresses, generally, from imperfect to perfect. Yet humans can begin from something more perfect than others do.

### Reply to Objection 2
This argument concerns sin through deliberate malice when proceeding from a habit's inclination.

### Reply to Objection 3
If understanding impenitence as habitual impenitence (a species of sin against the Holy Ghost), it can precede sin: a human who has never sinned can hold the purpose of not repenting should sin subsequently occur.
