# Question 9: The Gift of Knowledge

## Article 1: Whether knowledge is a gift?

### Objection 1
Knowledge cannot be considered a gift, since the gifts of the Holy Ghost surpass natural faculty. Yet knowledge represents an effect of natural reasoning—"a demonstration is a syllogism which produces knowledge." Therefore, knowledge is not a gift of the Holy Ghost.

### Objection 2
The gifts of the Holy Ghost are common to all holy persons. However, Augustine states that "many of the faithful lack knowledge though they have faith." This suggests knowledge is not a gift.

### Objection 3
Since gifts are more perfect than virtues, one gift should suffice for one virtue's perfection. Understanding corresponds to faith. Knowledge appears to correspond to no particular virtue, so it seems knowledge is not a gift.

### On the contrary
Knowledge is enumerated among the seven gifts (Isaiah 11:2).

### I answer that
Grace perfects what nature can achieve. When the human intellect assents to truth through natural reasoning, it is perfected in two ways: by grasping the truth and by forming sure judgment about it.

For the human intellect to assent perfectly to faith's truth, two things are required. First, a sound grasp of what is proposed to be believed—this pertains to understanding. Second, sure and right judgment to discern what should be believed from what should not—this requires the gift of knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 1
Certitude of knowledge varies according to each nature's conditions. Humans form sure judgment through discursive reasoning, acquiring knowledge through demonstration. God possesses sure judgment without discursive process, through simple intuition. The gift of knowledge resembles God's knowledge as a participated likeness, though not discursive or argumentative but absolute and simple.

### Reply to Objection 2
Two kinds of knowledge regarding matters of belief exist. One is knowledge of what one ought to believe by discerning beliefs from non-beliefs—this gift is common to all holy persons. The other is knowledge about beliefs themselves, knowing not only what to believe but how to communicate faith, persuade others, and refute deniers. This knowledge belongs among gratuitous graces, not given to all. Augustine clarifies: "It is one thing to merely know what to believe, another to know how to dispense what one believes to the godly and defend it against the ungodly."

### Reply to Objection 3
Gifts are more perfect than moral and intellectual virtues but not more perfect than theological virtues. All gifts are ordained toward perfecting theological virtues as their end. Therefore, several gifts can be ordained to one theological virtue.

## Article 2: Whether the gift of knowledge is about Divine things?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "knowledge begets, nourishes and strengthens faith." Since faith concerns Divine things—its object being the First Truth—the gift of knowledge must also concern Divine things.

### Objection 2
The gift of knowledge is more excellent than acquired knowledge. Yet acquired knowledge about Divine things exists, such as metaphysics. Therefore, the gift of knowledge must concern Divine things.

### Objection 3
According to Romans 1:20, "the invisible things of God...are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made." If knowledge of created things exists, knowledge of Divine things must also exist.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "The knowledge of Divine things may be properly called wisdom, and the knowledge of human affairs may properly receive the name of knowledge."

### I answer that
Sure judgment about something is formed chiefly from its cause. Therefore, judgment's order should follow causes' order. The judgment formed through the first cause is the first and most perfect judgment.

Where something most perfect is found, the genus's common name is appropriated to less perfect members, while a special name applies to the most perfect member. Since knowledge implies judgment's certitude, when certitude derives from the highest cause, this knowledge receives a special name: wisdom. The absolutely wise person knows God, the absolutely highest cause.

Divine things' knowledge is called wisdom; human things' knowledge is called knowledge—the common name denoting judgment's certitude, appropriated to judgment formed through secondary causes. Thus, knowledge as a distinct gift from wisdom concerns only human or created things.

### Reply to Objection 1
Although faith's matters are Divine and eternal, faith itself exists temporally in the believer's mind. Thus, knowing what one ought to believe belongs to the gift of knowledge, but knowing the very things believed in themselves, through a kind of union with them, belongs to wisdom. Wisdom corresponds more to charity, which unites the mind to God.

### Reply to Objection 2
This objection takes knowledge in its generic sense. Knowledge constitutes a special gift only when restricted to judgments formed through created things.

### Reply to Objection 3
Every cognitive habit regards formally the means through which things are known and materially the things known through the means. Since the formal aspect predominates, sciences drawing conclusions about physical matter from mathematical principles are reckoned among mathematical sciences. Similarly, since man knows God through creatures, this pertains formally to knowledge rather than wisdom materially. Conversely, judging creatures according to Divine things pertains to wisdom rather than knowledge.

## Article 3: Whether the gift of knowledge is practical knowledge?

### Objection 1
Augustine states that "knowledge is concerned with the actions in which we make use of external things." Since knowledge concerning actions is practical, the gift of knowledge must be practical.

### Objection 2
Gregory says: "Knowledge is nought if it hath not its use for piety...and piety is useless if it lacks knowledge's discernment." This indicates knowledge directs piety, which cannot apply to speculative science. Therefore, the gift of knowledge is practical, not speculative.

### Objection 3
The Holy Ghost's gifts exist only in the righteous. Yet speculative knowledge can exist in the unrighteous, as James 4:17 indicates. Therefore, the gift of knowledge is practical, not speculative.

### On the contrary
Gregory states: "Knowledge on her own day prepares a feast, because she overcomes ignorance's fast in the mind." Ignorance is removed only by both speculative and practical knowledge. Therefore, the gift of knowledge is both.

### I answer that
As previously stated, the gift of knowledge, like understanding's gift, serves faith's certitude. Faith consists primarily in speculation, being founded on the First Truth. Yet since the First Truth is also our last end—for which actions are performed—faith extends to works, according to Galatians 5:6: "Faith...worketh by charity."

Consequently, the gift of knowledge primarily regards speculation, insofar as one knows what ought to be held by faith. Yet secondarily, it extends to works, since faith's knowledge and its conclusions direct our actions.

### Reply to Objection 1
Augustine speaks of the gift of knowledge insofar as it extends to works. Action is ascribed to knowledge, yet not solely or primarily. Through knowledge it directs piety.

### Reply to Objection 2
The reply becomes clear from this explanation.

### Reply to Objection 3
As stated regarding understanding's gift, not everyone who understands possesses understanding's gift, but only one understanding through a grace-habit. Similarly, only those judging rightly about faith's matters and actions through bestowed grace—never wandering from justice's straight path—possess the gift of knowledge. This constitutes holy things' knowledge, according to Wisdom 10:10: "She conducted the just...through the right ways...and gave him the knowledge of holy things."

## Article 4: Whether the third beatitude, "Blessed are they that mourn," corresponds to the gift of knowledge?

### Objection 1
Evil causes sorrow and grief; good causes joy. Since knowledge brings good to light rather than evil—"the straight line rules both itself and the crooked line"—the beatitude of mourning does not suitably correspond to the gift of knowledge.

### Objection 2
Considering truth is knowledge's act. Yet truth's consideration brings no sorrow but rather joy, as Wisdom 8:16 states: "Her conversation hath no bitterness...but joy and gladness." Therefore, the beatitude of mourning does not suitably correspond to knowledge's gift.

### Objection 3
The gift of knowledge consists in speculation before operation. Since "the speculative intellect is not concerned about things to be sought or avoided," sorrow does not correspond to its speculation. Therefore, the beatitude is not suitably reckoned to the gift of knowledge.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "Knowledge befits the mourner, who has discovered that he has been mastered by the evil which he coveted as though it were good."

### I answer that
Right judgment about creatures belongs properly to knowledge. Through creatures, man's aversion from God is occasioned, according to Wisdom 14:11: "Creatures...are turned to an abomination...and a snare to the feet of the unwise"—those not judging rightly about creatures, deeming perfect good to consist in them. They sin by placing their last end in creatures, losing true good.

By forming right creature judgment through the gift of knowledge, man becomes aware of this loss. Thus the sorrow beatitude corresponds to knowledge's gift.

### Reply to Objection 1
Created goods cause spiritual joy only insofar as they are referred to Divine good, the proper spiritual joy cause. Spiritual peace and resulting joy correspond directly to wisdom's gift. To knowledge's gift corresponds first sorrow for past errors and consequently consolation, since right judgment directs creatures to Divine good. Sorrow appears in this beatitude as merit; the resulting consolation appears as reward, begun in this life and perfected in the next.

### Reply to Objection 2
Man rejoices in truth's very consideration. Yet he may grieve for the thing whose truth he considers. Thus sorrow is ascribed to knowledge.

### Reply to Objection 3
No beatitude corresponds to knowledge insofar as it consists in creature speculation, since man's beatitude consists not in considering creatures but in contemplating God. Man's beatitude does consist somewhat in creatures' right use and well-ordered love—concerning the wayfarer's beatitude. Thus, beatitude relating to contemplation is ascribed to understanding and wisdom, which concern Divine things, not to knowledge.
