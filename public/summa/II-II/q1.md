# Question 1: Faith

## Article 1: Whether the object of faith is the First Truth?

### Objection 1
It seems the object of faith is not the First Truth. We are asked to believe not only in God's divinity but also in Christ's human nature, Church sacraments, and creatures. Therefore, faith's object extends beyond the First Truth alone.

### Objection 2
Further, faith and unbelief oppose each other. Since unbelief can concern anything in Holy Scripture, faith must also concern all scriptural content—including things about humans and creatures. Thus faith's object is not limited to the First Truth.

### Objection 3
Furthermore, faith is distinguished alongside charity. Charity loves not only God but also our neighbor. Therefore, faith's object cannot be exclusively the First Truth.

### On the contrary
Dionysius states that "faith is about the simple and everlasting truth." This is the First Truth, so faith's object is the First Truth.

### I answer that
Every cognitive habit has two aspects: the material object (what is known) and the formal aspect (how it is known). In geometry, conclusions are materially what is known, while demonstration is the formal means.

Regarding faith's formal aspect, it is nothing but the First Truth. Faith assents only to what God reveals; thus Divine Truth is its foundation.

Materially, faith concerns many things—God, Christ's humanity, sacraments, creatures—but only insofar as they relate to God. As medicine considers everything relative to health, faith considers all things in relation to God.

### Reply to Objection 1
Things concerning Christ's humanity, sacraments, and creatures come under faith insofar as they direct us toward God. We assent to them because of Divine Truth.

### Reply to Objection 2
The same principle applies: all scriptural content comes under faith through its relation to God.

### Reply to Objection 3
Charity likewise loves our neighbor for God's sake, so God is properly its object.

## Article 2: Whether the object of faith is something complex, by way of a proposition?

### Objection 1
The object of faith is the First Truth, which is simple. Therefore faith's object cannot be something complex like a proposition.

### Objection 2
The symbol expresses faith not as propositions but as things. It says "I believe in God...almighty," not "God is almighty." Therefore faith concerns things, not propositions.

### Objection 3
Faith is succeeded by vision according to 1 Corinthians 13:12. The vision of God is simple—the Divine Essence itself. Therefore faith must likewise be about something simple.

### On the contrary
Faith stands between science and opinion. Since science and opinion concern propositions, faith must concern propositions, making its object something complex.

### I answer that
What is known exists in the knower according to the knower's mode. The human intellect knows truth through synthesis and analysis. Thus simple things are known by our intellect with a certain complexity, while God's intellect knows complex things simply.

Faith's object can be considered two ways. First, regarding the thing believed—in this sense, the object is simple (the thing itself). Second, regarding the believer—in this respect, the object is complex like a proposition.

Both viewpoints contain truth.

### Reply to Objection 1
This argument considers faith's object from the thing believed's perspective.

### Reply to Objection 2
The symbol mentions things about which faith operates, insofar as the believer's act terminates in them. The believer's act does not terminate in propositions but in things. Just as science forms propositions to know things through them, faith does likewise.

### Reply to Objection 3
Heavenly vision will apprehend the First Truth in itself, not through propositions. Faith, however, does not apprehend the First Truth as it is in itself; the comparison fails.

## Article 3: Whether anything false can come under faith?

### Objection 1
Something false can come under hope and charity. Many hope for eternal life who won't obtain it; many are loved as good who aren't. Therefore, something false can come under faith.

### Objection 2
Abraham believed Christ would be born. Yet after Abraham, God might not have taken flesh—it depended on His will. Thus what Abraham believed could have been false.

### Objection 3
The ancients believed in Christ's future birth. After His birth, before His preaching, believing He was yet to be born would be false. Therefore something false can come under faith.

### Objection 4
We believe the true Body of Christ is in the Eucharist. But if bread wasn't properly consecrated, only bread would be there. Therefore something false could come under faith.

### On the contrary
No virtue perfecting the intellect relates to the false as evil of the intellect. Faith is a virtue perfecting the intellect. Therefore nothing false can come under faith.

### I answer that
Nothing comes under any power except through the formal aspect of its object. Color cannot be seen except through light; conclusions cannot be known except through demonstration. Faith's formal aspect is the First Truth. Nothing false can stand under the First Truth, just as non-being cannot stand under being or evil under goodness. Therefore nothing false can come under faith.

### Reply to Objection 1
Since truth is the good of the intellect, virtues perfecting the intellect exclude falsehood entirely. Virtues perfecting the appetitive faculty do not exclude falsehood entirely, since one can act justly while holding false opinions about one's action. Faith perfects the intellect, while hope and charity perfect the appetitive faculty.

However, neither can anything false come under hope. One hopes for eternal life not by personal power but through God's grace; if one perseveres, one will certainly obtain it. Likewise, charity loves God wherever He is; it matters not whether God is in the individual loved for God's sake.

### Reply to Objection 2
That "God would not take flesh" was possible in itself after Abraham's time. Yet in God's foreknowledge, it had a necessity of infallibility. In coming under faith this way, it cannot be false.

### Reply to Objection 3
After Christ's birth, believing in Him meant believing in Christ's birth at some time. Those deceived about the specific timing erred through human conjecture, not through faith. A believer may hold false opinions through human conjecture, but false opinion cannot result from faith itself.

### Reply to Objection 4
The believer's faith concerns not the bread's accidents but that Christ's true body is under sensible bread when properly consecrated. If unconsecrated, nothing false comes under faith.

## Article 4: Whether the object of faith can be something seen?

### Objection 1
Our Lord told Thomas: "Because thou hast seen Me, thou hast believed." Therefore vision and faith concern the same object.

### Objection 2
The Apostle, speaking of faith's knowledge, says: "We see now through a glass in a dark manner." Therefore what is believed is seen.

### Objection 3
Faith is spiritual light. Under every light something is seen. Therefore faith concerns things seen.

### Objection 4
"Every sense is a kind of sight," Augustine states. Yet faith comes "by hearing," according to Romans 10:17. Therefore faith is of things seen.

### On the contrary
The Apostle states in Hebrews 11:1: "Faith is the evidence of things that appear not."

### I answer that
The intellect assents to things in two ways. First, moved by the object itself, which is known either in itself (first principles, held by understanding) or through something else already known (conclusions, held by science). Second, not through the object sufficiently moving it, but through voluntary choice, turning toward one side rather than another—this produces opinion if accompanied by doubt, or faith if accompanied by certainty.

Things "seen" move the intellect or senses to knowledge of themselves. Therefore, neither faith nor opinion can concern things seen by senses or intellect.

### Reply to Objection 1
Thomas saw one thing and believed another. He saw the Man but believed Him to be God, professing his faith: "My Lord and my God."

### Reply to Objection 2
Things under faith can be considered two ways. Particularly, they cannot be both seen and believed simultaneously. Generally—under the aspect of credibility—the believer sees them. Without seeing they should be believed through signs or similar evidence, one would not believe.

### Reply to Objection 3
Faith's light makes us see what we believe. Just as virtue habits show what befits that habit, faith shows what befits right faith to assent to.

### Reply to Objection 4
Hearing concerns words signifying faith's matters, not the things themselves believed. Thus these things are not seen.

## Article 5: Whether those things that are of faith can be an object of science?

### Objection 1
Where science is lacking, ignorance exists. We are not ignorant of things we must believe, since such ignorance savors of unbelief. Therefore faith's objects can be objects of science.

### Objection 2
Science is acquired through reasons. Sacred writers employ reasons for matters of faith. Therefore such things can be objects of science.

### Objection 3
Demonstrated matters are objects of science. Philosophers demonstrated God's existence and unity. Therefore faith matters can be objects of science.

### Objection 4
Opinion is further from science than faith is. Yet opinion and science can concern the same object. Therefore faith and science can also.

### On the contrary
Gregory states: "When a thing is manifest, it is the object not of faith but of perception." What is an object of science is perceived. Therefore faith and science do not concern the same things.

### I answer that
All science derives from self-evident "seen" principles; science's objects must be seen. As stated, one and the same thing cannot be both believed and seen by the same person. Therefore, one and the same thing cannot be both an object of science and belief for the same person.

However, what is vision or science for one person may be believed by another. We hope to see what we now believe about the Trinity; angels already see what we believe. Similarly, what produces scientific knowledge for one person—even in the wayfarer state—may be believed by another lacking that demonstration.

Nevertheless, what is proposed equally to all as credible is equally unknown scientifically by all. Such matters of faith simply, therefore, cannot be objects of both faith and science.

### Reply to Objection 1
Unbelievers are ignorant of faith matters—they neither see nor know them in themselves, nor know them to be credible. The faithful know them not through demonstration but by faith's light, which shows they should be believed.

### Reply to Objection 2
Holy men's reasons proving faith matters are not demonstrations. They are either persuasive arguments showing that proposed doctrine is not impossible, or proofs from faith's principles—Holy Scripture's authority—as Dionysius declares. Whatever bases itself on these principles is as proven to believers as conclusions from self-evident principles are to all. Thus theology is a science.

### Reply to Objection 3
Things provable by demonstration are reckoned among faith articles not because all believe them simply but because they are necessary presuppositions to faith matters. Those not knowing them by demonstration must first know them by faith.

### Reply to Objection 4
Science and opinion about the same object can be in different people. One person may know and think differently about the same thing. Similarly, one may know the Godhead's unity by demonstration and the Trinity by faith. In one person, about the same object in the same respect, science is incompatible with opinion or faith. Science demands its object be impossible otherwise; opinion deems its object possible otherwise. What is faith's object is also deemed impossible otherwise through faith's certainty. Yet science and faith cannot concern the same object in the same respect because science's object is seen while faith's is unseen.

## Article 6: Whether those things that are of faith should be divided into certain articles?

### Objection 1
All Holy Scripture's content constitutes matters of faith. These cannot be reduced to a specific number due to their multitude. Therefore distinguishing articles of faith seems superfluous.

### Objection 2
Material differences multiply indefinitely, so art ignores them. Faith's formal aspect—the First Truth—is one and indivisible. Thus faith matters cannot be distinguished materially into articles.

### Objection 3
An article was defined by some as "an indivisible truth concerning God, exacting our belief." Belief is voluntary; no one believes against will. Therefore faith matters should not be divided into articles.

### On the contrary
Isidore states: "An article is a glimpse of Divine truth tending thereto." We glimpse Divine truth only through analysis, since things that are one in God are manifold in our intellect. Therefore faith matters should be divided into articles.

### I answer that
"Article" derives from Greek _arthron_, meaning the fitting together of distinct parts. Small body parts fitting together are limb articulations. In grammar, articles show gender, number, case. In rhetoric, articles compose sentences from individually pronounced words.

Matters of Christian faith contain distinct articles insofar as they divide into parts fitting together. Faith's object is something unseen concerning God. Any matter specially unseen constitutes a special article. When several matters are known or unknown under the same aspect, distinct articles are not distinguished. Thus one encounters one difficulty seeing God suffered and another seeing He rose—so resurrection differs from passion. That He suffered, died, and was buried presents the same difficulty; if one is accepted, the others are not difficult. Thus all belong to one article.

### Reply to Objection 1
Some things proposed for belief are matters of faith in themselves; others are matters of faith only in relation to others—just as in sciences certain propositions are proposed for their own account while others manifest others. Since faith's chief object consists in things we hope to see—according to Hebrews 11:2, "Faith is the substance of things to be hoped for"—things directly ordering us to eternal life are intrinsically matters of faith. These include the Trinity of Persons in Almighty God, Christ's Incarnation mystery, and similar things; these are distinct articles of faith. Other scriptural content is proposed for believing chiefly for manifesting those matters—for instance, Abraham's two sons, a dead man rising at Eliseus' bones' touch—these are related to manifest the Divine mystery or Christ's Incarnation. Such things should not form distinct articles.

### Reply to Objection 2
Faith's formal aspect can be considered two ways. First, from the believed thing's perspective, providing one formal aspect for all faith matters—the First Truth—so no distinction of articles. Second, from our perspective, where the formal aspect is that something is unseen. From this perspective, various distinct articles of faith exist.

### Reply to Objection 3
This article definition derives from Latin etymology rather than Greek meaning, carrying little weight. Yet belief is exacted not through coercion's necessity but through end's necessity: "He that cometh to God must believe that He is," and "Without faith it is impossible to please God," the Apostle declares.

## Article 7: Whether the articles of faith have increased in course of time?

### Objection 1
Faith is "the substance of things to be hoped for." The same things are hoped for at all times. Therefore the same things are believed at all times.

### Objection 2
Development occurs in human-devised sciences due to discoverers' ignorance. Faith doctrine was not devised by humans but delivered by God. Since God cannot lack knowledge, faith knowledge was perfect from the beginning and did not increase.

### Objection 3
Grace operates as orderly as nature. Nature always begins with perfect things. Therefore grace's operation began with perfect things; those first delivering faith knew it most perfectly.

### Objection 4
As Christ's faith was delivered through apostles, Old Testament knowledge was delivered by early fathers to later ones. The apostles received mysteries most fully and earliest. Therefore faith knowledge has not increased over time.

### On the contrary
Gregory states: "The holy fathers' knowledge increased over time...the nearer they were to Our Savior's coming, the more fully they received salvation's mysteries."

### I answer that
Faith articles relate to faith doctrine as self-evident principles relate to natural reason teaching. Among these principles exists a certain order where some contain others implicitly. All principles reduce to "The same thing cannot be affirmed and denied simultaneously."

Similarly, all articles are contained implicitly in certain primary faith matters: God's existence and His providence over human salvation, according to Hebrews 11: "He that cometh to God must believe that He is, and is a rewarder to them that seek Him." God's existence includes all eternally existing in God, wherein happiness consists. Belief in His providence includes what God dispenses in time for human salvation—the way to happiness. Thus some subsequent articles are contained in others: faith in mankind's redemption includes belief in Christ's Incarnation, Passion, etc.

Consequently, regarding the substance of faith articles, they have not increased over time. Whatever later peoples believed was contained implicitly in earlier fathers' faith. However, the number of explicitly believed articles increased as time passed. Some later peoples knew explicitly what earlier peoples did not know explicitly. The Lord told Moses: "I am the God of Abraham, Isaac, Jacob...and My name Adonai I did not show them." David said: "I have had understanding above ancients." The Apostle states that Christ's mystery "in other generations was not known, as it is now revealed to His holy apostles and prophets."

### Reply to Objection 1
Among humans the same things were always hoped for through Christ. Yet as they acquired this hope through Christ, the further removed from Christ's time, the further from obtaining what they hoped for. Thus the Apostle says: "All these died according to faith, not having received the promises, but beholding them afar off." The further off something is, the less distinctly it is seen; thus those nigh to Christ's coming had more distinct knowledge of hoped-for goods.

### Reply to Objection 2
Progress in knowledge occurs two ways. First, from the teacher's side—one or many making progress over time, as in human-devised sciences. Second, from the learner's side—the master with perfect art knowledge does not deliver it all at once from the outset, as the disciple cannot absorb it; he condescends to capacity, instructing gradually. Thus humans made progress in faith knowledge. The Apostle compares the Old Testament state to childhood.

### Reply to Objection 3
Two things are requisite for actual generation: an agent and matter. In the active cause's order, the more perfect is naturally first; nature begins with perfect things, since only something already perfect brings the imperfect to perfection. In the material cause's order, the imperfect comes first; nature proceeds from imperfect to perfect. In faith's manifestation, God is the active cause with perfect eternal knowledge; humans are like matter receiving God's action influx. Hence among humans, faith knowledge must proceed from imperfection to perfection. Although some humans acted as active causes as faith doctors, the Spirit's manifestation is given for common good. Thus faith knowledge was imparted to instructing Fathers as necessary for the people's instruction, openly or figuratively.

### Reply to Objection 4
Grace's ultimate consummation was effected by Christ; His time is called the "fulness of time." Thus those nearest Christ—before Him like John the Baptist or after Him like the apostles—had fuller mystery knowledge. Regarding human state, perfection comes in youth; a state is more perfect the nearer it approaches one's youthful time.

## Article 8: Whether the articles of faith are suitably formulated?

### Objection 1
Things demonstrable do not belong to faith as belief objects for all. God's existence can be demonstrated; philosophers proved this. Therefore "There is one God" should not be a faith article.

### Objection 2
As it is necessary believing God almighty, so believing Him all-knowing and provident. Some erred on both points. Therefore God's wisdom and providence should be faith articles like omnipotence.

### Objection 3
Knowing the Father equals knowing the Son (John 14:9). Therefore there should be one article about Father and Son, and likewise about the Holy Ghost.

### Objection 4
The Father's person is no less than the Son's or Holy Ghost's. Yet there are multiple articles about the Holy Ghost and Son. Therefore there should be multiple articles about the Father.

### Objection 5
As certain things are appropriated to the Father's and Holy Ghost's persons by appropriation, so something is appropriated to the Son's Godhead. The Father's work (creation) and Holy Ghost's work ("He spoke by prophets") are faith articles. Therefore something appropriated to the Son's Godhead should be a faith article.

### Objection 6
The Eucharist sacrament presents special difficulty beyond other articles. Therefore it should have been mentioned in a special article. Consequently there seems insufficient article number.

### On the contrary
The Church's authority formulates articles thus.

### I answer that
As stated, things we shall see in eternal life, and by which we are brought to eternal life, belong to faith. Two things are proposed to be seen eternally: the Godhead's secret—seeing which constitutes happiness—and Christ's Incarnation mystery, "by Whom we have access" to God's sons' glory, according to Romans 5:2. Thus it is written: "This is eternal life: that they may know Thee, the true God, and Jesus Christ Whom Thou hast sent."

Therefore the first distinction in faith matters is: some concern the Godhead's majesty; others pertain to Christ's human nature mystery, the "mystery of godliness." Regarding the Godhead's majesty, three things are proposed: first, the Godhead's unity (first article); second, the Persons' trinity (three articles for three Persons); third, proper Godhead works. The first work concerns the nature's order—the creation article. The second concerns grace's order—sanctification articles united in one. The third concerns glory's order—the resurrection and eternal life article. Thus seven articles concern the Godhead.

Similarly, seven articles concern Christ's human nature: conception/incarnation; virginal birth; Passion, death, burial; descent into hell; Resurrection; Ascension; coming for judgment. Thus there are fourteen articles total.

Some distinguish twelve articles: six concerning the Godhead, six concerning humanity, uniting the three Persons in one article and dividing glorification into two—body resurrection and soul glory—while uniting conception and nativity in one.

### Reply to Objection 1
By faith we hold many God truths philosophers could not discover by natural reason—providence, omnipotence, that He alone should be worshipped. All are contained in the Godhead unity article.

### Reply to Objection 2
The Godhead term implies a kind of watching over things. In intellect-possessing beings, power does not work except through will and knowledge. Thus God's omnipotence includes universal knowledge and providence. He could not accomplish all He wills below unless He knew things and exercised providence.

### Reply to Objection 3
We have one knowledge of Father, Son, and Holy Ghost regarding Essence unity (first article). Yet regarding Person distinction through origin relations, knowing the Father includes knowing the Son, for He would not be Father without a Son; the Holy Ghost is their bond. This warranted one article for three Persons. However, regarding each Person, certain points must be observed about which some erred. Thus distinguishing three articles about the Persons is justified. Arius believed the Father's omnipotence and eternity but not the Son's co-equality and consubstantiality. Macedonius required an article about the Holy Ghost. Similarly, Christ's conception and birth, and Resurrection and eternal life, can be united or distinguished as presenting special or unified difficulty.

### Reply to Objection 4
The Son and Holy Ghost are sent to sanctify creatures, requiring belief in multiple things. Hence more articles concern the Son's and Holy Ghost's persons than the Father's, Who is never sent.

### Reply to Objection 5
A creature's sanctification through grace and consummation through glory is also effected by charity-gift, appropriated to the Holy Ghost, and wisdom-gift, appropriated to the Son. Thus each work belongs by appropriation, but under different aspects, to both Son and Holy Ghost.

### Reply to Objection 6
Two things exist in the Eucharist sacrament. First, that it is a sacrament—in this respect like other sanctifying grace effects. Second, that Christ's body is miraculously contained there—thus included under God's omnipotence, like all miracles ascribed to God's almighty power.

## Article 9: Whether it is suitable for the articles of faith to be embodied in a symbol?

### Objection 1
Holy Scripture is faith's rule; nothing may lawfully be added or subtracted. Deuteronomy 4:2 states: "You shall not add to the word that I speak to you, neither shall you take away from it." Therefore making a symbol as faith's rule after Holy Scripture's publication was unlawful.

### Objection 2
The Apostle says there is but "one faith." The symbol is faith's profession. Therefore there should not be multiple symbols.

### Objection 3
The symbol's faith confession concerns all faithful. Not all faithful are competent believing in God—only those having living faith. Therefore the symbol should not say: "I believe in one God."

### Objection 4
Descent into hell is a faith article. Yet the Fathers' symbol does not mention it. Therefore the symbol is inadequately expressed.

### Objection 5
Augustine states we believe Peter or Paul, but speak only of "believing 'in'" God. The Catholic Church is merely a created being. Therefore saying "In the One, Holy, Catholic and Apostolic Church" seems unfitting.

### Objection 6
A symbol is drawn up as a faith rule. A faith rule should be proposed publicly to all. Therefore every symbol besides the Fathers' should be sung at Mass. Consequently publishing faith articles in a symbol seems unfitting.

### On the contrary
The universal Church cannot err, governed by the Holy Ghost, the Spirit of truth. Our Lord promised His disciples: "When He, the Spirit of truth, is come, He will teach you all truth." The symbol is published by the universal Church's authority. Therefore it contains nothing defective.

### I answer that
The Apostle states: "He that cometh to God must believe that He is." A person cannot believe unless the truth to believe is proposed to him. Hence faith truth must be collected together so all may more easily access it, preventing anyone straying through faith ignorance. The symbol's name derives from its being a faith maxims collection (_symballein_—fitting together).

### Reply to Objection 1
Faith truth is contained in Holy Scripture diffusely, under various expressions, sometimes obscurely. Gathering faith truth from Scripture requires lengthy study and practice, unattainable by all needing faith knowledge—many too busy for study. Thus gathering a clear summary from Holy Scripture's sayings to propose to all was necessary. This adds nothing to Holy Scripture but gathers from it.

### Reply to Objection 2
The same faith doctrine is taught in all symbols. Nevertheless, as errors arise, people need more careful faith truth instruction, lest heretics corrupt simple minds' faith. This necessitated formulating several symbols, which differ only in making explicitly what another contains implicitly, due to heretic obstinacy.

### Reply to Objection 3
The symbol's faith confession is drawn up in the person, as it were, of the whole Church united by faith. The Church's faith is living faith—found in those of the Church not only outwardly but by merit. Thus the symbol expresses faith confession in keeping with living faith, so even lacking living faith should endeavor acquiring it.

### Reply to Objection 4
No heretic error about hell descent arose, necessitating more explicit treatment. Thus it is not repeated in the Fathers' symbol but supposed as already settled in the Apostles' symbol. A subsequent symbol does not cancel a preceding one but expounds it.

### Reply to Objection 5
If we say "In" the holy Catholic Church, this is verified insofar as faith is directed to the Holy Ghost sanctifying the Church—meaning "I believe in the Holy Ghost sanctifying the Church." Better and more customary is saying simply "the holy Catholic Church," omitting "in," as Pope Leo observes.

### Reply to Objection 6
The Fathers' symbol, an Apostles' symbol explanation, was drawn up after faith spread and the Church reached peace, so it is sung publicly at Mass. The Apostles' symbol, drawn up during persecution before faith was made public, is said secretly at Prime and Compline, as though against past and future error darkness.

## Article 10: Whether it belongs to the Sovereign Pontiff to draw up a symbol of faith?

### Objection 1
A new symbol edition becomes necessary to explain faith articles. In the Old Testament, articles were increasingly explained as Christ's time approached, since faith truth became clearer through greater nearness to Christ. Since this reason ceased with the New Law's advent, there is no need for increasingly explicit faith articles. Therefore the Sovereign Pontiff's authority should not draw up new symbol editions.

### Objection 2
No person can do what the universal Church forbade under anathema. The first Council of Ephesus forbade, under anathema pain, making new symbol editions beyond the Nicene. The Council of Chalcedon repeated this. Therefore the Sovereign Pontiff lacks authority publishing new symbol editions.

### Objection 3
Athanasius, not the Sovereign Pontiff but Alexandria's patriarch, published a symbol sung in the Church. Therefore symbol publication belongs to bishops generally, not particularly to the Sovereign Pontiff.

### On the contrary
A general council drew up the symbol. Such councils are convoked only by the Sovereign Pontiff's authority. Therefore symbol drawing up belongs to the Sovereign Pontiff's authority.

### I answer that
A new symbol edition becomes necessary to address arising errors. Consequently, symbol publication authority belongs to that authority finally deciding faith matters, so all hold them with unwavering faith. This belongs to the Sovereign Pontiff's authority, "to whom the Church's more important and difficult questions are referred." Our Lord told Peter, whom He made Sovereign Pontiff: "I have prayed for thee, Peter, that thy faith fail not, and thou, being once converted, confirm thy brethren."

The reason is that there should be one faith for the whole Church. This could not be secured unless faith questions arising are decided by the one presiding over the whole Church, so it holds firmly to his decision. Consequently, the Sovereign Pontiff alone has authority publishing new symbol editions and other matters concerning the whole Church, such as convening general councils.

### Reply to Objection 1
Faith truth is sufficiently explicit in Christ's and the apostles' teaching. However, since some men are so evil-minded as to pervert apostolic teaching and other scriptures to their destruction, expressing faith explicitly against arising errors became necessary as time passed.

### Reply to Objection 2
The council's prohibition and sentence targeted private individuals lacking authority deciding faith matters. This did not remove a subsequent council's power drawing up new symbol editions—containing not new faith but the same faith more explicitly. Every council considered that subsequent councils would expound matters more fully if heresy made this necessary. Consequently, this belongs to the Sovereign Pontiff, by whose authority the council is convoked and its decision confirmed.

### Reply to Objection 3
Athanasius drew up a faith declaration not as a symbol form but rather as doctrine exposition. Yet since it briefly contained faith's complete truth, the Sovereign Pontiff's authority accepted it as a faith rule.
