# Question 11: Heresy

## Article 1: Whether heresy is a species of unbelief?

### Objection 1
Jerome notes that heresy derives from a Greek word meaning "choice," which involves the appetitive power, not the intellect where unbelief resides.

### Objection 2
Vices take their species from their end. Since heresy's end appears to be temporal profit, especially dominion and glory (vices of pride or covetousness), heresy should be classified under these rather than unbelief.

### Objection 3
Heresy belongs to "works of the flesh" as mentioned in Galatians. Since unbelief pertains to the understanding, not flesh, heresy cannot be a species of unbelief.

### On the contrary
Falsehood opposes truth. Since heretics devise false opinions, heresy opposes the truth upon which faith rests, making it a species of unbelief.

### I answer that
Choice concerns things directed toward an end, with the end presupposed. In faith matters, the will assents to truth as its proper good. Believers assent to someone's words, with the person whose words are accepted holding primary importance like an end, while secondary truths are means to that end.

Those holding Christian faith correctly assent through will to Christ regarding what truly belongs to His doctrine. Deviation from Christian faith rectitude occurs two ways: First, unwillingness to assent to Christ entirely (characteristic of pagans and Jews). Second, intending to assent to Christ yet failing in choosing what Christ actually taught, preferring personal suggestions instead.

Therefore, heresy is a species of unbelief belonging to those professing Christian faith while corrupting its dogmas.

### Reply to Objection 1
Choice relates to unbelief similarly to how will relates to faith, as stated above.

### Reply to Objection 2
Vices derive their species from proximate ends but their genus and cause from remote ends. In heresy, adherence to one's own false opinion provides the proximate end determining its species, while pride or covetousness as remote ends reveal its cause.

### Reply to Objection 3
Heresy and sect both derive from "cutting off" or choosing. Each belongs to flesh's works not through the unbelief act itself regarding its immediate object, but through its cause—either seeking undue ends (pride/covetousness) or imagination's illusion producing error, which connects with flesh through bodily organ dependence.

## Article 2: Whether heresy is properly about matters of faith?

### Objection 1
Just as heresies existed among Jews and Pharisees, so among Christians. Yet their dissensions were not about faith matters, so heresy should not be limited to faith topics.

### Objection 2
Faith's matter is believed things. However, heresy concerns works and Scripture interpretations too. Jerome states: "Whoever expounds Scripture in any sense but that of the Holy Ghost...may be called a heretic." Therefore heresy extends beyond faith's matter.

### Objection 3
Holy doctors differed on faith matters—Augustine and Jerome on legal observance cessation—yet without heresy. So heresy does not properly concern faith matters.

### On the contrary
Augustine says those holding "mischievous and erroneous opinions" who stubbornly resist correction regarding "pernicious and deadly doctrines" contrary to faith dogmas are heretics.

### I answer that
Heresy denotes Christian faith corruption. Having false opinions on matters outside faith (geometry, for example) does not corrupt Christian faith. Only false opinions on faith matters do so.

Things belong to faith in two ways: directly and principally (articles of faith), or indirectly and secondarily (matters whose denial corrupts some faith article). Heresy can occur either way, as can faith itself.

### Reply to Objection 1
Jewish and Pharisaic heresies concerned Judaism/Pharisaism opinions, just as Christian heresies concern Christian faith matters.

### Reply to Objection 2
Distorting Scripture meaning contrary to what the Holy Ghost revealed constitutes improper exposition. Confession being a faith act means inordinate words about faith matters may corrupt faith.

### Reply to Objection 3
Augustine states that opinions held without obstinate fervor—with truth-seeking and readiness to correct upon discovering truth—should not be deemed heretical. Doctors differed on inconsequential matters or undefined faith matters. Once Church authority defined them, obstinate denial would constitute heresy. The Sovereign Pontiff chiefly holds this authority.

## Article 3: Whether heretics ought to be tolerated?

### Objection 1
Paul commands modest admonition of those resisting truth "if peradventure God may give them repentance." If heretics face death, they lose repentance opportunity, contradicting apostolic command.

### Objection 2
Paul states: "There must be...heresies, that they...who are reproved, may be manifest among you." So heretics warrant tolerance.

### Objection 3
Christ commanded servants to suffer cockle growth until harvest (world's end). Holy men explain cockle signifies heretics. Therefore heretics should be tolerated.

### On the contrary
Paul commands: "A man that is a heretic, after the first and second admonition, avoid."

### I answer that
Two points require observation: the heretic's side and the Church's side. From the heretic's perspective, their sin deserves not only excommunication but death. Corrupting faith quickening souls surpasses forging money supporting temporal life. Secular authority condemns counterfeiters to death; heretics deserve greater condemnation.

From the Church's side, mercy seeks the wanderer's conversion, so she does not condemn immediately but follows apostolic direction: "after the first and second admonition." If stubbornness continues, the Church, abandoning conversion hope, seeks others' salvation by excommunicating and separating the heretic, then delivering him to secular authority for death.

Jerome comments on Galatians 5:9, "A little leaven": "Cut off the decayed flesh, expel the mangy sheep from the fold, lest the whole house, the whole paste, the whole body, the whole flock, burn, perish, rot, die."

### Reply to Objection 1
The modesty Paul commands requires first and second heretic admonitions; if unwilling to retract, he must be reckoned already "subverted," per the apostle's words.

### Reply to Objection 2
Heresy's profit—strengthening faithful constancy and prompting careful Scripture study—occurs beside heretics' intention. What they intend is faith corruption, causing great harm. We should consider their direct intention and expel them rather than tolerate what is beside their intention.

### Reply to Objection 3
Excommunication does not mean uprooting. Paul notes excommunication aims at spirit salvation on the Lord's day. Yet complete heretic uprooting through death does not contradict Christ's command, understood as applying when cockle cannot be removed without removing wheat.

## Article 4: Whether the Church should receive those who return from heresy?

### Objection 1
Scripture states: "Thou hast prostituted thyself to many lovers; nevertheless return to Me saith the Lord." Since Church judgment is God's judgment, those guilty of unbelief's spiritual prostitution should be received.

### Objection 2
Christ commanded Peter forgiving his offending brother "not...till seven times, but till seventy times seven times," meaning forgiveness for each sin. Therefore Church should receive heretics as often as they relapse.

### Objection 3
Heresy is unbelief. Other unbelievers wishing conversion are received. Therefore heretics should be received too.

### On the contrary
The Decretal states those relapsing into abjured error must face secular tribunal. They should not be received by Church.

### I answer that
Following Christ's institution, the Church extends charity to all—friends and persecutors alike. Charity involves wishing and working neighbors' good. Good is twofold: spiritual (soul health, charity's chief object) and temporal (bodily life, possessions, reputation, dignity).

Regarding spiritual good, heretics returning however often receive Church admission to Penance, opening salvation's way. Regarding temporal good, charity does not bind us wishing such good except relating to eternal salvation. If one individual's temporal good becomes eternal salvation's obstacle for many, charity does not demand wishing that good.

If heretics were always received preserving their lives and temporal goods, this might prejudice others' salvation. They might infect others if relapsing, and escaping punishment might assure others of heresy safety.

The Church admits first-time heretic returners to Penance, safeguards their lives, and sometimes restores ecclesiastical dignities if conversion appears sincere—often done for peace's sake. However, falling again after reception indicates faith inconstancy. Readmitted returners face Penance admission but are not freed from death's penalty.

### Reply to Objection 1
God's tribunal always receives sincere returners, as God searches hearts. The Church cannot imitate this, presuming relapsed returners are not sincere. She does not bar salvation's way but neither protects from death's sentence.

### Reply to Objection 2
Christ addressed Peter regarding sins against oneself—always forgivable with sparing repentant brothers. These words do not apply to sins against neighbors or God, left not to our discretion.

### Reply to Objection 3
Other unconverted unbelievers show no faith inconstancy signs like relapsed heretics do; therefore the comparison fails.
