# Question 10: Unbelief in General

## Article 1: Whether unbelief is a sin?

### Objection 1
Unbelief appears not to be a sin because every sin contradicts nature, yet Augustine states that "to be capable of having faith...is natural to all men; whereas to have faith...belongs to the grace of the faithful." Therefore, lacking faith is not sinful.

### Objection 2
No one sins through what cannot be avoided, since all sin is voluntary. Since faith requires preaching first, unbelief cannot be avoided and thus is not sinful.

### Objection 3
The seven capital sins encompass all sins, yet unbelief does not fit within them, so unbelief is not a sin.

### On the contrary
Faith is a virtue, and unbelief opposes it. Therefore unbelief is a sin.

### I answer that
Unbelief can be understood two ways. First, as mere negation—lacking faith without resisting it—which bears the character of punishment rather than sin, particularly in those never exposed to the faith. Second, unbelief as active opposition to faith, when someone "refuses to hear the faith, or despises it." This second understanding completes the notion of unbelief as sin.

Those lacking faith through never hearing it are not damned for unbelief itself but for other sins they cannot remedy without faith. As Christ said, "If I had not come, and spoken to them, they would not have sin"—referring specifically to disbelief in Christ.

### Reply to Objection 1
Human nature includes the capacity that "man's mind should not thwart his inner instinct, and the outward preaching of the truth." In this respect, unbelief contradicts nature.

### Reply to Objection 2
This objection treats unbelief as pure negation.

### Reply to Objection 3
Unbelief as sin arises from pride, through which "man is unwilling to subject his intellect to the rules of faith." Alternatively, vices opposed to theological virtues are not reduced to capital vices but precede them.

## Article 2: Whether unbelief is in the intellect as its subject?

### Objection 1
All sin resides in the will according to Augustine, so if unbelief is sin, it must be in the will, not the intellect.

### Objection 2
Unbelief is sinful through contempt of faith's preaching, and contempt pertains to the will.

### Objection 3
Augustine's gloss indicates that rectitude of will determines whether error is serious—suggesting the will, not intellect, is the seat of unbelief's sin.

### On the contrary
Things contrary to each other reside in the same subject. Faith resides in the intellect, so unbelief must also.

### I answer that
Sin occurs in the power that is the principle of the sinful act. While the will is sin's first universal principle (since all sin is voluntary), each sin has a proper proximate principle. For unbelief, the proper act is dissent, which is an intellectual act moved by the will.

Therefore, unbelief resides in the intellect as its proximate subject, while the will is its first moving principle—the same relationship as faith bears to the intellect.

### Reply to Objection 1
This clarifies how unbelief relates to both powers.

### Reply to Objection 2
The will's contempt causes the intellect's dissent, which completes unbelief. The cause is in the will; unbelief itself is in the intellect.

### Reply to Objection 3
One believing a wicked angel to be good has not dissented from a matter of faith because "his mind does not depart from a true and right judgment." However, adhering to Satan toward wickedness and error is not without sin.

## Article 3: Whether unbelief is the greatest of sins?

### Objection 1
Augustine hesitated whether a very wicked Catholic should be preferred to a heretic with no other fault, suggesting unbelief is not absolutely greatest.

### Objection 2
Unbelief excuses or diminishes sin, as Paul's persecution in unbelief received mercy because done "ignorantly in unbelief."

### Objection 3
Greater sins deserve greater punishment. Yet believers deserve worse punishment than unbelievers for the same act, so unbelief is not greatest.

### On the contrary
Augustine states that infidelity is "the sin to which all others may be traced," making it singularly great.

### I answer that
Every sin formally consists in aversion from God. The greater the separation from God, the graver the sin. Unbelief separates one most from God because it prevents true knowledge of Him. False knowledge of God keeps one from approaching Him.

Therefore, unbelief is greater than any sin involving moral corruption alone, though not necessarily greater than sins against other theological virtues, which merit separate consideration.

### Reply to Objection 1
A sin graver in kind may be lesser in specific circumstances. The heretic's graver sin can be diminished by circumstances, while the Catholic's sin can be aggravated.

### Reply to Objection 2
Unbelief includes ignorance as accessory and resistance to faith as principal. The resistance makes it grave; the ignorance provides some excuse, especially when not from malice.

### Reply to Objection 3
An unbeliever receives greater punishment for unbelief itself than other sinners receive for any single sin. However, when comparing the same act (like adultery), the believer sins more gravely due to knowledge of truth through faith and insult to the faith's sacraments.

## Article 4: Whether every act of an unbeliever is a sin?

### Objection 1
A gloss on Romans 14:23 states "the whole life of unbelievers is a sin," implying every action is sinful.

### Objection 2
Faith directs intention, and no good comes without right intention, so unbelievers cannot perform good actions.

### Objection 3
Since faith precedes all virtuous acts, and unbelievers lack faith, they cannot do good works.

### On the contrary
Cornelius, while unbelieving, had his alms deemed acceptable to God.

### I answer that
Mortal sin removes sanctifying grace but does not wholly corrupt natural goodness. Unbelievers lack grace but retain some natural good. They cannot perform meritorious works requiring grace, yet can perform naturally good acts within their natural capacity.

Just as believers can commit actual sin without referring it to faith's end, unbelievers can perform good deeds in matters not referred to their unbelief.

### Reply to Objection 1
The saying means unbelievers' lives cannot be sinless (since no sin is removed without faith) or that actions flowing from unbelief are sinful. The authority adds: "everyone that lives or acts according to his unbelief, sins grievously."

### Reply to Objection 2
Faith directs intention toward the supernatural end; natural reason can direct intention toward a connatural good.

### Reply to Objection 3
Unbelief does not completely destroy natural reason, leaving some knowledge of truth enabling generically good deeds. Regarding Cornelius: he was not truly an unbeliever (his works would not please God else), but held implicit faith before the Gospel was fully revealed. Peter was sent to instruct him fully.

## Article 5: Whether there are several species of unbelief?

### Objection 1
Since faith and unbelief are contrary about the same object (the First Truth), and species derive from formal, not material principles, unbelief should not have multiple species based on different errors.

### Objection 2
Error can occur infinitely, so if species corresponded to various errors, unbelief would have infinite species, making their study impossible.

### Objection 3
The same person cannot belong to different species, yet one person can err about multiple truths, so diversity of errors does not create species diversity.

### On the contrary
Multiple vices oppose each virtue because "good happens in one way, but evil in many ways." Since faith is a virtue, multiple species of vice oppose it.

### I answer that
Conformity to a rule occurs one way; breach occurs many ways. Thus many vices oppose one virtue. Vices opposed to each virtue can be considered as having determinate species by their different relations to the virtue, or infinite species by corruption of various conditions required for the virtue.

Regarding unbelief: if considered in comparison to faith, there are determinate species. Since unbelief resists faith, this occurs either before faith is accepted (pagans and heathens), or after acceptance by corrupting the figure of truth (Jews), or by corrupting the very manifestation of truth (heretics). Thus three species may be reckoned generally.

If species are distinguished by various errors in matters of faith, species are not determinate but infinite, as Augustine observes.

### Reply to Objection 1
Sin's formal aspect can be considered as the sinner's intention (determining species) or as evil (a privation). Unbelief's formal aspect as that to which it turns (false opinion) determines its species. Thus, like charity is one in adhering to sovereign good while multiple vices turn from it to various temporal goods, faith is one in adhering to First Truth while multiple species of unbelief follow various false opinions.

### Reply to Objection 2
This considers species according to various error points.

### Reply to Objection 3
Faith is one because it believes many things in relation to one Truth, so unbelief, erring in many things, remains one insofar as all relate to one. Yet one person may hold various unbelief species, as one may have various vices and diseases.

## Article 6: Whether the unbelief of pagans or heathens is graver than other kinds?

### Objection 1
The most important in faith is belief in God's unity, which heathens deny by believing in many gods, so their unbelief is gravest.

### Objection 2
More detestable heresies contradict more and more important truth points. Heathens deny faith entirely, contradicting more points than Jews or heretics, so their unbelief is gravest.

### Objection 3
Good diminishes evil. Jews have good in accepting the Old Testament; heretics in accepting the New Testament; heathens accept neither, so they sin most gravely.

### On the contrary
2 Peter 2:21 states it is worse to turn back after knowing the way of justice than never to have known it. Heathens have not known it; heretics and Jews have abandoned it after knowing it. Therefore their sin is graver.

### I answer that
Two considerations apply to unbelief. First, its relation to faith: one who resists faith after accepting it sins more gravely than one who resists without accepting it—like failing to fulfill a promise is worse than never promising. Thus heretics who profess Gospel faith while corrupting it sin more gravely than Jews who never accepted Gospel faith. Since Jews accepted the Old Law's figure, their unbelief is graver than heathens who accepted nothing.

Second, corruption of matters of faith: since heathens err on more points than Jews, and these on more than heretics (except extreme cases like Manichees), heathen unbelief is graver by this measure.

However, the first gravity—guilt from resisting faith—surpasses the second from its mere absence, since resistance is what constitutes unbelief's guilt. Therefore, speaking absolutely, heretic unbelief is worst.

## Article 7: Whether one ought to dispute with unbelievers in public?

### Objection 1
Paul commands "contend not in words, for it is to no profit, but to the subverting of the hearers." Public dispute with unbelievers necessarily involves contentious words.

### Objection 2
An imperial law confirmed by canons forbids debating matters the holy councils have decided, which includes all faith matters.

### Objection 3
Arguments presume doubt about a matter, but faith matters are certain and should not be doubted.

### On the contrary
Acts recounts Paul strengthening his arguments against Jews and disputing with Greeks.

### I answer that
Two considerations apply to faith disputes: the disputant's intention and the hearers' condition.

Regarding the disputant: if disputing as though doubting faith's truth rather than holding it certain while confuting errors, he would sin as a doubter and unbeliever. Disputing to refute errors is praiseworthy.

Regarding hearers: well-instructed, firm believers face no danger from faith disputes. For simple, wavering believers: if persecuted by unbelievers attempting to corrupt their faith, public dispute is necessary (provided competent defenders exist) to strengthen them and prevent unbeliever deception. Silence would strengthen error. However, where no such pressure exists and simple people have never heard dissenting views, public disputation is dangerous since their firm faith stems from never hearing opposition.

### Reply to Objection 1
Paul forbids disorderly disputations of contentious words, not all sound disputes.

### Reply to Objection 2
The law forbids disputations arising from doubting faith, not those safeguarding it.

### Reply to Objection 3
Faith disputes confirm it and confute errors. As Peter 3:15 commands being "ready always to satisfy everyone that asketh you a reason of that hope and faith." Titus 1:9 requires ability "to convince the gainsayers."

## Article 8: Whether unbelievers ought to be compelled to the faith?

### Objection 1
Matthew 13:28-30 (the cockle parable) with Chrysostom's gloss forbids slaying heretics lest innocent wheat be destroyed, so unbelievers should not be compelled.

### Objection 2
A synod's decree states regarding Jews: "for the future, none are to be compelled to believe."

### Objection 3
Augustine states "it is possible for a man to do other things against his will, but he cannot believe unless he is willing," so unbelievers should not be compelled.

### Objection 4
God declares "I desire not the death of the sinner," and we should conform our will to God's, so we should not desire unbelievers' death.

### On the contrary
Luke 14:23 commands: "Compel them to come in," referring to entering God's house through faith.

### I answer that
Two categories of unbelievers exist. First, those never receiving faith (heathens and Jews) should never be compelled to believe, since belief depends on will. However, they should be compelled if possible to prevent hindering faith through blasphemy, evil persuasion, or persecution. Christ's faithful wage war with unbelievers not to force belief but to prevent faith obstruction.

Second, those who accepted faith then rejected it (heretics and apostates) should be compelled, even bodily, to fulfill their promise and hold what they received.

### Reply to Objection 1
The parable forbids not excommunication but slaying heretics, as Chrysostom clarifies. Augustine later acknowledged that law-fear proved beneficial. When a heretic's crime is publicly known and hateful with no defenders threatening schism, discipline should not slacken.

### Reply to Objection 2
Jews receiving no faith should not be compelled; those receiving it should be compelled to keep it.

### Reply to Objection 3
Like taking a vow is voluntary but keeping it obligatory, accepting faith is voluntary but keeping received faith is obligatory. Augustine told Count Boniface that Christ compelled Paul, then taught him.

### Reply to Objection 4
Augustine explains that just as David's house needed peace requiring Absalom's death in rebellion, the Catholic Church's maternal sorrow is healed by deliverance of many nations.

## Article 9: Whether it is lawful to communicate with unbelievers?

### Objection 1
Paul permits eating at an unbeliever's table if invited; Chrysostom permits dining with pagans "without reservation," showing communication with unbelievers is lawful.

### Objection 2
Paul asks "What have I to do to judge them that are without?" If the Church forbids communication as punishment, it should not apply to unbelievers outside the Church.

### Objection 3
Masters must communicate with unbelieving servants through commands. Christians can have Jewish, pagan, or Saracen servants, so communication with unbelievers is lawful.

### On the contrary
Deuteronomy 7:2-3 forbids leagues and mercy toward certain nations; a gloss on Leviticus states we must shun idolaters and their disciples.

### I answer that
Communication prohibition has two bases: punishing the person communicated with, or protecting those who communicate. Both appear in Paul's excommunication reasoning.

Regarding the first: the Church does not forbid communicating with unbelievers never receiving Christian faith (pagans and Jews) as punishment since it lacks spiritual authority over them, only temporal judgment for misdeeds. However, as punishment, the Church forbids communicating with those who received faith then rejected it (heretics and apostates) through excommunication.

Regarding the second: distinction by persons' firmness in faith is necessary. Those firm in faith may communicate with heathens or Jews, as conversion is more likely than aversion. Simple, weak believers should avoid communication, especially intimate familiarity without necessity.

### Reply to Objection 1
This suffices for the first objection's reply.

### Reply to Objection 2
The Church exercises judgment over some unbelievers regarding temporal punishment, withdrawing faithful communion for specific grave sins.

### Reply to Objection 3
A servant ruled by his believing master is more likely converted than the reverse, so faithful need not dismiss unbelieving servants. If danger through such communication exists, the master should remove the servant per Christ's command about scandalizing members.

## Article 10: Whether unbelievers may have authority or dominion over the faithful?

### Objection 1
Paul commands servants under unbelieving masters "count their masters worthy of all honor," and Peter requires subjection "not only to the good and gentle, but also to the froward," establishing unbelievers' authority.

### Objection 2
Members of Caesar's household are his subjects. Saints were in Nero's (an unbeliever's) household, so unbelievers have authority over faithful.

### Objection 3
A slave is his master's instrument for everyday life. Believers can work on unbelievers' farms, so unbelievers have dominion over faithful.

### On the contrary
Those in authority judge those under them. But Paul forbids faithful going before unbelieving judges. Therefore unbelievers cannot have authority over faithful.

### I answer that
This question requires two considerations.

First, establishing new unbelieving authority over faithful should not be allowed since it provokes scandal and endangers faith.

Second, regarding existing authority: dominion and authority are human law institutions; the faithful/unfaithful distinction arises from Divine law. Since Divine law does not abolish human law, this distinction does not eliminate unbelieving authority over faithful in itself.

However, the Church can justly abolish such authority through her Divine-granted power, since unbelievers forfeit power over faithful by unbelief. The Church sometimes does, sometimes does not. Among unbelievers subject to the Church temporally, she decreed that if a Jew's slave became Christian, the slave received freedom without payment.

However, the Church has not applied this to unbelievers not temporally subject to her, avoiding scandal.

### Reply to Objection 1
Caesar's authority preceded the faithful/unfaithful distinction, so conversion did not cancel it.

### Reply to Objection 2
The faithful in the emperor's household could defend other faithful.

### Reply to Objection 3
Slaves are subjects throughout life under overseers in everything, unlike craftsmen's laborers subject for specific works. Thus unbelieving dominion over faithful is more dangerous than their employment.

## Article 11: Whether the rites of unbelievers ought to be tolerated?

### Objection 1
Unbelievers sin observing their rites; not preventing preventable sin seems consent. Therefore tolerating their rites is sinful.

### Objection 2
Jewish rites compare to idolatry. Christian princes closed then destroyed idol temples. Therefore Jewish rites likewise should not be tolerated.

### Objection 3
Unbelief is the greatest sin. Other grave sins like adultery and theft are punished by law, not tolerated. Therefore unbelief's manifestation in rites should not be tolerated.

### On the contrary
Gregory states of Jews: "They should be allowed to observe all their feasts, just as hitherto they and their fathers have for ages observed them."

### I answer that
Human government derives from and should imitate Divine government. Although God is all-powerful and supremely good, He permits certain evils to prevent greater goods' loss or greater evils' consequence. Similarly, human authorities rightly tolerate certain evils lest certain goods be lost or greater evils ensue.

Thus, although unbelievers sin in their rites, they may be tolerated either for resulting good or avoided evil. From Jewish rite observance comes a good: their rites, foreshadowing the truth we hold, make our enemies witness our faith and represent it figuratively. Therefore they are tolerated.

Other unbelievers' rites—neither truthful nor profitable—should never be tolerated except to avoid evil like scandal or disturbance. The Church has tolerated even heretic and pagan rites when unbelievers were numerous.

## Article 12: Whether the children of Jews and other unbelievers ought to be baptized against their parents' will?

### Objection 1
Marriage bond is stronger than parental authority, yet unbelief breaks the marriage bond. Therefore unbelief dissolves parental authority, permitting children's baptism against parents' will.

### Objection 2
One should aid those in everlasting death danger more than temporal death danger. Since unbelieving parents' children face everlasting death danger, they should be taken and baptized.

### Objection 3
A bondsman's children are bondsmen under the master's power. Jews are kings' and princes' bondsmen; therefore their children are likewise, so princes can baptize Jewish children as they wish.

### Objection 4
Man belongs more to God, from Whom he has his soul, than his carnal father. Therefore Jewish children should not be unjustly withheld from baptism consecrating them to God.

### Objection 5
Baptism avails for salvation more than preaching. If omitting preaching is imputed as sin, much more is failing to baptize capable children a sin.

### On the contrary
No injustice should be done anyone. Baptizing Jewish children against parental will would unjustly deprive Jews of parental authority over Christian children. Therefore it should not occur.

### I answer that
Church custom has great authority and ought to be observed since Catholic doctors' doctrine derives its authority from the Church. The Church never customarily baptized Jews' children against parental will, though many powerful Catholic princes like Constantine and Theodosius existed, with whom very holy bishops like Sylvester and Ambrose had friendly relations who would certainly have obtained this if reasonable.

This custom has two reasons. First: danger to faith. Children baptized before reason's use, upon reaching perfect age, might easily be persuaded by parents to renounce unknowingly embraced faith—detrimental to faith itself.

Second: it contradicts natural justice. A child is naturally part of its father. Initially, it is not distinct bodily in the mother's womb; after birth and before reason's use, it is enfolded in parental care like a spiritual womb. Since man without reason differs not from irrational animals, a son before reason, according to natural law, belongs to his father. Thus taking a child before reason's use from parental custody against parents' will contradicts natural justice.

Once the child begins having free-will's use, it begins belonging to itself, capable of caring for itself regarding Divine and natural law matters, and should be persuaded (not compelled) to embrace faith. Then it can consent to faith and be baptized even against parental will; but not before reason's use.

### Reply to Objection 1
In marriage, both have free-will's use and can assent to faith independently. This does not apply to children before reason's use, but the comparison holds after reaching reason's use, if willing conversion.

### Reply to Objection 2
No one should be snatched from natural death against civil law's order. Similarly, none should break natural law's order, whereby children are fathers' custody, to rescue from everlasting death.

### Reply to Objection 3
Jews are princes' bondsmen by civil bondage, which does not exclude natural or Divine law's order.

### Reply to Objection 4
Man is directed to God through reason, whereby he can know Him. Therefore children before reason's use are naturally directed to God through parents' reason, under whose natural care they lie.

### Reply to Objection 5
Preaching omission's resulting peril threatens only those charged with preaching duty. Providing salvation's sacraments for unbelievers' children is their parents' duty. Hence danger threatens them if children, deprived of sacraments, fail obtaining salvation.
