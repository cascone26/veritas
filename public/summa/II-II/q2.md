# Question 2: The Act of Faith

## Article 1: Whether to believe is to think with assent?

### Objection 1
The Latin term "cogitatio" implies research, yet Damascene states that "faith is an assent without research." Therefore, thinking appears incompatible with faith's nature.

### Objection 2
Faith resides in reason rather than the cogitative power (which belongs to sensation). Thus thought seems disconnected from faith.

### Objection 3
Belief is an intellectual act concerning truth, while assent appears to be an act of will rather than intellect. Therefore, believing cannot be thinking with assent.

### On the contrary
Augustine defines believing precisely as "thinking with assent."

### I answer that
"Thinking" has three meanings. First, broadly as any intellectual consideration. Second, more strictly as intellection accompanied by inquiry before reaching perfect certitude. Third, as an act of the cogitative power concerning particulars.

If taken broadly, "thinking with assent" incompletely captures belief, since one thinks with assent about scientifically known conclusions. If taken in the second sense, this definition perfectly expresses belief's nature.

Among intellectual acts, some possess firm assent without inquiry (as in science or understanding). Others involve unformed thought without firm assent (as in doubt, suspicion, or opinion). The act of believing combines firm assent with unformed thought—it cleaves firmly to one side yet lacks the perfection of clear vision. Thus believing is uniquely described as "thinking with assent," distinguishing it from all other intellectual acts concerning truth.

### Reply to Objection 1
Faith lacks the natural rational research that demonstrates what is believed, but possesses inquiry regarding motivations for belief—for instance, that God has uttered these things and confirmed them through miracles.

### Reply to Objection 2
"Thinking" here signifies an intellectual act rather than an act of the cogitative power, as explained above.

### Reply to Objection 3
The believer's intellect is determined to one object not by reason but by will; thus assent here represents an intellectual act as determined by will toward a single object.

## Article 2: Whether the act of faith is suitably distinguished as believing God, believing in a God and believing in God?

### Objection 1
One habit produces one act. Since faith is a single habit and virtue, distinguishing three acts of faith seems unreasonable.

### Objection 2
That which is common to all faith acts should not be reckoned as a particular faith act. "Believing God" is common to all faith since faith rests on First Truth. Therefore, distinguishing it from other acts seems inappropriate.

### Objection 3
Unbelievers can be said to "believe in a God," yet this cannot constitute an act of faith. Therefore, it should not be numbered among faith's acts.

### Objection 4
Movement toward an end belongs to will, whose object is goodness and purpose. Since believing is an intellectual act rather than volitional, "believing in God" (implying movement toward purpose) should not be reckoned a faith species.

### On the contrary
Augustine makes precisely this distinction in his writings on the Word and Gospel sermons.

### I answer that
Every power's or habit's act depends on its relation to its object. Faith's object admits three considerations since believing is an intellectual act moved by will to assent.

Regarding intellect alone, two aspects appear in faith's object. The material object—everything proposed to belief insofar as referred to God—yields the act "believing in a God." The formal aspect—the medium of assent to faith points—yields "believing God," since First Truth is that formal object to which one adheres for assenting to whatever one believes.

When considering the object as moved by will, the act becomes "believing in God," since First Truth relates to will as an end.

### Reply to Objection 1
These three denote not different faith acts but one identical act having different relations to faith's object.

### Reply to Objection 2
This suffices as reply to the second objection.

### Reply to Objection 3
Unbelievers cannot truly "believe in a God" as faith understands it, for they do not believe God exists under conditions faith determines. As the Philosopher observes, "defectively knowing simple things means not knowing them at all."

### Reply to Objection 4
As stated previously, will moves intellect and soul powers toward their end. In this respect, an act of faith is "believing in God."

## Article 3: Whether it is necessary for salvation to believe anything above natural reason?

### Objection 1
A thing's perfection seems sufficiently secured by natural endowments. Since faith matters surpass human reason as unseen things, believing them appears unnecessary for salvation.

### Objection 2
It is dangerous assenting to matters one cannot judge true or false by tracing them to first principles. One cannot do this regarding faith matters. Therefore, believing them is dangerous and unnecessary for salvation.

### Objection 3
Man's salvation rests on God. God's invisible attributes are clearly understood through created things (Romans 1:20). What is clearly seen intellectually is not an object of belief. Therefore, believing certain things is unnecessary for salvation.

### On the contrary
Hebrews 11:6 states: "Without faith it is impossible to please God."

### I answer that
Where one nature subordinates to another, two elements perfect the lower nature: one regarding its proper movement, another regarding the higher nature's movement. Water naturally moves toward earth's center, but the moon's movement carries it in tidal circulation. Planets have proper westward motion but move eastward via the first heaven.

The rational creature alone is immediately subordinate to God. Other creatures attain only particulars, partaking divine goodness in being alone (inanimate things), or in being and living with singular knowledge (plants and animals). The rational nature, apprehending universal notions of good and being, directly relates to universal being's principle.

Rational creature perfection consists not merely in what naturally belongs to it but also in supernatural participation of divine goodness. Man's ultimate happiness consists in supernatural vision of God, to which he cannot attain unless God teaches him (John 6:45). Man acquires this learning gradually, according to his nature, and every learner must believe to acquire perfect science. The Philosopher notes "a learner must believe."

Therefore, arriving at perfect heavenly happiness requires first believing God as a disciple believes a teaching master.

### Reply to Objection 1
Since man's nature depends on a higher nature, natural knowledge does not suffice for perfection; supernatural knowledge is necessary.

### Reply to Objection 2
Just as man assents to first principles through natural intellectual light, a virtuous man judges rightly concerning virtue through virtue's habit. Similarly, through faith's light which God bestows, man assents to faith matters rather than opposing them. Consequently, "there is no condemnation to those in Christ Jesus" whom He has enlightened by faith.

### Reply to Objection 3
In many ways faith perceives God's invisible things more elevately than natural reason proceeding from creatures to God. As written: "Many things are shown to you above man's understanding."

## Article 4: Whether it is necessary to believe those things which can be proved by natural reason?

### Objection 1
Nothing superfluous appears in God's works. Using alternative means where one suffices is superfluous. Therefore, receiving by faith what natural reason can know seems wasteful.

### Objection 2
Science and faith do not address the same object. All naturally knowable things become science objects, so there appears no need believing what natural reason proves.

### Objection 3
All scientifically knowable things seem equivalent; if some are proposed as faith objects, all should be believed. But this is false. Therefore, believing naturally provable things is unnecessary.

### On the contrary
It is necessary believing God is one and incorporeal—things philosophers prove by natural reason.

### I answer that
Man must accept by faith things that both surpass reason and those knowable by reason for three reasons.

First, man arrives more quickly at divine truth knowledge. The science proving God's existence comes last in human research, requiring preceding sciences, so man would arrive late in life at God's knowledge through reason alone.

Second, knowledge becomes more general. Many cannot advance in science through mental dullness, occupations, temporal needs, or learning laziness. All these would lack God's knowledge unless divine things came to them through faith's guise.

Third, for certitude's sake. Human reason is very deficient regarding God. Philosophers investigating human affairs have fallen into many errors and disagreed among themselves. Therefore, for men to possess certain, doubt-free God knowledge, divine matters necessarily came through faith, as if God Himself told them, Who cannot deceive.

### Reply to Objection 1
Natural reason's researches do not suffice mankind for divine knowledge even of provable matters; thus believing these is not superfluous.

### Reply to Objection 2
Science and faith cannot be in the same subject concerning the same object, yet what is one person's science object can be another's faith object.

### Reply to Objection 3
Although all scientifically knowable things share one scientific aspect, they do not equally lead man to beatitude; hence not all are equally proposed for belief.

## Article 5: Whether man is bound to believe anything explicitly?

### Objection 1
No man is bound doing what is not within his power. Believing explicitly is not in man's power, since "how shall they believe Him of whom they have not heard? How shall they hear without a preacher? How shall they preach unless they be sent?" (Romans 10:14-15). Therefore, man is not bound believing anything explicitly.

### Objection 2
Just as faith directs us to God, so does charity. Man is not bound keeping charity precepts but merely being ready for them, as evident from "turn to him also the other cheek." Therefore, man need only be ready to believe what God proposes, without believing anything explicitly.

### Objection 3
Faith's goodness consists in obedience: "obedience to the faith in all nations." Obedience virtue does not require keeping fixed precepts but only readiness. Therefore, faith requires only readiness to believe whatever God may propose, without explicit belief.

### On the contrary
Hebrews 11:6 states: "He that comes to God must believe that He is, and is a rewarder to them that seek Him."

### I answer that
Law precepts concerning virtuous acts (means to salvation) depend on the habit-object relation. Virtue acts require terminating in the virtue's proper direct object.

Two things appear in any virtue's object: the proper direct object and the accidental, secondary object. Fortitude's proper object is facing death dangers and charging enemies with self-endangerment for common good. That a soldier be armed or strike with his sword relates to fortitude indirectly.

As virtuous acts are required for precept fulfillment, the virtuous act must terminate in its proper direct object. However, precept fulfillment does not require terminating in things having accidental or secondary relation to the proper object, except in certain places and times.

Faith's direct object comprises that which makes one blessed, as stated previously. Its indirect, secondary object comprises all things delivered by God in Holy Scripture—for instance, that Abraham had two sons or David was Jesse's son.

Therefore, regarding faith's primary articles, man is bound believing them just as bound having faith. Regarding other faith points, man is not bound explicit belief but only implicit belief or readiness to believe whatever Scripture contains. One is bound believing explicitly only when it becomes clear these things are contained in faith doctrine.

### Reply to Objection 1
If only things doable without grace count as within man's power, man is bound doing many things impossible without healing grace—like loving God and neighbor and believing faith articles. With grace's aid, this is possible, "for to whomsoever it is mercifully given from above is mercifully given; from whom it is withheld it is justly withheld, as punishment for previous or original sin."

### Reply to Objection 2
Man is bound definitely loving those lovable things which are charity's proper direct objects—namely God and neighbor. The objection concerns charity precepts which are consequence rather than direct objects of charity.

### Reply to Objection 3
Obedience virtue properly resides in will; thus will's readiness in subjection to authority suffices for obedience's act as its proper direct object. But particular precepts are accidental or consequent to that direct object.

## Article 6: Whether all are equally bound to have explicit faith?

### Objection 1
All are bound what is necessary for salvation, as charity precepts evidence. Since certain explicit beliefs are salvifically necessary, all are equally bound having explicit faith.

### Objection 2
None should be tested in matters he is not bound believing. Yet simple persons are sometimes tested on slight faith articles. Therefore, all are bound believing everything explicitly.

### Objection 3
If simple persons are bound only implicit rather than explicit faith, their faith must be implied in the learned's faith. But this seems unsafe since the learned can err. Therefore, simple persons should also have explicit faith, making all equally obligated.

### On the contrary
Job 1:14 states: "The oxen were ploughing, and asses feeding beside them." Gregory explains the asses signify the simple, who should stay by the learned (oxen) in faith matters.

### I answer that
Faith matters' unfolding results from divine revelation, which surpasses natural reason. Divine revelation reaches lower-degree persons through their superiors in order: men through angels, lower angels through higher ones, as Dionysius explains.

Therefore, faith's unfolding necessarily reaches lower-degree men through higher-degree ones. Thus, like higher angels possessing fuller divine knowledge than lower angels as Dionysius states, higher-degree men whose business is teaching others are obligated fuller faith knowledge and more explicit belief.

### Reply to Objection 1
Faith articles' unfolding is not equally necessary for all salvation, since higher-degree persons whose duty is teaching others must believe explicitly more things than others must.

### Reply to Objection 2
Simple persons should not be tested about subtle faith questions unless suspected of heretical corruption, which typically corrupts simple people's faith. If found free from obstinate heterodox sentiment due to simplicity, it is no fault of theirs.

### Reply to Objection 3
The simple have faith implied in the learned's only insofar as the latter adhere to divine teaching. Thus the Apostle says: "Be followers of me, as I also am of Christ." Therefore, divine truth rather than human knowledge rules faith; if learned persons stray from this rule, they do not harm simple ones' faith if these believe the learned are right—unless the simple obstinately hold individual errors against the universal Church's faith, which cannot err, since Our Lord said to Peter: "I have prayed for you that your faith fail not."

## Article 7: Whether it is necessary for the salvation of all, that they should believe explicitly in the mystery of Christ?

### Objection 1
Man is not bound believing what angels are ignorant about, since faith's unfolding results from divine revelation reaching man through angels. Yet angels were ignorant of the Incarnation mystery. Therefore, men were not bound explicitly believing Christ's Incarnation mystery.

### Objection 2
John the Baptist was a teacher nearest Christ, regarding whom Christ said none born of women surpassed him. Yet John appears not to have explicitly known Christ's mystery, asking Him: "Art Thou He that art to come, or look we for another?" Therefore, even teachers were not bound explicit Christ faith.

### Objection 3
Many gentiles obtained salvation through angels' ministry, as Dionysius states. Yet gentiles apparently had neither explicit nor implicit Christ faith, having received no revelation. Therefore, it was unnecessary for all salvation to explicitly believe Christ's mystery.

### On the contrary
Augustine states: "Our faith is sound if we believe no man, old or young, is delivered from death's contagion and sin's bonds except by the one Mediator of God and men, Jesus Christ."

### I answer that
As stated previously, faith's object properly includes that thing through which man obtains beatitude. Christ's Incarnation and Passion mystery comprises the way men obtain beatitude, for "there is no other name under heaven given to men whereby we must be saved." Therefore, belief in some form of Christ's mystery was necessary at all times and for all persons, though this belief differed according to times and persons.

Before sin's state, man explicitly believed Christ's Incarnation insofar as intended for glory's consummation, but not as intended delivering from sin through Passion and Resurrection, since man had no foreknowledge of future sin. However, he apparently foreknew the Incarnation from saying: "Wherefore a man shall leave father and mother, and shall cleave to his wife," which the Apostle says is a great mystery "in Christ and the Church." It is incredible the first man was ignorant of this sacrament.

After sin, man explicitly believed Christ not only as to Incarnation but also as to Passion and Resurrection, whereby the human race is delivered from sin and death. They would not foreshadow Christ's Passion through certain sacrifices both before and after the Law otherwise. The learned knew these sacrifices' meaning explicitly; simple folk, veiled under sacrifice, believed them ordained by God regarding Christ's coming, thus their knowledge was veiled. As stated previously, nearer ones to Christ possessed more distinct mystery knowledge.

After grace was revealed, both learned and simple are bound explicit faith in Christ's mysteries, especially those publicly proclaimed throughout the Church—particularly articles referring to the Incarnation mentioned above. Regarding other detailed Incarnation points, men are bound believing more or less explicitly according to individual state and office.

### Reply to Objection 1
The Kingdom of God's mystery was not entirely hidden from angels, as Augustine observes, yet certain aspects were better known to them when Christ revealed them.

### Reply to Objection 2
John the Baptist did not inquire from ignorance of Christ's coming in flesh, since he clearly professed belief saying: "I saw, and I gave testimony, that this is the Son of God." Thus he did not ask "Art Thou He that hast come?" but "Art Thou He that art to come?" regarding the future, not past. Likewise, he was not ignorant of Christ's future Passion, having already said: "Behold the Lamb of God, behold Him who taketh away the sin of the world," thus foretelling future immolation. Since other prophets foretold this, we may say with Gregory that he inquired whether Christ would descend into hell in His own Person. But he need not have explicitly believed before its fulfillment that Christ Himself would go there.

One might also reply, as Ambrose observes, that John made this inquiry not from doubt or ignorance but from devotion. Or with Chrysostom, that he inquired not ignorantly himself but because he wished his disciples satisfied on this point through Christ. Therefore, Christ framed His answer instructing the disciples through His works' signs.

### Reply to Objection 3
Many gentiles received Christ revelations, as their predictions show. Thus Job states: "I know that my Redeemer liveth." The Sibyl too foretold Christ things, as Augustine states. We read that at Constantine and Irene's time, a tomb was discovered containing a man on whose golden breast plate was inscribed: "Christ shall be born of a virgin, and in Him I believe."

If however some were saved without any revelation, they were not saved without faith in a Mediator. Though not explicitly believing, they had implicit faith through believing in divine providence—that God would deliver mankind according to His will and according to the Spirit's revelation to truth-knowing ones, as stated in Job 35:11: "Who teacheth us more than the beasts of the earth."

## Article 8: Whether it is necessary for salvation to believe explicitly in the Trinity?

### Objection 1
The Apostle states those coming to God must "believe that He is, and is a rewarder to them that seek Him." One can believe this without believing the Trinity. Therefore, Trinity explicit belief was unnecessary for salvation.

### Objection 2
Our Lord said: "Father, I have manifested Thy name to men," which Augustine expounds as meaning not the name "God" but "My Father." Augustine adds: "God is known to all nations through His world-making; God is known in Judea as not worshipped with false gods; but God as this Christ's Father, taking away the world's sin, is made known to men now, which they previously knew not." Therefore, before Christ's coming, that Paternity and Filiation existed in Godhead was unknown and not explicitly believed.

### Objection 3
That which we must explicitly believe regarding God is heavenly happiness' object. Now sovereign good, understood to be in God, can be conceived without personal distinction. Therefore, Trinity explicit belief was unnecessary for salvation.

### On the contrary
The Old Testament expresses the Trinity of Persons in many ways; Genesis 1:26 says: "Let us make man to Our image and likeness." Therefore, believing the Trinity was necessary from the beginning for salvation.

### I answer that
Explicitly believing Christ's mystery is impossible without Trinity faith, since the mystery includes that God's Son took flesh, that He renewed the world through the Holy Ghost's grace, and that He was conceived by the Holy Ghost.

Therefore, just as before Christ, the learned explicitly believed Christ's mystery while simple folk believed it implicitly and veiled, so too with the Trinity's mystery. Consequently, when grace was revealed, all were bound explicit Trinity mystery faith. All reborn in Christ receive this through the Trinity's invocation in baptism, as Matthew 28:19 states: "Baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son and of the Holy Ghost."

### Reply to Objection 1
Explicit faith in those two things was necessary at all times and for all people, but was not sufficient at all times and for all people.

### Reply to Objection 2
Before Christ's coming, Trinity faith lay hidden in the learned's faith, but through Christ and the apostles it was shown to the world.

### Reply to Objection 3
God's sovereign goodness as we now understand it through effects can be understood without Trinity Persons; but as understood in itself and as seen by the Blessed, it cannot be understood without Persons Trinity. Moreover, divine Persons' mission brings us to heavenly happiness.

## Article 9: Whether to believe is meritorious?

### Objection 1
Charity is the principle of all merit. Now faith, like nature, is charity's preamble. Just as natural acts are not meritorious since we do not merit by natural gifts, neither is faith's act meritorious.

### Objection 2
Belief is a mean between opinion and scientific knowledge. Scientific consideration is not meritorious, nor is opinion meritorious. Therefore, belief is not meritorious.

### Objection 3
He assenting to a faith point either has sufficient believing motive or not. If he has sufficient motive, this seems to imply no merit on his part, since he is not free believing or not. If he lacks sufficient motive, this marks levity (Sirach 19:4: "He that is hasty to give credit, is light of heart"), so he gains no merit. Therefore, believing is by no means meritorious.

### On the contrary
Hebrews 11:33 states the saints "by faith obtained promises," which would not occur if they did not merit by believing.

### I answer that
As stated previously, our actions are meritorious insofar as they proceed from free-will moved by grace from God. Therefore, every human act proceeding from free-will, if referred to God, can be meritorious. Now the act of believing is an intellectual act assenting to divine truth at the will's command moved by God's grace, so it is subject to free-will regarding God. Consequently, faith's act can be meritorious.

### Reply to Objection 1
Nature compares to charity (merit's principle) as matter to form, while faith compares to charity as preceding disposition to ultimate form. The subject or matter cannot act except by the form's virtue, nor can a preceding disposition before form's advent. But after the form's advent, both subject and preceding disposition act by the form's virtue, which is the chief action principle. Just as fire's heat acts by fire's substantial form virtue, neither nature nor faith can produce a meritorious act without charity. But when accompanied by charity, faith's act is made meritorious thereby, just as nature's act and the free-will's natural act.

### Reply to Objection 2
Science has two aspects: the scientist's assent to scientific fact and his consideration thereof. Science's assent is not subject to free-will because the scientist must assent through demonstration force, so scientific assent is not meritorious. But actual consideration of what one scientifically knows is subject to free-will since considering or not considering is his choice. Hence scientific consideration can be meritorious if referred to charity's end—God's honor or neighbor's good.

In faith's case, both aspects are subject to free-will, so both respects permit the faith act to be meritorious. In opinion's case, there is no firm assent since it is weak and infirm, as the Philosopher observes, so it does not seem proceeding from the will's perfect act. Thus regarding assent, it does not appear very meritorious, though it can be regarding actual consideration.

### Reply to Objection 3
The believer has sufficient believing motive, for he is moved by divine teaching authority confirmed by miracles, and moreover by divine invitation's inward instinct. Hence he does not believe lightly. He lacks sufficient reason for scientific knowledge, however, so he does not lose merit.

## Article 10: Whether reasons in support of what we believe lessen the merit of faith?

### Objection 1
Gregory states: "There is no merit in believing what reason shows." If human reason provides sufficient proof, faith merit is wholly taken away. Therefore, any human reasoning supporting faith matters diminishes believing's merit.

### Objection 2
Whatever lessens virtue's measure lessens merit amount, since "happiness is virtue's reward." Human reasoning diminishes faith's virtue measure, since faith's essence involves the unseen. The more something is supported by reasons, the less unseen it is. Therefore, human reasons supporting faith matters diminish faith's merit.

### Objection 3
Contrary things have contrary causes. An opposition to faith increases faith merit whether through persecution forcing renunciation or through arguing persuasion. Therefore, supporting reasons diminish faith merit.

### On the contrary
First Peter 3:15 states: "Being ready always to satisfy every one that asketh you a reason of that faith which is in you." The Apostle would not give this advice if it implied faith merit's diminution.

### I answer that
As stated previously, faith's act is meritorious insofar as subject to will. Now human reason supporting what we believe may stand in twofold relation to the believer's will.

First, as preceding the will's act—for instance, when a man either lacks will or lacks prompt will to believe unless moved by human reasons. In this way human reason diminishes faith's merit. It has been said that in moral virtues, a preceding passion makes the virtuous act less praiseworthy. Just as man ought performing moral virtue acts from reason's judgment, not from passion, he ought believing faith matters from divine authority, not from human reason.

Second, human reasons may be consequent to the believer's will. When a man's will is ready believing, he loves the truth he believes, he thinks out and takes to heart whatever supporting reasons he can find. In this way human reason does not exclude faith's merit but signs greater merit. Thus again in moral virtues, a consequent passion signs a more prompt will, as stated. We have indication thereof in the Samaritans saying to the woman (who types human reason): "We now believe, not for thy saying."

### Reply to Objection 1
Gregory refers to the case of a man unwilling believing faith matters unless induced by reasons. But when a man is willing believing what is of faith on God's authority alone, though he may have reasons demonstrating some matters (as God's existence), his faith's merit is not thereby lost or diminished.

### Reply to Objection 2
The reasons brought forward in faith's authority support are not demonstrations producing intellectual vision to the human intellect; therefore they do not cease being unseen.

But they remove faith obstacles by showing what faith proposes is not impossible. Thus such reasons do not diminish faith merit or measure. However, demonstrative reasons supporting faith preambles (though not faith articles) diminish faith's measure since they make the believed thing seen. Yet they do not diminish charity's measure, which makes the will ready believing them even if unseen. Therefore, merit's measure is not diminished.

### Reply to Objection 3
Whatever opposes faith, whether in thoughts or outward persecution, increases faith merit insofar as will shows itself more prompt and firm in believing. Thus martyrs had greater faith merit through not renouncing faith through persecution. Even the wise have greater faith merit through not renouncing faith through philosophers' or heretics' opposing reasons.

However, things favorable to faith do not always diminish the will's promptness for believing, and therefore do not always diminish faith merit.
